# The City of Peace: The Founding of Baghdad

## Al-Mansur and the Search for a Capital

The Abbasid Caliph al-Mansur Abu Jafar (may Allah have mercy on him), who ruled from 136 to 158 AH / 754 to 775 CE, was in many respects the true founder of the Abbasid state. While his brother Abul Abbas al-Saffah had been the first Abbasid Caliph and presided over the defeat of the Umayyads, al-Mansur was the man who gave the new caliphate its permanent character — its institutions, its capital, its administrative structures, and its claim to be the center of the civilized world.

For the first years of his caliphate, al-Mansur governed from Anbar, on the Euphrates, and later from Hashimiyya near Kufa. But the political geography of Iraq presented problems: Kufa was a hotbed of Alid sympathy and Shia political organizing, having been the base of Ali ibn Abi Talib's (may Allah be pleased with him) caliphate and the city from which Husayn's fateful journey to Karbala had been encouraged. A capital too close to Kufa would mean governing under constant pressure from populations with complicated loyalties.

Al-Mansur needed a site that was administratively ideal, militarily defensible, economically advantageous, and politically neutral. He found it in 145 AH / 762 CE: a location on the western bank of the Tigris River, about thirty kilometers north of Ctesiphon (the former Sassanid capital), at a point where the Tigris and Euphrates came closest to each other. The area was called Madinat al-Salam — the City of Peace — though it quickly became known as Baghdad, from the Persian name of a village that had occupied the site.

## The Site Selection

Al-Tabari's account of the founding of Baghdad preserves al-Mansur's own explanation of why he chose the site, in words that reveal the Caliph's strategic thinking:

"This island between the Tigris in the east and the Euphrates in the west is a marketplace for the world. Everything that arrives via the Tigris from Wasit, Basra, Ahvaz, Fars, Oman, Yemen, China, and India will land here. And what comes from the Euphrates from Jazira, Syria, and Egypt will land here. It will be the center of the world, and the land routes from east and west will be united in it. Provision boats will come to us on the Tigris from Mosul, Diyar Rabi'a, Azerbaijan, and Armenia. They will come to us on the Euphrates from Diyar Mudar, al-Raqqah, Syria, Egypt, and the Maghrib. We will be, by the grace of Allah, in the center like the heart is in the chest."

This was not poetry — it was commercial and logistical analysis of extraordinary precision. Al-Mansur understood that whoever controlled the waterways of Mesopotamia controlled the trade routes of the known world. Baghdad's location at the convergence of the Tigris and Euphrates watershed systems meant that goods from India, China, East Africa, Central Asia, the Mediterranean, and the Arabian Peninsula would all pass through or near it.

## The Design: The Round City

The most distinctive feature of the original city of Baghdad was its design: a perfect circle. This plan — known as al-Mudawwara (the Round City) — was unprecedented in Islamic urban history and has no close parallel in the ancient Near East. The choice of the circular plan may reflect Persian antecedents (some scholars note circular city plans in pre-Islamic Iranian tradition) or may simply have been the most efficient geometric form for the caliph's specific purpose.

The basic design, as al-Tabari and al-Khatib al-Baghdadi describe it:

At the absolute center stood the Caliph's palace — Qasr al-Dhahab (the Palace of the Golden Gate), surmounted by a great green dome (al-Qubba al-Khadra) that served as both a visual landmark and a symbolic statement of the Caliph's centrality to the cosmic and political order. The Prophet ﷺ had said: "The successor (khalifah) is the shadow of Allah on earth" — al-Mansur literalized this theology in stone and mortar. Next to the palace was the Grand Mosque, also at the center of the circle.

Surrounding the central palace and mosque was a ring of administrative buildings — government offices, the quarters of the court, storehouses. Beyond that, a broad ring road. Beyond that, a band of residential and commercial buildings. And enclosing it all, two massive circular walls with four gates, one at each cardinal point: the Kufa Gate (south), the Basra Gate (southeast, toward the river), the Khurasan Gate (northeast), and the Syrian Gate (northwest). Each gate opened onto a road that led straight to the center of the city, like spokes in a wheel.

The outer wall, according to classical accounts, had a circumference of approximately 6,000 cubits — roughly 2.3 kilometers in diameter. A moat surrounded the outside wall. The combined defensive system of moat, outer wall, space, and inner wall was formidable by the standards of the age.

## The Construction

Al-Mansur assembled a workforce of extraordinary size. Al-Tabari records that 100,000 workers were employed in the construction — architects, engineers, craftsmen, laborers — drawn from across the empire. Funds were allocated from the treasury on a scale commensurate with the ambition of the project. The work began in 145 AH and was completed approximately four years later, in 149 AH.

Before the first stone was laid, al-Mansur consulted scholars about the astrologically favorable time to begin construction — an act that reflects the intellectual environment of the time, in which astronomical knowledge (which Muslim scholars had inherited from Persian and Greek traditions) was applied to practical questions. The scholar and astrologer Nawbakht al-Farsi and his colleague Mashallah provided the calculations.

Al-Mansur himself reportedly walked the circumference of the planned city to assess its layout personally. According to some accounts, he had ashes spread on the ground in the circular pattern of the city plan and then walked it, examining the arrangement of the gates and roads and making adjustments before construction began.

## The City's Growth Beyond All Planning

The original Round City was designed as the administrative and ceremonial heart of the caliphate — not as a commercial city. The bazaars and markets were initially located inside the walls, but al-Mansur soon moved them outside, concerned about security and the presence of foreign merchants with potentially dangerous access to the palace. New commercial districts grew up on both sides of the Tigris.

By the reign of Harun al-Rashid (r. 170-193 AH / 786-809 CE), Baghdad had grown far beyond the Round City into a metropolis of extraordinary size and complexity. Settlement spread on both banks of the Tigris, connected by pontoon bridges. The east bank — al-Rusafa — became a secondary urban center of equal importance to the original west bank city. By the 9th century CE, Baghdad was the largest city in the world outside China, with a population estimated by various scholars at between 500,000 and 1.5 million people.

## Harun al-Rashid: Baghdad at Its Height

The golden age of Baghdad is associated above all with Harun al-Rashid — the fifth Abbasid Caliph and the ruler during whose reign the city reached its greatest power and cultural brilliance. Harun al-Rashid was a contemporary of Charlemagne, the Frankish emperor of Western Europe, and the contrast between the two courts was stark: Baghdad was a city of hundreds of thousands, of libraries and hospitals, of scholars and translators and poets, of markets selling goods from China and Spain under the same roof. Charlemagne's Aachen was a town of a few thousand, still emerging from the darkness that had descended on Western Europe after Rome's fall.

The relationship between Harun al-Rashid and Charlemagne was one of diplomatic correspondence and even gift exchange — Charlemagne sent ambassadors to Baghdad, and Harun sent back an elephant (named Abul Abbas) and a remarkable mechanical water clock that astonished the Franks, who had never seen anything like it. The encounter illustrates the civilizational gap between the Islamic world of 8th century CE and contemporary Christian Europe.

## The House of Wisdom

The institution most associated with Baghdad's intellectual golden age is Bayt al-Hikmah — the House of Wisdom. Founded in some form by al-Mansur himself and greatly expanded under al-Rashid and then al-Ma'mun (r. 198-218 AH), the House of Wisdom was a combination of library, translation bureau, and research center without parallel in the medieval world.

Under al-Ma'mun's patronage, translators worked systematically through the entire corpus of Greek science and philosophy, rendering into Arabic the works of Aristotle, Plato, Euclid, Ptolemy, Galen, Hippocrates, and scores of other Greek, Persian, and Indian authors. The works of Archimedes were translated. Ptolemy's Almagest became available to Arabic-speaking astronomers. Euclid's Elements became the basis for Arabic mathematics.

From this translation movement grew the distinctively Islamic sciences: algebra (al-jabr), developed by al-Khwarizmi (whose name gives us "algorithm"); advanced astronomy (al-Battani's solar year calculation was more accurate than any European measurement for centuries); optics (Ibn al-Haytham); medicine (Ibn Sina's Canon of Medicine dominated European medical education for five centuries after being translated into Latin).

Baghdad was the city where this happened. The Round City that al-Mansur had built as a symbol of power became the world's greatest center of learning.

## The Mongol Destruction

The catastrophe came in 656 AH / 1258 CE. Hulagu Khan, grandson of Genghis Khan, led the Mongol army into Iraq. The Abbasid Caliph al-Musta'sim was defeated, captured, and killed. Baghdad was sacked for three weeks in what remains one of the most devastating acts of urban destruction in recorded history. The canals that irrigated Mesopotamian agriculture were broken. The House of Wisdom was destroyed — its books thrown into the Tigris, which reportedly ran black with ink. Hundreds of thousands of people were killed.

Al-Tabari's account of the city's founding stands in permanent counterpoint to this destruction — a reminder of what Baghdad had been before the catastrophe, and of the extraordinary vision that created it.

## The Legacy

Baghdad's founding in 145 AH by al-Mansur established the template for what an Islamic imperial capital could be: a planned city that expressed theological meaning through architecture, a commercial hub that made its ruler the center of world trade, and a cultural center that would eventually attract the greatest scholars, scientists, poets, and thinkers of the medieval world.

The Round City itself survived only a few centuries before being absorbed into the expanding urban fabric. But the idea it embodied — of a city designed from the center outward, radiating order and Islamic authority — influenced urban planning across the Islamic world for centuries.
