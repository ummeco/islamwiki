# Uthman's Standard Codex: Unifying the Ummah Around One Quran

## The Crisis That Demanded a Solution

By the caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him), the Islamic state had expanded into an empire of unprecedented size. From the eastern shores of the Mediterranean to the highlands of Persia, from Egypt to Azerbaijan, millions of people had embraced Islam. The faith was carried by Arab armies and spread by scholars and traders. But this very success generated a new kind of problem — one that had the potential to fracture the religious unity of the Muslim community at its most fundamental level.

Different regions of the Islamic world had received the Quran through different Companions. In Kufa and surrounding Iraq, the reading of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with him) — one of the most senior Companions, personally taught by the Prophet ﷺ — held authority. In Syria, the reading of Ubayy ibn Ka'b (may Allah be pleased with him) was standard. In Basra, the people followed the reading of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari (may Allah be pleased with him). In other areas, other Companions' recitations were the reference.

This was not necessarily wrong — the Prophet ﷺ had himself taught the Quran in the various Arab dialects, and the famous hadith of the ahruf sab'ah (seven modes of revelation) established that the Quran was revealed to accommodate the linguistic diversity of the Arab tribes. Multiple valid recitations existed, and the senior Companions had each learned directly from the Prophet ﷺ.

The danger lay not in the fact of variant readings — but in what was happening among ordinary Muslims who did not understand the scholarly context. They were not seeing variant readings as different valid transmissions of the same divine text. They were arguing that their reading was right and others were wrong. And when Muslims argue over the Word of Allah, the consequences extend far beyond an academic debate.

## Hudhaifa's Warning

The man who brought this crisis to Uthman's attention was Hudhaifa ibn al-Yaman (may Allah be pleased with him), one of the most trusted Companions of the Prophet ﷺ and a man renowned for his knowledge of the hidden affairs of the community. He had been serving in the military campaigns against Armenia and Azerbaijan — the frontier regions where soldiers from different parts of the empire came together.

What he observed alarmed him. Muslims from different regions were reciting different versions of the same passages, each insisting their recitation was correct, and the friction was becoming ugly. He heard men say, "My reading is better than yours" — words that, in the context of the Word of God, carried the seeds of serious sectarian division.

Hudhaifa returned to Uthman and said, with urgent directness: "O Commander of the Faithful, take this ummah in hand before they differ about the Book as the Jews and Christians differed about theirs." This was not a metaphor. Hudhaifa was pointing to the historical reality of how earlier scriptures had fragmented: through accumulated variant textual traditions that ultimately became irreconcilable, feeding schism after schism. He was asking Uthman to prevent the same fate.

Uthman, recognizing the gravity of the situation, consulted the senior Companions. Their consensus was to act. The account is preserved in detail in Sahih al-Bukhari, in the Book of the Virtues of the Quran (Kitab Fada'il al-Quran).

## The Committee and the Methodology

Uthman sent to Hafsah bint Umar (may Allah be pleased with her), the widow of the Prophet ﷺ and daughter of Umar, requesting the suhuf — the sheets compiled under Abu Bakr's order by Zayd ibn Thabit. These sheets had remained in Hafsah's custody since Umar's death and represented the most carefully verified written collection of the Quran in existence.

He then formed a committee of four: Zayd ibn Thabit al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him), who had led the original compilation under Abu Bakr; Abdullah ibn Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him), a young Companion of exceptional scholarly reputation; Sa'id ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him), known for the purity and precision of his Arabic — his speech was said to be the closest to that of the Prophet ﷺ; and Abd al-Rahman ibn al-Harith ibn Hisham (may Allah be pleased with him), another senior Companion.

Uthman gave the committee a specific instruction: in cases of disagreement over how a word should be written or pronounced, write it in the dialect of Quraysh — the tribe of the Prophet ﷺ and the purest form of literary Arabic. This instruction did not override the multiple valid recitations (qira'at); it established a script that could accommodate most of them without forcing a single phonetic representation, while anchoring the text to the most authoritative oral tradition.

The committee worked from Hafsah's suhuf as their primary reference, cross-checking against the memories of the major Companions and other written records. Zayd's role was central — he had the greatest continuity with the original compilation project.

## The Uthmani Codex: One Text for the Entire Ummah

The result of the committee's work was what scholars call al-Mushaf al-Uthmani — the Uthmanic codex. The key features of this standardization were:

First, the arrangement: the surahs were arranged in the order that the Prophet ﷺ had established through revelation guidance, not in the order of their length or revelation sequence as some personal copies had arranged them.

Second, the script: the Arabic script of the time was consonantal — it did not include the diacritical marks (tashkeel) or vowel indicators that were later added to facilitate non-Arab readers. This was deliberate: an undotted, unvoweled consonantal skeleton could accommodate multiple valid recitation patterns (qira'at), preserving more of the breadth of the revelation without forcing all readings into a single phonetic mold.

Third, the distribution: Uthman ordered that seven identical copies be made and sent to the major cities of the Islamic world, each accompanied by a reciter who could teach the standard reading. The cities that received copies included Mecca, Medina (the master copy), Kufa, Basra, Syria, Yemen, and Bahrain. One copy was kept with Uthman himself.

## The Burning of Other Copies

This is the aspect of Uthman's decision that has generated the most discussion across Islamic history. He ordered that the personal copies of the Quran held by individual Companions — copies that differed in arrangement, included marginal annotations, or contained some textual variations — be burned.

This was not a suppression of revelation. Every verse in every personal copy was represented in the Uthmani codex. What was being eliminated was the possibility of future confusion: the marginal notes that scholars like Ibn Mas'ud had added to explain meanings; the different ordering of surahs in personal collections; the occasional variant readings that, while valid transmissions, created the kind of confusion Hudhaifa had witnessed on the frontiers.

The scholarly controversy about Ibn Mas'ud's copy is real and documented. He objected to this process — not because he believed the Uthmani codex was wrong, but because he felt slighted that Zayd ibn Thabit, a younger man who had been a child when Ibn Mas'ud was already a senior Companion and teacher, was placed in charge. His objection was personal as much as textual. Classical scholars such as Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani have addressed this in detail, noting that Ibn Mas'ud's specific disagreements were about the ordering and scope of certain surahs (particularly Surah al-Fatiha and the Mu'awwidhatain being listed as separate surahs), not about the authenticity of the text itself.

The decision to burn was drastic but necessary. Uthman understood — and the senior Companions who supported him understood — that a single authoritative text, while requiring the sacrifice of personal copies, was infinitely preferable to a future in which each region's version diverged further from the others over generations of copying and transmission.

## The Significance of the Seven Ahruf

A theological question arises naturally from this standardization: did the Uthmani codex reduce the Quran from seven ahruf (modes) to one? Scholars of Quranic sciences, from al-Zarqani to Ibn al-Jazari, have addressed this at length.

The position of the majority of scholars is that the Uthmani script preserved multiple valid ahruf within its consonantal skeleton, even if not all seven modes as they had existed in the earliest period. The subsequent development of the formal science of qira'at (canonical recitations) — particularly the ten canonical readings established by Ibn Mujahid in the fourth Islamic century based on chains going back to the Companions — demonstrates that the Uthmani script was broad enough to accommodate considerable legitimate variation.

The Prophet ﷺ had said: "This Quran was revealed in seven ahruf, so recite from it whichever is easiest for you" (Bukhari and Muslim). The Uthmanic standardization did not contradict this — it channeled the diversity through a verified, authoritative transmission system rather than allowing unverified divergence.

## One Quran Across 1.8 Billion Muslims

The most remarkable testament to what Uthman accomplished is the fact visible today: every Quran in every mosque, in every home, in every app, in every corner of the world, contains the same text. A Muslim in Jakarta recites the same words as a Muslim in London, Lagos, Lahore, or Los Angeles. No other scripture on earth can claim this degree of textual unity across such geographical and cultural diversity.

This unity traces directly to the Uthmani codex. The copies Uthman sent to the major cities became the templates for all subsequent copying. For nearly 1,400 years, through the fall of empires and the rise of others, through wars, migrations, and the spread of printing, the text of the Quran has not changed by a single letter.

Ibn Kathir (may Allah have mercy on him), in his monumental Tafsir, praises Uthman's decision without reservation. Al-Suyuti, in al-Itqan fi Ulum al-Quran, provides exhaustive technical analysis. Both scholars, representing different centuries of Islamic scholarship, converge on the same conclusion: Uthman's standardization was among the greatest services ever rendered to this religion.

It was, as Allah promised in Surah al-Hijr (15:9): "Verily, it is We who have sent down the Reminder, and it is We who are its Guardians." The divine mechanism of that guardianship, in this instance, worked through the piety, wisdom, and decisive action of the third Caliph of Islam.
