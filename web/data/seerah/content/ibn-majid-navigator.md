# Ibn Majid: The Lion of the Sea

Ahmad ibn Majid al-Najdi (c. 836-906 AH / 1432-1500 CE) was the greatest Arab navigator of the medieval period and one of the most important figures in the history of maritime navigation. Known as the Lion of the Sea (Asad al-Bahr) and the Master of Navigation, he compiled the most comprehensive body of knowledge about the Indian Ocean's navigation techniques, routes, stars, and seasonal winds that had ever been assembled.

## Life

Ahmad ibn Majid was born around 836 AH (1432 CE) in Julfar (present-day Ra's al-Khaimah in the UAE). He came from a family of navigators — he mentions his father and grandfather as also being experts in the science of navigation (ilm al-bahr). He spent his career sailing the Indian Ocean, accumulating decades of direct navigational experience.

He died around 906 AH (1500 CE), at the same historical moment when Portuguese ships under Vasco da Gama were permanently altering the balance of power in the Indian Ocean.

## Works

Ibn Majid was prolific, producing approximately 40 works on navigation, sailing, and astronomy. His writings were preserved in manuscripts that were discovered and studied by Western scholars primarily in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries CE.

**Kitab al-Fawa'id fi Usul Ilm al-Bahr wal-Qawa'id** (The Book of Useful Information on the Principles and Rules of Navigation). Ibn Majid's major prose work is a comprehensive treatise on the science of navigation. It covers:
- The principles of celestial navigation using stars
- The characteristics of the Indian Ocean's seasonal winds (monsoons)
- The sailing routes and their timing
- The use of the kamal (a simple navigation instrument using a rectangular piece of wood and a knotted string to measure the altitude of stars above the horizon)
- Descriptions of ports, anchorages, hazards, and coastlines from East Africa to China

The work represents the accumulated expertise of generations of Arab navigators organized into a coherent educational text.

**Navigation Poems (urjuzas).** Most of Ibn Majid's works are in verse — long poems using specific meters to encode navigational information in a memorable form. Sailors could memorize these poems and carry the knowledge with them without requiring manuscripts.

## The Vasco da Gama Question

A Portuguese historian, João de Barros, writing in the sixteenth century CE, claimed that Vasco da Gama was guided from Malindi (on the East African coast) to India by a skilled Moorish (Muslim) pilot who was in fact Ahmad ibn Majid. This account has been widely repeated.

Historians and scholars have debated the claim. Ibn Majid's own writings do not mention guiding da Gama, and several details in the Portuguese account do not match what is known about Ibn Majid's career. The identification remains uncertain. What is clear is that Arab navigators provided essential knowledge and guidance for the Portuguese penetration of the Indian Ocean.

If the identification is correct, Ibn Majid would bear the ironic distinction of having guided the expedition that would ultimately destroy the Arab maritime dominance of the Indian Ocean — a tragic intersection of individual expertise and historical consequence.

## Scientific Contributions

Ibn Majid's navigational science combined practical experience with mathematical and astronomical knowledge. He described:
- The use of 32 stars as altitude markers (the Arab equivalent of latitude measurement)
- Detailed monsoon calendars indicating the optimal timing for different routes
- The system of thumb lines (rhumb lines) before they were formally described in European cartography
- Comprehensive pilot charts of the Indian Ocean, Red Sea, Persian Gulf, and adjoining waters

His descriptions of the East African coast, the Indian subcontinent, Southeast Asia, and China are valuable geographical documents.

## Legacy

Ibn Majid's works represent the culmination of centuries of Arab navigational knowledge of the Indian Ocean. Within a decade of his death, Portuguese ships using some of the routes he described — and perhaps some of his own knowledge — had established trading posts across the Indian Ocean, eventually displacing Arab merchants from many routes they had dominated.

His work was rediscovered by Gabriel Ferrand, a French scholar who published the first edition of his texts in 1921-1923 CE. His reputation was subsequently established by the Soviet scholar T.A. Shumovskiy, who produced comprehensive Russian editions and translations of his navigational texts in the 1950s.
