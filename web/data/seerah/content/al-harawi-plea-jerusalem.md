# Al-Harawi's Plea for Jerusalem

In 493 AH (1099 CE), Abu Said al-Harawi, a Sufi scholar and jurist from Khurasan, arrived in Baghdad carrying devastating news: Jerusalem had fallen to the Crusaders. His appearance before the Abbasid court became one of the most famous acts of public grief and advocacy in Islamic history, a solitary voice demanding that Muslim rulers wake from their complacency and act.

## Background

Abu Said Ali ibn Ali ibn Tahir al-Harawi (d. 575 AH / 1179 CE) was a scholar of Sufi inclination and a student of Islamic jurisprudence. He had been living in Jerusalem when the Crusaders besieged and captured the city in Sha'ban 492 AH (July 1099 CE). He witnessed or learned directly of the massacre that followed the fall of the city.

Al-Harawi made his way to Baghdad, then the seat of the Abbasid Caliphate. Though the Abbasid Caliphs had been stripped of effective political and military power by the Seljuk Sultans, the Caliph al-Mustazhir Billah still held enormous spiritual authority as the successor to the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) and the nominal head of the Sunni Muslim world.

## The Speech

When al-Harawi arrived at the court of the Caliph, he refused to show the usual courtly decorum. He wept openly and made no attempt to disguise his grief. According to Ibn al-Athir and other chroniclers, he addressed the assembled officials and scholars with words that cut through all pretense of dignity:

He pointed to the beards and turbans of the assembled men and mocked their complacency. Jerusalem had fallen. Tens of thousands of Muslims had been killed. The Aqsa Mosque — the third holiest site in Islam and the site from which the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) had ascended to the heavens — was now in the hands of those who called themselves warriors of Christ.

Al-Harawi reportedly said, in words that varied between the accounts: "How dare you sleep in the shade of comfortable lives while your brothers in Syria have no dwelling place save the saddles of camels and the bellies of vultures? Blood has been spilled, beautiful young women have been shamed, men have been killed, and still you slumber."

He described the scene at Jerusalem after the Crusader conquest, the massacre of the defenders, the burning of the synagogue, the desecration of the holy sites. He described the shock of the survivors and the grief of those who had fled.

## The Context of Failure

The poignancy of al-Harawi's speech lay not just in its eloquence but in the stark reality it exposed. The Muslim world at the turn of the sixth century AH was profoundly fragmented:

The Abbasid Caliphate had no army and no real power. The Seljuk Sultanate, which held political authority over Iraq, Syria, and much of the eastern Islamic world, was divided between competing princes and emirs who were more interested in fighting each other than in defending the frontiers of Islam. The Fatimid Caliphate in Cairo, which had actually controlled Jerusalem until shortly before the Crusader siege, had briefly recaptured the city from the Seljuks in 491 AH (1098 CE) and then lost it to the Crusaders a year later — a catastrophic piece of timing.

Al-Harawi's plea fell largely on deaf ears in Baghdad. The Caliph issued pious statements but took no meaningful action. The Seljuk princes showed little urgency. A relief force sent to confront the Crusaders was defeated at the Battle of Ascalon shortly after the fall of Jerusalem, and the Muslim world settled into an uneasy coexistence with the new Crusader states.

## Ibn al-Athir's Account

The detailed account of al-Harawi's plea comes primarily from Ibn al-Athir's *al-Kamil fit-Tarikh*. Ibn al-Athir was himself deeply moved by the events of 492-493 AH and lamented the failure of Muslim rulers to respond effectively. He used al-Harawi's speech as a way to dramatize the contrast between the catastrophe that had occurred and the inadequacy of the Muslim political response.

Ibn al-Qalanisi, a contemporary Damascene chronicler, also recorded the shock that spread through the Muslim world following the fall of Jerusalem, including among the ruling class of Syria.

## Legacy

Al-Harawi's plea became a reference point for later Muslim calls to action. When Salah ad-Din al-Ayyubi (Saladin) began his military career and eventually worked toward the reconquest of Jerusalem, scholars and poets invoked the memory of Jerusalem's fall and the weeping of al-Harawi to motivate and inspire.

The episode illustrates a recurring pattern in Islamic history: the scholar or saint who stands before worldly power and speaks an unpopular truth. Al-Harawi's willingness to weep publicly, to abandon courtly decorum, and to shame those more powerful than himself placed him in a long tradition of Islamic public moral witness.

Jerusalem was recaptured by Salah ad-Din in Rajab 583 AH (October 1187 CE), nearly a century after al-Harawi's plea.
