# Independence and Partition: The Birth of Pakistan and Muslim South Asia

## The Midnight Hour

At midnight on August 14-15, 1947 CE (28-29 Ramadan 1366 AH), the subcontinent of British India was partitioned into two independent states: Pakistan and India. India's first Prime Minister, Jawaharlal Nehru, delivered his famous "Tryst with Destiny" speech in the Indian Constituent Assembly. In Karachi, Muhammad Ali Jinnah became Governor-General of Pakistan. The moment of independence was also the moment of one of the greatest human catastrophes in recorded history: a partition that within weeks would displace approximately 14-17 million people and result in the deaths of perhaps 200,000 to 2 million in communal violence.

The partition of the subcontinent, and the creation of Pakistan as the world's first explicitly Muslim-majority nation-state, was the culmination of decades of Muslim political thought, communal tension, and colonial policy. Its consequences — the shape of South Asian politics, the Kashmir dispute, and the ongoing relationship between Islam and governance in Pakistan and Bangladesh — continue to define the region.

## The Making of the Two-Nation Theory

The argument that eventually justified partition — the two-nation theory — held that Muslims and Hindus were not merely two religious communities sharing a common Indian identity but two separate nations with distinct cultures, histories, and political aspirations that could not be accommodated within a single democratic state.

The intellectual origins of this position are often traced to Muhammad Iqbal's 1930 Allahabad Address, in which he proposed a Muslim state in northwestern India. But the political theory was developed most rigorously by Muhammad Ali Jinnah (1293-1367 AH / 1876-1948 CE), a Bombay lawyer who had spent much of his early career as an Indian nationalist — a member of both the Congress Party and the Muslim League, known as the "Ambassador of Hindu-Muslim Unity" — before concluding in the 1930s that unity was impossible on terms acceptable to Muslims.

Jinnah's argument (articulated in a famous speech to the Muslim League in 1940 at Lahore, where the "Pakistan Resolution" was passed): India was not one nation but two. A democratic united India would inevitably mean permanent Hindu-majority rule, given the demographic realities. Muslim culture, Muslim personal law, Muslim educational institutions, and Muslim political representation could only be guaranteed in a state where Muslims were the majority.

The Congress Party's position — that India was a single nation with many religions, and that a secular democratic constitution would protect everyone equally — was dismissed by the Muslim League as either naive or dishonest. The Congress leadership, dominated by upper-caste Hindus, had consistently failed to address Muslim concerns about representation, language (Urdu vs. Hindi), and cultural rights in a way that satisfied the Muslim League.

## The Road to Partition

The final sequence of events was rapid and chaotic. The British, exhausted by World War II and facing a growing independence movement, announced their intention to leave India. Lord Mountbatten was appointed the last Viceroy with a mandate to arrange a swift transfer of power.

Various schemes for a united India with constitutional protections for minorities were proposed and collapsed. The Cabinet Mission Plan of 1946 — which proposed a complex federal arrangement keeping India united with significant regional autonomy — briefly seemed viable but was torpedoed by Congress leader Jawaharlal Nehru's public statements that Congress would not be bound by its provisions once in power. For the Muslim League, this confirmed that a united India would mean Muslim subordination.

Communal violence in Calcutta in August 1946 — the "Great Calcutta Killings," in which thousands died in days of Hindu-Muslim violence — demonstrated how explosive the situation had become. Similar violence erupted in Bihar, Noakhali (Bengal), and Punjab.

By 1947, the choice had narrowed: a rapid partition into two states, with all the human suffering that entailed, or continued colonial rule with no resolution in sight. Mountbatten chose rapid partition; the date was brought forward from June 1948 to August 1947 with minimal preparation for the border demarcation.

## The Partition: The Radcliffe Lines

Sir Cyril Radcliffe, a British lawyer who had never been to India, was given five weeks to draw the borders of the new states through Punjab and Bengal — the two provinces that had to be partitioned because they had mixed Muslim and Hindu-Sikh populations. He worked from maps and census data without any significant consultation with the people who would live with his decisions.

The Radcliffe Lines — announced only after Independence Day — divided the Punjab in a way that left Sikh holy sites (including parts of Amritsar) on the Indian side and Muslim majorities on both sides of various districts. The borders cut through farms, families, canal irrigation systems, and railway lines that had been built as integrated systems.

The result was the largest and fastest mass migration in human history. Approximately 7-8 million Hindus and Sikhs moved from Pakistani Punjab and West Bengal to India; approximately 7-8 million Muslims moved from Indian Punjab and East Bengal to Pakistan. The violence that accompanied this migration — train massacres, village massacres, abductions of women, arson — claimed between 200,000 and 2 million lives. Estimates vary because no accurate count was ever made.

The trauma was not only physical but civilizational. Muslim families who had lived for centuries in Delhi, Lucknow, and the Punjab plains had to abandon homes, businesses, graves, and communities. Hindu and Sikh families who had lived for generations in Lahore, Multan, and Rawalpindi suffered the same. The intercommunal violence involved neighbors killing neighbors — Muslims who had worked alongside Hindus and Sikhs for generations, and vice versa. This rupture left lasting scars on the collective memory of all three communities.

## Pakistan: The Question of Islamic Governance

Pakistan was created as a homeland for Muslims, but what kind of state it should be was debated from the beginning. Jinnah's own statements were ambiguous and have been contested by partisans on both sides. In some speeches, he seemed to envision a secular state with equal rights for minorities. In others, he spoke of Pakistan as a state where Islamic principles would govern. His famous speech to the Constituent Assembly on August 11, 1947, in which he said that "in course of time, all distinctions of Hindu, Muslim, Christian will vanish" and that all citizens would be equal, was seized upon by secular Pakistani nationalists as defining his vision; religious parties dismissed it as tactical reassurance to minorities.

The Objectives Resolution, passed by Pakistan's Constituent Assembly in March 1949 after Jinnah's death (he died in September 1948, barely a year after Pakistan's founding), declared that "sovereignty over the entire universe belongs to Almighty Allah alone" and that the Muslim majority would be enabled to "order their lives in the individual and collective spheres in accordance with the teachings and requirements of Islam as set out in the Holy Quran and Sunnah." This became the preamble to successive Pakistani constitutions and is embedded in the Constitution of 1973.

The tension between Pakistan as an Islamic state (with Shariah as its governing framework) and Pakistan as a Muslim-majority democracy with civil liberties for all citizens has never been fully resolved. This ongoing tension has shaped Pakistani politics, its relationship with its Hindu, Christian, and Ahmadiyya minorities, and its relationship with the international community.

## Muslims Who Stayed: India's Muslim Community

A crucial and often overlooked dimension of the partition is the fate of Muslims who chose to remain in India — or who had no practical choice to leave. Approximately 35-40 million Muslims remained in India after partition (today, India's Muslim population exceeds 200 million — the world's second-largest national Muslim population after Indonesia).

These Muslims faced immediate questions of identity and loyalty: would they be treated as potential fifth columnists with loyalties to Pakistan? Their community had lost many of its social and intellectual leaders who had emigrated to Pakistan; the Urdu language that had been the cultural medium of Muslim India was gradually marginalized in northern Indian states; and the partition violence created communal memories that took generations to overcome.

Indian Muslims chose integration into the new Indian democratic framework, contributing significantly to Indian public life in law, medicine, the arts, literature, and politics. They also faced discrimination, periodic communal violence, and the ongoing challenge of maintaining Islamic practice and identity within a secular Hindu-majority democracy.

## The Kashmir Dispute

Perhaps the most consequential unresolved legacy of partition is the dispute over Kashmir. The Muslim-majority princely state of Jammu and Kashmir had a Hindu Maharaja, Hari Singh, who hesitated to join either India or Pakistan. When Pakistani-backed tribal forces invaded in October 1947, Hari Singh asked India for military assistance and signed the Instrument of Accession to India. Indian troops arrived; the war ended in a ceasefire line (the Line of Control) that divided the region, with approximately two-thirds under Indian control and one-third under Pakistani control.

Both nations claim the entire territory; the dispute has been the cause of three wars (1947, 1965, 1971) and a near-nuclear confrontation in 1999. The UN resolutions calling for a plebiscite have never been implemented. The human costs — particularly in the Indian-administered Kashmir Valley, which has experienced repeated cycles of armed insurgency and military repression since the late 1980s — have been enormous.
