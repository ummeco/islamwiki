# The Revelation of Surah al-Baqarah

## The Longest Surah, the Medinan Foundation

Surah al-Baqarah — the Cow — is the longest surah in the Quran: 286 verses across approximately 62 pages in the standard Mushaf. It was revealed in Medina across multiple occasions spanning a significant portion of the Medinan period, with different scholars identifying its revelation as extending from the first to the ninth year after the Hijra. Unlike the short, concentrated surahs of the Meccan period — which addressed matters of faith, prophethood, the afterlife, and spiritual formation — Surah al-Baqarah is a comprehensive legislative and theological document: it contains the change of qiblah, the establishment of fasting, the laws of zakat, the permission for fighting in self-defense, major legislation on marriage and divorce, detailed rules of financial transactions, and some of the most theologically significant passages in the entire Quran.

The Prophet ﷺ described it as the peak of the Quran (sanam al-Quran) and its brightness. He said: "Do not make your homes like graves — verily, the shaytan flees from a house in which Surah al-Baqarah is read." Scholars understand this statement as indicating both the surah's power as a protection and its function as the defining legislative and theological statement of the Medinan community.

## What the Surah Contains

Surah al-Baqarah covers a range of subjects that defies simple summary. Its major themes and legislative sections include:

**The three types of people:** The surah opens by distinguishing three groups — the believers (muttaqun), the disbelievers (kafirun), and the hypocrites (munafiqun) — establishing the moral taxonomy of the Medinan community that would govern all subsequent Quranic discourse about the political and social situation of Islam.

**The story of the Bani Israel:** A substantial portion of the surah is addressed to the Children of Israel — reviewing the covenant Allah made with them, the prophets they were sent, the ways in which they broke the covenant, and the lessons these failures hold for the Muslim community. The story of Baqarah (the cow) — from which the surah takes its name — is told here: the Bani Israel's lengthy and obstructive questioning when Musa commanded them to slaughter a cow, which Allah had prescribed as the resolution of a murder inquiry, became a parable of the dangers of excessive questioning and the importance of simple obedience.

**The change of qiblah:** Verses 142-150 address the change of the prayer direction from Jerusalem to Mecca, answering objections and establishing the theological rationale.

**The fast of Ramadan:** Verses 183-187 establish the obligatory fast, its duration, its exceptions, and the Night of Decree.

**The permission to fight:** Verse 190 is among the first verses permitting armed defense: "Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress limits; indeed, Allah does not like transgressors." This verse, and those following it, established the principle that fighting is permitted in defense but is bounded by conditions — establishing the foundation of Islamic just war theory.

**Financial legislation:** Verses 275-281 address riba (usury/interest) with extraordinary force: "Those who consume riba will stand on the Day of Judgment like one possessed by shaytan from the madness of his touch." The prohibition of riba — one of the most economically consequential prohibitions in Islamic law — is established here. These verses also contain the most detailed legislation on debt documentation in the Quran: "O you who believe, when you contract a debt for a specified term, write it down." (2:282 — the longest single verse in the Quran)

**The throne verse (Ayat al-Kursi):** Verse 255, the greatest verse in the Quran according to the Prophet ﷺ himself, describing Allah's sovereignty, knowledge, and sustaining of creation.

**The last two verses:** The surah closes with verses 285-286, described by the Prophet ﷺ as two lights given to him specifically — not given to any prophet before him. They affirm the believers' testimony and end with the supplication: "Our Lord, do not burden us with what we have no ability to bear. And pardon us and forgive us and have mercy upon us."

## Why It Was Revealed in This Order and Period

The Medinan context of Surah al-Baqarah explains its legislative character. The Meccan revelations built the theological and spiritual foundation: monotheism, prophethood, the afterlife, the Quran's nature, the moral formation of the individual. The Medinan revelations built on this foundation to address the requirements of a governing community: laws of prayer, fasting, charity, fighting, marriage, divorce, commercial transactions, and the relationship between the Muslim community and the People of the Book.

Surah al-Baqarah is, in the architecture of the Quran, the first great statement of what it means to be a Muslim community rather than merely a Muslim individual. Its legislative sweep — from the spiritual (iman, qiblah, fasting, salah) to the economic (zakat, riba, debt) to the social (marriage, divorce, family law) — represents Islam's claim to govern the whole of human life, not merely the interior life of piety.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Learn Surah al-Baqarah and Surah Ali Imran, for they are the zahrawain — the two brilliant ones — they shade their companions on the Day of Judgment as if they were clouds." The image is of shade against the heat of the Day of Judgment, provided by two surahs whose knowledge and practice equips the believer for what awaits.

## A Surah That Took Years to Complete

Surah al-Baqarah was not revealed in a single sitting but over the course of years — individual sections revealed as specific situations arose (the qiblah change, the Ramadan fast, the permission for fighting, the laws of usury and debt) and assembled into the surah's comprehensive final form under the Prophet's ﷺ direction. He directed the scribes of revelation where to place each new section within the surah's structure. The Quran's final arrangement — which places al-Baqarah second among the surahs, after al-Fatiha, despite the Meccan surahs having been revealed earlier in many cases — reflects the Prophet's ﷺ direction under revelation, not a chronological ordering.

The last verse of Surah al-Baqarah (or one of the last, depending on scholarly opinion) to be revealed was verse 281: "And fear a Day when you will be returned to Allah. Then every soul will be compensated for what it earned, and they will not be wronged." Ibn Abbas narrated that this verse was revealed nine days before the Prophet's ﷺ death — placing the completion of the longest surah of the Quran among the final revelations of his prophetic career. The surah that was being revealed from the beginning of the Medinan period was still being completed in the Prophet's ﷺ last days — a reminder that Quranic revelation was a living process, responsive to events and circumstances, until it was complete in the final months of his life.
