# The Final Crusader States: Mamluk Victory and the Liberation of the Coast

## A Century of Stalemate

When Saladin liberated Jerusalem on 27 Rajab 583 AH (October 2, 1187 CE), many Muslims believed the Crusader presence in the Levant was effectively over. It was not. The Third Crusade (585-591 AH) restored the Crusaders' coastal position, and the Treaty of Jaffa (591 AH) left them in control of a string of port cities and fortresses that they would hold for another century.

The Crusader states of the 7th and early 8th centuries AH were remnants — reduced from their original territorial extent, dependent on supply and reinforcement from Europe, increasingly isolated as European enthusiasm for crusading waned. But they were real military powers with formidable fortifications, professional soldiers, and access to Mediterranean trade wealth. Their coastal cities — Acre, Tyre, Sidon, Beirut, Tripoli — were among the most prosperous ports in the medieval Mediterranean.

It was the Mamluk Sultanate that finally ended this presence, systematically and deliberately, over the course of about forty years — completing what Saladin had begun with a thoroughness that left no Crusader foothold in the Levant.

## Sultan Qalawun: The Systematic Eliminator

The architect of the Mamluk campaign against the Crusader states in the final phase was Sultan al-Mansur Qalawun (r. 678-689 AH / 1279-1290 CE), one of the most capable and methodical rulers the Mamluk Sultanate produced. Qalawun came to power in his late fifties — an experienced commander and administrator — and approached the task of eliminating Crusader strongholds with careful deliberation.

His strategy was methodical rather than dramatic: isolate individual fortresses or cities diplomatically and militarily, deny them reinforcement from the sea and from neighboring positions, then attack with overwhelming force. He preferred to negotiate surrender when possible (reducing his own casualties), attack when necessary.

**Marqab (684 AH / 1285 CE)**: The massive Hospitaller castle on the Syrian coast, one of the most formidable fortifications in the medieval world, was besieged and taken after its garrison, recognizing the impossibility of relief, negotiated terms and departed.

**Latakia (684 AH)**: Taken the same year.

**Tripoli (688 AH / 1289 CE)**: The major Crusader city of Tripoli fell after a siege of about a month. The city was sacked and largely destroyed — Qalawun did not want it rebuilt and refortified — and its population scattered. A smaller settlement arose at a different location.

The fall of Tripoli shocked the remaining Crusader positions. It demonstrated that the Mamluks had both the military capacity and the political will to take any fortification they chose.

Qalawun died in 689 AH (1290 CE), during preparations for the assault on Acre — the largest remaining Crusader city. He reportedly said on his deathbed that his successor must complete the task.

## The Siege and Fall of Acre: 690 AH / 1291 CE

His son and successor, al-Ashraf Salah al-Din Khalil ibn Qalawun (r. 689-693 AH / 1290-1293 CE), fulfilled his father's charge. In the spring of 690 AH (1291 CE), he assembled the largest army the Mamluks had yet deployed for a single operation: estimates range from 60,000 to 200,000 soldiers, with a massive train of siege engines including catapults that required dozens of oxen to move.

Acre (Arabic: Akka) was the most important Crusader city remaining — the effective capital of what was left of the Kingdom of Jerusalem, its most prosperous port, and the headquarters of the great military orders (Hospitallers, Templars, Teutonic Knights). Its population at the time of the siege has been estimated at 30,000 to 60,000, including perhaps 12,000-15,000 soldiers of varying quality.

The siege began in late Rabi al-Awwal 690 AH (April 1291 CE). The Mamluk siege engines subjected the walls to continuous bombardment. Sorties by the garrison achieved little. Attempts to bring naval reinforcement from Cyprus were frustrated. The city's towers fell one by one.

The final assault came on 17 Safar 690 AH (May 18, 1291 CE). The Mamluk forces broke through the defenses in multiple places simultaneously. The fighting inside the city was intense and costly on both sides. Many of the civilian population were able to flee to the harbor where ships took them to Cyprus. Many were killed. The military orders fought with great tenacity in their respective fortresses; the Templar fortress held out for another ten days before falling.

The fighting for the Templar fortress was particularly intense: according to Ibn Abd al-Zahir's account, the garrison refused to surrender, and when the Mamluks finally breached the structure it collapsed, killing both defenders and a Mamluk unit that had already entered the building.

## The Collapse of the Remaining Cities

The fall of Acre triggered an immediate and complete evacuation of the remaining Crusader positions. The residents of Tyre, Sidon, Beirut, and Tortosa abandoned their cities within weeks — sometimes within days — and sailed for Cyprus. They recognized that without Acre, the other positions were indefensible and that the Mamluk campaign would continue until nothing remained.

By late Rabi al-Awwal 690 AH (summer 1291 CE), the entire Levantine coast had been cleared of Crusader presence. The Kingdom of Jerusalem, founded in 492 AH (1099 CE), had lasted 192 years. It had been reduced from a territorial kingdom to a coastal strip, then to a single city, and now ceased to exist entirely.

Sultan al-Ashraf Khalil ordered the demolition of the walls and harbor facilities of Acre and other coastal cities — he wanted to prevent any future Crusader landing from using existing fortifications as an immediate base. This was a strategically sound decision though it entailed the destruction of cities that had been prosperous commercial centers.

## The Account of Ibn Abd al-Zahir

The primary Islamic source for the fall of Acre is the account of Muhyi al-Din ibn Abd al-Zahir, who served as the official secretary (*katib*) to Sultan Baybars and later Sultan Qalawun, and who continued to write under al-Ashraf Khalil. His biography of al-Ashraf Khalil, *al-Fath al-Wahhaj wa al-Mathlab al-Baha'aj*, provides detailed accounts of the siege and fall of Acre based on official documents and eyewitness reports. It is the most important Arabic source for these events.

Al-Maqrizi's *al-Suluk* provides additional detail drawn from earlier sources. European accounts — from the *Templar of Tyre* chronicle and letters preserved in the papal archives — provide the Crusader perspective.

## Theological Significance and Juridical Principles

The Mamluk liberation of the Levantine coast was understood by Muslim scholars as the fulfillment of an Islamic obligation. The lands of al-Sham (Greater Syria) had been part of the Islamic world for over 600 years before the First Crusade; their occupation by the Crusaders was an injustice requiring rectification.

The Islamic legal framework for such conflicts is established in the Quran: *"Permission [to fight] has been given to those who are being fought, because they were wronged. And indeed, Allah is competent to give them victory"* (Surah al-Hajj, 22:39). The defense and recovery of Muslim lands from unjust occupation is among the clearest cases of obligatory jihad in Islamic jurisprudence.

At the same time, Islamic law prohibits collective punishment of civilian populations and requires the protection of non-combatants, including dhimmi communities. The Mamluk commanders' treatment of civilians — allowing escape by sea from Acre and other cities — generally reflected this principle, even if the military operations themselves were inevitably destructive.

## The Legacy: A Changed Mediterranean

The fall of Acre in 690 AH ended the Crusading presence in the Levant but did not end the Crusading movement itself. Further crusading efforts were proposed and occasionally launched in the following two centuries, but none succeeded in establishing a permanent presence in the Holy Land. The geographical reality had changed: without a foothold on the Syrian or Palestinian coast, European powers had no base from which to operate.

The Mamluks subsequently built a prosperous state that controlled the trade routes between Asia and Europe through Egypt and Syria. Cairo became one of the world's great cities. The Nile valley and Syrian coast flourished economically, even as the Crusader commerce was rerouted through different channels.

For Islam, the fall of Acre represented the completion of a century of struggle to recover what had been lost during the First Crusade. Jerusalem had been recovered by Saladin in 583 AH; the coastal cities had been recovered by the Mamluks between 663 and 690 AH. The task was complete.

The lesson Islamic historians drew was one of patient perseverance in the face of long odds. The struggle from the First Crusade (492 AH / 1099 CE) to the fall of Acre (690 AH / 1291 CE) spanned 198 years — nearly two full centuries. The Muslim response moved through phases of disunity and defeat, then produced figures of extraordinary capability (Zengi, Nur al-Din, Saladin, Baybars, Qalawun, al-Ashraf Khalil) who, combined with the underlying resilience of Muslim populations in the region, ultimately prevailed.

*"And do not be weak in pursuit of the enemy. If you should be suffering — so are they suffering as you are suffering, but you expect from Allah that which they expect not"* (Surah al-Nisa, 4:104).
