# Bilal ibn Rabah: The First Muadhdhin and the Sound of Freedom

## The Man Behind the Voice

Bilal ibn Rabah (may Allah be pleased with him) was born into slavery in Mecca, the son of an Abyssinian mother named Hamama — and it is from her that some accounts derive his full name: Bilal ibn Rabah al-Habashi, the Abyssinian. His father Rabah was also enslaved. From birth, Bilal had no legal status beyond being the property of another man. He was owned by Umayyah ibn Khalaf of the Banu Jumah clan of the Quraysh — a man who would become one of the most persistent and cruel persecutors of the early Muslim community, and whose fate on the battlefield of Badr would be inseparably intertwined with the man he had once owned.

Bilal's physical appearance is described in the sirah sources: tall, lean, dark-skinned, with a thin beard and hair that was described as "sparse at the temples." His voice was extraordinary — deep, powerful, resonant — a voice that, when it was finally raised in the call to prayer, would be heard across the rooftops of Medina and would echo through Islamic history for fourteen centuries.

He accepted Islam in the earliest period — approximately twelve years before the Hijrah — among the small group of enslaved and vulnerable people who heard the message and responded. The sirah literature does not record the precise moment of his conversion or the exact words the Prophet ﷺ spoke to him, but what is clear is that Bilal was among the first wave of Muslims, and that his acceptance of Islam was known to his master Umayyah ibn Khalaf almost immediately.

## Torture in the Name of Ahad

What followed was a period of persecution that the Islamic tradition has preserved in remarkable detail as one of the most affecting accounts of human endurance in the faith's early history. Umayyah ibn Khalaf's torture of Bilal was systematic, public, and designed to break him through physical suffering.

Ibn Hisham describes the method: at the hottest part of the day, when the sun above Mecca was at its most brutal and the sand and rocks had absorbed hours of heat, Umayyah would bring Bilal out into the open ground and lay him on his back in the burning sand. He would then place a large, flat rock on his chest — heavy enough that Bilal could not rise, heavy enough to constrict his breathing, heavy enough that each breath required effort. Umayyah or his agents would then demand that Bilal say the words of disbelief: renounce Muhammad ﷺ, praise the idols al-Lat and al-Uzza, and declare himself done with this new religion.

Bilal's response, session after session of this torture, was always the same. He would say: "Ahad. Ahad." — "One. One." The word Ahad — the divine name meaning "the One, the Singular, the Indivisible" — became his invocation, his prayer, his resistance, and his identity all at once. Pressed beneath a boulder in the scorching midday heat, he called upon the oneness of the God he had accepted, and nothing Umayyah did could make him say otherwise.

The Prophet ﷺ, passing by where Bilal was being tortured, said to him — and the narration is recorded in Ibn Hisham and in other sirah sources: "Sabr, ya Bilal — ahad, ahad." "Be patient, O Bilal — One, One." He could not intervene directly at that moment; he was himself without tribal protection powerful enough to override Umayyah's ownership. But he bore witness, and his words became Bilal's endurance, naming what Bilal was already doing.

## Abu Bakr and the Price of Freedom

The liberation of Bilal (may Allah be pleased with him) came through Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him), whose generosity in freeing enslaved Muslims was one of the most concrete expressions of his faith in those early years. When Abu Bakr saw the state of Bilal — the wounds, the exhaustion, the marks of repeated torture — he went to Umayyah ibn Khalaf and negotiated a purchase.

The narrations differ in the precise details. Some accounts in Ibn Hisham state that Abu Bakr paid seven uqiyya of gold; others say nine uqiyya; a well-known narration says five uqiyya. All agree that the price was substantial — Abu Bakr did not bargain down. He paid what was asked and more.

A revealing exchange is recorded in some sirah accounts: Umayyah's relatives said to him, after the purchase, that he could have asked more because Abu Bakr would have paid it. Umayyah reportedly said he would have sold Bilal for a single dirham if he had been asked. When Abu Bakr heard this report, he said: "And I would have paid a hundred uqiyya if that had been the price." This exchange captures something essential: Umayyah saw only a stubborn slave; Abu Bakr saw a human soul whose faith was worth more than any sum.

Abu Bakr freed not only Bilal but several other enslaved Muslims in this period: Amir ibn Fuhayrah, who would later die as a martyr; Zinnirah the Roman woman; Umm Ubays; Lubayba; and others. His father reportedly criticized him for freeing enslaved people who had no apparent worldly value to him, suggesting he should free enslaved people who were young and strong and could be useful. Abu Bakr replied — and this exchange is preserved in Ibn Hisham — that his purpose was not usefulness to himself but the pleasure of Allah.

## The Muadhdhin of Islam

The call to prayer — the adhan — is one of the most recognizable sounds in Islamic civilization. Five times a day, from mosques across the world, a human voice rises and declares: "Allahu Akbar. Allahu Akbar. Ashhadu an la ilaha ill-Allah. Ashhadu anna Muhammadan Rasul-Allah. Hayya ala al-salah. Hayya ala al-falah." The man whom the Prophet ﷺ chose to be the first in history to give this call was Bilal ibn Rabah.

After the Hijrah to Medina and the construction of the Prophet's mosque, the question of how to call the community to prayer was discussed among the companions. Various suggestions were made — blowing a horn like the Jews, ringing a bell like the Christians. Then Abdullah ibn Zayd al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him) came to the Prophet ﷺ and described a dream in which he had heard the words of the adhan. Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) also reported having heard these words in a dream. The Prophet ﷺ confirmed the adhan and instructed that it be used.

But the confirmation of the words was only one part of the decision. The other part was who would give the call. The Prophet ﷺ chose Bilal. He said: "O Bilal, arise and call to the prayer." This choice was deliberate and its symbolism was profound. The man who had said "Ahad! Ahad!" under a rock in the heat of Mecca would now stand and say "Allahu Akbar!" for all of Medina to hear. The voice that had whispered the name of God in torture would now proclaim it from a height. The slave who had been owned by one of Islam's enemies would now be the herald of Islam's prayer.

Bilal stood to give the adhan, and when the Muslims heard it, those who had not yet known him heard a voice that moved them. The Prophet ﷺ listened to it with visible emotion. Abu Bakr wept. The adhan of Bilal became the sound of the early Muslim community gathering, and there is something in the knowledge that this sound was first given by a formerly enslaved Abyssinian man that continues to resonate across centuries.

## At the Conquest of Mecca: Atop the Ka'bah

The Conquest of Mecca in 8 AH was the culmination of a journey that had begun with the first revelation twenty years earlier. The Prophet ﷺ entered the city of his birth — which had expelled him and tortured his companions — as its conqueror. He entered with his head bowed in humility, reciting Surah al-Fath, the Chapter of Victory. And at a moment that no one who witnessed it would ever forget, he called for Bilal.

Bilal (may Allah be pleased with him) climbed atop the Ka'bah — the ancient house of Allah that had been filled with 360 idols, around which the Quraysh had circled in their shirk for generations — and he gave the adhan from its roof. His voice rang out over the rooftops of Mecca, over the heads of the people who had once tortured him and enslaved him, over the city where Umayyah ibn Khalaf had pressed a rock onto his chest and demanded he deny God.

The Qurayshi leaders who witnessed this were reportedly incensed. Some said it was an outrage that a slave was standing atop the Ka'bah. Attab ibn Usayd said: "Praise be to Allah that my father died before seeing this day." Khalid ibn Usayd said something similar. Others who had accepted Islam more recently but still carried the pride of Qurayshi aristocracy found it difficult to accept.

Abu Sufyan — who had just accepted Islam and was watching — said nothing critical, reportedly remarking carefully that he would not say what he thought. Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) replied: "If you remain silent, the Messenger of Allah will come to know whatever is in your heart."

But for Bilal, there was nothing to say. He had already said everything, over and over, in the midday heat of Mecca: Ahad. Ahad. Now he said Allahu Akbar from the rooftop of the Ka'bah, and the world had changed.

## The Footsteps in Paradise

The Prophet ﷺ heard something once that no one else could hear. In a hadith recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, the Prophet ﷺ said to Bilal at Fajr time: "O Bilal, tell me about the most hopeful deed you have done since you accepted Islam, for I heard the sound of your sandals in front of me in Paradise." Bilal replied: "I have not done any deed that seems more hopeful to me than that I do not become ritually impure at any hour of the night or day without praying as many rak'ahs as are decreed for me."

This hadith is among the most tender in the entire corpus — the Prophet ﷺ telling his companion that he has seen him in Paradise, identified him by the sound of his footsteps. The deed Bilal identified as his most hopeful was not a dramatic act of sacrifice or a large donation of wealth. It was a quiet practice of consistent tahara (ritual purity) and nafl (voluntary) prayer. The simplicity of his answer reflects the character of a man whose faith had been forged in suffering and expressed in constancy.

## Life After the Prophet

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ died in 11 AH, Bilal ibn Rabah (may Allah be pleased with him) could not bear to stay in Medina. The city was saturated with the presence of the Prophet ﷺ — every street, every mosque, every moment of the daily rhythm had been shaped by him. And the adhan, which Bilal had given for the Prophet ﷺ for years, was now a call to a prayer the Prophet ﷺ would not lead.

Bilal went to Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) and asked permission to go to Syria to participate in the jihad. He told Abu Bakr that he had heard the Prophet ﷺ say that a person who strives in the path of Allah is better than one who remains at home. He said, in some narrations, that he could not give the adhan again, because every time he reached the words "I bear witness that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah," he would weep at the memory.

Abu Bakr wept and gave him permission. Bilal went to Syria and participated in the early campaigns there. He gave the adhan publicly only twice after the Prophet's death. The first was when Umar ibn al-Khattab visited Syria and asked him to give the adhan one more time. Bilal did, and the companions who were present — men who had not wept openly since the Prophet's death — broke down at the sound of his voice and the memories it carried. The second occasion, according to some accounts, was when Umar entered Jerusalem.

Bilal died in Damascus, approximately in 20 AH, during the caliphate of Umar. He was somewhere between sixty and seventy years old. His final words, according to some accounts, were: "What happiness! Tomorrow I will meet my beloved ones — Muhammad ﷺ and his companions."

The sound of Bilal's adhan is preserved not in any recording — none existed — but in the tradition itself, in the practice of every muadhdhin who has stood in every mosque in every century since, declaring what Bilal first declared: that Allah is greater than everything that stands before Him, and that Muhammad ﷺ is His Messenger.
