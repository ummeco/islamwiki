# The Prophet's Outreach During the Hajj Season

After the deaths of Khadijah and Abu Talib and the failure of the Ta'if mission, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ faced a strategic situation of the greatest difficulty. He had no protector in Mecca beyond Mut'im ibn Adi's personal guarantee, his community remained under threat, and the task of establishing a base from which Islam could grow appeared stalled. Yet the annual Hajj season offered something unique: it brought delegations from every corner of Arabia to Mecca for religious observances, creating a temporary gathering of the entire Arab world at a single location.

Beginning after the Ta'if journey and continuing through approximately the final three years of the Meccan period, the Prophet ﷺ used each Hajj season as an opportunity for systematic outreach — going from encampment to encampment, tribe by tribe, presenting the message of Islam and asking for support and protection. This campaign of Hajj-season dawah ultimately produced the connections with Medina that made the Hijra possible.

## The Method and Approach

The Prophet ﷺ did not enter this campaign alone or naively. Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) was often with him, and the narrations preserve details of how Abu Bakr served as an effective introduction. Abu Bakr had an encyclopedic knowledge of Arab tribal genealogies — he was known as one of the greatest experts in nasab (genealogy) in all of Arabia — and this allowed him to approach delegations by establishing familiar connections and introducing the Prophet ﷺ in terms that each tribe's pride in its ancestry would recognize.

The Prophet's ﷺ message to each tribe was consistent in its core: "O people, say 'There is no god but Allah' and you will succeed, and through this you will have dominion over the Arabs and the non-Arabs will submit to you." Some accounts add: "And you will be kings after your deaths" — meaning the reward of Paradise.

This formulation was simultaneously a theological claim and a political one. It acknowledged that the tribes were interested in worldly outcomes — dominion, success, standing — and it situated the message of tawhid as the path to those outcomes as well as to the ultimate outcome of the next world. The Prophet ﷺ was not presenting Islam as a path of mere spiritual improvement divorced from worldly realities; he was presenting it as the truth that would reshape reality itself.

Abu Bakr would introduce the Prophet ﷺ by his genealogy: "This is the son of Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib" — establishing his lineage in the Hashimite clan, which was a respected lineage. The introductions were calculated to maximize the chance of a fair hearing.

## The Tribes That Rejected

The responses of the various tribes are preserved in Ibn Hisham's Seerah with unusual specificity — the names of the tribes, their encampment locations, the words of their chiefs, and the grounds for their rejections. This specificity suggests that the accounts derive from eyewitness testimony preserved carefully in the early community.

**Banu Amir ibn Sa'sa'a** was one of the significant tribes approached. The Prophet ﷺ presented Islam to them and their chief Bayharah ibn Firas responded with words that revealed the tribal power dynamics at work: "By Allah, if I had this young man from Quraysh, I could consume the Arabs with him." He then said to the Prophet ﷺ: "Tell me — if we follow you and Allah gives you victory over your opponents, would the authority after you be ours?" The Prophet ﷺ replied: "Authority belongs to Allah — He places it where He wills." Bayharah refused, saying: "We should expose our necks to the Arabs for your sake, and then if Allah gives you victory, someone else gets the authority? We have no need of your matter." Another elder of the tribe is reported to have warned the Prophet ﷺ that his own people (Quraysh) who knew him best had rejected him, suggesting he desist.

The response of Banu Amir illustrates a recurring pattern: tribes were interested in political alliance only if it came with political benefit. The Prophet's ﷺ refusal to guarantee them authority after his time was the correct answer — the authority of Islam belongs to Allah — but it was not the answer that Bayharah wanted.

**Kinda** was approached and declined. Their response is not preserved in as much detail as Banu Amir's, but Ibn Hisham records that they turned the Prophet ﷺ away.

**Kalb** — specifically the Banu Abdallah clan within Kalb — were approached. The Prophet ﷺ reportedly told them: "O Banu Abdallah — your ancestor's name was beautiful." (Abdullah means "servant of Allah.") But they also rejected him.

**Banu Hanifa** gave one of the most dismissive responses: they received the Prophet ﷺ with outright hostility and told him his people would have more knowledge of him than them — meaning that since Quraysh, who knew him, rejected him, there was no reason for others to accept.

**Banu Abs** rejected the message as well.

**Banu Nasr** and several other tribes are mentioned in the sources as having been approached and having declined.

Some of the rejections were accompanied by active hostility. After the Prophet ﷺ left each camp, a man among the tribe — sometimes named, sometimes not — would follow him and call out to people: "Don't follow this man!" The Quraysh had organized efforts to discredit him even in the pilgrimage season, sending men to counter his message in the camps.

## Abu Lahab's Counter-Campaign

Among the most persistent of those countering the Prophet's ﷺ mission in the Hajj encampments was his own uncle, Abu Lahab ibn Abd al-Muttalib. The narrations preserved in Ibn Hisham describe Abu Lahab following the Prophet ﷺ from camp to camp, arriving just after him or walking beside him, and telling each tribe: "This man is calling you to abandon your ancestors' religion and to follow him. Don't listen to him."

The irony of a man's own uncle following him to undermine him in front of strangers added to the difficulty of the Prophet's ﷺ task. Abu Lahab's presence gave the tribal leaders a Hashimite authority figure who confirmed their hesitation — if the Prophet's own clan member was warning against him, why should outsiders trust him?

This sustained counter-campaign by Abu Lahab is part of why the Prophet ﷺ asked his first tribal contacts to keep his approach quiet (as he had asked Dimad at Ta'if) — by the time the Hajj outreach was underway, Abu Lahab had turned the counter-campaign into a Hajj-season activity.

## The Significance of His Message About Dominion

The Prophet's ﷺ consistent message — "say la ilaha illallah and you will succeed, and you will have dominion over the Arabs and non-Arabs" — has been discussed extensively by the scholars. Some have seen it as purely eschatological: the success and dominion of the next world. But the historical outcome — Islam did spread throughout Arabia and then through Persia, Byzantium, and beyond — suggests that the prediction was at least partly about worldly political reality.

The scholars note that this message demonstrated the Prophet's ﷺ confidence in the ultimate outcome of his mission even when every immediate indicator suggested failure. He was presenting himself to tribes as a leader who would produce worldly success at a time when he had no army, no political base, no formal territory. This confidence either reflected genuine prophetic knowledge of the future or was the most implausible form of delusion — and the historical record confirms the former.

## The Six Men of Khazraj: The Turning Point

Amid the rejections, one meeting in approximately the ninth or tenth year before the Hijra (scholars vary on the exact year, most placing it about three years before the Hijra) produced a completely different outcome. The Prophet ﷺ met six young men from the Khazraj tribe of Yathrib — the city that would later be called Medina al-Munawwara.

The six responded to Islam immediately. Their specific names, the fact that they came from Yathrib (Medina), and the broader context of why they were receptive are covered in detail in the subsequent account of the first Aqaba pledge. But in the context of the Hajj outreach campaign, their conversion represented the first breakthrough after the sustained campaign of rejections.

Their receptiveness was not accidental. The people of Yathrib had been living as neighbors to the Jewish tribes — Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza — for generations. These Jewish tribes spoke of a prophet who was expected, and the Khazraj had grown up hearing about the concept of prophethood in a way that the tribes of the Hejaz and Najd had not. When the Prophet ﷺ presented himself and described his message, they had a conceptual framework that made the claim comprehensible in a way it was not for purely pagan tribal cultures.

## What the Campaign Accomplished

By ordinary measures, the Hajj season outreach appeared to produce meager results: years of effort, systematic rejection by every major tribe of Arabia, Abu Lahab's counter-campaign undermining each approach, and then a handful of young men from Khazraj who converted. The cumulative picture is one of patient failure redeemed by a single unlikely success.

But the scholars of seerah note that Allah's management of events operates on a logic different from immediate human calculation. The rejections themselves were a form of preparation — they established across Arabia that the Prophet ﷺ had presented his message openly, had asked for support and been refused, and had conducted himself throughout with integrity. The eventual triumph of Islam could not be attributed to political manipulation or coercion; it came after public rejection and deliberate, transparent outreach.

The connection to Yathrib, once made through the six Khazraj men, grew with extraordinary speed. Within two years, twelve would come to the second Aqaba pledge; within three, seventy-two would come for the third and decisive pledge that preceded the Hijra. The apparent failure of the Hajj outreach was the seed of the Islamic state.

The Prophet's ﷺ method throughout this campaign was notable for what it was not: he did not promise things he could not guarantee (he refused to promise authority to Banu Amir ibn Sa'sa'a), did not misrepresent the message's demands, did not use deception to secure commitments. Each rejection came after honest presentation. The tribes knew what they were declining.
