# Yom Kippur / Ramadan War

## Setting and Strategic Context

The Yom Kippur War — known in the Arab and Muslim world as the Ramadan War (حرب رمضان) or the October War — broke out on October 6, 1973 CE / 10 Ramadan 1393 AH. Its timing was carefully chosen: October 6 fell on Yom Kippur, the holiest day of the Jewish calendar, when Israeli society largely shuts down and military preparedness is at its lowest. For Arab Muslims, the war was fought during Ramadan, the month of fasting, worship, and, historically, many of Islam's greatest battles — including Badr and the conquest of Mecca.

The war's background lies in the unresolved consequences of the Six-Day War of 1967. Egyptian President Anwar Sadat (who succeeded Nasser upon his death in September 1970) and Syrian President Hafez al-Assad jointly concluded that diplomatic efforts to recover their occupied territories — Egyptian Sinai and Syrian Golan Heights — had failed. UN Resolution 242 remained unimplemented. The United States, under Nixon and Kissinger, showed little urgency in pressing Israel to withdraw. Sadat had reportedly offered a partial peace deal to Israel in 1971 that was rejected.

Sadat's strategic calculation was original and sophisticated. He did not believe Egypt could militarily destroy Israel or recover all of Sinai through force alone. Rather, he wanted to demonstrate Arab military capability in a limited, controlled offensive that would shatter the post-1967 myth of Israeli military invincibility and create the political conditions for a diplomatic process. Assad, whose objectives were different — full recovery of the Golan — agreed to the joint plan.

## The Opening Assault: A Military Masterpiece

At 2:00 pm on October 6, in carefully coordinated operations on two fronts, Egypt and Syria launched their attacks.

On the Egyptian front, 222 aircraft crossed the Suez Canal simultaneously at low altitude, striking Israeli airfields, missile batteries, and headquarters in the Sinai. Within twenty minutes, 1,000 artillery guns opened fire along the full length of the canal. Within ten minutes of the artillery barrage, Egyptian engineers deployed bridges and ferries at 10 crossing points along the canal. 8,000 infantrymen crossed in the first wave, using high-pressure water cannons to blast through the massive sand berms Israel had constructed on the eastern bank. Egyptian anti-tank guided missiles (Sagger missiles, Soviet-supplied) devastated the Israeli armored units that rushed to the canal. By October 8, five Egyptian infantry divisions held a bridgehead 10-15 kilometers deep on the eastern bank of the canal, and approximately 1,000 Israeli soldiers were dead — more casualties than in any comparable period of the Six-Day War.

On the Syrian front, approximately 1,400 Syrian tanks (against approximately 200 Israeli) poured into the Golan Heights, aiming to reach the Sea of Galilee and cut Israel in two. The battle on the Golan was among the most intense armored combat since World War II. A small Israeli force in the southern Golan held off a vastly larger Syrian force in the "Valley of Tears" (Emek HaBacha), losing most of their tanks but halting the Syrian advance long enough for Israeli reserves to mobilize.

## The Israeli Counterattack

Israel mobilized its reserve forces with extraordinary speed — within 24-48 hours, hundreds of thousands of reservists were reporting to units. By October 10-11, Israeli forces had driven the Syrians back beyond the pre-war ceasefire line on the Golan and were advancing into Syria itself, within 40 kilometers of Damascus. Syrian forces were reinforced by Iraqi and Jordanian units but could not reverse the Israeli advance.

On the Egyptian front, the situation was more complex. An Egyptian attempt to advance further east on October 14 — beyond the cover of their anti-aircraft missile umbrella — exposed Egyptian armor to Israeli air attack for the first time. Israel destroyed approximately 250 Egyptian tanks in a single day. On October 15-16, Israeli forces under General Ariel Sharon identified a gap between the Egyptian Second and Third Armies and drove a wedge through, reaching the western bank of the Suez Canal at the Bitter Lakes. Israeli forces rapidly expanded their bridgehead on the western bank, advancing toward Cairo and encircling the Egyptian Third Army on the eastern bank.

## Superpower Intervention and the Ceasefire

Both superpowers intervened with massive resupply operations. The United States conducted Operation Nickel Grass — airlifting approximately 22,000 tons of military equipment to Israel. The Soviet Union responded with its own airlift and sealift to Egypt and Syria. Nixon placed US nuclear forces on alert (DEFCON 3) when the Soviet Union appeared to be preparing to send forces to Egypt.

UN Security Council Resolution 338 was passed on October 22, calling for a ceasefire in place. Fighting continued for several days after the resolution as Israel sought to complete the encirclement of the Egyptian Third Army. A second ceasefire on October 25 was enforced.

## The Oil Embargo and Its Connection

The Ramadan War directly triggered the Arab oil embargo (covered separately in this collection). Saudi King Faisal, who had long warned the United States that continued support for Israel over Arab rights in Jerusalem and Palestine would force action, authorized Saudi Arabia's participation in the OAPEC oil embargo on October 17. The United States' massive resupply of Israel provided the immediate justification. Oil prices quadrupled within months, demonstrating for the first time the economic leverage of Arab oil-producing states.

## Outcomes and the Road to Camp David

The war ended with complex and mixed results. Egypt had achieved Sadat's primary strategic objective — demonstrating Arab military capability and shattering the myth of Israeli invincibility. Egyptian forces remained on the eastern bank of the canal, regaining a foothold in Sinai for the first time since 1967. But the Egyptian Third Army was encircled and rescued only by US and Soviet diplomatic pressure on Israel.

Israel recovered from the initial shock and ultimately encircled the Egyptian Third Army, but suffered more than 2,500 dead — far more than the quick, decisive victories of 1948 and 1967. The Agranat Commission of Inquiry found serious intelligence failures and lapses in military preparedness. Defense Minister Moshe Dayan and Prime Minister Golda Meir, though initially politically damaged, eventually resigned.

Sadat had created the political conditions he sought for diplomacy. Henry Kissinger's "shuttle diplomacy" produced disengagement agreements between Israel and Egypt (January 1974) and Israel and Syria (May 1974), with partial Israeli withdrawals. Sadat's subsequent historic visit to Jerusalem (November 1977) and the Camp David Accords (1978) — brokered by President Jimmy Carter — led to the Egypt-Israel Peace Treaty of March 1979. Egypt recovered all of Sinai in stages, completed in 1982. In exchange, Egypt became the first Arab state to recognize Israel.

## Sadat's Assassination

The peace treaty made Sadat deeply unpopular in the Arab world and among Egyptian Islamists. He was accused of abandoning Palestine and legitimizing Israeli occupation of Jerusalem and the West Bank. On October 6, 1981 — the eighth anniversary of the Ramadan War — Sadat was assassinated by members of Islamic Jihad (led by Khalid Islambouli) during a military parade in Cairo. The assassination was carried out by soldiers who broke from the parade and threw grenades and opened fire on the reviewing stand. Sadat's successor, Hosni Mubarak, maintained the Egypt-Israel peace treaty.

## The War in Islamic Memory

The Ramadan War holds a complex and significant place in Islamic consciousness. Its timing during the sacred month of Ramadan — and the initial Arab victories — was widely interpreted as a sign of divine assistance for those who struggle in God's cause. The Battle of Ramadan — as Islamic scholars and preachers referred to it — temporarily restored a sense of dignity and capability after the humiliation of 1967. Sermons in mosques across the Muslim world during October 1973 celebrated the Egyptian crossing of the canal as a return of the spirit of Islamic military history.

The war's ultimate mixed outcome — Arab military achievement followed by Israeli reversal and the political settlement — was interpreted differently by different Muslim thinkers. For those committed to political Islam, it demonstrated that Muslim military effort was necessary but that political leadership committed to Islamic principles, not secular nationalism, was the essential ingredient for full liberation. For pragmatists, it demonstrated that military means alone could not recover occupied land without diplomatic frameworks. The debate about the proper response to occupation and injustice — armed resistance, political diplomacy, or a combination — continues in Islamic political thought to the present day.
