# Sending of Mu'adh ibn Jabal to Yemen

In 10 AH, shortly before the Farewell Hajj, the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) appointed Mu'adh ibn Jabal al-Khazraji al-Ansari as his representative (governor, teacher, and judge) in Yemen. The appointment and the instructions the Prophet gave him became one of the most important sources for Islamic jurisprudence, governance, and judicial methodology.

## Mu'adh ibn Jabal

Mu'adh ibn Jabal was among the most learned of the Companions in Quranic knowledge and Islamic law. The Prophet said of him: "The most knowledgeable of my nation in matters of halal (permitted) and haram (forbidden) is Mu'adh ibn Jabal." He was from the Ansar — the Muslims of Medina — and had embraced Islam early. He was approximately 18 or 19 years old at the time of his appointment to Yemen, demonstrating the Prophet's practice of entrusting responsibility to capable young companions.

## The Instructions

As Mu'adh prepared to depart, the Prophet asked him a series of questions that have become famous in Islamic jurisprudential literature:

**Prophet:** "How will you judge when a matter is brought before you?"
**Mu'adh:** "I will judge according to the Book of Allah."
**Prophet:** "And if you do not find it in the Book of Allah?"
**Mu'adh:** "Then by the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah."
**Prophet:** "And if you do not find it in the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah?"
**Mu'adh:** "I will exert my own reasoning (ijtihad) and I will not fall short."

The Prophet reportedly struck Mu'adh's chest in approval and said: "Praise be to Allah who has enabled the messenger of the Messenger of Allah to do what the Messenger of Allah is pleased with."

This dialogue (known in the hadith literature as the "Hadith of Mu'adh") is the foundational text for Islamic jurisprudential method: Quran first, then Sunnah, then ijtihad (independent scholarly reasoning). It is cited in virtually every classical work of usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence), though scholars have debated the authenticity of its chain of transmission.

## The Mission

Mu'adh's mission in Yemen was comprehensive:
- **Da'wah (invitation to Islam):** He was to invite the people of Yemen to Islam, teach them the fundamentals of the faith.
- **Teaching:** He was to instruct them in prayer, zakah, and the Quranic recitation.
- **Judicial function:** He was to adjudicate disputes according to Islamic law.
- **Administrative function:** He was to collect zakah from the Muslims and jizyah from the People of the Book (Christians and Jews in Yemen).

The Prophet gave him specific instructions about zakah collection, emphasizing that he should take from the wealthy what is due and distribute it to the poor — and that he should not take the most prized possessions of the people.

## The Farewell

When Mu'adh mounted to depart, the Prophet walked alongside him and gave him further counsel, delivering what classical sources record as an extended farewell of personal advice. The Prophet said to him, among other things: "O Mu'adh, perhaps you will not see me after this year, and perhaps you will come upon my mosque and my grave."

Mu'adh wept at these words, understanding them as an indication that the Prophet knew his death was near. He left for Yemen, and the Prophet died shortly thereafter. Mu'adh was still in Yemen when he learned of the Prophet's death.

## Significance for Islamic Jurisprudence

The dialogue between the Prophet and Mu'adh about judicial method is among the most cited texts in Islamic legal theory:

**The hierarchy of sources.** Quran → Sunnah → ijtihad represents the foundational hierarchy of Islamic legal reasoning. Scholars like Imam al-Shafi'i built their systematic jurisprudence on this structure.

**The legitimacy of ijtihad.** The Prophet's approval of Mu'adh's intention to exercise independent reasoning confirmed that ijtihad is not only permissible but required when the primary sources do not directly answer a question.

**The Prophet's method of delegation.** The Prophet's questioning of Mu'adh before his departure exemplifies his practice of verifying qualifications and establishing clear guidelines before entrusting major responsibilities.

## Mu'adh's Later Career

Mu'adh remained in Yemen until the death of Abu Bakr, after which Umar ibn al-Khattab summoned him to Medina to serve as a consultant on legal matters. He later went to Syria, where he died during the plague of Amwas (18 AH / 639 CE), along with many senior companions.

He is remembered in Islamic tradition as one of the greatest scholars among the Companions, a model of the scholar-administrator who combined legal learning with practical governance.

## Sources

The account of Mu'adh's appointment is in Sunan Abi Dawud, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, and Musnad Ahmad. The famous dialogue on judicial method is referenced across the books of usul al-fiqh, including al-Shafi'i's *al-Risalah*, the earliest systematic work of Islamic legal theory.
