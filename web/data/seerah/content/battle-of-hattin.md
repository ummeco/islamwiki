# The Battle of Hattin: The Turning Point in the Liberation of Jerusalem

## Eighty-Eight Years of Occupation

In 492 AH / July 1099 CE, the armies of the First Crusade stormed Jerusalem after a siege of approximately five weeks. The sack that followed was among the most brutal in medieval history: Muslim and Jewish inhabitants of the city were massacred without quarter. Contemporary Crusader sources celebrate the killing; contemporary Muslim sources describe it with horror. The Fatimid garrison had been expelled before the final assault; the civilian population had nowhere to flee.

For eighty-eight years after this event, Jerusalem — al-Quds, the city of the Isra' wal-Mi'raj, the site of the Masjid al-Aqsa to which the Prophet ﷺ had been transported on his Night Journey (Quran 17:1), the first qibla of the Muslims — remained under Crusader rule. The Masjid al-Aqsa was converted into the headquarters of the Knights Templar (who called it the "Temple of Solomon" based on its association with the Temple Mount). The Dome of the Rock received a cross on its summit.

The Muslim world mourned and burned with the desire to reclaim the holy city. Nur al-Din Zengi came close but died before achieving it. Salahuddin al-Ayyubi was the one Allah ﷻ chose to be the instrument of its return.

## The Strategic Situation Before Hattin

By 582-583 AH / 1186-1187 CE, Saladin had unified Egypt, Syria, and significant parts of the Jazira under his authority — forming a strategic encirclement of the Crusader states that left them no room for expansion and limited their ability to receive effective reinforcement. The Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem, the County of Tripoli, and the Principality of Antioch were wealthy and militarily capable, but they were surrounded.

The internal politics of the Crusader states were themselves advantageous to Saladin. The kingdom was riven by factional disputes. The powerful magnate Raynald of Châtillon — lord of the Transjordan and the castle of Kerak — was the most belligerent element in the Crusader leadership, consistently violating truces that other Crusader lords were willing to observe. He had launched a sea raid into the Red Sea in 1182-1183 CE that reached as far as the Hijaz, attacking Muslim ships and threatening the holy cities — an action that horrified the Muslim world and that Saladin publicly swore to punish by killing Raynald personally.

In 582 AH / late 1186 CE, Raynald attacked a large Egyptian-Syrian caravan moving under the protection of an existing truce, capturing its goods, killing some of its escort, and imprisoning the merchants. Saladin sent to King Guy of Lusignan demanding justice; Guy was unable or unwilling to restrain Raynald. Saladin formally announced that the truce was void and began his campaign.

The imad al-Din al-Isfahani, Saladin's court secretary and eyewitness historian, records that Saladin gathered his commanders and announced: "When we move against the Franks, we will do so properly. We will not make a false start. We will strike at their heart."

## The Forces at Hattin

Saladin's forces in the summer of 583 AH / 1187 CE were the largest he had assembled: estimates in the sources range from approximately twelve thousand cavalry to much higher figures for the total army. The core of his force was the professional cavalry (ajnad), supplemented by volunteer warriors from across the Syrian and Egyptian domains who had come to participate in jihad for the liberation of Jerusalem. The morale of his army was exceptional — they understood the religious significance of what they were attempting and had prepared spiritually as well as militarily.

The Crusader force assembled by King Guy of Lusignan was the maximum military strength of the kingdom: approximately 1,200 knights (the cream of Crusader heavy cavalry), 4,000-5,000 other cavalry, and approximately 12,000-20,000 infantry. The "True Cross" — the Crusaders' most sacred relic, which they carried into battle in a golden reliquary, believed to guarantee divine protection — was brought from Jerusalem by the Bishop of Acre.

The kingdom had stripped its fortresses of their garrisons to assemble this force — a recognition that this was not a border skirmish but an existential confrontation.

## The Battlefield of Hattin: Geography as Destiny

Saladin's strategic genius at Hattin lay in his manipulation of terrain, logistics, and psychology. The battlefield was chosen — or rather forced upon the Crusaders — by Saladin's control of the region's water sources.

The Crusader army had assembled near the springs of Saffuriyya (modern Zippori), a well-watered position in the hills of Galilee. Saladin besieged the town of Tiberias on the Sea of Galilee, capturing it on 24 Rabi al-Akhir 583 AH / June 26, 1187 CE (though the citadel held out). The wife of the Count of Tripoli, Eschiva, was trapped in the citadel.

The Crusader commanders debated their response at Saffuriyya: attack to relieve Tiberias, or hold their well-watered position and wait for Saladin to extend himself? The Count of Tripoli, Raymond III — whose wife was in the citadel — argued against the march, recognizing that it would require crossing waterless terrain in the July heat. King Guy, pressured by Raynald of Châtillon and the Grand Master of the Templars, overruled Raymond and ordered the advance.

It was exactly what Saladin had hoped they would do. Ibn al-Athir records in al-Kamil that Saladin, when his scouts reported the Crusader army was marching, said "God give them no counsel" — recognizing that his enemy had made the decisive error.

## The March and the Night of Fire

The Crusader army began its march from Saffuriyya toward Tiberias on 3 Rabi al-Akhir 583 AH / July 2, 1187 CE. The distance was approximately sixteen miles across open hill country in the height of a Galilean summer. Water sources along the route were few, and Saladin had ensured that his forces occupied or denied those that existed.

From the moment the Crusader army marched, Saladin's cavalry attacked its flanks and rear incessantly — not seeking a decisive engagement but harassing, slowing, exhausting, and demoralizing. The infantry, who formed the outer shell of the Crusader formation protecting the cavalry within, bore the brunt of this constant pressure. Men fell from heat, thirst, and arrow wounds.

By the afternoon of the first day, the army had made little progress and was already in serious difficulty. The Count of Tripoli urged the king to push on to the springs of Hattin, still some miles ahead. Raynald and the Templars reportedly demanded the army halt and make camp where it was, on the plateau. Guy ordered the halt.

The Muslim forces set fire to the dry scrub around the Crusader camp that night, filling the air with smoke and ash. The Crusader horses and men, already tormented by thirst, spent the night breathing smoke. By morning, the situation was approaching crisis.

The Crusaders resumed their march on 4 Rabi al-Akhir 583 AH / July 4, 1187 CE. They could see the Sea of Galilee ahead and below them — water, visible and tantalizingly close, but separated from them by Saladin's entire army. They camped on and around the twin hills called the Qurn Hattin — the Horns of Hattin — a volcanic formation that rose above the surrounding plateau. The springs of Hattin were nearby but in Muslim hands.

## The Battle

The battle of the following morning was not a contest between two comparable forces. It was the methodical destruction of an exhausted, thirst-maddened, demoralized army that had effectively already lost before the fighting began.

Saladin's forces set the scrub on fire again, surrounding the Crusader position with smoke and flame. Muslim archers delivered volley after volley into the packed Crusader ranks. The infantry, unable to endure further, broke and fled for the hills — where they were ridden down or captured by Muslim cavalry. The few who reached the summits of the Horns fell to their knees in exhaustion, too spent to fight.

Three Crusader cavalry charges attempted to break through the Muslim encirclement. Imad al-Din al-Isfahani, who was present at the battle, describes watching these charges from near Saladin's position: the Muslim ranks parted to let the charges through, then closed around them. After each charge the Muslim lines simply reformed. Saladin's son al-Afdal, who was also watching, reportedly exclaimed after the second charge "We have beaten them!" Saladin answered: "Silence — we have not beaten them until that tent falls" — pointing to King Guy's command tent, which still stood. The third charge failed like the first two. The tent fell. The battle was over.

## The Captives and the Aftermath

The Crusader leadership was almost entirely captured: King Guy of Lusignan, Raynald of Châtillon, the Grand Master of the Templars Gerard de Ridefort, hundreds of knights and barons. The True Cross was captured. The Crusader military power in Palestine was effectively destroyed in a single morning.

Saladin's treatment of the captives reflected both his character and his calculation. King Guy he treated with the formal courtesy due to a fellow king: he provided him with a cool drink and reassured him. "Kings do not kill kings," he reportedly said. Guy would eventually be ransomed.

For Raynald of Châtillon, there was no mercy. Saladin confronted Raynald in front of the assembled captives, reminded him of his violation of the caravan truce, and offered him the choice of conversion to Islam. Raynald refused contemptuously. Saladin struck him with his sword, and his guards finished the execution. This was not gratuitous cruelty — Saladin had publicly sworn to kill Raynald, and the execution fulfilled that specific vow in the specific circumstances that had occasioned it.

The Knights Templar and Knights Hospitaller were executed en masse — recognizing that these military-religious orders, if ransomed, would simply return to combat against Muslims. Other knights and soldiers were ransomed. The non-military captives were treated according to the norms of Islamic law.

## The Fall of Jerusalem

In the weeks and months following Hattin, the Crusader fortresses and cities of Palestine fell one after another. Acre fell within a week. Ascalon, Caesarea, Jaffa, Beirut followed. By Rajab 583 AH / September 1187 CE, only Jerusalem itself, the city of Tyre, and a few isolated fortresses remained in Crusader hands.

Jerusalem surrendered on 27 Rajab 583 AH / October 2, 1187 CE — the anniversary of the Isra' wal-Mi'raj. The negotiated surrender and Saladin's merciful treatment of the Christian population contrasted completely with the Crusader massacre of 1099. The cross was removed from the Dome of the Rock. The call to prayer (adhan) rose from the Masjid al-Aqsa for the first time in eighty-eight years. Saladin entered the mosque and led the first Jumu'ah prayer in the liberated city.

## The Theological Significance

Muslim scholars and chroniclers understood Hattin and the liberation of Jerusalem in explicitly Quranic terms. Allah ﷻ says: "And if you are patient and fear Allah, their plot will not harm you at all. Indeed, Allah is encompassing of what they do." (Quran 3:120). And: "If Allah should aid you, no one can overcome you; but if He should forsake you, who is there that can aid you after Him?" (Quran 3:160).

The eighty-eight years of occupation were understood as a trial — a consequence of Muslim disunity, moral weakness, and departure from the genuine practice of Islam. The liberation was understood as divine assistance responding to sincere repentance, genuine effort, and the quality of a leader who combined military ability with personal piety and justice.

Ibn Shaddad records that after entering Jerusalem, Saladin wept — then gave himself completely to the work of restoring the city's Islamic character, distributing waqf endowments, repairing the Masjid al-Aqsa, and ensuring the rights of all the city's inhabitants.
