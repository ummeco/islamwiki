# Grand Mosque Seizure (1979)

The Grand Mosque Seizure (1 Muharram 1400 AH / 20 November 1979 CE) was the armed takeover of the Masjid al-Haram in Makkah by a group of several hundred militants led by Juhayman al-Utaybi. The seizure, which took place on the first day of the Islamic year 1400 AH, lasted approximately two weeks before Saudi security forces, with foreign assistance, retook the mosque. The event shocked the Muslim world and had lasting consequences for Saudi Arabian society and politics.

## Background

### The Leader: Juhayman al-Utaybi

Juhayman ibn Muhammad ibn Sayf al-Utaybi was a former corporal in the Saudi National Guard and a student of Shaykh Abd al-Aziz ibn Baz at the Islamic University of Madinah. He became increasingly radicalized, gathering followers who shared his view that the Saudi royal family had betrayed Islamic principles through corruption, Western influence, and deviation from the strict Salafi path.

Juhayman's group, known as al-Jama'a al-Salafiyya al-Muhtasiba (the Salafi Group that Commands Right and Forbids Wrong), operated as a semi-clandestine movement for several years before the seizure. They distributed pamphlets criticizing the Saudi government, the introduction of television, the employment of women, and ties with Western nations.

### The Mahdi Claim

Central to the plot was the proclamation that Muhammad ibn Abdullah al-Qahtani, Juhayman's brother-in-law, was the Mahdi (the awaited guided one). The timing was deliberate: the dawn of the year 1400 AH had eschatological significance for some Muslims, and various hadiths about the Mahdi's appearance were interpreted to match al-Qahtani's characteristics.

The mainstream scholarly position, then and now, is that no one can claim to be the Mahdi or have the Mahdi proclaimed for them in this manner. The hadiths about the Mahdi describe someone who will be recognized by the ummah through divine appointment, not through armed insurrection.

## The Seizure

### The First Day

On the morning of 1 Muharram 1400 AH, as tens of thousands of worshippers gathered for the Fajr prayer, Juhayman's group, numbering between 200 and 500 armed men, smuggled weapons into the mosque in coffins and under robes. After the Fajr prayer, they locked the gates, posted snipers on the minarets, and announced over the mosque's loudspeaker system that the Mahdi had appeared.

The imam of the Masjid al-Haram, Shaykh Muhammad ibn Subayil, was forced at gunpoint to hand over the microphone. The militants declared their allegiance to al-Qahtani as the Mahdi and called on the Muslim world to follow suit.

Thousands of worshippers were trapped inside the mosque, effectively becoming hostages. Some were released in the following hours and days, but many remained trapped throughout the siege.

### The Saudi Response

The Saudi government was initially caught off guard. The first counter-assault by Saudi security forces was repulsed with significant casualties, as the militants were well-armed and had fortified their positions within the massive mosque complex. The minarets provided excellent sniper positions overlooking the surrounding streets.

King Khalid convened an emergency meeting and obtained a fatwa from the Senior Council of Ulema, led by Shaykh Abd al-Aziz ibn Baz, authorizing the use of force to retake the mosque. Islamic law strictly prohibits fighting within the Sacred Mosque, but the scholars ruled that the extraordinary circumstances necessitated armed intervention to end the desecration.

### The Siege

The siege lasted approximately two weeks. The militants retreated into the vast network of rooms and underground chambers (the qabw) beneath the mosque, making a direct assault extremely difficult and costly. Saudi forces, supplemented by National Guard units, attempted multiple incursions into the underground areas, suffering heavy casualties from ambushes in the narrow corridors.

The Saudi government received tactical assistance from French GIGN (Groupe d'Intervention de la Gendarmerie Nationale) special forces advisors, who helped plan the final assault. Some accounts state that tear gas (or a stronger chemical agent) was pumped into the underground chambers to flush out the remaining militants.

### The End

By 4 December 1979 (approximately 14 Muharram 1400 AH), the last resistance was crushed. Muhammad al-Qahtani, the proclaimed Mahdi, had been killed during the fighting, which by the militants' own logic invalidated the Mahdi claim (authentic hadiths indicate the Mahdi would not be killed in such a manner). Juhayman and the surviving militants were captured.

## Casualties and Damage

Official Saudi figures stated that 127 Saudi soldiers and civilians were killed and 461 wounded. Among the militants, 117 were killed during the siege. The actual numbers may have been higher. The mosque itself suffered significant damage from the fighting, particularly the underground areas.

## Aftermath

### Executions

Juhayman al-Utaybi and 62 of his followers were publicly beheaded on 9 January 1980 in eight different cities across Saudi Arabia, the largest mass execution in modern Saudi history.

### Impact on Saudi Society

The Grand Mosque seizure had profound consequences for Saudi Arabia. Paradoxically, the government's response to the crisis was to move in a more conservative direction, granting the religious establishment greater influence over social policy. This was partly to counter the militants' accusations of laxity and partly because the government felt it needed stronger religious legitimacy.

Restrictions on women's public life were tightened, entertainment was further limited, the religious police (mutawwa'in) were given expanded authority, and religious education received increased funding. Many of these policies remained in place for decades.

### Regional Consequences

The seizure occurred during a turbulent period: the Iranian Revolution had taken place earlier in 1979, and the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan would begin in December 1979. Together, these events reshaped the political landscape of the Muslim world for a generation.

The event also exposed the vulnerability of the holiest site in Islam to armed attack, leading to a comprehensive overhaul of security arrangements for the Masjid al-Haram and for the Hajj more broadly.
