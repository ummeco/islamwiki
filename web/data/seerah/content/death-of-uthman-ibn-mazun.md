# The Death of Uthman ibn Mazun

## The First of the Muhajirun to Die in Medina

Uthman ibn Mazun al-Jumahi was one of the earliest Muslims — he had accepted Islam in the early Meccan period, migrated to Abyssinia with the first and second migrations, and then made the Hijra to Medina. He died in Medina in 2 AH — the second year after the Hijra — becoming the first of the Muhajirun (those who had migrated from Mecca) to die in Medina, and the first companion of the Prophet ﷺ to be buried in al-Baqi' al-Gharqad, the cemetery that would become the resting place of thousands of the Prophet's ﷺ companions.

His death was not dramatic in the way that the deaths of early martyrs at Badr and Uhud would be. He died of illness. But it was significant — the first death in the new city, the first burial in the new cemetery, the first parting from a community that was still finding its foundations. The Prophet ﷺ was present at his death and responded to it in ways that the hadith record preserves with care.

## The Prophet's ﷺ Response

When Uthman ibn Mazun died, the Prophet ﷺ stood over him and kissed him on the forehead — a kiss of farewell and love. The Prophet ﷺ wept as he did so, and his tears fell on Uthman's face. He said: "How blessed are you, O Abu al-Sa'ib — neither the world touched you nor did you touch the world."

This statement — a double expression of the purity of Uthman's life and his disengagement from the world's corruptions — is the Prophet's ﷺ own summary of who Uthman ibn Mazun was. He was not a warrior-companion, not a political figure, not a scholar known for extensive narration. He was a man of extraordinary personal piety who lived lightly and died as he had lived.

Uthman ibn Mazun had been known during his life for a severity of personal asceticism that the Prophet ﷺ had actually redirected at one point. Uthman wished to take a vow of celibacy and abandon all worldly pleasures entirely — the Islamic equivalent, in his mind, of the monastic ideal. The Prophet ﷺ forbade this. He said: "There is no rahbaniyya (monasticism) in Islam." He told Uthman that the Islamic path was moderation and balance — that even the Prophet ﷺ himself ate, drank, slept, lived with his family, and engaged with the world. The Muslim was not to abandon the world but to use it rightly.

This exchange became one of the most important statements in Islamic ethics about the relationship between piety and ordinary life. Uthman accepted the correction and continued his life of piety within the bounds the Prophet ﷺ had established — but his reputation for deep spiritual seriousness was such that when he died, the Prophet's ﷺ words of praise were unqualified.

## The Burial and al-Baqi'

Uthman ibn Mazun was buried in al-Baqi' al-Gharqad — a graveyard near Masjid al-Nabawi that became, over the following decade, the cemetery of the Medinan Muslim community and of the Prophet's ﷺ family and closest companions. The Prophet ﷺ marked Uthman's grave with a stone, saying: "I mark the grave of my brother by it." The marking of the grave was both personal — an act of memory and love — and practical, ensuring that the grave of the first Muhajir buried in Medina would be identifiable.

The Prophet ﷺ returned to Baqi' regularly during his Medinan years, greeting the inhabitants of the graves and praying for them. His practice of visiting graves — and his specific prayer taught for those visits: "Peace upon the inhabitants of this abode from among the believers and Muslims, may Allah have mercy on those who came before and those who will come later" — established visiting graves as a recommended practice for remembrance of death and prayer for those buried.

When the Prophet's ﷺ daughter Ruqayyah died, followed by his son Ibrahim, he visited al-Baqi'. When his wife Khadijah had already been buried in Mecca, he could not visit her there but instructed followers to pray for the inhabitants of graves wherever they might be. The community in al-Baqi' grew: by the time the Prophet ﷺ died in 11 AH, the majority of his family and many of his closest companions had been buried there.

## What Uthman's Death Meant for the Community

The death of Uthman ibn Mazun in 2 AH is a small event in the seerah's larger narrative — easily overlooked between the dramatic episodes of the first Ramadan fast, the Battle of Badr, and the change of qiblah. But its significance is disproportionate to its drama.

It established, first, that the community of the Hijra was now truly living — and dying — in Medina. The Muhajirun had left Mecca. Some had hoped for a quick return; most were adjusting to a permanent relocation. The first death among them in the new city was the final mark of permanence: this was not a temporary displacement but a migration. Medina was home. Its soil would receive their bodies.

It established, second, the cemetery of al-Baqi' as the burial ground of the early Islamic community — a place that millions of Muslims visit annually during Hajj and Umrah to stand among the companions of the Prophet ﷺ and pray for them and remember their own mortality.

It established, third, the practice of the Prophet ﷺ of personally attending the deaths and burials of his companions — of kissing the faces of the dead, of weeping, of marking graves, of praying for those buried. These practices were not incidental. They were the Prophet's ﷺ demonstration that death was to be honored, that grief was permitted and natural, that the dead were to be prayed for, and that the connection between the living and the dead was maintained through remembrance and du'a.
