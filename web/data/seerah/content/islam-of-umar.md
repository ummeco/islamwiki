# The Conversion of Umar ibn al-Khattab

## The Man the Quraysh Sent to Kill the Prophet ﷺ

Umar ibn al-Khattab was, before his conversion, one of the most formidable opponents of the early Muslim community in Mecca. He was physically imposing, socially connected, intellectually confident, and completely convinced that Muhammad ﷺ was tearing the Qurayshi social fabric apart. In the early years of Islam — when the community was small, persecuted, and meeting in secret — Umar was precisely the kind of man that made persecution effective: not a thug but an enforcer of tribal consensus, someone who gave the opposition to Islam a face of respectable, rational resistance.

His conversion came in approximately the sixth year of prophethood — the same year as the first migration to Abyssinia — and it came as a shock to everyone. It remains one of the most dramatic personal transformations in Islamic history, told in multiple narrations that differ in specific details but agree in their essential arc: the man who left his house to kill the Prophet ﷺ arrived at the house of his sister and brother-in-law to find them reciting Quran.

## The Day He Set Out

The classical sources record that Umar left his house one day in a state of determination, carrying his sword, with the explicit intention of killing the Prophet ﷺ. The Quraysh had reached a point of exasperation — the Muslims had not broken, the Prophet ﷺ had not stopped preaching, and a group had now escaped the Quraysh's reach entirely by migrating to Abyssinia. Something had to end.

On the way, Umar encountered a man — identified in some accounts as Nu'aym ibn Abdullah, a companion who had secretly accepted Islam — who warned him: before worrying about Muhammad, Umar should tend to his own house. His sister Fatimah bint al-Khattab and her husband Sa'd ibn Zayd had accepted Islam. Umar redirected.

## The Scene at His Sister's House

When Umar arrived at Fatimah's house, he could hear something being recited inside. He knocked and entered. What he had heard was the recitation of Surah Ta-Ha — the twentieth chapter of the Quran, revealed in Mecca, one of the great sustained addresses to the story of Musa and the nature of prophethood. A teacher named Khabbab ibn al-Aratt — who would later describe himself as one of the most persecuted of the companions — was inside and hid when Umar arrived.

Umar accused his sister and brother-in-law of following Muhammad ﷺ. He struck Sa'd ibn Zayd and then, when Fatimah defended her husband and stood between Umar and her husband, struck her and drew blood. Fatimah's response was the response of a woman who had crossed a line of commitment she was not willing to uncross: "Do what you will, Umar. We have submitted to Islam."

The sight of his sister bleeding — hurt by his own hand, refusing to recant — broke something in Umar. He asked to see what they had been reading. Fatimah told him he was ritually impure and would need to wash before touching the pages. Umar washed, took the leaf on which Surah Ta-Ha was written, and read.

## The Quran That Changed Everything

The words of Surah Ta-Ha — which open with Allah addressing the Prophet ﷺ directly: "We have not sent down this Quran upon you so that you should be in distress, but only as a reminder for the one who fears" (20:1-3) — struck Umar with a force he could not account for in purely rational terms. He read. He kept reading. Ibn Ishaq and Ibn Hisham record that Umar said about that moment: "How beautiful and noble is this speech."

Khabbab emerged from hiding. Umar said: Take me to Muhammad.

## The Confrontation at Dar al-Arqam

The Prophet ﷺ and the Muslim community were at the time meeting at Dar al-Arqam — the house of al-Arqam ibn Abi al-Arqam on the hill of al-Safa, which served as the first de facto headquarters of the Muslim community. When news reached the door that Umar ibn al-Khattab was outside with his sword, fear moved through the assembled company. Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib — who had converted not long before under equally dramatic circumstances — said: Let him in. If he comes for good, well. If he comes with evil intent, I will kill him with his own sword.

The Prophet ﷺ came to the door himself, took hold of Umar's garment, and spoke directly to him. Multiple narrations record the Prophet's words at this moment as a prayer addressed to Allah, asking Allah to strengthen Islam through Umar ibn al-Khattab — a prayer the Prophet ﷺ had been making repeatedly, asking that one of two men, either Amr ibn Hisham (Abu Jahl) or Umar ibn al-Khattab, accept Islam.

Umar declared his Islam. He was present in the heart of the Muslim community — in Dar al-Arqam — with his sword, as a Muslim.

## The Immediate Impact

Umar's conversion, like Hamza's before it, immediately shifted the balance in Mecca. Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, a companion who witnessed it, said: "Umar's Islam was a conquest, his migration was a victory, and his leadership was a mercy." Ibn Mas'ud also reported that before Umar's conversion, Muslims could not pray openly at the Kaaba. After his conversion, Umar insisted that the Muslims pray there publicly — and they did.

Umar himself made his Islam known immediately and without concealment. He went to Abu Jahl, whom he knew well, and told him directly: I have become Muslim. He then made a public declaration around the Kaaba, openly inviting the Quraysh to respond. No one did. The calculations of intimidation and social leverage that the Quraysh had used so effectively against individual Muslims suddenly encountered a man who operated on exactly those same terms of personal authority and physical presence — and was now on the other side.

The Prophet ﷺ gave Umar the title al-Faruq — the one who distinguishes between truth and falsehood — a name that would define Umar's character across his life. He was not a subtle man. He was a man of absolute clarity — and when that clarity turned toward the truth, it became one of the greatest forces in the history of Islam.

## What His Conversion Meant

The timing of Umar's conversion, following closely on the first migration to Abyssinia and on Hamza's conversion, represents a pivotal shift in the position of the early Muslim community. Three things had changed within a few years of open persecution: the community had established the principle of migration as a legitimate response to oppression; Hamza had demonstrated that the Muslims had a physical defender; and now Umar had joined, making the Muslim community no longer a group that could be persecuted without social consequence.

The Prophet ﷺ had prayed for Umar by name. That prayer had been answered. And the answer would echo for the rest of Islamic history — through the greatest years of the rightly-guided caliphate, through the Islamic conquests of Persia and Byzantium, through Umar's personal humility and extraordinary governance as the second Caliph, and through his martyrdom at the hands of Abu Lu'lu'ah al-Majusi in the mosque in Medina in 23 AH. From the man with the sword on his way to kill the Prophet ﷺ to the Amir al-Mu'minin who administered the largest empire of his age: the distance between those two points is one of the most remarkable journeys in human history, and it began with a leaf of Surah Ta-Ha and a bleeding sister who refused to deny her Islam.
