# Abdul Qadir al-Jilani: The Scholar and the Sultan of Saints

## Death in Baghdad

Abu Muhammad Muhyi al-Din Abd al-Qadir ibn Abi Salih al-Jilani departed this world in Baghdad on the night of Saturday, 10 Rabi al-Akhir 561 AH / February 11, 1166 CE. He was buried in the madrasa that had been his home and place of teaching for decades. His tomb, in what is now the Bab al-Sheikh neighborhood of Baghdad, remains standing today — though the building over it has been repeatedly expanded and rebuilt over the centuries.

He was, by most calculations, approximately ninety years old at the time of his death. The long life was entirely in keeping with the character of his career: he had not become famous until middle age, had taught and preached for decades, and had produced work whose influence would only grow after his death. Of all the Sufi figures in Islamic history, none has exercised broader or more lasting influence than Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani, and the Qadiriyya order that traces its origins to him remains today the most widespread Sufi order in the world — present from West Africa to Southeast Asia, from Central Asia to the Caribbean.

## Origins and Education

Abd al-Qadir was born in approximately 470 AH / 1077 CE in Jilan — the mountainous region south of the Caspian Sea corresponding to modern Gilan province in northern Iran. The people of Jilan had converted to Islam gradually; the region retained a distinctive culture and produced notable scholars and rulers. His mother's family traced descent from the Prophet ﷺ through al-Hasan ibn Ali; his father's family from al-Husayn ibn Ali — making him a sayyid from both lineages, a claim that later devotional literature emphasized and that his biographers record.

He came to Baghdad as a young man of approximately eighteen years — traveling, according to accounts, with the encouragement of his mother, who sewed forty gold coins into his clothing and extracted from him the promise to always tell the truth, whatever the consequences. A famous story, reported by multiple sources, records that he encountered bandits on the road who asked if he carried anything. He replied honestly that he had forty dinars in his garments. The surprised brigands released him, reportedly so impressed by his truthfulness in potentially dangerous circumstances that several of them repented and converted.

Whether literal or exemplary, this story captures the central virtue that all accounts of Abd al-Qadir emphasize: uncompromising commitment to truth and to the Sunna, regardless of personal cost.

## Scholarly Formation in Baghdad

Baghdad in the late 5th century AH / late 11th century CE was still, despite the political turbulence of the Buwayhid period and its aftermath, the greatest center of Sunni Islamic scholarship in the world. The Nizamiyyah Madrasa had recently been founded; the great mosques contained scholars of the highest caliber; and the Hanbali tradition — committed to the strictest possible adherence to Quran and Sunnah — was particularly strong in the city.

Abd al-Qadir studied fiqh in the Hanbali school under Qadi Abu Sa'd al-Mukharrami, who eventually appointed him to teach at his madrasa. He also studied Shafi'i fiqh (demonstrating breadth beyond mere sectarian loyalty), hadith under multiple teachers, and Arabic language sciences. His education was thorough in the transmitted Islamic sciences — the 'ulum naqliyya — before he turned to the spiritual development that would define his legacy.

He studied the spiritual sciences (tasawwuf) under two notable teachers: al-Mukharrami himself and the Sufi Abu'l-Khayr al-Dabbas. The combination of rigorous Hanbali jurisprudence and authentic Sufi spiritual discipline under qualified teachers was to be the defining characteristic of Abd al-Qadir's approach — and what made him acceptable to Hanbali scholars who were often deeply suspicious of Sufi practices.

## The Preaching and Its Impact

Abd al-Qadir began preaching publicly in Baghdad approximately 521 AH / 1127 CE, initially in small gatherings and then, as his reputation grew, in ever-larger venues. He eventually gave his public sermons (majalis) in the ruined madrasa of Ibn Aqil — a large open space that could accommodate the enormous crowds he attracted.

The accounts of these sermons in "Bughyat al-Talib" (the principal early biographical collection compiled by his son) and in Ibn Kathir's "al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya" describe scenes of extraordinary religious intensity. Crowds of thousands would gather. People of all social classes — rulers, scholars, merchants, laborers, women, non-Muslims — came to hear him. His preaching was direct, powerful, and uncompromising: he called people to account for their sins, reminded them of death and the Day of Judgment, criticized rulers and scholars alike for falling short of their religious obligations, and offered the path of sincere repentance and return to Allah ﷻ.

Ibn Kathir, the careful and authoritative historian and hadith scholar, writes in al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya that Abd al-Qadir was "the most complete of the shaykhs of his time in knowledge, state, and religion." He notes that Abd al-Qadir was known for speaking harshly to rulers when they came to him — not flattering them for the sake of material gain — and for the consistency of his personal practice. Ibn Kathir, who was Hanbali in his own legal orientation, reports Abd al-Qadir's biography with admiration and careful discrimination, taking note of the extraordinary reports about him while maintaining his characteristic historical restraint.

The conversions attributed to his preaching were numerous. Many Jews, Christians, and followers of other religions reportedly embraced Islam after hearing him. Many notorious sinners made sincere tawbah (repentance). The tradition of massive public preaching — the mawlid-style gathering of enormous crowds to hear a single scholar's exhortation — is partly traceable to Abd al-Qadir's Baghdad sermons.

## His Scholarship: The Major Works

Abd al-Qadir was not merely a preacher but a scholar of substance. His two principal written works have been continuously read, copied, and commented upon for nearly nine centuries.

**"Al-Ghunya li-Talibi Tariq al-Haqq"** (The Sufficient Provision for Seekers of the Path of Truth) is a comprehensive work in Islamic ethics and jurisprudence. It covers the obligations of daily worship, the conditions of prayer, fasting, zakat, and Hajj, the ethics of social interaction, marriage, and commerce, and the internal states of the heart. It is fundamentally a Hanbali fiqh work that integrates the spiritual dimension into the legal — demonstrating that the law and the spiritual path are one, not two separate things.

**"Futuh al-Ghayb"** (Revelations of the Unseen) is a collection of seventy-eight discourses (maqalat) on spiritual themes: the nature of trust in Allah (tawakkul), the meaning of patience (sabr), the states of the spiritual traveler (salik), the qualities of the sincere seeker. These discourses are direct, vivid, and demanding — they presuppose a reader committed to rigorous self-examination and genuine transformation, not comfortable religious practice.

Both works reflect a scholar who held himself and his readers to the highest standards of Quran and Sunnah compliance — who insisted that genuine spirituality must begin with scrupulous legal observance and that any claimed spiritual state that bypassed or contradicted the Shari'a was not authentic.

## The Qadiriyya Order

In the generations after Abd al-Qadir's death, his spiritual legacy was transmitted through a silsilah (chain) of teachers and students that eventually formalized into the Qadiriyya order. His sons established centers of his teaching in Baghdad. His disciples carried his methods and teachings to other cities. Over centuries, the Qadiriyya spread across the entire Islamic world.

Today the Qadiriyya is found in virtually every Muslim country: dominant in Turkey, Iraq, and parts of the Arab world; widespread in West Africa, East Africa, and the Swahili coast; present in South Asia (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh); reaching Southeast Asia. Its universality is a testament to Abd al-Qadir's reputation as a figure of authentic Islamic scholarship combined with genuine spiritual power — a combination that has appealed across cultural and geographical contexts.

## A Necessary Clarification: What Islam Prohibits

Any honest account of Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani must include a clarification that he himself, based on everything he taught and wrote, would insist upon.

The practices that have developed at his shrine in Baghdad, and at shrines attributed to him elsewhere, including: calling upon Abd al-Qadir by name to grant requests, seeking his intercession to obtain worldly or spiritual benefits, making oaths in his name, circling his tomb, kissing it or prostrating near it for blessing — these practices are not endorsed by Islamic theology. They represent innovations (bid'ah) that contradict the fundamental principle of tawhid: that all worship, invocation, and seeking of help in matters beyond normal human capacity belongs to Allah ﷻ alone.

The Prophet ﷺ explicitly warned against the elevation of graves: "Do not sit on graves, and do not pray towards them" (Muslim). He warned: "O Allah, do not make my grave an idol that is worshipped" (Ahmad). Ibn Taymiyya and later Hanbali scholars — the very school Abd al-Qadir belonged to — were among the most consistent critics of shrine veneration precisely because it contradicts the tawhid that the Prophets ﷺ were sent to establish.

Abd al-Qadir's own writings emphasize this: in al-Ghunya, he is explicit that all tawassul (seeking intercession) must be directed to Allah, and that the deceased, however righteous, do not have the ability to hear, respond to, or benefit those who call upon them. He would not have sanctioned being called upon himself.

Respecting Abd al-Qadir means learning from his genuine scholarly and spiritual legacy — his books, his example of Hanbali legal rigor combined with authentic spiritual discipline — not attaching practices to his name that he never authorized and that violate the tawhid he spent his life teaching.

## The Legitimate Legacy

What Abd al-Qadir genuinely gives the Muslim community is: a model of the scholar who combines breadth of Islamic knowledge with depth of spiritual sincerity; a demonstration that Hanbali strictness and genuine Sufi inner development are not opposites; an example of public preaching that was direct, uncompromising, and effective; and two written works that remain valuable resources for anyone seeking to combine legal scrupulousness with genuine inner transformation.

That legacy is rich enough. It needs no supplementation from theological innovation.
