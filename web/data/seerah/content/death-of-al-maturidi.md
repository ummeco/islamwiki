# Abu Mansur al-Maturidi: The Theologian of Samarkand

## The Other Founder of Sunni Theological Reason

Among the many remarkable features of classical Islamic intellectual history is this: at roughly the same historical moment in the early fourth century AH, two scholars in widely separated cities independently arrived at very similar solutions to the same theological crisis. In Basra, Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari was breaking from the Mu'tazilites and developing a rational Sunni theology in the Arabic-speaking heart of the Abbasid caliphate. In Samarkand, far to the east in the thriving cities of Transoxiana (what is now Uzbekistan), Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (may Allah have mercy on him) was developing, largely independently, a parallel rational theology rooted in the Hanafi legal tradition.

Al-Maturidi died in Samarkand in 333 AH / 944 CE, leaving behind two major works — Kitab al-Tawhid and Ta'wilat Ahl al-Sunnah — that became the foundational texts of the Maturidi school of theology. His school, carried and developed by subsequent generations of Hanafi scholars across Central Asia, Persia, Turkey, the Indian subcontinent, and the Balkans, represents the theological orientation of perhaps forty to forty-five percent of the world's Sunni Muslims — a larger proportion in numerical terms than even the Ash'ari school, given the demographic weight of South Asian, Central Asian, and Turkish Muslim communities, which are overwhelmingly Hanafi-Maturidi.

Yet al-Maturidi is far less famous in the Arabic-speaking world and in Western scholarship than al-Ash'ari, partly because the scholarly language of the eastern Islamic world was Persian and Turkish rather than Arabic, and partly because the Arabic theological tradition that came to dominate Islamic higher education centered more on the Ash'ari curriculum. His relative obscurity in some quarters is a historical accident that does not reflect his actual theological importance.

## Samarkand: A Great Center of Eastern Islamic Learning

To understand al-Maturidi, one must appreciate the city and region in which he worked. Samarkand, in the province of Transoxiana (Ma wara' al-Nahr — "that which is beyond the [Amu Darya] river"), was one of the most ancient and sophisticated cities in the Islamic world. Conquered by Qutayba ibn Muslim in 87 AH / 706 CE, it had been an important center of commerce and culture for millennia before the Islamic period. The Silk Road passed through it, connecting the Mediterranean world with China. Under the Samanid dynasty (204-395 AH), which ruled Khurasan and Transoxiana as vassals of the Abbasid Caliphate before effectively becoming independent, Samarkand and Bukhara became major centers of Islamic scholarship — producing figures as diverse as al-Bukhari (from Bukhara), al-Tirmidhi (from nearby Tirmidh), and al-Maturidi himself.

The scholarly tradition in this region was overwhelmingly Hanafi in legal methodology, following Abu Hanifah's school in preference to the other madhabs. The Hanafi tradition had a particularly strong foothold in Central Asia and Persia, where it had been carried by the early Islamic scholars who came east with the conquests and by subsequent generations of local scholars who trained in the tradition. Al-Maturidi worked within this Hanafi framework, drawing on the theological implications of Hanafi legal methodology and developing them into a systematic theological school.

His teachers are known from the biographical tradition: he traced his learning through a chain of Hanafi scholars back to Abu Hanifah's student Abu Yusuf and to Muhammad al-Shaybani, and through the al-Maturidi's own teachers Nusayr ibn Yahya and Muhammad ibn Muqatil al-Razi. This connection to the Hanafi tradition was not incidental but foundational: the Maturidi school is, in a meaningful sense, the theological dimension of Hanafi jurisprudence, just as the Ash'ari school is closely associated with the Shafi'i and Maliki legal traditions.

## Kitab al-Tawhid: The First Systematic Maturidi Theology

Al-Maturidi's Kitab al-Tawhid (Book of Divine Unity) is the foundational text of his theological school. It addresses systematically the central questions of Islamic theology: the existence and nature of Allah, the divine attributes, human reason and revelation, the nature of the Quran, human acts and divine creation, prophethood, and eschatology.

His approach, like al-Ash'ari's but developed somewhat differently, uses rational argumentation while remaining grounded in the transmitted texts of Quran and Sunnah. Like al-Ash'ari, he was reacting against Mu'tazilism — the dominant rationalist theology that had imposed the Mihna on the scholarly community earlier in the ninth century — but also against simplistic literalism that he found theologically inadequate.

On the divine attributes, al-Maturidi takes a position generally similar to al-Ash'ari's: Allah's attributes — knowledge, power, will, life, hearing, sight, speech — are genuinely real and genuinely distinct in some sense from His essence, but in a way unique to Allah that does not imply plurality or composition. The attributes are not identical to the essence (contra extreme Mu'tazilite denial) but neither are they separate from it in any way that would imply multiple eternal beings.

On the Quran: the eternal, uncreated Speech of Allah is the Quran as divine meaning and reality; the sounds and letters and ink through which humans encounter and express it are created. This is the standard Sunni position on the question that generated the Mihna.

On human action: this is where the Maturidi school most distinctly differs from both Mu'tazilism and from some Ash'ari formulations. Al-Maturidi and his school emphasize human choice and moral accountability more robustly than some Ash'ari articulations, arguing that humans genuinely choose and that this genuine choice is the basis for moral responsibility, while Allah creates the capacity, the circumstance, and ultimately the action. The resolution of divine omnipotence and human freedom that the Maturidi school offers has been criticized as leaving residual tension, but it reflects a genuine theological commitment to preserving the meaningfulness of human moral agency.

## The Approximately Forty Differences with the Ash'ari School

Classical scholars who compared the Ash'ari and Maturidi schools compiled lists of the issues on which the two traditions disagree. The standard count is approximately forty subsidiary questions — not fundamental disagreements, since both schools affirm the core Sunni positions, but differences in analysis, terminology, and conclusion on specific theological questions.

Among the best-known differences: Maturidis generally hold that reason alone, without revelation, can establish the existence of Allah and basic moral obligations — this is a more rationalist position than some Ash'ari formulations, which place greater emphasis on the necessity of revelation even for knowledge of Allah's existence. Maturidis also generally hold that faith (iman) does not increase or decrease in its essence — it is either present or absent — while Ash'aris hold that faith can increase and decrease through acts of worship and obedience. On the question of whether Allah can be described as having a "direction" (jiha), the two schools nuance their denials differently.

These differences are real but subsidiary. Neither school considers the other heterodox, and scholars across the centuries have recognized both as firmly within the bounds of Ahl us-Sunnah wal-Jama'ah. A Shafi'i scholar following Ash'ari theology and a Hanafi scholar following Maturidi theology are both in the mainstream of Sunni orthodoxy; their differences are the kind of internal theological disagreements that Islamic scholarship has always accommodated.

## Ta'wilat Ahl al-Sunnah: Quran Commentary

Al-Maturidi's second major work, Ta'wilat Ahl al-Sunnah — sometimes called simply al-Ta'wilat — is a comprehensive Quran commentary representing the Hanafi-Maturidi interpretation of the entire Quran. Unlike Tafsir al-Tabari, which is primarily a collection of transmitted opinions, al-Maturidi's Tafsir is more explicitly a theological reading: he uses the Quran as the primary source for establishing theological principles and responds throughout to Mu'tazilite, Shia, and other deviant readings.

The Ta'wilat became the standard Quran commentary within the Hanafi-Maturidi tradition, particularly in Central Asia and later in the Ottoman scholarly world. It was completed and transmitted by al-Maturidi's students and represents the developed form of his exegetical thinking.

## The Ottoman Continuation and Global Reach

The enormous global reach of Maturidi theology became apparent when the Ottoman Empire adopted the Hanafi-Maturidi synthesis as its official scholarly orientation. From the fourteenth century onward, as the Ottomans expanded their control across Anatolia, the Balkans, the Arab world, and eventually parts of Africa and Asia, the Hanafi legal school and Maturidi theology traveled with the institutional support of the empire's religious establishment (the Ulema hierarchy under the Shaykh al-Islam).

This meant that the madrasas of Istanbul, Cairo (under Ottoman rule), Damascus, Sarajevo, Skopje, and eventually Delhi and beyond trained scholars in the Hanafi-Maturidi tradition. When scholars from these regions went on to teach in their home communities, they carried this theological orientation with them. The result is that today, Muslim communities in Turkey, the Balkans, Central Asia, Afghanistan, Pakistan, India, Bangladesh, and much of the Middle East that has been historically Hanafi follow the Maturidi theological orientation — whether or not they know the name of the Samarqandi scholar who articulated its foundations a millennium ago.

## The Death of al-Maturidi and His Lasting Legacy

Al-Maturidi died in Samarkand in 333 AH / 944 CE. The exact date and circumstances of his death are not preserved in the biographical tradition with the same detail as some other major scholars, reflecting partly the relative lack of Arabic-language biographical coverage for Central Asian scholars compared to Iraqis and Egyptians.

His tomb in Samarkand — in the Chokurdiza quarter of the city — remained a visited site through subsequent centuries and into the modern period. The city of Samarkand, which eventually fell to the Mongols and was rebuilt under Timur (Tamerlane) in the late fourteenth century as one of the most splendid capitals in the Islamic world, has maintained its association with al-Maturidi's memory.

The Ahl us-Sunnah consensus, articulated by scholars across the centuries, is clear: the Maturidi and Ash'ari schools are both legitimate expressions of Sunni theological reason, both firmly opposed to the major theological deviations that Islamic scholarship has identified, and both deserving of respect and study. The diversity within Sunni theology — Athari, Ash'ari, and Maturidi approaches each emphasizing different aspects of the balance between transmitted text and rational analysis — is a feature, not a flaw: it represents the breadth and depth of the Islamic intellectual tradition's engagement with the most fundamental questions about Allah, creation, and the human relationship to both.
