# The Prophet's Departure for the Farewell Hajj

In Dhul Qa'dah, 10 AH (February 632 CE), the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) announced his intention to perform the pilgrimage (Hajj) — an announcement that sent the entire Islamic world into motion and drew the largest single gathering of Muslims in the Prophet's lifetime to accompany him on what would be his final Hajj.

## The Announcement

The Prophet had performed the lesser pilgrimage (Umrah) several times but had not performed the full Hajj since the early days in Mecca. The conquest of Mecca in 8 AH had returned the Ka'bah to monotheistic worship and removed the polytheists from control of the holy sites. In 9 AH, Abu Bakr had led the Muslims in Hajj and the Prophet had sent Ali ibn Abi Talib to proclaim that henceforth no polytheist would be permitted to perform Hajj and no one should circumambulate the Ka'bah naked — customs that had characterized the pre-Islamic pilgrimage.

By 10 AH, the conditions were right for the Prophet to lead the Hajj himself. When news spread that he intended to go, people came from all directions. Classical sources speak of tens of thousands — the estimates ranging from 90,000 to 124,000 pilgrims, though the higher figures are traditionally accepted in hadith literature.

## The Departure

The Prophet departed from Medina on the 25th or 26th of Dhul Qa'dah, 10 AH — approximately five or six days before the start of Dhul Hijjah. He entered the state of ihram at Dhul Hulayfah (Abyar Ali), the miqat (designated point of entry into the pilgrimage state) for the people of Medina. He bathed, anointed himself with perfume, and put on the two sheets of white unsewn cloth that constitute the ihram garment.

Aisha reported that she herself applied perfume to the Prophet at the miqat — she could see the scent of musk glistening in the parting of his hair.

## The Talbiyah

Upon entering ihram, the Prophet began reciting the talbiyah — the pilgrimage invocation:

*Labbayk Allahumma labbayk. Labbayk la sharika laka labbayk. Inna al-hamda wan-ni'mata laka wal-mulk. La sharika lak.*

("Here I am, O Allah, here I am. Here I am, You have no partner, here I am. Verily all praise, grace, and sovereignty belong to You. You have no partner.")

The sound of the talbiyah rose from tens of thousands of pilgrims across the desert landscape — one of the accounts most powerfully preserved in the seerah literature for its sensory and emotional impact.

## The Type of Hajj

The Prophet performed what is called Hajj al-Qiran — combining the Umrah and Hajj in a single ihram without exiting the consecrated state between them. This is one of the three valid forms of Hajj. He brought sacrificial animals (budun) with him from Medina, which bound him to the Hajj from the beginning.

When he saw that some of his companions who had not brought animals were frustrated (having to remain in ihram), he expressed regret: "If I had not brought sacrificial animals, I would have entered into Umrah as you did." This statement has been debated by the schools of jurisprudence as evidence for which form of Hajj is most meritorious.

## Significance of the Journey

The Farewell Hajj (Hajjat al-Wada) was so called because it was the Prophet's last pilgrimage and effectively his farewell to the community. This name was not known in advance — but the Prophet's references during the journey to the likelihood that this was his last, and his request that those present transmit his words to those absent, were recognized even at the time as signals of an approaching end.

The Farewell Hajj codified Islamic pilgrimage practice in detail. Every rite performed by the Prophet during these days — the tawaf (circumambulation), the sa'i (walking between Safa and Marwa), the standing at Arafat, the throwing of pebbles at Mina, the sacrifice — was observed and recorded by tens of thousands of witnesses, making the Hajj rites among the most multiply-attested practices in Islamic history.

The Prophet said during the Hajj: "Take your Hajj rites from me; I do not know whether I shall ever perform Hajj after this year." This direct statement, reported across the hadith collections, is why the journey bears the name Farewell Hajj.

## Sources

The Farewell Hajj is documented exhaustively in Sahih Muslim (Kitab al-Hajj, the "Jabir hadith" which is among the longest and most detailed single hadiths in the major collections), Sahih Bukhari, Sunan Abi Dawud, Sunan al-Nasa'i, and the major seerah works.
