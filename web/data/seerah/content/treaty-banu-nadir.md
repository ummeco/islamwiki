# Treaty with Banu Nadir

The treaty between the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and Banu Nadir — one of the major Jewish tribes of Medina — was established as part of the broader constitutional framework of the Medinan community following the hijra in 1 AH (622 CE). This treaty was part of the Constitution of Medina and governed the relationship between the Muslim community and Banu Nadir for approximately three years before its violation led to the tribe's expulsion in 4 AH.

## Banu Nadir's Position in Medina

Banu Nadir were settled in a fortified agricultural district south of the main city. They were prosperous farmers and had established economic relationships across the Hijaz. They included scholars learned in the Torah and were respected across tribal Arabia for their knowledge and established status.

As a party to the Constitution of Medina, Banu Nadir were part of the covenant community (ummah) established by the Prophet. They had obligations of mutual defense with the Muslim community and rights of legal autonomy within their own community.

## Terms of the Treaty

The treaty terms as recorded in the Constitution of Medina and supplementary agreements established:

**Blood money obligations.** Each tribal group, including Banu Nadir, was responsible for ransoming its own members who were taken captive and paying blood money for its own members who committed wrongful killing. The Muslim community and the Jewish tribes shared these obligations within the wider covenant.

**Mutual defense.** Banu Nadir, along with other parties, was obligated to contribute to the costs of defense against external attack on Medina.

**Non-aggression.** The parties agreed not to make war on each other or to support external enemies against the covenant community.

**The Prophet's arbitration.** Disputes were to be referred to the Prophet as supreme arbiter of the community.

**Religious autonomy.** Banu Nadir retained complete religious autonomy — "the Jews have their din (religion) and the Muslims have their din." They practiced Judaism without interference and maintained their own courts and community institutions.

**Economic relations.** Trade and economic interaction between the communities was permitted and regulated.

## The Purpose of the Treaty

The Constitution of Medina and its tribal sub-agreements served several practical functions:

**Creating a unified defense.** Medina faced ongoing threats from the Quraysh of Mecca and from hostile tribes across Arabia. A covenant community that included the Jewish tribes created a larger and more defensible entity than the Muslim community alone could have constituted.

**Regularizing the existing multi-tribal society.** Medina was already a complex multi-tribal environment. The treaty formalized existing relationships rather than creating a wholly new political order.

**Giving the Prophet political authority.** By making the Prophet the supreme arbiter, the treaty elevated his authority beyond the Muslim community and gave him a recognized constitutional role in the broader Medinan political framework.

## The Treaty's End

The treaty was violated, according to Islamic sources, when Banu Nadir participated in a plot to assassinate the Prophet during a visit he made to their quarter to seek their contribution to a blood money payment. The Prophet left when he received information about the planned assassination and declared the treaty broken.

After a siege of approximately fifteen days, Banu Nadir accepted expulsion from Medina and went to Khaybar (and some to Syria). The Quran's Surah al-Hashr (Chapter 59) is understood as addressing this event.

## Legacy

The treaties established between the Prophet and the various Medinan Jewish tribes — Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza — represent the first systematic attempt in Islamic history to define the rights and obligations of non-Muslim communities within a Muslim-governed political entity. The dhimma system that developed under the subsequent caliphates drew on these precedents and the principles they embodied: religious autonomy, protection of life and property, shared defense obligations, and clear consequences for treaty violation.

## Sources

The treaty terms are preserved in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya* (drawing on Ibn Ishaq) and in Abu Ubayd's *Kitab al-Amwal*. Modern scholarly analysis in W. Montgomery Watt's *Muhammad at Medina* and R.B. Serjeant's studies of the Medinan documents.
