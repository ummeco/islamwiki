# The Abolition of the Ottoman Caliphate: The Wound That Has Not Healed

## The Most Traumatic Date in Modern Islamic History

March 3, 1924 CE / 28 Rajab 1342 AH: the Grand National Assembly of Turkey voted to abolish the Ottoman Caliphate. With that vote, the last man to hold the title of Caliph — Abdulmecid II — was bundled into a car at 5:00 in the morning, driven to the Orient Express, handed a sum of money, and expelled from Turkey. He died in Paris in 1944, never permitted to return.

No event in modern Islamic history has had comparable theological and political significance. The Caliphate had existed, in various forms, since the death of the Prophet ﷺ in 11 AH / 632 CE. Even in its weakest moments — when the Mongols destroyed Baghdad and killed the Abbasid Caliph in 656 AH / 1258 CE — a nominal successor was quickly established. The Muslim world had not been without a recognized Caliph for over 1,300 years. Now it was.

## The Sequence of Events

The abolition of the Caliphate was the final act of a two-stage process. In November 1922, the Turkish Grand National Assembly had abolished the Ottoman Sultanate — separating the political-military office of Sultan from the religious office of Caliph. Sultan Mehmed VI, who had collaborated with the Allied occupation of Istanbul, was deposed and left Turkey on a British warship. His cousin Abdulmecid II was elected Caliph by the National Assembly, but as a purely ceremonial religious figure with no political authority whatsoever.

This arrangement was unstable from the beginning. Mustafa Kemal (Atatürk) and the nationalist leadership of Turkey had no use for an Islamic Caliphate, even a ceremonial one. They were building a secular Turkish national state on the French Jacobin model — a state in which religion would be strictly subordinated to and controlled by the government, and ultimately removed from public life. The Caliphate, however nominal, was a potential focus for opposition to this project and a claim by foreign Muslims to have a stake in Turkish political life.

When international Muslim leaders — including the Aga Khan and Amir Ali of India — wrote to Turkish newspapers in January 1924 requesting that the Caliphate be given a more substantial role and that Turkish leaders support its prestige in the Muslim world, Atatürk used this foreign "interference" as justification for immediate abolition. On March 3, 1924, the Caliphate was abolished by law. The religious courts (qadi courts) and the Ministry of Religious Endowments were also abolished in the same package of legislation.

## Abdulmecid II: The Last Caliph

Abdulmecid II (1868-1944 CE) was a cultivated man — an accomplished painter whose works are still exhibited, a lover of Western classical music, fluent in French, and by all accounts a dignified and thoughtful person. He had accepted the ceremonial Caliphate in good faith, received delegations from Muslim countries, corresponded with Islamic scholars, and attempted to fulfill his symbolic role while remaining entirely non-political.

His expulsion was abrupt and humiliating. Given no warning, he was told hours before the vote that he was to leave the country. He spent the rest of his life in Paris, largely in poverty — the Ottoman family's properties had been confiscated. He died in 1944 and was initially buried in Paris; his remains were later moved to Medina, where he rests today near the graves of the Companions.

His final public statement as Caliph, issued shortly before his expulsion, was dignified: he accepted his fate but expressed his hope that Islam would endure and that the Caliphate would be restored in God's time.

## The Muslim World's Response: Shock and Searching

The abolition triggered immediate shock across the Muslim world. From India to Egypt to Indonesia, Muslims who had regarded the Ottoman Caliphate as the symbolic center of their community woke to find it gone. The response was diverse:

In India, the Khilafat Movement — which had been organized to defend the Ottoman Caliphate since 1919 under the leadership of the brothers Muhammad Ali and Shaukat Ali, with Gandhi's support — collapsed. The movement had already been weakening, but the abolition removed its reason for existence. The trauma accelerated the turn of Muslim political thought in India toward the separate homeland project that would eventually produce Pakistan.

In Egypt, King Fuad briefly considered claiming the Caliphate for himself and convened a conference in Cairo in 1926 to discuss the question. But no consensus emerged — no ruler or scholar had the combination of religious prestige, military power, and universal Muslim acceptance that a Caliph would need.

Sharif Hussein of Mecca declared himself Caliph in 1924, but he had been expelled from the Hejaz by Ibn Saud's forces even before the year was out. His claim generated no serious support.

## The Scholarly Debate: Is the Caliphate Obligatory?

The abolition generated an intense scholarly debate about the nature and obligation of the Caliphate in Islamic law.

The traditional Sunni position, expressed by scholars from al-Mawardi (writing the *Ahkam al-Sultaniyya* in the 5th century AH) onward, was that establishing an imam/caliph is fard kifaya — a collective obligation on the Muslim community. The community must have a recognized leader who implements Islamic law, protects the frontiers, establishes justice, and represents Muslim unity. Al-Mawardi, al-Juwaini, Ibn Khaldun, and the majority of classical scholars held this position.

The most controversial Islamic scholarly response to the abolition was Ali Abd al-Raziq's book *al-Islam wa Usul al-Hukm* (Islam and the Foundations of Governance), published in 1925 by an Egyptian scholar trained at al-Azhar and at Oxford. Abd al-Raziq argued that the Caliphate was not a religious obligation — that Islam had established no specific form of government, that the Prophet ﷺ had been a spiritual messenger not a political ruler, and that Muslims were free to organize their political life in whatever form suited their circumstances. This argument conveniently legitimized Egypt's secular constitutional monarchy.

Al-Azhar was not persuaded. A scholarly council unanimously condemned the book, stripped Abd al-Raziq of his al-Azhar degree, and declared his arguments contrary to Islamic consensus. The weight of classical scholarship on this question is clear: the Muslim community has a collective obligation to establish Islamic governance, though the specific form this takes can vary with circumstances.

Rashid Rida, the Syrian scholar and founder of *al-Manar* journal, wrote extensively on the Caliphate in the 1920s in his *al-Khilafa* (The Caliphate). He argued that the Caliphate must be restored but acknowledged the difficulty of doing so given the fragmented state of the Muslim world; he also argued that the Caliphate's restoration should be based on scholarship and Islamic principles rather than on a political ruler with merely nominal Islamic identity.

## Atatürk's Secularization Program

The abolition of the Caliphate was the first step in a comprehensive program to remove Islam from Turkish public life. Over the following years:

- Religious courts were abolished (1924) and replaced by the Swiss Civil Code
- The Arabic call to prayer (adhan) was replaced by a Turkish-language version (1932-1950)
- The Arabic script, used to write Ottoman Turkish, was replaced by the Roman alphabet (1928) — effectively cutting Turkish youth off from the entire corpus of Ottoman Islamic literature
- The Sufi orders (tariqa) were banned (1925) — their meeting houses closed, their leadership exiled
- The hijab was banned in government buildings and universities
- Islamic education was removed from the public school curriculum
- The Directorate of Religious Affairs (Diyanet) was created to bring Islam under strict state control

These measures were experienced by traditional Muslims in Turkey and worldwide as a war on Islam itself. The state was not merely separating religion and government — it was actively suppressing Islamic practice in public life.

## The Impact on Global Islamic Thought

The loss of the Caliphate accelerated several trends in Islamic thought:

The Muslim Brotherhood (founded in Egypt in 1928, just four years after the abolition) was a direct response to the Caliphate's loss. Hassan al-Banna's founding message explicitly referenced the trauma of 1924 and the need for Islamic revival as the path to restoring Muslim political unity.

The conviction that secular nationalism had failed Muslims — that the Turkish national state which had killed the Caliphate was not a model to be admired — became central to political Islamic thought across the 20th century.

The absence of a recognized Muslim authority created a vacuum filled by competing voices — Saudi Wahhabi scholars, Egyptian Muslim Brotherhood ideologues, Pakistani Jamaat-e-Islami thinkers, and eventually more extreme groups. Each claimed to represent authentic Islam and the path toward restored Muslim dignity.

The long-term absence of any institution capable of representing the collective voice of the global Muslim community has made coordinated Muslim responses to international crises (Palestine, Bosnia, Chechnya, Myanmar) fragmented and often ineffective.

## The Hadith on the Caliphate

The Prophet ﷺ addressed the question of Islamic governance in hadith preserved in the major collections. In the hadith collected by Imam Ahmad in his Musnad: "The Prophethood will remain among you as long as Allah wills it to remain, then Allah will raise it when He wills to raise it. Then there will be the Caliphate upon the Prophetic method, and it will remain as long as Allah wills it to remain, then Allah will raise it when He wills to raise it. Then there will be hereditary rule, and it will remain as long as Allah wills it to remain, then Allah will raise it when He wills. Then there will be oppressive rule, and it will remain as long as Allah wills, then Allah will raise it when He wills. Then there will be the Caliphate upon the Prophetic method."

Muslim scholars have interpreted this hadith as giving both a description of history and a promise of eventual restoration. The current era — which many scholars characterize as the era of "oppressive rule" (mulk jabri) — will not be permanent. The community's duty is to prepare for restoration through knowledge, practice, and unity.
