# The Story of Julaybib

Julaybib was a companion of the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) whose story — preserved in Sahih Muslim and the biographical collections — illustrates the Prophet's approach to social inequality, the dignity of the marginalized, and the definition of human worth in Islam.

## Who Was Julaybib?

Julaybib's name itself is a diminutive form — suggesting someone of low status or significance, a person who might be overlooked. His lineage was unknown. The accounts describe him as physically unattractive and of low social standing. He was not from a tribe that would have given him social protection or status in the tribal Arabian world, and he had no family to arrange his marriage — marriage in that society being a family-to-family arrangement that required proper lineage and social standing.

He appears to have been a client (mawla) of the Ansar, living among the Muslim community in Medina without the tribal connections that would normally give a man social standing.

## The Prophet's Intercession

Despite his low social position, Julaybib was a devoted companion. The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) was aware of his situation and personally interceded to arrange his marriage — going directly to an Ansari family to ask for their daughter's hand on Julaybib's behalf.

When the Prophet came to an Ansari man and asked him to give his daughter in marriage to Julaybib, the man was taken aback. He told the Prophet he would consult his wife. When the man told his wife, she was even more resistant: Julaybib was not the kind of match she had envisioned for her daughter. The daughter, however, heard the exchange and asked: "Who has sent you to ask for me?" When told it was the Messenger of Allah himself, she said: "Would you reject a request from the Messenger of Allah? Give me in marriage to him, for he will not bring loss to me."

The Prophet came again, and the girl's words were conveyed to him. He prayed for her: *"O Allah, shower goodness upon her and do not make her life difficult."*

## The Marriage and Death

Julaybib and the young woman were married. The marriage, however, was brief. Shortly afterward, Julaybib went out with the Muslim army on an expedition. In the fighting that followed, he fought until he was killed.

When the Prophet asked after the battle where Julaybib was, he was told the man had been killed. The Prophet went to find him. He found Julaybib's body amid seven of the enemy whom he had killed before falling. The Prophet looked at him and said:

*"He killed seven, and then was killed? This one is of me, and I am of him."*

He repeated this three times.

The Prophet then carried Julaybib's body on his own arms to the grave that was prepared for him.

## "He Is of Me and I Am of Him"

The phrase the Prophet used — "this one is of me, and I am of him" — is one of the most striking formulations of belonging in the seerah literature. The same phrase (or close variants) is used by the Prophet for Ali ibn Abi Talib, Salman al-Farisi, and a small number of others. It expresses the deepest form of spiritual and personal identification.

For Julaybib — a man of unknown lineage, low social status, and physical unattractiveness — to be described by the Prophet as "of me" placed him in the highest company the Islamic tradition recognizes.

## The Widow's Honor

The wife of Julaybib — who had accepted the marriage over her parents' initial reluctance, on the basis of the Prophet's recommendation — was subsequently known as one of the most sought-after women in Medina after her husband's death. The accounts say that no widow in Medina had more suitors than she, because of the honor that had been attached to her through her marriage to Julaybib and the Prophet's regard for him.

## Theological Significance

Julaybib's story has been referenced across Islamic literature for several teachings:

**The irrelevance of lineage to spiritual worth.** In a tribal society where lineage determined virtually everything — marriage eligibility, social protection, political participation — Julaybib had none of the markers of significance. His worth, in the Prophet's evaluation, was measured entirely by his faith and his courage.

**The Prophet's personal care for the marginalized.** The Prophet did not merely teach social equality; he personally intervened to ensure that a man of no tribal standing could marry and be accepted in the community. He carried the man's body on his own arms.

**The value of trusting the Prophet's judgment.** The girl's readiness to trust the Prophet's recommendation over her family's social calculation is cited as an example of trusting the Prophet's wisdom in matters of character over worldly appearance.

## Sources

The story is recorded in Sahih Muslim (Kitab Fada'il al-Sahaba), Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*.
