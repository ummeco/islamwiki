# The Death of Ibrahim ibn Muhammad

## The Son Born in Medina

Ibrahim ibn Muhammad was the Prophet's ﷺ son by Maria al-Qibtiyya — the Coptic Christian woman who had been sent to the Prophet ﷺ as a gift by the Muqawqis, the ruler of Egypt, in response to the Prophet's ﷺ letter inviting him to Islam. Maria came to Medina and accepted Islam. She bore the Prophet ﷺ a son who was named Ibrahim — a name of deep significance: the Prophet ﷺ said he named him after Ibrahim al-Khalil (Abraham the Friend of Allah), the patriarch and father of the monotheist tradition that Islam understood itself as the completion of.

Ibrahim was born in Dhul-Hijjah 8 AH — the same year as the Conquest of Mecca. He was the Prophet's ﷺ only son to survive birth after the early deaths of his three sons by Khadijah: Qasim, Abdullah (also called al-Tayyib and al-Tahir), and a third whose name is disputed in the sources, all of whom died in infancy. The birth of Ibrahim brought the Prophet ﷺ great joy. He performed the 'aqiqah — the sacrifice associated with the birth of a child — slaughtered two sheep, shaved Ibrahim's head, and gave the weight of the hair in silver as charity. He distributed the charity and gave Ibrahim his name on the seventh day, following the Sunnah he was himself establishing.

## The Care and the Joy

Ibrahim was nursed by a woman of the Ansar and lived in her household during his infancy, as was common practice in Arabia. The Prophet ﷺ visited him regularly, and the narrations preserve small details of these visits: Ibrahim would reach for his father, the Prophet ﷺ would hold him close and kiss him, the smell of the child — the narrations describe the Prophet ﷺ smelling his neck. These are among the most intimate personal details in the seerah literature, preserved because the companions who witnessed them understood that what they were seeing was the Prophet ﷺ as a father, and that this too was part of the prophetic example.

The Prophet ﷺ expressed joy about Ibrahim openly. He reportedly said that Ibrahim's birth meant the Qurayshi slur "abtar" (cut off, meaning without sons to carry the lineage) no longer applied — though the Quran had already answered this in Surah al-Kawthar. The joy of Ibrahim's birth, in the same year as the Conquest of Mecca, gave the period of 8 AH a quality of abundance that the companions noted.

## The Death

Ibrahim died in Rabi' al-Awwal 10 AH at approximately eighteen months of age. The Prophet ﷺ was present at his death, or arrived shortly before it. He held Ibrahim as he died, weeping. The companions around him were surprised to see the Prophet ﷺ weep — some had understood the hadith discouraging wailing and extreme expressions of grief to mean that all emotional display at death was discouraged. The Prophet ﷺ clarified: "The eye weeps and the heart grieves, and we say only what our Lord is pleased with. And we are indeed grieved by your departure, O Ibrahim." He distinguished between the natural sorrow of a father — tears, grief in the heart — and the forbidden practices of wailing, beating oneself, and tearing clothing. Both the grief and the limit of its expression were prophetic teaching delivered in the most personally costly moment.

## The Eclipse

On the day Ibrahim died, there was a solar eclipse. Some companions said: "The sun has eclipsed because of the death of Ibrahim." They were associating a cosmic event with the son of the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet ﷺ corrected them immediately and clearly: "The sun and moon do not eclipse for the death or life of anyone. When you see an eclipse, make du'a and pray." The correction was made on the very day of his son's death — the grief of losing Ibrahim did not prevent the Prophet ﷺ from correcting an error that, if unchallenged, would have introduced a form of superstition into the community's understanding.

The response to the eclipse is studied as an example of the Prophet's ﷺ consistent refusal to allow his own personal circumstances — even grief — to become the basis for false religious beliefs. He had just lost a son; some companions were attributing a natural astronomical event to that loss; and the Prophet ﷺ corrected them on the same day, firmly and clearly.

## The Grief in Context

Ibrahim was the last child of the Prophet ﷺ to die before him. With Ibrahim's death, the Prophet ﷺ had buried three sons and three daughters — all his children except Fatimah — and Fatimah herself would die six months after her father. The sustained personal losses of the Medinan period — children, companions, family — are a dimension of the seerah that is sometimes understated in favor of the military and political narrative. The Prophet ﷺ who wept at Ibrahim's death, who wept at Ruqayyah's grave, who kissed the forehead of Jafar ibn Abi Talib's children after Mutah — was a man who experienced loss repeatedly, who did not suppress it or consider it beneath his station, and who handled it each time with the same combination of honest grief and trust in Allah that he had modeled and taught from the first revelations.

## Ibrahim in the Hadith Literature

The hadith about the Prophet's ﷺ grief at Ibrahim's death — "The eye weeps and the heart grieves, and we say only what our Lord is pleased with" — is one of the most quoted statements on Islamic grief psychology. It establishes that emotional response to loss is natural and permitted; that the prohibition is on expressions that imply rejection of Allah's decree (wailing, cursing fate, extreme physical expressions of grief); and that the combination of sincere feeling and sincere submission to Allah's will is not a contradiction but the prophetic norm. The statement was made while the Prophet ﷺ was holding his dead infant son — the most personally costly context imaginable — which gives it a weight that no theoretical pronouncement on grief could equal.
