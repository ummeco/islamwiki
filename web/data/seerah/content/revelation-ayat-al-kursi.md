# Ayat al-Kursi — The Greatest Verse in the Quran

## The Verse the Prophet ﷺ Named the Greatest

Ayat al-Kursi — the Throne Verse — is the 255th verse of Surah al-Baqarah. It is 50 Arabic words that the Prophet ﷺ himself identified as the greatest verse in the Quran. Abu Hurayrah narrated that the Prophet ﷺ said: "The master of all speech is the Quran. The master of the Quran is Surah al-Baqarah. The master of Surah al-Baqarah is Ayat al-Kursi." Abd ibn Ka'b, a leading Quranic scholar among the companions, asked the Prophet ﷺ directly: "Which verse in the Book of Allah is the greatest?" The Prophet ﷺ replied: "Ayat al-Kursi." And he struck Abd ibn Ka'b on the chest, saying: "May knowledge gladden you, O Abu al-Mundhir."

## The Text of the Verse

In the original Arabic, Ayat al-Kursi reads:

*Allahu la ilaha illa huwa al-Hayyu al-Qayyum. La ta'khudhuhu sinatun wa la nawm. Lahu ma fi al-samawati wa ma fi al-ard. Man dha alladhi yashfa'u 'indahu illa bi-idhnihi. Ya'lamu ma bayna aydihim wa ma khalfahum wa la yuhituna bi-shay'in min 'ilmihi illa bima sha'. Wasi'a kursiyyuhu al-samawati wa al-ard wa la ya'uduhu hifdhuhuma wa huwa al-'Aliyyu al-'Adhim.*

Its meaning in English:

"Allah — there is no deity except Him, the Ever-Living, the Sustainer of existence. Neither drowsiness overtakes Him nor sleep. To Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth. Who is it that can intercede with Him except by His permission? He knows what is before them and what will be after them, and they encompass not a thing of His knowledge except for what He wills. His throne extends over the heavens and the earth, and their preservation tires Him not. And He is the Most High, the Most Great."

## The Nine Attributes

Classical scholars of tafsir have identified nine or more divine attributes established or implied in Ayat al-Kursi:

1. **Tawhid** — absolute oneness: "there is no deity except Him"
2. **al-Hayy** — the Ever-Living, whose life has no beginning and no end and is not dependent on anything else
3. **al-Qayyum** — the Self-Subsisting Sustainer of all existence, on whom all things depend for their continued being
4. **Freedom from need for rest** — "neither drowsiness overtakes Him nor sleep" — His sustaining of creation requires no respite
5. **Absolute ownership** — "to Him belongs whatever is in the heavens and whatever is on the earth" — absolute sovereignty, not shared with any partner
6. **The conditionality of intercession** — "who is it that can intercede with Him except by His permission" — no being can approach Allah on behalf of another without His explicit permission
7. **Comprehensive knowledge** — "He knows what is before them and what will be after them" — the entirety of past and future is known to Him simultaneously
8. **The limitation of created knowledge** — "they encompass not a thing of His knowledge except for what He wills" — human and angelic knowledge is derivative, bounded, and granted
9. **The throne's expanse** — "His kursi extends over the heavens and the earth" — the divine sovereignty encompasses the entirety of creation
10. **Freedom from fatigue** — "their preservation tires Him not" — maintaining the universe requires no effort, no drain on an infinite sustainer
11. **al-'Aliyy** — the Most High — elevated above all creation in essence and attribute
12. **al-'Adhim** — the Most Great — incomparable in majesty

The kursi — "throne" in the translation — is understood differently by different schools of Islamic theology. The Athari position, based on a narration attributed to Ibn Abbas, distinguishes between the kursi, which encompasses the heavens and earth, and the arsh (throne), which is even greater. Whatever the precise metaphysical referent, the verse uses the image of a throne that spans the entirety of the heavens and earth to convey the absolute, unconstrained sovereignty of Allah over all that exists.

## The Protective Power of Ayat al-Kursi

The Prophet ﷺ made specific statements about the protective power of Ayat al-Kursi in practice. In the hadith of Abu Hurayrah and the shaytan — preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari — a jinn came to steal from the zakat food and was caught three times by Abu Hurayrah. On the third occasion, the jinn asked to be released and offered to teach Abu Hurayrah something that would benefit him. He said: "When you go to your bed, recite Ayat al-Kursi — then a protector from Allah will remain with you and no shaytan will come near you until morning." The Prophet ﷺ, when Abu Hurayrah reported this, said: "He told you the truth, though he is a liar."

The Prophet ﷺ also said: "Whoever recites Ayat al-Kursi after every obligatory prayer, nothing stands between him and Paradise except death." This statement — placing the recitation of a single verse after every prayer in the causal chain leading to Paradise — has made Ayat al-Kursi perhaps the most widely recited Quranic verse in the daily practice of Muslims across all traditions and schools.

## The Place of Ayat al-Kursi in Muslim Practice

Ayat al-Kursi is recited after every obligatory prayer by observant Muslims following the prophetic instruction. It is recited at bedtime. It is recited as a protection before leaving the home. It is recited over the sick, over newborns, over those in fear. It is taught to children as among the first Quranic passages to memorize after al-Fatiha.

No verse in the Quran is recited more frequently in the daily lives of Muslims around the world than Ayat al-Kursi — which makes it, in terms of actual lived practice, the most present piece of Quranic text in human consciousness across fourteen centuries. The verse the Prophet ﷺ named the greatest has also, through the prophetic instructions about its recitation, become the most practically used.

The revelation of Ayat al-Kursi within Surah al-Baqarah — in the context of the Medinan period's comprehensive legislative and theological output — places it within the frame of the ummah's formation: this verse was given to a community being built, not merely a community being sustained. The greatest description of Allah's majesty in the Quran was revealed in the period when the Muslim community most needed to understand — not just believe but know, in the comprehensive Quranic sense of knowledge that produces action — exactly who it was they were submitting to.
