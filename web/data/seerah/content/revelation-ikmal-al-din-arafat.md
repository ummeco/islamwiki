# Revelation of the Verse of Completion of Religion at Arafat

On the ninth of Dhul Hijjah, 10 AH, as the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) stood on the plain of Arafat delivering the Farewell Sermon, a verse of the Quran was revealed that is understood by Muslims as the divine declaration of the completion of Islam as a religion:

*"This day I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favor upon you and have approved for you Islam as religion."* (Quran 5:3, al-Ma'idah)

## Context of the Revelation

The verse appears in Surah al-Ma'idah, which is one of the last major surahs to be revealed chronologically. The specific verse (5:3) is embedded within a passage dealing with dietary regulations — a legal context that might seem incongruous with its enormous theological significance. But its appearance in that context is not accidental: Islamic scholars have understood it as Allah declaring the completeness of the divine guidance across all domains of life, from the most fundamental acts of worship to the details of daily law.

## The Companions' Reaction

When Umar ibn al-Khattab heard this verse, he wept. A Jewish man who had been present reportedly approached Umar and said: "O Umar, you recite a verse that, if it had been revealed to us, we would have taken that day as an Eid (celebration)." Umar replied: "I know when and where it was revealed — it was revealed on a day that is itself an Eid (the Day of Arafat) and in a place that is itself a celebration (the plain of Arafat)."

Umar's weeping was understood as recognition of what the verse implied: if the religion was complete, the Prophet's mission was complete — and the Prophet's death was near. The completion of the revelation meant that the living channel of divine guidance would soon be closed.

## Theological Significance

**Finality of revelation.** This verse is understood as the Quranic confirmation that the revelation to the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) was complete and that no further prophetic message would follow. Combined with the Quranic designation of the Prophet as the "Seal of the Prophets" (Quran 33:40), it establishes the finality of prophethood in Islam.

**Completeness of the Sharia.** The verse asserts that the divine guidance conveyed through the Prophet is sufficient and complete for human guidance in all times and places. Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) is grounded in this principle: the sources of Islamic law — Quran, Sunnah, ijma (scholarly consensus), and qiyas (analogical reasoning) — are sufficient to derive rulings for all circumstances, because the religion itself is complete.

**Favor completed.** The phrase "completed My favor upon you" refers to the divine blessing of guidance — the gift of a complete, preserved, and comprehensible revelation. Islamic theology holds that the greatest divine gift to humanity is guidance (hidayah), and this verse declares that gift complete.

## The Day of Arafat

The day on which this verse was revealed — the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, the Day of Arafat — is in Islamic tradition one of the most sacred days of the year. The Prophet said: "There is no day on which Allah frees more people from the Fire than the Day of Arafat." Fasting on the Day of Arafat (for those not performing Hajj) is recommended, with the Prophet saying it expiates the sins of the previous year and the coming year.

The convergence of this verse's revelation with the Day of Arafat — already the climactic day of the pilgrimage — deepened its significance in Islamic consciousness. The completion of the religion was announced on the day that represents the spiritual apex of Islamic worship, at the place that represents the geography of divine mercy.

## Position in Islamic Law and Theology

Islamic scholars across the centuries have referenced Quran 5:3 in discussions of:
- The prohibition of religious innovation (bid'ah) — the religion is complete; additions are therefore unauthorized
- The sources of Islamic law — everything needed is provided; the task is derivation, not addition
- The finality of prophethood — the completion of revelation confirms that no new prophet will add to or alter the established religion

The Imam of Maliki jurisprudence, Malik ibn Anas, reportedly said regarding anything for which there is no precedent in the early Muslim community: "There is no good in it" — grounding his conservatism precisely in the completeness expressed in this verse.

## Sources

The circumstances of the verse's revelation are recorded in Sahih Bukhari and Sahih Muslim. The Quranic tafsir of Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, and al-Qurtubi provide extensive commentary on the verse's meaning and implications.
