# The Ayyubid Dynasty: Saladin's Legacy of Sunni Revival

## The End of the Fatimid Era

When Salahuddin al-Ayyubi (Saladin) formally ended the Fatimid Caliphate of Egypt in 567 AH / 1171 CE, he accomplished something that the Sunni Islamic world had hoped for since the Fatimids established themselves in Egypt in 358 AH: the restoration of the largest, wealthiest Muslim country to mainstream Sunni governance and practice.

The manner of the transition is historically instructive. Saladin did not storm the palace or execute the last Fatimid Caliph. Al-Adid ibn al-Hafiz, the final Fatimid Caliph, was gravely ill when Saladin had effectively taken control of Egypt (he had been appointed vizier by al-Adid in 564 AH). When al-Adid died naturally in 567 AH, Saladin simply did not arrange the appointment of a successor. The Friday khutbah was changed to invoke the Abbasid Caliph al-Mustadi in Baghdad. Two hundred years of Ismaili rule ended not with a battle but with the death of a caliph and a change in the words spoken from the pulpit.

The smoothness of this transition reflected Saladin's characteristic political intelligence and his genuine commitment to Islamic unity. He had no interest in public humiliation of the Fatimid legacy that might inflame sectarian passions. He wanted Sunni restoration, not a Sunni-Shia civil war. He got what he wanted.

## Saladin's Background: The Kurdish General in Fatimid Service

Salahuddin Yusuf ibn Ayyub was born approximately 532 AH / 1137 CE in Tikrit (in modern Iraq) to a Kurdish family from the Rawadiyya clan that had served the Zengid rulers of Syria and Iraq. His father Najm al-Din Ayyub and his uncle Shirkuh were both senior military commanders under Nur al-Din Zengi, the Sunni ruler of Syria and the most serious opponent of the Crusaders in the mid-twelfth century.

Shirkuh led three military expeditions to Egypt between 559-564 AH, ostensibly to help the Fatimid Caliphate against Crusader pressure but effectively to extend Zengid influence southward. Saladin accompanied his uncle on these expeditions, though he reportedly did not initially want to go to Egypt. After Shirkuh's sudden death in 564 AH, Saladin became vizier to the Fatimid Caliph al-Adid — the most powerful position in Egypt.

The paradox of his early career is striking: the man who would become the greatest champion of Sunni Islam in his era spent several years serving as the chief minister of the Ismaili Shia Fatimid Caliphate. Saladin navigated this position with care, maintaining Fatimid ceremonial forms while steadily consolidating his own power, placing his own relatives in key military and administrative positions, and building the personal loyalty networks that would allow him to act independently when the time came.

## The Restoration of Sunni Institutions

Once the Fatimid Caliphate ended, Saladin moved systematically to restore Sunni institutions throughout Egypt:

**The Friday Khutbah**: The most symbolically important change was the khutbah in the Friday prayer — now delivered in the name of the Abbasid Caliph al-Mustadi, not a Fatimid Imam. This change, replicated in every mosque in Egypt, announced unambiguously to the entire Muslim world that Egypt had returned to the Sunni fold.

**Al-Azhar Mosque**: Saladin ended the Friday prayer at al-Azhar (which had been associated with Fatimid Shia practice) and began the process of transforming it into a Sunni institution. The formal restoration of Sunni Friday prayer at al-Azhar came under the Mamluks, but Saladin's reign set the direction.

**The Madrasas**: Saladin built multiple madrasas in Cairo and throughout Egypt promoting Shafi'i and Maliki jurisprudence. He brought in Sunni scholars from Syria and Iraq, endowed these institutions with waqf properties, and established Sunni legal education as the norm throughout Egypt.

**The Legal System**: Egyptian courts reverted to Shafi'i and Maliki jurisprudence, replacing the Ismaili legal system the Fatimids had imposed. The position of chief qadi was filled by a Maliki scholar — Sadr al-Din ibn Darbas — reflecting Saladin's sensitivity to the predominantly Maliki legal culture of the Egyptian population.

**The Cairo Citadel**: Saladin began construction of the great citadel on the Muqattam hills southeast of Cairo — the fortress-palace that would serve as the seat of Egyptian government from his time until the nineteenth century. He also opened Cairo (previously a royal enclosure under the Fatimids) to the general population, integrating it with Fustat into a single urban area.

## The Wars Against the Crusaders

Saladin's political consolidation of Egypt — completed by approximately 570 AH — was followed by the unification of Syria and Egypt under his authority. By 573 AH, he controlled Egypt, Syria, Yemen, and significant parts of the Jazira (upper Mesopotamia), forming a territorial ring around the Crusader states that Nur al-Din had envisioned but never achieved.

His wars against the Crusader states built steadily toward the defining moment of his career — and arguably the defining moment of medieval Islamic history.

The immediate casus belli was provided by Raynald of Châtillon, lord of the castle of Kerak east of the Dead Sea. Raynald had twice attacked Muslim caravans and pilgrims in violation of existing truces — including an audacious 1182-1183 CE raid into the Red Sea that threatened the holy cities of Mecca and Medina. In 582 AH / 1187 CE, he attacked a large caravan from Egypt to Syria, capturing its goods and imprisoning its merchants despite a current truce. Saladin swore to kill Raynald personally and began his campaign.

## The Sunni Theological Orientation

Saladin was not merely a political champion of Sunnism; his personal theological commitments were genuine and consistent. Ibn Shaddad Baha al-Din, who served as Saladin's qadi al-askar (military judge) and personal companion in the later years of his life, wrote "al-Nawadir al-Sultaniyya wal-Mahasin al-Yusufiyya" — an eyewitness biography that provides the most reliable and detailed account of Saladin's character and practice.

Ibn Shaddad describes a man who: performed his five daily prayers on time, including when on campaign; wept when hearing the Quran recited; distributed charitable gifts so consistently and generously that he died with almost no personal wealth; treated prisoners with justice; and maintained consistent personal piety without ostentation. He was not a man who performed religion for political purposes — his practice predated his power and continued throughout it.

His theological position was clearly Ash'ari Sunni. He was harsh toward theological innovation: when his legal scholars confirmed that the Ismaili theological content previously taught in Egypt was deviant, he ended it. He was a patron of scholars in the mainstream Sunni tradition and sought the companionship of men of learning throughout his campaigns.

## Jerusalem: Liberation and Honor

The Battle of Hattin in 583 AH / 1187 CE destroyed the military power of the Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem. Saladin then moved systematically through the Crusader territories, taking city after city. Jerusalem (al-Quds, Bayt al-Maqdis) surrendered to him on 27 Rajab 583 AH / October 2, 1187 CE — the anniversary of the Isra' wal-Mi'raj (the Night Journey and Ascension of the Prophet ﷺ) — a coincidence that Muslim sources treated as divinely arranged.

The contrast between the Crusader conquest of Jerusalem in 492 AH / 1099 CE — when the Crusaders massacred the Muslim and Jewish population of the city with extraordinary brutality, leaving blood reportedly ankle-deep in the streets according to some accounts — and Saladin's conquest eighty-eight years later was stark. Saladin negotiated the terms of surrender with the Frankish defenders: those who could pay a ransom of ten dinars (men), five dinars (women), or one dinar (children) could leave with their property. Those who could not were to be freed without ransom. He personally contributed funds for the ransoms of the poor.

Saladin's brother al-Adil reportedly asked Saladin to give him one thousand of the poor captives as a gift — which Saladin did, whereupon al-Adil immediately freed them. Saladin's close companions and counselors then made similar requests to free the poor captives, until a very large number had been liberated without ransom.

Ibn Shaddad records that the Patriarch of Jerusalem, who left the city with enormous wealth in church treasuries while poor Christian citizens went into captivity for lack of ransom money, was allowed to take his wealth unmolested by Saladin — who, when advised to seize the property, replied that he had given his word when negotiating the surrender and would not violate it.

## The Personal Character of Saladin

Saladin's character, as preserved by contemporaries and later biographers, was defined by generosity to an extraordinary degree. He repeatedly gave away the contents of the treasury. Ibn Shaddad describes him dying with only a dinar and thirty-six or forty dirhams to his name — after controlling an empire whose revenues were enormous. He had given the rest away.

His court poet Imad al-Din al-Isfahani — another eyewitness biographer — records that Saladin wore simple clothes, ate simple food, and maintained none of the elaborately luxurious ceremonial that other rulers of comparable power maintained. He made himself accessible to petitioners — a practice many rulers avoid because it is both time-consuming and emotionally draining — and spent hours hearing individual complaints and requests.

He reportedly said near the end of his life: "These are my riches" — gesturing to the scholars, soldiers, and servants around him — "because I have given away everything else." And then: "When I die, shroud me in nothing but what I died in."

He died in Damascus on 27 Safar 589 AH / March 4, 1193 CE, of a fever. He was approximately fifty-five or fifty-six years old. His biographers record that there was not enough money in his treasury to pay for the funeral preparations, and that his companions had to borrow the necessary funds.

## The Ayyubid Legacy

Saladin's successors — his son al-Afdal, his brother al-Adil, and al-Adil's son al-Kamil — continued Ayyubid rule over Egypt and Syria until the Mamluk generals seized power in 648 AH / 1250 CE (the same year as the Mongol sack of Baghdad). The Ayyubid period saw the continued development of Sunni institutions in Egypt, the construction of significant architecture (including al-Kamil's famous madrasa adjacent to the future Mosque of Sultan Hasan), and the maintenance of Egypt as a center of Islamic learning.

The Ayyubids' most enduring legacy, beyond the physical liberation of Jerusalem and the restoration of Sunni governance to Egypt, was the demonstration that Muslim rulers could be genuinely pious, genuinely just, and genuinely effective — that these qualities were not mutually exclusive. Saladin remained, for centuries after his death, the model against which Muslim rulers were measured.
