# Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari: Defender of the Sunnah Through Rational Theology

## The Scholar Who Changed Sides — and the Theological World

Few conversions in the history of Islamic intellectual life were as dramatic or as consequential as that of Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn Ismail al-Ash'ari (may Allah have mercy on him). For approximately forty years — from his youth until the age of about forty — he was a devoted student and defender of the Mu'tazilite rationalist theology, sitting at the feet of its greatest living representative, al-Jubbai. Then, in approximately 300 AH, he made a public and theatrical break with everything he had been taught, mounted the pulpit of the Great Mosque of Basra, and declared himself a defender of the Sunnah.

What followed was a career of theological writing and teaching that gave Sunni Islam one of its most powerful intellectual tools: a systematic rational theology (kalam) that defended the positions of Ahl us-Sunnah against Mu'tazilite challenges using the Mu'tazilites' own philosophical methods. The school that emerged from al-Ash'ari's work — the Ash'ari school of theology — became and remains the dominant theological tradition among Shafi'i and Maliki Muslims, representing the approach to aqeedah taken by a very large proportion of the world's Sunni Muslims.

Al-Ash'ari was born in Basra in approximately 260 AH / 874 CE. He died in Basra (or, according to some accounts, in Baghdad) around 324 AH / 935 CE, at approximately sixty years of age. His death marked the end of the formative phase of what became the Ash'ari school; his students and their students would develop, systematize, and spread his theological approach across the Islamic world.

## Forty Years as a Mu'tazilite: The Background

The Mu'tazilite school of theology, founded in the eighth century and reaching its peak influence during the Mihna of the ninth century, was characterized by a commitment to rational analysis as the primary tool of theological reasoning. The Mu'tazilites held five principal doctrines: (1) the absolute unity of Allah, which they interpreted as requiring the denial that Allah's attributes were distinct from His essence (to avoid what they called "multiplicity in the divine"); (2) divine justice, which they interpreted as requiring that all of Allah's acts be comprehensible to human reason as "good"; (3) the promise and threat (al-wa'd wal-wa'id) — that Allah is bound by His promise to reward the righteous and punish the wicked; (4) the intermediate position — that a Muslim who commits major sin is neither a believer nor an unbeliever; (5) commanding good and forbidding evil.

Al-Jubbai, al-Ash'ari's teacher, was the leading Mu'tazilite theologian of his generation. Al-Ash'ari studied with him for decades and was regarded as one of his most capable students — perhaps his intended successor as the leading voice of Basran Mu'tazilism. His break from this tradition was therefore not the defection of a fringe student but the departure of someone thoroughly trained in the tradition and entirely capable of articulating its strongest arguments.

## The Three Brothers: The Argument That Broke the School

Classical biographical sources preserve an account of the intellectual turning point in al-Ash'ari's relationship with al-Jubbai — a philosophical dialogue that exposed, in al-Ash'ari's judgment, an irresolvable contradiction in Mu'tazilite theology. The account appears in al-Subki's Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyyah al-Kubra among other sources.

Al-Ash'ari posed the following problem to al-Jubbai: imagine three brothers — one who died as a righteous believer, one who died as a sinner, and one who died as a young child before reaching the age of accountability. What is the fate of each, according to Mu'tazilite theological principles?

Al-Jubbai answered: the righteous one goes to Paradise; the sinful one goes to the Fire (or faces punishment); the child goes to a middle or neutral state, since he was not accountable for acts of obedience or disobedience.

Al-Ash'ari pressed further: could the child complain to Allah that he was deprived of the opportunity to perform righteous deeds that would have earned him the highest Paradise?

Al-Jubbai replied: Allah knew that if the child had lived to adulthood, he would have become a sinner, so by taking him early, Allah did him a favor. He therefore cannot complain.

Al-Ash'ari then asked: could the sinful brother not make the same complaint — that Allah knew he would sin, and yet allowed him to live to adulthood and incur punishment that the child was spared? Surely it would have been "more just," by the Mu'tazilite principle of divine justice, to take the sinful brother early as well?

According to the biographical tradition, al-Jubbai could not answer this without contradicting one of his school's principles. Either divine justice is not comprehensible to human reason in the way the Mu'tazilites claimed, or there is an unresolvable tension in the school's position. For al-Ash'ari, this argument crystallized a deeper dissatisfaction: the Mu'tazilites had tied themselves to a conception of divine justice that was, in practice, a constraint they imposed on Allah based on human rational intuitions. The Quran and the Sunnah did not support such a constraint.

## The Public Break: Mounting the Pulpit in Basra

The famous scene of al-Ash'ari's public departure from Mu'tazilism has the quality of theater. He reportedly appeared in the Great Mosque of Basra, wrapped in a garment, and called for the people's attention. He announced: "Whoever knows me, knows me; whoever does not know me, I am Ali ibn Ismail al-Ash'ari, and I used to hold that the Quran is created, that Allah cannot be seen in the next world, and that creatures create their own actions. I repent of these positions, and I intend to refute the Mu'tazilites and expose their shame and disgrace."

He then descended from the pulpit, withdrew for approximately fifteen days, and emerged with several refutations of Mu'tazilite theology — including the two texts that survive from this period, al-Luma' fi al-Radd 'ala Ahl al-Zaygha wal-Bida' (The Gleam: A Refutation of the People of Deviation and Innovation) and al-Ibanah 'an Usul al-Diyanah (The Elucidation of the Foundations of Religion).

The al-Ibanah is particularly significant because it explicitly identifies al-Ash'ari's position as that of "Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal and what he transmitted from the salaf" — aligning his new theology with the Hanbali hadith scholars rather than with the rationalist tradition he was leaving. This alignment was not purely rhetorical: al-Ash'ari genuinely held the positions of the Athari tradition on the fundamental questions of aqeedah, including the affirmation of Allah's attributes as transmitted in the Quran and Sunnah without tawil.

## The Theological Method: Using Kalam in Defense of Sunnah

Al-Ash'ari's contribution to Sunni intellectual life was the development of a method: using the tools of rational theological argumentation (kalam) that the Mu'tazilites had pioneered, but deploying those tools in defense of Sunni positions rather than against them. This was a significant methodological move that not all Sunni scholars endorsed.

Some Hanbali scholars, in al-Ash'ari's own time and afterward, argued that the use of kalam was itself problematic — that the rational categories inherited from Greek philosophy were not appropriate tools for Islamic theology, that the proper response to theological error was to cite Quran and Sunnah and leave it at that, and that engaging with rationalist theology on its own terms legitimized an illegitimate enterprise. This was Ahmad ibn Hanbal's general position, though Ahmad was himself capable of argumentation when required.

Al-Ash'ari's response — developed by his successors — was pragmatic: the Mu'tazilites had already introduced rational theological challenges into the discourse; ordinary Muslims and educated people were encountering these arguments; leaving them unanswered on the grounds that rational theology was illegitimate would not make the challenges go away. The community needed scholars who could respond to philosophical objections with philosophical rigor, while ensuring that the conclusions of the rational argument aligned with the transmitted revelation.

His key positions: (1) Allah's attributes are real and distinct in some sense from His essence, contra the Mu'tazilites who denied any real distinction to avoid "multiplicity"; (2) human acts are "acquired" (kasb) by humans in a meaningful sense, while being created by Allah — a solution to the problem of free will and divine omnipotence that was difficult to articulate precisely but preserved both human moral responsibility and divine sovereignty; (3) the Quran as Allah's eternal Speech is uncreated in its meaning, while the sounds and letters through which it is expressed are created; (4) believers will see Allah in the next world, as transmitted in authentic hadith.

## The Ash'ari School After al-Ash'ari

Al-Ash'ari's most important major works — beyond al-Luma' and al-Ibanah — is Maqalat al-Islamiyyin (The Doctrines of the Muslims), which is one of the most comprehensive heresiographical works in Islamic history, cataloguing the theological positions of every major group and sub-group with remarkable detail. It remains an indispensable primary source for the history of Islamic theology.

His students and their students systematized and developed his approach into the school that bears his name. Al-Baqillani (338-403 AH), al-Juwayni (419-478 AH, called Imam al-Haramayn), al-Ghazali (450-505 AH), Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (544-606 AH), and many others refined the Ash'ari theological system, addressed new philosophical challenges, and spread the school across the Shafi'i and Maliki scholarly worlds.

The Ahl us-Sunnah consensus that emerged through medieval Islamic scholarship recognized both the Athari/Hanbali approach and the Ash'ari/Maturidi approach as valid within the bounds of Sunni orthodoxy. Neither is heretical; both oppose the genuine theological deviations of the Mu'tazilites, the Jahmiyyah, and others. Scholars may follow either approach, and the great majority of scholars affiliated with the Shafi'i or Maliki schools have historically been Ash'ari in their theology.

Al-Ash'ari died in Basra around 324 AH / 935 CE. His grave is there, though the exact location has not been consistently identified in later sources. He left behind a school of thought that has shaped Sunni Islamic theology for over a millennium.
