# The Retreat in Cave Hira

## The Years of Preparation

In the years immediately before the first revelation, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ developed a practice of solitary spiritual retreat (khalwa) in a cave on the mountain known as Jabal al-Nur — the Mountain of Light — approximately three kilometers from the Sacred Mosque in Mecca. The cave itself, carved into the peak of the mountain, is a small natural chamber barely large enough for a person to lie down in, facing the direction of the Ka'bah.

The retreat was called tahannuth — a practice of religious seclusion and worship that the Prophet ﷺ undertook each year for the month of Ramadan, spending days at a time in the cave in prayer, contemplation, and worship. He would bring provisions sufficient for a period of seclusion, and Khadijah would resupply him or visit him as needed. He would also feed and give water to the poor who came to him during his time there.

This practice of retreat is one of the most significant indicators that the Prophet ﷺ, even before revelation came to him, was spiritually oriented in a direction entirely distinct from the polytheism of Mecca around him. He was not worshipping the idols of the Ka'bah during his retreats in the cave. He was engaged in a form of worship directed toward the One God — the God of Ibrahim — which scholars of the seerah describe as an expression of the natural monotheism (fitrah) that remained alive in him despite the pagan environment.

## The Threshold Signs: True Dreams

The first signs that revelation was approaching came in the form of what the Prophet ﷺ himself described as "al-ru'ya al-salihah" — the righteous visions. These were dreams that were so vivid and accurate that they came true exactly as he had seen them, "like the break of dawn." (Bukhari) The period of these true dreams preceded the first revelation by approximately six months.

These dreams were not ordinary sleep-visions. They were characterized by an unprecedented clarity and by their subsequent fulfillment in waking reality. The early Muslim community understood them as divine acclimatization: Allah was gradually preparing the Prophet ﷺ for the experience of receiving revelation, beginning with a gentler form of divine communication during sleep before moving to the full encounter with the angel in the waking state.

## The Cave's Significance

Jabal al-Nur — the Mountain of Light — derives its name from this period: it is the mountain where the divine light of revelation first descended on the Prophet ﷺ. The cave (called Ghar Hira) is approximately 270 meters up the mountain, accessible today by a steep pathway. Pilgrims and visitors climb it to mark the place where the prophetic mission began.

The choice of this cave — physically removed from Mecca, requiring effort to reach, impossible to reach casually — reflects the nature of what occurred there. The Prophet ﷺ was seeking distance from the world below: from the noise of the market, the polytheism of the Ka'bah, the social pressures and obligations of Qurayshi life. In the cave's silence and solitude, stripped of all worldly distraction, he could direct his attention entirely toward the One whose existence he sensed without yet knowing how to fully worship.

## The Night of Revelation

The culmination of the retreat practice came in the final nights of Ramadan — the night that Islamic tradition identifies as Laylat al-Qadr (the Night of Decree), in the last ten nights of Ramadan, when the Prophet ﷺ was approximately forty years old and in his usual annual retreat. What happened that night — the descent of Jibril, the embrace, the first verses of Surah al-Alaq — is treated in the following event (First Revelation) as a separate and central account.

What the retreat in Cave Hira represents is the preparation: years of spiritual orientation, of removing himself from the world to be with Allah, of tahannuth that kept his heart clear and his spiritual faculties open, so that when the angel came, he was in a state of readiness even though the encounter itself was overwhelming and unexpected.

## The Cave in Islamic Memory

The Cave of Hira holds a permanent place in Islamic piety and memory as the place where the prophetic mission began. The hadith that the Prophet ﷺ used to retreat there is recorded in both Bukhari and Muslim as the opening frame for the narration of the first revelation. Aisha's account of this period — preserved in Bukhari's first hadith (Kitab Bad al-Wahy) — begins: "The first thing that was bestowed upon the Prophet ﷺ of revelation was the righteous vision in sleep..."

Later Islamic scholars, reflecting on the practice of tahannuth, have noted that the Prophet's ﷺ years of seclusion in the cave represent a template: that spiritual purification and solitary devotion have a role in preparing the heart for divine knowledge. The Sufi tradition drew extensively on this model. More broadly, the retreat period affirms that divine missions do not begin without preparation — that before the word came, the heart was ready.

## Mountains in the Prophetic Tradition

The Quran and hadith literature consistently associate mountains with moments of divine communication. Musa عليه السلام received the Torah on Mount Sinai (Jabal al-Tur). The Prophet ﷺ gave his Farewell Sermon at Mount Arafat. The Cave of Thawr (Jabal Thawr) sheltered the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr during the Hijrah. Mountains, in the Quranic worldview, are created entities that glorify Allah — "And there is not a thing except that it glorifies His praise, but you do not understand their glorification" (Surah al-Isra, 17:44) — and they are described in Surah al-Ahzab (33:72) as among the creation that was offered the trust (amana) and declined it.

Jabal al-Nur — the Mountain of Light — rises approximately 634 meters above sea level near Mecca. The name "al-Nur" (the Light) is a post-prophetic designation, given in recognition of the revelation that descended there. Before the revelation, it was simply a mountain. After it, the name honored what had happened at its peak. Today, hundreds of thousands of pilgrims and visitors make the steep forty-minute climb each year to stand at the narrow entrance to the cave and reflect on what occurred within.

## The Tahannuth Practice in Context

The practice of tahannuth — religious seclusion and devotion — that the Prophet ﷺ observed in Cave Hira was not unique to him among the Arabs of his era. There was an awareness among some in Arabia, especially among the hanifs (those who had turned away from polytheism toward monotheism without having received a prophet), that worship required solitude and directed attention toward the one God. What was unique about the Prophet's tahannuth was its regularity, its intensity, and the direction in which it led: straight to the first revelation.

Scholars have noted that the choice of Ramadan — the same month in which the Quran was eventually fully revealed to the Prophet ﷺ, the same month in which fasting would be prescribed — for this annual retreat is itself significant. The sacred month was already being observed in some fashion in the Arab tradition, and the Prophet ﷺ chose it as the time of his deepest seclusion.
