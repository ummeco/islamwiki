# Battle of al-Qadisiyyah

The Battle of al-Qadisiyyah was one of the most decisive engagements in Islamic history, fought in 15 AH (636 CE) near the city of al-Hirah in present-day Iraq. The Muslim forces, commanded by Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him), faced the Sasanian Persian army led by the renowned general Rustam Farrokhzad.

## Background

Following the death of the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and the consolidation of the Arabian Peninsula under Islamic rule during the Riddah Wars, the Rashidun Caliphate under Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) turned its attention to the neighboring empires. The Sasanian Empire, which had ruled Mesopotamia and Persia for centuries, had been weakened by prolonged wars with the Byzantine Empire and internal political instability.

Early Muslim raids into Sasanian territory had already proved successful under commanders like Khalid ibn al-Walid and al-Muthanna ibn Harithah. The Sasanian court, recognizing the growing Muslim threat, assembled a massive army under Rustam to push the Arabs back into the desert.

## The Battle

The Muslim army numbered approximately 30,000 to 36,000 fighters, while the Sasanian force was significantly larger, estimated between 60,000 and 120,000, supported by war elephants that struck fear into the hearts of those who had never faced them.

The battle lasted three to four days. On the first day, known as Yawm Armath (Day of Agitation), the Sasanian elephants caused considerable disruption to the Muslim cavalry. Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, who was ill and commanded from a nearby structure, directed his forces to target the elephants' howdahs and trunks.

The second day, Yawm Aghwath (Day of Succor), saw the arrival of reinforcements from the Syrian front, including veterans of the battles against the Byzantines. Their experience bolstered Muslim morale and turned several engagements in their favor.

The decisive third and fourth days brought fierce fighting that continued through the night, a rare occurrence in pre-modern warfare. This night battle is known as Laylat al-Harir (Night of Clangor). By dawn, a sandstorm blew into the faces of the Sasanian troops. In the confusion, Rustam was killed, and the Sasanian army broke and fled.

## Aftermath

The victory at al-Qadisiyyah opened the path to the Sasanian capital of Ctesiphon (al-Mada'in), which fell to the Muslims shortly after. The battle effectively ended Sasanian control over Mesopotamia and marked the beginning of the Islamic conquest of Persia.

The spoils included the famous Carpet of Chosroes, an enormous jeweled carpet from the royal palace, which was sent to Medina and divided among the Muslims. Umar ibn al-Khattab wept when he saw the wealth, recognizing the test that prosperity would bring to the ummah.

## Significance

Al-Qadisiyyah is remembered as a turning point that brought Islam to the ancient civilizations of Mesopotamia and Persia. Within a generation, the former Sasanian territories would become heartlands of Islamic civilization, producing some of the greatest scholars, scientists, and thinkers in Muslim history.
