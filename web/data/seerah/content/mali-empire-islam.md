# The Mali Empire and Islam in West Africa: Faith, Gold, and Learning

## The Heart of the Old World

When Ibn Battuta traveled to Mali in 753 AH (1352 CE), he found a Muslim empire of extraordinary scale and sophistication that most of the Islamic world barely knew existed. The Mali Empire at its height stretched from the Atlantic coast of modern Senegal and Gambia to the great bend of the Niger River in modern Mali and Niger — a territory larger than Western Europe. Its rulers had been Muslim for more than a century. Its cities sheltered Islamic scholars of genuine learning. Its trade routes carried gold and salt across the Sahara to North Africa, the Mediterranean, and eventually Europe.

The story of Islam in West Africa is the story of one of the great religious transformations in human history — a transformation accomplished not by conquering armies but by the patient work of merchants, scholars, and the gradual adoption of a faith that offered both spiritual truth and practical benefits to rulers and populations who encountered it.

## The Ghana Empire and Early Islam

Before the Mali Empire, the Ghana Empire (not to be confused with the modern nation of Ghana, which lies to the southeast) had dominated the West African savannah from approximately the 5th to the 13th centuries CE. Arab geographers like al-Masudi and al-Bakri wrote detailed descriptions of Ghana in the 4th and 5th centuries AH, noting that the court employed Muslim ministers and scribes but the ruling dynasty itself remained non-Muslim (following traditional African religious practices). Muslims lived in a separate quarter of the capital city with their own mosques and judges.

This pattern — Muslim merchants and administrators within a non-Muslim state — was the seedbed from which full Islamization grew. Muslim merchants brought the gold trade; Muslim scribes brought literacy in Arabic; Muslim judges brought a written legal system. The benefits of engagement with the Islamic world were visible to any perceptive ruler.

The collapse of the Ghana Empire in the 13th century CE cleared the way for the rise of Mali.

## Sundiata and the Foundation of the Mali Empire

The Mali Empire was founded by Mansa (Emperor) Sundiata Keita after his victory over the Sosso king Sumanguru Kante at the Battle of Kirina in approximately 633 AH (1235 CE). The griot tradition of West Africa preserves the epic of Sundiata — one of the great oral literature traditions of the continent. Sundiata himself was from the Mandinka people, and his personal religious practice reflects the transitional nature of West African Islam in this period: he appears to have been a Muslim by identity while also drawing on traditional religious elements. The distinction between nominal and practiced Islam, and between elite/urban Islam and the religious lives of rural populations, is a feature of West African Islamic history throughout this period.

What matters historically is that Sundiata founded a state that rapidly became more thoroughly Islamic in its ruling institutions, and that his successors were increasingly devout Muslims who drew on Islamic legal and political frameworks.

## Mansa Musa: The Emperor Who Astonished the World

Mansa Musa ibn Abu Bakr Keita (r. approximately 712-737 AH / 1312-1337 CE) is the Mali ruler whose fame reached across the entire medieval world. He was a devout Muslim who is reported to have said that he wished to appoint a deputy and make the hajj (pilgrimage) to Mecca — not a brief journey from Cairo, but a transcontinental expedition from the Atlantic coast of Africa across the Sahara to Egypt and thence to Arabia.

The pilgrimage he undertook in 724 AH (1324-1325 CE) was one of the most extraordinary events of the medieval world. His retinue of approximately 60,000 people (court officials, wives, soldiers, servants, scholars), 500 slaves each carrying a golden staff, and 80-100 camels each bearing gold dust, represented a display of wealth without parallel in the medieval period. Modern economic calculations suggest the gold he carried, in contemporary values, represented tens of billions of dollars — though such comparisons are necessarily approximate.

Al-Umari (Shihab al-Din Ahmad ibn Fadl Allah al-Umari), an Egyptian court official who gathered extensive information about Mansa Musa from people who had met him in Cairo, wrote in his *Masalik al-Absar fi Mamalik al-Amsar* (Pathways of Vision through the Realms): "This sultan Musa told me that his country was very extensive and he had very many cities. The banks of the River Niger were populated on both sides... The greater part of the country consists of vast plains and deep forests." Al-Umari also documents the extraordinary economic effect of Mansa Musa's generosity: he distributed gold so lavishly in Cairo and along his route that the price of gold in Egypt fell significantly and did not recover for over a decade. Contemporary Egyptian economists documented this inflation — the first recorded case in history of a single person's expenditure causing measurable macroeconomic disruption.

Mansa Musa performed hajj in Mecca in 724 AH, fulfilling one of the five pillars of Islam, and returned with scholars, a library of Islamic texts, and — most importantly — the architect Abu Ishaq Ibrahim al-Sahili al-Gharnati, a poet and architect from Granada in al-Andalus.

## Abu Ishaq al-Sahili and the Architecture of Learning

Abu Ishaq al-Sahili (known in West African tradition as Al-Sahili or Al-Tuwajin) transformed the physical landscape of the Mali Empire. He designed and oversaw the construction of mosques at Timbuktu (the famous Djinguereber Mosque, whose current form largely reflects his design), Gao, and other cities. He also built a palace for Mansa Musa.

Al-Sahili's mosque design was adapted to the West African context: the characteristic Saharan-Sudanese architectural style that emerged — earthen construction, wooden beams protruding from walls for scaffolding during repairs, minarets like tapering towers — became the distinctive visual tradition of West African Islam. The mosques of Timbuktu, Djenné (the Great Mosque of Djenné, though reconstructed in the 20th century, follows the ancient template), and hundreds of other cities across the Sahel reflect this tradition.

## Timbuktu: The City of Scholars

Timbuktu (Tombouctou) became, under Malian and later Songhai patronage, one of the great centers of Islamic scholarship in the world — a claim that may surprise those whose mental image of Africa in the medieval period is defined by European colonial narratives. At its intellectual peak in the 9th-10th centuries AH (15th-16th centuries CE), Timbuktu had approximately 180 Quran schools, three major universities (the Sankore, Djinguereber, and Sidi Yahia mosques served simultaneously as mosques, madrasas, and libraries), and a population of perhaps 25,000 people — of whom an extraordinary proportion, by medieval standards, were engaged in scholarly activity.

The manuscript tradition of Timbuktu is among the most remarkable discoveries of modern Islamic scholarship. Estimates suggest that between 100,000 and 700,000 manuscripts survive in private libraries and institutions in Timbuktu and the surrounding region — works of theology, jurisprudence, Quran commentary, history, astronomy, mathematics, music, and poetry. Many of these works are now being digitized and studied as part of international preservation efforts. The existence of this tradition — an African Islamic scholarly heritage of first-rate importance — was virtually unknown to the wider world until the late 20th century.

The scholars of Timbuktu produced original works of Islamic scholarship. Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti (963-1036 AH / 1556-1627 CE) was one of the most prolific — a Maliki jurist who wrote over 40 works and whose famous response to the question of enslaving Black Africans (*Mi'raj al-Su'ud*) is a landmark in Islamic jurisprudence on slavery. He was forcibly taken to Marrakesh by the Saadian dynasty after their conquest of the Songhai Empire (1000 AH / 1591 CE) and continued his scholarly production there.

## Ibn Battuta in Mali: An Eyewitness Assessment

Ibn Battuta's account of his visit to Mali in 753-754 AH (1352-1353 CE) is the most important eyewitness description of the empire at its height. His account is characteristically both admiring and critical — as a trained Maliki jurist, he assessed what he saw against Islamic standards and noted both praiseworthy and deficient practices.

He praised: the security of travel throughout the empire ("There is complete security in their country. Neither traveler nor inhabitant in it has anything to fear from robbers or men of violence"); the observation of Friday prayers and Ramadan; the memorization of the Quran by many children; the presence of Islamic scholars at court.

He criticized: certain practices that had survived from pre-Islamic tradition, including the baring of women's heads and shoulders in public, certain court ceremonial practices that he found inappropriate for a Muslim court, and specific treatment of visiting scholars that he considered insufficiently respectful.

This combination of genuine Islamic practice with surviving pre-Islamic custom was characteristic of Islamic societies in their early centuries after conversion — a pattern observed in the early Arab conquests, in Persia, in Anatolia, and in every region where Islam spread into an existing culture. Islamic jurisprudence has specific frameworks (*'urf*, or custom) for navigating this transition.

## The Songhai Successor and Askia Muhammad's Hajj

The Mali Empire gradually declined after Mansa Musa's death, undermined by internal succession disputes and the rising power of the Songhai kingdom to the east. The Songhai Empire under Sunni Ali (r. 868-898 AH / 1464-1493 CE) expanded rapidly but his relationship with the Islamic scholars of Timbuktu was troubled — he was seen as insufficiently pious.

His successor, Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr (known as Askia Muhammad or Askia the Great, r. 898-941 AH / 1493-1528 CE), was a devout Muslim who sought legitimacy through Islamic learning and the hajj. He performed hajj in approximately 902 AH (1497 CE) with a retinue comparable in some ways to Mansa Musa's, though less financially spectacular. He received the symbolic title of Caliph of Sudan (representative of Islamic authority in Sub-Saharan Africa) from the Abbasid Caliph in Cairo — a recognition that reinforced his Islamic legitimacy.

Under Askia Muhammad, the Songhai Empire became even more thoroughly Islamic than Mali had been: Islamic law was applied in the cities, scholars were honored and consulted, and Timbuktu reached its intellectual peak.

## The Faith of West Africa: A Permanent Legacy

The Islamization of West Africa, initiated by the Ghana Empire's merchant communities, developed by the Mali Empire, and consolidated by the Songhai Empire, created a Muslim population across the Sahel and savannah regions of West Africa that persists to the present day. Modern Mali, Senegal, Gambia, Guinea, Burkina Faso, Niger, and northern Nigeria are predominantly Muslim — a direct legacy of this medieval history.

The method of this Islamization — through trade, scholarship, royal conversion, and patient teaching — demonstrates that the strongest form of Islamic da'wah is the life well lived in accordance with the Quran and Sunnah. When merchants and scholars demonstrate through their character that Islam is true, rulers and populations listen.
