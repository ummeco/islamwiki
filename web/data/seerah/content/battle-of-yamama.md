# Battle of Yamama

The Battle of Yamama (11 AH / 633 CE) was the decisive military confrontation of the Riddah Wars — the campaigns launched by the first Caliph Abu Bakr as-Siddiq to suppress the apostasy movements that swept Arabia after the Prophet's death. It was the largest and bloodiest of these battles, fought against the forces of Musaylimah ibn Habib al-Kadhdhab ("the Liar"), who had claimed prophethood during the Prophet's own lifetime and built a mass following in the Yamama region (in present-day central Arabia).

## Musaylimah and the False Prophecy

Musaylimah ibn Habib al-Hanafi had sent a delegation to the Prophet claiming shared prophethood, asserting that revelation had come to him in the Yamama as it had come to Muhammad in the Hijaz. He reportedly wrote to the Prophet: "From Musaylimah the Messenger of Allah, to Muhammad the Messenger of Allah. Now, I am your partner in the affair." The Prophet replied dismissively: "The earth belongs to Allah who bequeaths it to whoever He wills among His servants."

After the Prophet's death, Musaylimah consolidated his following among the Banu Hanifah tribe — a large and powerful confederation of Yamama. His movement had religious, tribal, and political dimensions. He reportedly produced "revelations" parodying the Quran and attracted followers with promises of reduced religious obligations. His army numbered, according to classical sources, anywhere from 40,000 to over 100,000 men, though modern historians treat these figures with caution.

## Khalid ibn al-Walid's Campaign

Abu Bakr appointed Khalid ibn al-Walid, the greatest Muslim general of the era, to lead the campaign. Khalid gathered a force from the Muhajirun (Meccan emigrants), the Ansar (Medinan Muslims), and allied tribes. His force was significantly outnumbered by Musaylimah's army.

The battle took place in the plains of Yamama. The fighting was fierce and at several points the Muslim army was pushed back. The turning point came when the Muslims regrouped under Khalid's leadership. Musaylimah's forces were pressed into an enclosed garden — known ever after as Hadiqat al-Mawt, the Garden of Death — where they made their last stand.

Abu Dujana and Wahshi ibn Harb (the man who had killed Hamzah at Uhud) both claim in the sources to have delivered mortal wounds to Musaylimah. The exact manner of his death is debated across sources, but he was killed in the battle. With his death, the Banu Hanifah's resistance collapsed.

## The Loss of Qurra'

The most significant long-term consequence of the Battle of Yamama for Islamic history was not military but textual. Among the Muslim dead at Yamama were a very large number of companions who had memorized the Quran — the Qurra' (reciters/memorizers). Classical sources state that 70 or more Huffaz (those who had memorized the entire Quran) were killed at Yamama, though the exact number is debated.

This catastrophic loss of memorizers alarmed Umar ibn al-Khattab. He went to Abu Bakr and urged: "The Quranic reciters were killed on the day of Yamama, and I am afraid that more of them may be killed in other battles, and much of the Quran may be lost." He proposed a formal compilation of the Quran into a single written document (mushaf).

Abu Bakr was initially reluctant — "How can I do something that the Messenger of Allah himself did not do?" — but was persuaded. He appointed Zayd ibn Thabit, the Prophet's chief scribe, to lead the collection effort. This compilation became the foundational document of the Islamic textual tradition, later standardized under Caliph Uthman ibn Affan.

## Aftermath

The defeat of Musaylimah effectively ended the most dangerous of the Riddah movements. The Banu Hanifah accepted Islam, and the Yamama region was reintegrated into the Muslim polity. The death tolls on both sides were heavy: classical sources speak of 1,200 Muslims killed, though figures from early sources require critical reading.

The garden where Musaylimah made his last stand — Hadiqat al-Mawt — became a symbol in Islamic historical memory: the place where a false prophet was finally defeated after attracting a massive following, and where some of the most devoted Companions of the Prophet gave their lives.

## Sources

The Battle of Yamama is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab Fada'il al-Quran), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Ridda*.
