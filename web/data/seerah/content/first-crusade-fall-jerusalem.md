# First Crusade and Fall of Jerusalem

The First Crusade (declared 489 AH / 1095 CE, culminating in 492 AH / 1099 CE) was a military expedition organized by the Latin Church that resulted in the capture of Jerusalem and the massacre of much of its Muslim and Jewish population. The fall of the city shocked the Islamic world and set in motion the conflicts of the Crusader period that would last nearly two centuries.

## The Call to Crusade

Pope Urban II proclaimed the Crusade at the Council of Clermont in 489 AH (November 1095 CE), calling on Christians of Western Europe to take up arms and march to the Holy Land. His primary stated objective was to assist the Byzantine Empire against the Seljuk Turks and to recover Jerusalem and the Christian holy sites. He promised spiritual rewards, including remission of sins, for those who participated.

The response was enormous. Over the following months, hundreds of thousands of people from across Western Europe took the cross, from knights and nobles to common pilgrims. The movement represented a convergence of religious devotion, political ambition, and social disruption on a massive scale.

## The March to Jerusalem

The Crusader armies, divided into several contingents under different lords, departed in 491 AH (1096 CE) and made their way across Byzantine territory into Anatolia. After a disastrous "People's Crusade" that preceded the main military forces, the organized Crusader armies defeated the Seljuks at the Battle of Dorylaeum (491 AH / 1097 CE) and captured Nicaea.

The campaign continued south through Anatolia. At Antioch, the Crusaders besieged the city for nearly a year before taking it in Jumada al-Awwal 491 AH (June 1098 CE) — only to be besieged themselves by a relieving Muslim army. They broke out of the siege and defeated the Muslim forces, which strengthened their sense of divine mission.

## The Siege of Jerusalem

The Crusader army arrived before Jerusalem in Rabi al-Awwal 492 AH (June 1099 CE). The city was defended by a Fatimid garrison under Iftikhar al-Dawla, who had expelled the Jewish and Christian populations of the city in anticipation of the siege and stocked provisions.

The Crusaders were estimated at 12,000 to 15,000 fighters by this point, significantly weakened by the long march and earlier sieges. Jerusalem's walls were formidable, and the initial assault on 13 June 1099 CE failed. The Crusaders needed timber to build siege equipment, and a Genoese fleet that arrived at Jaffa provided the necessary materials.

Two massive siege towers and multiple catapults were constructed. On 14 July 1099 CE, the northern siege tower, commanded by Godfrey of Bouillon, reached the wall near Herod's Gate, and soldiers crossed onto the battlements. Jerusalem fell the following day, 15 July 1099 CE (22 Sha'ban 492 AH).

## The Massacre

The fall of Jerusalem was followed by one of the most notorious massacres in medieval history. The Crusaders killed the majority of the Muslim and Jewish inhabitants of the city. Contemporary Crusader chronicles described the bloodshed with apparent pride, and accounts of the massacre shocked both the Islamic world and even some observers in Christian Europe.

The Jewish community of Jerusalem, who had fought alongside the Muslim defenders, took refuge in the main synagogue. It was burned with them inside. Survivors fled to the streets and were killed.

Iftikhar al-Dawla, the Fatimid governor, was allowed to depart with his personal guard in exchange for surrendering the Tower of David, a concession made to prevent a last stand in the citadel.

Contemporary Muslim chroniclers described the events with grief and outrage. Ibn al-Athir, writing in the early seventh century AH, recounted that 70,000 Muslims were killed in the Aqsa Mosque alone, a figure most historians consider an exaggeration but which reflects the magnitude of the trauma.

## The Islamic Response

The Muslim world's initial response to the fall of Jerusalem was fragmented and ineffective. The Fatimid Caliphate in Cairo, which controlled Jerusalem, was weak and focused on internal disputes. The Seljuk Sultanate was similarly fragmented between competing princes. The Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad had no military power.

Preachers and scholars lamented the catastrophe. Abu Said al-Harawi traveled to Baghdad and gave an impassioned speech before the Abbasid court, weeping and demanding action to retake the holy city. But for nearly a century, the Crusader states established in the Levant were able to maintain their position against a divided Islamic world.

It was not until 583 AH (1187 CE) that Salah ad-Din al-Ayyubi (Saladin) defeated the Crusaders at the Battle of Hattin and recaptured Jerusalem, treating its population with mercy that stood in stark contrast to the Crusader conquest of 1099.

## Legacy

The fall of Jerusalem in 492 AH became one of the defining traumas of Islamic historical memory. It demonstrated the consequences of Muslim political fragmentation and was repeatedly invoked by later scholars and leaders as a call to unity. The Crusader period shaped Islamic conceptions of defensive jihad, governance, and solidarity.

The establishment of the Crusader states also disrupted the traditional networks of Islamic scholarship, trade, and pilgrimage in the Levant. The final expulsion of the Crusaders from the region came only in 690 AH (1291 CE) with the fall of Acre to the Mamluks.
