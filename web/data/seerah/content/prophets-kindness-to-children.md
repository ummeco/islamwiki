# The Prophet's Kindness to Children

The Prophet Muhammad's (peace and blessings be upon him) relationship with children is one of the most warmly documented aspects of his character in the hadith literature. His affection for children, his playfulness with them, his physical demonstrations of love, and his theological articulation of children's rights established a model that directly challenged the cultural norms of pre-Islamic Arabia, where infanticide, the devaluing of daughters, and harsh treatment of children were common.

## Physical Demonstrations of Affection

The Prophet publicly kissed children — an act that carried theological and cultural weight. Aqra' ibn Habis al-Tamimi, a Bedouin chief, saw the Prophet kiss his grandsons Hasan and Husayn and said: "I have ten children and I have never kissed any of them." The Prophet replied: "Whoever does not show mercy will not be shown mercy."

This exchange established that public affection for children was not a weakness or an undignified act but a form of mercy (rahmah) that was intrinsically connected to divine mercy. The refusal to show affection was not strength but a failure of the rahma that is central to Islamic character.

He would also carry children on his shoulders, allow them to ride on his back while he was in sujud (prostration), and prolong his prostration to avoid disturbing a grandchild who had climbed on him.

Anas ibn Malik, who served the Prophet for ten years from the age of approximately ten, said: "I never saw anyone more merciful to children than the Messenger of Allah."

## The Prophet and His Grandchildren

The Prophet's relationship with his grandsons Hasan and Husayn ibn Ali was a centerpiece of his domestic life in Medina. He would interrupt his own sermons to pick them up when they stumbled. He prayed with them on his shoulders. He said: "Hasan and Husayn are the masters of the youth of Paradise." He would say of them: "O Allah, I love them, so love them."

His daughter Fatimah's sons received an exceptional degree of demonstrative affection from a man who had grown up in a culture where male stoicism was the norm.

## Children in the Mosque

The Prophet integrated children into the mosque community. He would shorten his prayers when he heard a child crying in the congregation, out of consideration for the child's mother. He did not segregate children from the main community worship.

He prayed with Abdullah ibn Abbas (who later became one of the greatest scholars of the Quran) from the time Abbas was a young child. He would position the child beside him in prayer and teach by example.

## The Theological Status of Children

The Prophet articulated the theological status of children who died before reaching the age of religious accountability (bulugh). When asked about children of the polytheists, he said: "Allah knows best what they would have done" — and in many narrations indicated their salvation. Muslim children who died young he described as in Paradise, waiting for their parents.

His personal loss — the death of his son Ibrahim at approximately 17 months, and the earlier deaths of his children Ruqayyah, Umm Kulthum, and Zaynab during his lifetime — gave his statements about children and death a personal depth. His weeping at Ibrahim's death while saying "The eye weeps and the heart grieves, but we say only what pleases our Lord" is one of the most humanizing moments in the seerah.

## The Status of Daughters

The Prophet's affection was explicitly and deliberately extended to daughters in a culture where daughters were sometimes buried alive. He said: "Whoever has a daughter and does not bury her alive and does not scorn her and does not prefer his son over her — Allah will enter him into Paradise." He said that raising daughters well was a shield from Hellfire.

His own treatment of his daughters — particularly Fatimah — was observed and documented. He rose when Fatimah entered a room. He called her "a part of me." His public honor for his daughters was a deliberate counter-cultural act.

## Legacy

The Prophet's model of child-rearing — combining warmth and mercy with moral formation, play with prayer, and public affection with theological seriousness — became the normative Islamic model of parenting. Islamic educational theory, from the classical period onward, drew on these hadith to argue for developmental sensitivity, the importance of play, the necessity of parental warmth, and the spiritual significance of the parent-child relationship.

## Sources

The accounts of the Prophet's kindness to children are scattered throughout Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Adab), Sahih Muslim, the Sunan collections, and the biographical literature. Primary narrators include Anas ibn Malik, Aisha bint Abi Bakr, Abu Hurayrah, and Umm Khalid.
