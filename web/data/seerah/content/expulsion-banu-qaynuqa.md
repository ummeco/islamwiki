# The Expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa

## The First Test of the Medina Covenant

Banu Qaynuqa was one of three Jewish tribes resident in Medina before the Hijra. They were primarily goldsmiths and market traders, operating in a fortified district in the city. When the Prophet ﷺ arrived and established the Constitution of Medina — the Sahifah that created a formal covenant between the Muslim Muhajirin, the Muslim Ansar, and the Jewish tribes — Banu Qaynuqa were signatories. The covenant obligated all parties to mutual defense against external attack, to refrain from supporting the enemies of any signatory, and to bring disputes to the Prophet ﷺ for arbitration.

The expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa in Shawwal 2 AH — shortly after the Battle of Badr — was the first test of that covenant's terms and the first instance in which the Prophet ﷺ was required to act as a governing authority adjudicating a breakdown in the Medinan social contract.

## The Precipitating Incident

The catalyzing incident involved a Muslim woman who came to the Banu Qaynuqa marketplace — the commercial quarter where the tribe conducted its goldsmith and trading business. A Jewish man of the tribe pinned the hem of her garment to the ground in a way that exposed her when she rose, causing public humiliation. A Muslim man who witnessed this killed the Jewish man in response, and was in turn killed by others of the tribe. What began as harassment escalated to murder, with the chain of violence reflecting the underlying tension between the Muslim community now consolidating power at Medina and a tribe that was formally allied with them but chafing at the new political reality.

Some narrations indicate that Banu Qaynuqa, emboldened after Badr — or perhaps in calculated defiance — openly stated that the Muslims should not be deceived by their victory over an untrained Qurayshi force. They said: "The men of Quraysh were not experienced warriors. If you fight us, you will find that we are real men." This public challenge to the Muslim community represented a direct breach of the covenant's terms: they were effectively aligning their interests with the enemies of the Muslim community and threatening open hostility.

## The Siege

The Prophet ﷺ gathered the tribe and reminded them of the covenant and the consequences of breaking it. He called them to Islam — the invitation that preceded any action against a community. They refused. He then besieged them in their fortified quarter. Banu Qaynuqa fortified themselves inside their stronghold. The siege lasted fifteen days, at the end of which they surrendered unconditionally.

The decision about what to do with them then fell to the Prophet ﷺ. He had determined to execute the adult males — the standard consequence under the laws of warfare recognized in that time and place for a group that had broken a treaty and taken hostile action. Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul — the leader of the hypocrites in Medina, who had longstanding relationships with Banu Qaynuqa — intervened. He grabbed the Prophet's ﷺ armor and pleaded on their behalf, pressing the request insistently until the Prophet ﷺ relented and agreed to exile rather than execution. The Prophet ﷺ told Abdullah ibn Ubayy: "They are yours" — meaning he was releasing them into Abdullah's guarantee rather than proceeding with execution.

## The Exile

Banu Qaynuqa were expelled from Medina, taking what they could carry but leaving behind their armaments and property. They settled initially in the area of Wadi al-Qura to the north of Medina, and eventually moved further, with some accounts placing them in the region of Syria. The tribe dissolved as a distinct community over the following generations.

The Quran addresses this episode in verses that later scholars associated with Banu Qaynuqa — particularly Surah al-Anfal (8:58): "If you fear betrayal from a people, throw [their treaty] back to them in an equal manner. Indeed, Allah does not like those who betray." The verse establishes the principle that preemptive action against a group that has signaled its intent to break a covenant is legally and morally permitted — that waiting for the betrayal to fully materialize before responding is not required.

## The Legal and Political Significance

The expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa established several principles that would govern the Prophet's ﷺ administration of Medina for the remaining nine years of the Medinan period:

First, the covenant was real and its terms were enforceable. The Prophet ﷺ did not treat the Constitution of Medina as aspirational or symbolic — it carried obligations that would be acted on if breached.

Second, the mercy shown in accepting exile rather than execution — because of Abdullah ibn Ubayy's intercession — established a precedent of proportionate response and willingness to accept a lesser consequence when an advocate intervened. The Prophet ﷺ was not seeking the maximum penalty; he accepted a measured response when offered.

Third, the tribal Jewish communities of Medina learned the practical consequence of breaking the covenant. Banu al-Nadir and Banu Qurayza — who would face the Prophet ﷺ in later years — had the example of Banu Qaynuqa before them. The treatment of Banu Qaynuqa was considerably more lenient than what Banu Qurayza would face after a far more serious betrayal during the Battle of the Trench.

## Context in the Seerah

The timing — immediately after Badr — is significant. Badr had transformed the Muslim community's standing in Arabia. A small, poorly equipped Muslim force had defeated a numerically and materially superior Qurayshi army. The victory carried theological weight (the Quran called it Yawm al-Furqan, the Day of Distinction) and political weight: it signaled that the Muslim community in Medina was a serious power.

Banu Qaynuqa's open challenge — "if you fight us, you will find that we are real men" — was a test of whether Badr's outcome would actually change Medina's internal power dynamics. The Prophet's ﷺ swift and decisive response to the treaty breach confirmed that it had. The Medinan period's defining characteristic was the building of a governing community, and governing requires enforcement of law. The expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa was the first major instance of that enforcement in the new city.
