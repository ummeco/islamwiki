# The Boycott of Banu Hashim

## The Most Severe Trial in Mecca

The persecution of individual Muslims in Mecca — the torture of slaves, the social pressure on the unprotected, the physical intimidation of converts without tribal backing — had not broken the movement. Two developments in rapid succession had made the Qurayshi position more difficult: the conversion of Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib, who represented a physical deterrent to direct assault, and the conversion of Umar ibn al-Khattab, whose social standing meant the opposition to Islam had lost one of its most effective enforcers. The Quraysh needed a different approach.

The approach they chose was collective punishment at the clan level. If individual Muslims could not be broken, perhaps the entire Banu Hashim clan — the Prophet's ﷺ clan, which had continued to protect him out of tribal obligation even if most of its members had not accepted Islam — could be forced to withdraw that protection by being made to suffer for it. In approximately the seventh year of prophethood (617 CE), the leading clans of the Quraysh gathered and formalized a boycott in written form. The document was hung inside the Kaaba, giving the agreement a sacred weight.

## The Terms of the Boycott

The boycott document committed the Quraysh and its allied clans to a comprehensive set of prohibitions against Banu Hashim and Banu Muttalib (who had sided with Banu Hashim):

- No trade with any member of either clan
- No marriage between any clan member and any member of Banu Hashim or Banu Muttalib
- No social interaction of any kind — no speaking, no visiting, no commerce
- No food to be sold to or obtained by any member of the boycotted clans

The intention was total economic and social strangulation. Without trade — the lifeblood of Meccan society — and without the ability to purchase food, the clans would face genuine deprivation and potentially starvation. The Quraysh calculated that the pain of the boycott would eventually force Abu Talib, the Prophet's uncle and protector, to withdraw his protection and hand the Prophet ﷺ over.

## Three Years in the Valley

The entire Banu Hashim and Banu Muttalib community — Muslim and non-Muslim alike, because the boycott targeted the clan, not just its Muslim members — withdrew to a valley on the outskirts of Mecca known as Shi'b Abi Talib (the gorge of Abu Talib). They lived there for approximately three years under conditions of genuine privation.

The narrations describe the sound of children crying from hunger reaching the surrounding areas of Mecca. Food occasionally reached the community through secret channels — sympathetic Meccans who, while not Muslim, were troubled by the sight of the children of Banu Hashim going hungry. Hakim ibn Hizam, a nephew of Khadijah and a man who would later accept Islam, is recorded as one of those who secretly sent food to the boycotted community, carrying provisions at night through hidden routes to avoid detection.

The Prophet's ﷺ wife Khadijah spent much of her remaining wealth during these three years providing for the community. Her generosity during this period — spending in the way of Allah at the cost of the wealth she had accumulated over a lifetime of trade — is one of the most direct expressions of her faith and her understanding of what the message required. The boycott years also accelerated her physical deterioration; she died in the year the boycott ended.

## How the Boycott Ended

After approximately three years, a group of Qurayshi leaders began to feel that the boycott had gone too far. The sight of children suffering, the moral discomfort of watching the persecution extend to non-Muslim members of Banu Hashim who had no direct stake in the theological dispute, and a growing sense that the boycott was not achieving its stated aim — all of these combined to produce dissent within the Quraysh leadership.

Among those who organized the end of the boycott was Hisham ibn Amr, who is credited with approaching five men of standing within the Quraysh — including Zuhayr ibn Abi Umayyah (whose mother was an aunt of the Prophet ﷺ) and Mut'im ibn Adi — and persuading them to stand up publicly against the boycott document. They went to the Kaaba, where the document was hung, and Zuhayr declared publicly that he would not abide by a covenant that cut off the ties of kinship and caused children to go hungry.

There was an additional element to the end of the boycott that the seerah literature records as miraculous: the Prophet ﷺ informed Abu Talib that Allah had sent insects to consume the boycott document, leaving only the words containing Allah's name intact. Abu Talib went to the Quraysh leadership and relayed this. When the document was retrieved and examined, it was found exactly as described — eaten away, with only the name of Allah remaining. The Quraysh had no choice but to accept that their covenant had been nullified from an unexpected direction.

## What the Boycott Cost

The three years in Shi'b Abi Talib extracted a price from the Prophet ﷺ and the community that went beyond physical deprivation. The boycott year coincided with the period that immediately preceded what Islamic history calls the Year of Sorrow (Aam al-Huzn) — the year in which both Abu Talib and Khadijah died in quick succession. The physical toll of the boycott years contributed directly to the deterioration of both. Abu Talib died within weeks of the boycott's end. Khadijah died shortly after. The two people who had protected and supported the Prophet ﷺ more than any others were taken in the same year.

The Prophet ﷺ described this period as among the most difficult of his life. He had endured three years of confinement and deprivation with his community, watching children go hungry, watching his wife spend down her wealth, watching his uncle refuse to break despite the pressure being applied to him. When it ended, the immediate reward was not relief but grief: the two deaths that followed were, in the Prophet's ﷺ own words, harder to bear than anything that had come before them.

## What the Boycott Reveals

The Quraysh's use of the boycott reveals something important about the nature of the early opposition to Islam. It was not purely theological — it was social, economic, and political. The Quraysh did not boycott the Prophet ﷺ because they had carefully examined his message and found it false. They boycotted his entire clan because his message threatened the existing order: the order of idol worship at the Kaaba, the income it generated, the social hierarchy it maintained, and the authority it conferred on the Quraysh leadership over the surrounding tribes.

The Prophet ﷺ and his companions were not simply being persecuted for their beliefs — they were being persecuted for being successful in sharing those beliefs and for refusing to compromise them. Every convert was evidence that the Qurayshi social order was not inevitable, and the Quraysh understood this. The boycott was the most extreme form of the same logic that had driven every previous act of persecution: eliminate the community before it eliminates the old order.

It failed. Three years of collective punishment produced neither the withdrawal of Abu Talib's protection nor the Prophet's ﷺ capitulation. It produced instead a community that had been tested at the level of physical survival and had not broken — and two deaths that, instead of weakening the Prophet ﷺ, drove him to expand his mission beyond Mecca and begin the search for a new base from which Islam could grow.
