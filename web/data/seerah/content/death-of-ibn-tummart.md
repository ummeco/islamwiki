# Death of Ibn Tumart and Rise of the Almohads

Muhammad ibn Tumart (470-524 AH / 1080-1130 CE) was the founder of the Almohad movement, one of the most significant Berber Muslim reform movements in the history of North Africa and al-Andalus. His uncompromising theology of divine unity and his fierce opposition to what he saw as anthropomorphic conceptions of God, combined with his political organization of the Masmuda Berbers of the Atlas Mountains, laid the foundation for the Almohad Caliphate that would rule Morocco and al-Andalus for over a century.

## Life and Education

Ibn Tumart was born in the village of Igiliz in the High Atlas Mountains of Morocco to a Masmuda Berber family. He traveled east for education, studying in Cordoba and then in the great centers of learning in the East — Cairo, Alexandria, and Baghdad. He reportedly encountered the teaching of al-Ghazali in Baghdad and was profoundly influenced by it, particularly the emphasis on purifying theology from anthropomorphism and restoring genuine Islamic practice.

He also studied directly under the disciples of al-Ghazali and absorbed his approach of combining rigorous Ashari theology with Sufi spirituality and opposition to scholarly complacency.

## Return and Reform

Returning to the Maghreb around 510 AH (1117 CE), Ibn Tumart immediately began a campaign of moral reform. He publicly destroyed wine jars, attacked what he considered immoral behavior, and confronted the Almoravid rulers of Morocco with their perceived theological errors. The Almoravids followed the Maliki school and were sympathetic to the approach of scholarly literalism in theology. Ibn Tumart accused them of anthropomorphism (tashbih) — conceiving of God in human-like terms — and of allowing women to ride unveiled in public.

His confrontational preaching made him persona non grata in Marrakesh, and he was expelled. He retreated to the Atlas Mountains, his homeland, where he established his base at Tinmel.

## The Mahdi Claim

Around 515 AH (1121 CE), Ibn Tumart made the extraordinary claim to be the Mahdi — the rightly guided one promised in prophetic tradition who would establish justice in the earth. This claim was central to his political program: it gave his followers absolute certainty that their cause was divinely ordained and that resistance to it was resistance to Allah's will.

He organized his followers with remarkable discipline, establishing a council, creating an internal hierarchy, and instituting rigorous military training. He called his movement al-Muwahhidun (those who proclaim divine unity) — hence the name "Almohads" in European languages.

## Theology

Ibn Tumart's theology was strictly anti-anthropomorphic. He insisted that all Quranic verses attributing human-like qualities to God (hands, face, etc.) must be interpreted allegorically, as the Ashari school taught. He went further than most Ashari scholars in his insistence on this point, condemning as disbelievers those who took such descriptions literally.

He composed works in the Berber language (Tamazight) to make Islamic theology accessible to his followers, pioneering the use of vernacular Berber for religious instruction.

## Death and Legacy

Ibn Tumart died in Tinmel in 524 AH (1130 CE), possibly without knowing that the Almohad movement he had founded would grow into one of the greatest powers of the medieval Mediterranean world.

His successor Abd al-Mumin ibn Ali took command of the movement and gradually conquered Morocco, defeating the Almoravids and capturing Marrakesh in 541 AH (1147 CE). The Almohad Empire subsequently expanded into al-Andalus, inheriting the cultural and intellectual achievements of Andalusian civilization while imposing a more rigorous theological framework.

Under the Almohads, the Jewish and Christian communities of North Africa and al-Andalus faced unprecedented persecution, including forced conversion — a dark chapter that differed significantly from the relative tolerance of earlier Islamic dynasties. However, the Almohad period also produced extraordinary cultural achievements: Ibn Rushd (Averroes), Ibn Tufayl, and Ibn Zuhr all lived and worked under Almohad patronage.
