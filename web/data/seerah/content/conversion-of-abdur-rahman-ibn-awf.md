# Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf: The Merchant of Paradise

## Origins and Character

Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf (may Allah be pleased with him) was born approximately ten years before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, making him roughly thirty years old when Islam first reached him. He came from the Banu Zuhra clan — the same clan as Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him) and as the Prophet's maternal lineage through his mother Aminah bint Wahb. This connection to the Prophet ﷺ through clan kinship was one of the bonds that placed the early Zuhrite converts in a natural proximity to the message.

His name in the age of ignorance (jahiliyyah) was Abd Amr or Abd al-Ka'bah — both carrying the associations of pre-Islamic religious life. It was the Prophet ﷺ who renamed him Abdur-Rahman, "the slave of the Most Merciful," when he accepted Islam. This renaming was itself a mark of honor and a statement of the transformation that faith had accomplished: the man who had once been named after the Ka'bah's idols was now named after the attribute of Allah that embraces all of creation.

Abdur-Rahman was a merchant of the first order. His commercial instincts were extraordinary — a combination of analytical precision, relationship-building, and an almost intuitive sense of where value was to be found and exchanged. Mecca, as the commercial center of the Arabian Peninsula, produced many wealthy traders, but Abdur-Rahman was among the most successful. He knew the trade routes, he knew the products, and he knew the men. His wealth at the time of his conversion was considerable.

## Conversion Through Abu Bakr

Abdur-Rahman (may Allah be pleased with him) was brought to Islam by Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) in the earliest days of the prophetic mission — approximately thirteen years before the Hijrah. He was among the group of early converts that Abu Bakr personally invited: Uthman ibn Affan, al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, Talhah ibn Ubaydullah, and Abdur-Rahman himself. These five men, brought to Islam through the direct invitation of a single companion, went on to shape the early Muslim community in ways that cannot be overstated.

The fact that Abu Bakr could bring these men to Islam through personal invitation speaks to his credibility and their readiness. These were not men who accepted Islam out of social pressure or confusion. They were men of standing, men with wealth and position, men who had much to lose and understood what they were accepting. Their conversion was deliberate, and its significance was felt immediately throughout Mecca.

Abdur-Rahman suffered the persecution common to early Meccan Muslims. He was not among the enslaved companions who bore the worst physical torture — that fell disproportionately on those without tribal protection. But the social and commercial pressure on early converts was real: boycotts, social ostracism, and the constant threat of violence shaped the lives of all those who declared their Islam in those years.

## The Emigration to Abyssinia and then Medina

Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf (may Allah be pleased with him) was among the companions who emigrated to Abyssinia in the first Hijrah of Islamic history, seeking refuge from the persecution of the Quraysh under the just rule of the Negus (al-Najashi). He returned to Mecca and later made the Hijrah to Medina with the main body of Muslims when the Prophet ﷺ gave permission.

It is in Medina that the most celebrated story of Abdur-Rahman's character unfolds. When the Muhajirun (emigrants from Mecca) arrived in Medina, the Prophet ﷺ instituted the institution of muakhah — brotherhood — pairing each Muhajir companion with an Ansari companion from Medina. This was a practical arrangement: the emigrants had left their wealth, their businesses, and their social networks behind. The Ansar (the Helpers of Medina) were to share with them.

Abdur-Rahman was paired with Sa'd ibn al-Rabi al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him) — a wealthy man of the Khazraj tribe. Ibn Sa'd records that Sa'd offered Abdur-Rahman a remarkable welcome: "I am among the wealthiest of the Ansar. Take half my wealth. I also have two wives — whichever of them pleases you, I will divorce her and you may marry her after her waiting period is complete." This was the spirit of the Ansar: open-handed, generous to a degree that the Quran praised with the word ithar (preferring others over oneself).

Abdur-Rahman's response was equally remarkable, but in a different way. He said: "May Allah bless you in your wealth and your family. Just show me where the marketplace is."

This response captures something essential about Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf. He was not a man who needed to be given things. He was a man who needed to be given opportunity. Show him the marketplace, and he would do the rest.

## The Marketplace and the Recovery of Wealth

The marketplace of the Banu Qaynuqa was shown to Abdur-Rahman, and he went to work. Within the first day, he was trading — buying and selling, using whatever small capital he had managed to bring from Mecca or had received from Sa'd ibn al-Rabi as a starting point. Within days he was profitable. Within weeks he was established. Within months his business was flourishing.

Al-Bukhari records that the Prophet ﷺ soon noticed evidence of Abdur-Rahman's improving circumstances — he detected traces of yellow color (perhaps from saffron used in a wedding preparation) and asked about it. Abdur-Rahman told him he had married a woman of the Ansar. The Prophet ﷺ said: "May Allah bless you; hold a wedding feast even if it is only with a sheep." This brief exchange, preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari, captures both the Prophet's care for his companions' personal lives and the speed with which Abdur-Rahman had established himself in Medina.

The recovery of Abdur-Rahman's fortune was not miraculous in the supernatural sense. It was a demonstration of skill, discipline, and character. He had a capacity for trade that went beyond the merely fortunate. Al-Dhahabi records in Siyar A'lam al-Nubala that Abdur-Rahman eventually became one of the wealthiest men in the entire Islamic world — his trading caravans are reported to have included hundreds of camels, and his business interests extended across the Arabian Peninsula into Syria and beyond.

## Generosity Without Limit

The wealth that Abdur-Rahman accumulated was not held for his own pleasure. He spent it in the path of Allah with a consistency and a scale that became legendary among the companions. The records in the sirah and hadith literature are striking in their specificity.

He donated five hundred horses equipped for battle in preparation for one expedition. He donated one thousand five hundred camels for another. He donated large sums of gold — the number five hundred uqiyya of gold (approximately thirteen kilograms) appears in several narrations. He freed many enslaved people throughout his lifetime, believing that the freedom of a human being was a noble expenditure.

One incident recorded by al-Tirmidhi and others captures the spirit of his generosity. A large caravan of his arrived in Medina — seven hundred camels laden with goods. The arrival of such a caravan caused a commotion in the city; the sound of the approaching laden animals was heard from a distance. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) asked about it, and when told that it was Abdur-Rahman's caravan, she recalled a statement she had heard from the Prophet ﷺ: "Abdur-Rahman will enter Paradise crawling." Abdur-Rahman, hearing this, said: "I fear that I will be among the last of you to enter Paradise." He immediately gave the entire caravan — all seven hundred camels with their loads — in the path of Allah.

The meaning of "entering Paradise crawling" is understood by scholars as referring to the extended accounting that awaits the wealthy on the Day of Judgment. The Quran and hadith literature repeatedly caution that wealth is a test (fitnah) and that those who accumulate it will face a more rigorous reckoning. Abdur-Rahman understood this. His response was not despair but action — he continued to give and give, attempting to balance the scales that his enormous fortune created.

## Among the Ten Promised Paradise

Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf (may Allah be pleased with him) was among the Ashra Mubashshara — the ten companions to whom the Prophet ﷺ explicitly promised Paradise by name. The hadith, recorded in collections of al-Tirmidhi and Abu Dawud and traced through multiple chains, names him alongside Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, al-Zubayr, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, Talhah, Said ibn Zayd, and Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah.

This promise did not make Abdur-Rahman complacent. The accounts of his life suggest the opposite: a man profoundly aware of the weight of the blessing he had been given, striving constantly to be worthy of it.

## His Role in the Succession to Umar

When Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) was fatally wounded in 23 AH, he convened a council of six companions to determine his successor: Ali ibn Abi Talib, Uthman ibn Affan, al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, Talhah ibn Ubaydullah, and Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf. Umar's instructions were clear: they were to choose one of their number as the next Caliph within three days.

Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf withdrew himself from consideration. He voluntarily removed his own name from the pool of candidates and took on the role of mediator and decision-maker — consulting widely among the Muslims of Medina, canvassing opinions among the Ansar and Muhajirun, meeting with leaders and ordinary people alike, and finally presenting the choice between Ali and Uthman. His method was methodical, exhaustive, and transparently conducted. He eventually gave his bay'ah to Uthman, and the council followed.

This act of self-withdrawal from the caliphate — at a moment when he had every credential to seek it — is itself a testament to Abdur-Rahman's character. He chose to serve the community rather than to lead it, in a moment when leadership was on offer.

## His Final Years and Death

Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf (may Allah be pleased with him) died in 32 AH, during the caliphate of Uthman. He was approximately seventy-two or seventy-five years old. He distributed much of his remaining wealth in bequests before his death — leaving large sums to the widows of Badr (the wives of companions who had fought at Badr) and others. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) said of him after his death: "May Allah give him the widest space in Paradise."

He was buried in al-Baqi' in Medina. Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) led his funeral prayer. Among those who carried his bier to the grave was Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him), who said of him: "Go, Abu Muhammad — you have met the world as it came to you, and you left it while it was still turning toward you."
