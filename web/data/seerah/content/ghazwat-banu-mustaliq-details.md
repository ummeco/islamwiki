# Detailed Account of the Banu Mustaliq Expedition

The expedition against Banu Mustaliq (also called Ghazwat al-Muraisi) in Sha'ban 5 AH (December 626 CE) was a pre-emptive military strike by the Prophet against a tribe that was reportedly preparing to attack the Muslim community. The expedition itself was militarily successful, but it was followed by two of the most significant internal crises of the Medinan period: the incident of the slander against Aisha (hadith al-ifk) and the revelation of the Quran's verses about it, and the exposure of the munafiqun (hypocrites) through the inflammatory speech of their leader Abdullah ibn Ubayy.

## The Campaign

The Prophet received intelligence that Banu Mustaliq — a clan of the Khuza'ah tribe — was assembling forces to attack Medina under the leadership of al-Harith ibn Abi Dirar. The Prophet organized a rapid strike, marching to meet them before they could fully assemble.

The Muslim force encountered Banu Mustaliq at the well of al-Muraisi. The battle was brief: the tribal forces broke quickly and were routed. Captives were taken, including Juwayriyah bint al-Harith — the daughter of the Banu Mustaliq chief — who became one of the Prophet's wives. Her marriage to the Prophet led directly to the freeing of all Banu Mustaliq captives held by the Muslims (as the companions were reluctant to keep the Prophet's wife's kinsmen enslaved), which in turn led the entire tribe to accept Islam.

## Abdullah ibn Ubayy's Speech

During or after the campaign, a water dispute broke out between a Muhajirun and an Ansar man. The Muhajirun man called to his fellow Muhajirun, and the Ansar man called to his fellow Ansar. The incident was quickly resolved, but it came to the attention of Abdullah ibn Ubayy, the leader of the munafiqun.

Abdullah used the incident as an occasion for one of his most overtly hostile speeches against the Prophet and the Muhajirun. He reportedly said (his companions transmitted the speech): "When we return to Medina, the mightier will expel the weaker from it" — meaning that the Medinan Arabs (Ansar) would eventually expel the Meccan emigrants (Muhajirun) from Medina.

His son, Abdullah ibn Abdullah ibn Ubayy, a sincere Muslim, heard of this. He went to the Prophet and asked permission to kill his own father if the Prophet wished, noting that he could bring his father's head without anyone else having to take responsibility. The Prophet refused, saying they should treat Abdullah ibn Ubayy gently for as long as he was among them.

Umar ibn al-Khattab also urged the Prophet to have Abdullah ibn Ubayy killed. The Prophet again refused, saying he did not want people to say that Muhammad killed his own companions.

This moment exposed the hypocrisy of Abdullah ibn Ubayy more clearly than any previous incident. The Quranic Surah al-Munafiqun (Chapter 63) is associated with this episode and directly addresses Abdullah ibn Ubayy's words.

## The Incident of the Slander (Hadith al-Ifk)

The most significant event arising from this expedition was the incident of the slander against Aisha. On the return journey from Banu Mustaliq, the army made a halt and then moved on. Aisha, who had been traveling in a litter on a camel, had left the litter to search for a necklace she had lost. When she returned, the army had already moved on, the litter-bearers not knowing she was not inside.

She remained where she was, waiting. Safwan ibn al-Muattal al-Sulami came upon her — he was traveling behind the army — and brought her on his camel to the Muslim camp.

Slanderers — particularly Mistah ibn Uthathah and Hassaan ibn Thabit, along with Hamnah bint Jahsh — began spreading rumors about Aisha's character based on this incident. The Prophet was distressed. Aisha herself was ill and did not know about the slander until later. The Prophet consulted his companions; some advised him to divorce Aisha, others (including Ali ibn Abi Talib) counseled caution.

After approximately a month, the Quran revealed verses (Quran 24:11-20) definitively declaring Aisha's innocence and condemning the slanderers. Those who had spread the slander received the hadd punishment for false accusation of chastity (qadhf).

## Significance

**The munafiqun exposed.** The expedition to Banu Mustaliq and its aftermath produced the most public exposure of Abdullah ibn Ubayy's hostility to the Prophet and to Islam. Surah al-Munafiqun was revealed describing the munafiqun's character and warning the Muslim community about them.

**Aisha's vindication.** The Quranic vindication of Aisha was among the most significant events in her life — and established the principle that Muslim women's honor is to be protected by the Quran itself. The verses from Surah al-Nur on this matter became foundational in Islamic law on the treatment of accusations of zina (fornication) and the requirements of evidence.

**Juwayriyah's marriage and its effects.** The diplomatic consequences of the Banu Mustaliq marriage — the freeing of an entire tribe's captives — illustrate the Prophet's use of marriage as a political and social instrument of peace and integration.

## Sources

The expedition is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi and the Aisha hadith al-ifk account), Sahih Muslim (the extended account of the hadith al-ifk), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, and al-Tabari's *Tarikh*.
