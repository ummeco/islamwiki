# Zayd ibn Harithah: The Prophet's Beloved, From Slave to Free Son

## A Child Stolen from His Family

The story of Zayd ibn Harithah begins in tragedy. He was the son of Harithah ibn Sharahil al-Kalbi, of the tribe of Banu Kalb in Arabia. According to the classical sources, including Ibn Hisham and Ibn Sa'd, Zayd was a boy of approximately eight years old when he accompanied his mother, Su'da bint Tha'laba, on a visit to her family. While staying among the Banu Ma'n, the encampment was raided by horsemen of the Banu Qayn tribe. The raiders took captives and plunder, and among those seized in the raid was the young Zayd ibn Harithah.

He was taken to the market of Ukaz — the great annual fair held in the region of Ta'if, which was one of the most important commercial gatherings in pre-Islamic Arabia, a venue for the trade of goods, poetry competitions, and the sale of captives. At Ukaz, Zayd was purchased by Hakim ibn Hizam ibn Khuwaylid — the nephew of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with her). Hakim brought him to Mecca as part of his household, and when Khadijah saw the boy and was pleased with him, Hakim gave him to his aunt as a gift.

Khadijah, after her marriage to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ (approximately 28 years before the Hijra), offered Zayd to her husband as a gift. The Prophet ﷺ accepted Zayd gratefully and immediately freed him. In Ibn Hisham's account, the Prophet ﷺ freed Zayd on the very day Khadijah gave him over — a gesture that illustrates both his instinctive generosity and the quality of his character even before prophethood required it of him formally.

## Zayd's Father Searches for His Son

Back in the lands of the Banu Kalb, Zayd's father Harithah was consumed by grief over the loss of his son. For years, he searched for Zayd through the networks of Arab tribes, inquiring at markets and gathering places whether anyone had heard of a Kalbi boy taken from the raid at Banu Ma'n. His longing for his son was so intense that, according to Ibn Hisham, he composed poetry lamenting his loss — an Arabic literary tradition of grief that preserved the memory of the missing child in verse:

The Arabic poetic lament over Zayd is one of the most moving passages in the early Seerah literature, with Harithah declaring that he wept until he could weep no more, that he did not know whether his son was alive or dead, that he prayed to the winds that blew across Arabia to carry his prayers to wherever his boy might be.

The search eventually bore fruit. Some men of the Banu Kalb who came to Mecca for pilgrimage or trade saw Zayd there and recognized him. When they returned home, they reported to Harithah: "We have seen your son Zayd in Mecca, in the care of Muhammad ibn Abdullah of the Banu Hashim."

## The Father Comes to Ransom His Son

Harithah and his brother Ka'b ibn Sharahil immediately set out for Mecca. When they arrived, they went to the Prophet ﷺ and stated their case: they were Zayd's father and uncle; they had come to ransom him; they would pay whatever price was asked. The Prophet ﷺ listened to their plea and then made a response that confounded their expectations.

He said: "There is something better than what you have proposed. Call for Zayd and give him the choice. If he chooses you, he is yours without any ransom. If he chooses me, by Allah, I am not a man to give up someone who has chosen me over others."

Harithah and Ka'b agreed to this arrangement — it was supremely fair, and they had every reason to expect that a boy of fifteen or sixteen would choose his own father over his master. They had no doubt about the outcome.

Zayd was called. He came and stood before his father and uncle — whom he recognized. The Prophet ﷺ asked him: "Do you know these men?" Zayd said: "Yes. This is my father, and this is my uncle." The Prophet ﷺ then said: "You know me and you have experienced life with me. Choose between them and me."

Zayd's response has echoed through Islamic history: "I would not choose anyone over you. You are to me as a father and an uncle."

His father and uncle were stunned. Harithah — speaking with the emotion of a father who could not comprehend why his own son was refusing to come home — said: "Woe to you, Zayd! Would you choose slavery over your own freedom, your family, and your kinsmen?" Zayd replied: "I have seen in this man something that will not allow me to choose anyone over him."

## The Ka'bah Proclamation

What the Prophet ﷺ did next is one of the most extraordinary gestures in the entire Seerah. He took Zayd by the hand and walked to the Ka'bah. In the gathering of the Quraysh — in the most public possible space in Mecca, before witnesses from across the tribes — he made a formal proclamation:

"Bear witness that Zayd is my son. He inherits from me and I inherit from him."

From that day forward, Zayd ibn Harithah was no longer called by his father's name. He was known throughout Arabia as Zayd ibn Muhammad. He was, in the social and legal terms of the time, the Prophet's adopted son — with full rights of inheritance and all the honors of that filial relationship. His father Harithah, moved by what he had witnessed, accepted the arrangement with good grace. He could see that his son had found something in this man that exceeded what family alone could offer. He returned home satisfied, with the knowledge that his son was alive, honored, and free.

## The Revelation of al-Ahzab

This status — "Zayd ibn Muhammad" — remained the way Zayd was known for years. But in the fifth year after the Hijra, a revelation descended that would change the understanding of adoption in Islam fundamentally. Allah revealed in Surah al-Ahzab (33:4-5):

*"Allah has not made for a man two hearts in his interior. And He has not made your wives whom you declare unlawful your mothers. And He has not made your adopted sons your sons. That is merely your saying by your mouths, but Allah says the truth, and He guides to the right way. Call them by the names of their fathers; it is more just in the sight of Allah."*

The verse was direct and clear: adopted children should be called by the names of their biological fathers, not their adoptive fathers. This was a significant reform of Arabian social practice. In pre-Islamic Arabia, adoption could involve a complete legal fiction — an adopted son was treated in every respect, including for purposes of inheritance and marriage prohibitions, as a biological son. The Quran was correcting this, not to eliminate the practice of caring for orphans and children in need, but to maintain the clarity of biological lineage.

From the time of this revelation, Zayd was called once again by his patronymic — Zayd ibn Harithah. But in a concession to the depth of the Prophet's ﷺ relationship with him, Zayd was also honored with a unique title that appears in the hadith literature: "Hibb Rasul Allah" — the Beloved of the Messenger of Allah.

## Zayd in the Islamic Era: Commander and Companion

After the Hijra to Medina, Zayd ibn Harithah (may Allah be pleased with him) became one of the Prophet's ﷺ most trusted Companions and military commanders. He was appointed to lead no fewer than eleven military expeditions — more than almost any other Companion in the Medinan period. The Prophet ﷺ trusted him with command at a level that spoke to a relationship built over decades of intimacy.

The Prophet ﷺ paired him in brotherhood with Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib (may Allah be pleased with him) — the Prophet's own uncle — when the brotherhood (mu'akha) was established between the Muhajirun (Emigrants from Mecca) and the Ansar (Helpers of Medina). This pairing of Zayd with one of the most senior and beloved Companions was a signal of his standing.

Zayd's son, Usamah ibn Zayd (may Allah be pleased with him), was similarly beloved by the Prophet ﷺ, who called him "Hibb Rasul Allah ibn Hibb Rasul Allah" — "The Beloved of the Messenger of Allah, son of the Beloved of the Messenger of Allah." Usamah was appointed to lead a military expedition at the very end of the Prophet's life — an appointment that caused some murmuring in Medina because of Usamah's youth. The Prophet ﷺ, in one of his last public addresses before his illness became fatal, defended the appointment and praised Usamah's fitness for command, honoring the son as he had honored the father throughout his life.

## The Battle of Mu'tah and Martyrdom

Zayd ibn Harithah's earthly story ended in the eighth year after the Hijra, at the Battle of Mu'tah in the region of al-Balqa (in modern Jordan). A Byzantine-backed force of enormous size — reported in some accounts as 100,000 men or more, with Arab allies — confronted the Muslim expeditionary force of approximately 3,000. The Prophet ﷺ, before the army departed from Medina, appointed Zayd as the commander.

He said — as recorded by al-Bukhari and others — that if Zayd was killed, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) would take command; if Ja'far was killed, Abdullah ibn Rawaha (may Allah be pleased with him) would take command; and if he fell, the Muslims should choose from among themselves.

At Mu'tah, Zayd ibn Harithah fought with the standard in his hand and was killed — a death that the Prophet ﷺ foresaw while still in Medina, narrating the progress of the battle in real time to his Companions in what is counted among the prophecies that were later confirmed by the survivors. When the news was formally confirmed, the Prophet ﷺ wept. He went to Zayd's house and embraced Zayd's children, and the daughter of Zayd later described how she saw the Prophet ﷺ crying as he held her and the tears fell on his face.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Weep for your brother." For Zayd, even in the time after the al-Ahzab verse had formally ended the legal fiction of adoptive sonship, remained in the Prophet's heart as his son in every sense that love could create.

## The Islamic Understanding of Adoption

Zayd's story is the principal Quranic reference point for the Islamic law of adoption (al-tabanni). The reform introduced in Surah al-Ahzab (33:4-5) — that adopted children must be called by their biological fathers' names — was a protection of several Islamic values simultaneously.

First, it protected lineage (nasab), which Islam counts among the five necessities (al-daruriyyat al-khams) that Islamic law is designed to protect alongside religion, life, intellect, and property. Correct lineage determines inheritance rights, marriage prohibitions, and tribal/family identity — all of which are distorted by the complete legal fiction of calling an adopted child by the adoptive father's name.

Second, it clarified the prohibited degrees of marriage. In pre-Islamic Arabia, an adopted son's wife was treated as a daughter-in-law and therefore permanently prohibited to the father even after divorce. The Quran specifically addressed this in the continuation of the al-Ahzab passage (33:37), clarifying that the former wife of an adopted son is not permanently prohibited — a ruling that had direct bearing on the Prophet's ﷺ own life circumstances.

Third, it reinforced the value of honesty and transparency in family relationships — that love and care for a child do not require a legal fiction, and that a child can be fully embraced, nurtured, and honored without erasing his actual origins.

What the reform did not do — and Islamic scholars unanimously affirm this — was prohibit the practice of caring for, raising, and loving children who were not one's biological offspring. The Arabic concept of kafala (guardianship) allows exactly this: a child without parents or family support can be given a loving home, education, sustenance, and every form of care, while retaining his own name and family lineage. The Quran's correction was surgical — eliminating the legal fiction of pretended biological relationship while preserving all the moral and emotional substance of caring for another's child.

Zayd ibn Harithah himself is the living proof that the relationship between a guardian and a child can be so deep that a boy chooses his guardian over his own father. No legal reform changed what Zayd had found in the Prophet ﷺ. It only ensured that the world knew who Zayd was — Harithah's son, not only Muhammad's beloved.
