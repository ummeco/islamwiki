# Death of Ibn Asakir

Abu al-Qasim Ali ibn al-Hasan ibn Hibat Allah ibn Asakir al-Dimashqi (499-571 AH / 1105-1176 CE) was the greatest historian of Damascus and one of the foremost hadith scholars of the sixth century AH. His monumental work *Tarikh Madinat Dimashq* (History of the City of Damascus) — the largest biographical dictionary of a single city ever compiled in the Islamic tradition — stands as an indispensable monument of Islamic historical scholarship.

## Life

Ibn Asakir was born in Damascus in 499 AH (1105 CE) to a distinguished scholarly family. He received his early education in Damascus and then embarked on extensive travels to collect hadith, spending years studying under scholars in Iraq, Khurasan, Isfahan, and the Hijaz. He reportedly sat under over a thousand teachers, more than eighty of them women.

He returned to Damascus and spent the rest of his life there, teaching, writing, and serving the scholarly community. He lived during the period of Nur ad-Din Zengi's rule over Damascus, and the two had a relationship of mutual respect: Nur ad-Din founded the Dar al-Hadith al-Nuriyya, the first institution in the Islamic world specifically dedicated to the study of hadith, and appointed Ibn Asakir as its director. The institution provided Ibn Asakir with resources, students, and the support to complete his monumental projects.

He died in Damascus in 571 AH (1176 CE), nine years after Nur ad-Din and less than a decade before Salah ad-Din's recapture of Jerusalem.

## Tarikh Madinat Dimashq

The *History of Damascus* is an astonishing work. In its complete form, it comprises approximately 80 volumes in modern editions. It includes biographical entries on every significant person associated with Damascus — those born there, those who lived there, those who visited, and those who transmitted hadith through the city — from the Prophet's time through Ibn Asakir's own era.

The scope is encyclopedic: caliphs, governors, judges, scholars, mystics, poets, merchants, and women scholars are all included. Each entry quotes hadiths, poems, anecdotes, and other primary sources relevant to the person, making the work simultaneously a biographical dictionary, a hadith collection, a history of Damascus, and an anthology of Arabic literature.

Ibn Asakir's meticulous citation of his sources, his critical assessment of chains of transmission, and his systematic organization make the work invaluable for historians of the Islamic world.

Among the most significant entries is the biography of Jesus (Isa, peace be upon him) — one of the most extensive collections of Islamic traditions about Jesus ever assembled, reflecting Damascus's deep connection to the Christian tradition and Islamic reverence for the prophet.

## Tabyin Kadhib al-Muftari

Ibn Asakir also wrote *Tabyin Kadhib al-Muftari* (Exposing the Lies of the Slanderer), a defense of the Ash'ari school of theology against its critics, and a biographical account of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari himself. This work is a primary source for understanding al-Ash'ari's life and for the history of Ash'ari theology.

## Legacy

The *Tarikh Madinat Dimashq* is a fundamental resource for any serious research on the medieval Islamic world. Historians of Syria, of hadith transmission, of Islamic biography, and of the Crusader period all depend on it. Later compilers of biographical dictionaries consistently drew on Ibn Asakir's material.

His death in 571 AH (1176 CE) left the scholarly world of Damascus at one of its great moments: the city was at the heart of the Muslim counteroffensive against the Crusaders, and Ibn Asakir had helped make it also a center of Islamic scholarship.
