# Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab's Reform Movement

## The Najd in the Eighteenth Century

The central Arabian Peninsula — the region known as Najd — was in the eighteenth century one of the more isolated and politically fragmented regions of the Islamic world. It fell outside the effective administrative reach of the Ottoman Empire, which controlled the Hejaz (the western coastal strip with Mecca and Medina) but did not govern the interior. Najd was divided among numerous tribal confederations and small principalities that fought, allied, and traded in a largely autonomous political world. Bedouin tribal culture, with its customs of raiding, tribal solidarity, and pre-Islamic practices that had survived Islamization in attenuated forms, coexisted with an Islamic scholarly tradition that was genuine but thin compared to the major urban centers of the Islamic world.

It was into this world that Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab (1115-1206 AH / 1703-1792 CE) was born in the village of al-Uyayna in Najd. His father was a Hanbali judge (qadi), and he received a thorough traditional Islamic education from childhood, memorizing the Quran at an early age and studying the standard Hanbali curriculum.

## Education and the Formation of His Views

Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's intellectual formation took him beyond Najd. He traveled to Medina for advanced study, sitting under scholars including Sheikh Abdullah ibn Ibrahim al-Najdi, a Hanbali scholar who introduced him to the writings of Ibn Taymiyyah (661-728 AH / 1263-1328 CE) and his student Ibn al-Qayyim al-Jawziyya (691-751 AH / 1292-1350 CE). These two scholars, who had lived in the Mamluk period and had their own battles with the religious establishment of their day, would become the primary intellectual influences on Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's thought.

He also traveled to Basra, in Ottoman Iraq, where he studied and reportedly came into contact with Sufi practices and popular religious customs that he found deeply problematic. It is traditionally said that his encounters in Basra with practices surrounding the veneration of local saints strengthened his resolve to preach against them. He returned to Najd via Ahsa and Huraimila (where his father now lived), and began preaching his reform program.

## The Core Theological Message

The theological program of Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab can be summarized around his understanding of tawhid (the oneness of God) and his critique of what he regarded as shirk (associating partners with God) in contemporary Muslim practice.

He distinguished several aspects of tawhid: tawhid al-rububiyya (acknowledging Allah as the sole Creator and Sustainer), tawhid al-uluhiyya (directing all worship exclusively to Allah), and tawhid al-asma' wal-sifat (affirming Allah's names and attributes without distortion). While he argued that most Muslims acknowledged the first category, many were failing in the second — by directing acts of worship, supplication, vows, and seeking of intercession to beings other than Allah.

His principal text, Kitab al-Tawhid (The Book of Monotheism), organized his argument systematically: it consisted of Quranic verses and hadith, followed by short explanatory notes, addressing practice after practice that he regarded as shirk or the means leading to shirk. Visiting graves with the intention of seeking intercession from the deceased, making vows at shrines, asking the dead for assistance, wearing amulets, seeking knowledge of the unseen through fortune-tellers — all of these he classified as violations of pure tawhid.

His intellectual debt to Ibn Taymiyyah was substantial. Ibn Taymiyyah had argued five centuries earlier against many of the same practices, had been imprisoned multiple times for his positions, and had developed the theological framework that Ibn Abd al-Wahhab deployed. Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's contribution was to systematize this critique, apply it to the specific conditions of eighteenth-century Arabia, and — crucially — find a political partner willing to implement it by force.

## The Alliance with Muhammad ibn Saud: 1157 AH / 1744 CE

The turning point in the movement's history came with the alliance formed between Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab and Muhammad ibn Saud, the ruler of the small oasis town of Dir'iyyah in Najd. The two men concluded a compact in approximately 1157 AH / 1744 CE: Ibn Saud would provide political protection and military support for the reform program; Ibn Abd al-Wahhab would provide religious legitimacy for Ibn Saud's political authority and military campaigns. The agreement reportedly included Ibn Saud's promise not to dislodge Ibn Abd al-Wahhab from his authority in religious matters, and Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's acknowledgment of Ibn Saud's political primacy.

This alliance between a religious scholar and a political ruler was not unprecedented in Islamic history — such compacts had formed the basis of Muslim polities from the Prophet's ﷺ own community in Medina through subsequent caliphates and sultanates. What was distinctive was the specific theological content of the reform program and the methods used to implement it.

## The First Saudi State: Expansion and Controversy

From the base at Dir'iyyah, the Saudi-Wahhabi forces began a decades-long campaign of conquest across Najd. Towns and tribes that accepted the reform program and pledged allegiance to Ibn Saud were incorporated peacefully; those that resisted were fought. By the end of the eighteenth century, most of Najd was under Saudi control.

The most controversial actions of the first Saudi state involved the destruction of structures associated with the veneration of the dead and what the reformers regarded as shirk. The tombs and shrines that dotted the Arabian landscape — including structures over the graves of companions of the Prophet ﷺ and other revered figures — were demolished as the Saudi forces advanced. This destruction was justified theologically on the basis of hadiths prohibiting the construction of structures over graves, but it produced intense opposition from Muslims across the world who regarded these sites as legitimate objects of pious visitation.

The Saudi forces raided into the Ottoman-controlled territories of Iraq in 1216 AH / 1802 CE, attacking Karbala — the site of the tomb of Husayn ibn Ali رضي الله عنه, the Prophet's ﷺ grandson, which was one of the most sacred sites in Shia Islam — and destroying much of the shrine complex. The following year they entered Medina and demolished several structures in the Baqi' cemetery. In 1218 AH / 1803 CE, Mecca itself was occupied. These actions drew condemnation from Ottoman authorities and from Muslim scholars across the Islamic world, who issued fatwas against the movement.

## Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's Scholarly Legacy

Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab died in 1206 AH / 1792 CE, having lived to see his movement gain control of most of the Arabian Peninsula. His scholarly output beyond Kitab al-Tawhid included Kashf al-Shubuhat (Clearing Away the Doubts), an apologetic work defending his theological positions against objections, commentaries on hadith collections, biographical studies of the prophets, and legal works within the Hanbali school.

His position within the Islamic scholarly tradition is genuinely contested, and the debate has intensified in the modern period. His defenders argue that he was a rigorous Hanbali scholar working squarely within the tradition of Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn al-Qayyim — two figures who are broadly respected in Sunni Islam — and that his critique of innovations in religious practice was not substantively different from critiques made by mainstream Sunni scholars throughout Islamic history. His critics — and there were many in his own time — argued that he too broadly categorized common Muslim practices as shirk, that he declared Muslims who disagreed with him to be apostates, and that his alliance with the Saudi military state gave his reform program a coercive character incompatible with the scholarly tradition of persuasion and debate.

Contemporary scholars continue to debate the degree to which Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's original positions were later distorted, hardened, or politicized by subsequent Saudi state patronage, and the degree to which his original writings justify the practices carried out in his name.

## The Ottoman-Egyptian Counterattack and the Fall of Dir'iyyah

The expansion of the first Saudi state into the Hejaz — including control of Mecca and Medina — represented a direct challenge to Ottoman authority, which derived enormous legitimacy from its role as "Servant of the Two Holy Mosques" and organizer of the annual Hajj. The Ottoman response was to commission Muhammad Ali Pasha of Egypt, the most effective military commander available, to retake the Hejaz and destroy the Saudi state.

Muhammad Ali's son Tusun launched the first campaign in 1226 AH / 1811 CE, taking the Hejaz back and advancing into Najd. His son Ibrahim Pasha completed the campaign with the siege and destruction of Dir'iyyah in 1233 AH / 1818 CE. The last Saudi ruler of the first state, Abdullah ibn Saud, was taken to Istanbul and executed. Dir'iyyah was razed to the ground.

The destruction of the first Saudi state appeared to end the movement. In fact, it only interrupted it. The Saudi dynasty reconstituted itself, and the second Saudi state (1820-1891) and eventually the third (Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, founded 1932) would continue the alliance between the Al Saud and the religious establishment rooted in Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's reform program.

## "Wahhabism" as a Term: Scholarly Caution Required

The term "Wahhabism" was applied to Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's movement by its opponents — originally as a polemical label, implying a heretical innovation named after its founder rather than a return to authentic Islam. Ibn Abd al-Wahhab himself never used the term; his followers called their approach the "Salafi" or "Hanbali" path, or simply Islam.

In contemporary discourse, "Wahhabism" is used in at least three distinct ways: as a synonym for the specific theology of Ibn Abd al-Wahhab and his immediate school; as a label for the religious establishment of Saudi Arabia and its international influence; and as a general term of abuse for any Islamic position the speaker regards as excessively strict or intolerant. These usages overlap but are not identical, and conflating them produces significant analytical confusion.

The theological questions Ibn Abd al-Wahhab raised — about the legitimacy of intercession through the dead, about the permissibility of shrine visitation, about the relationship between popular Islamic practice and Quranic prescription — are questions that Islamic scholars have debated for centuries and continue to debate. These are genuine scholarly questions within the Sunni tradition, not matters on which there is a single orthodox answer universally agreed upon by all scholars.

## Significance and Continuing Debate

The movement initiated by Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab in eighteenth-century Najd has had an influence on global Islamic practice that far exceeds what could have been predicted from its origins in a peripheral region. The discovery of oil in Saudi Arabia in the twentieth century gave the Saudi state enormous financial resources to disseminate its religious orientations internationally — funding mosques, schools, and publications across the Muslim world. The resulting spread of certain theological positions associated with Ibn Abd al-Wahhab has reshaped the religious landscape of Islam in Africa, Asia, and among Muslim diaspora communities in the West.

Evaluating this legacy requires distinguishing between the original scholarly work of Ibn Abd al-Wahhab — which must be assessed on its merits as Islamic theology — and the political and financial mechanisms through which certain positions associated with his movement have been spread. These are related but separate questions.
