# Death of Ibn Hazm al-Andalusi

Abu Muhammad Ali ibn Ahmad ibn Said ibn Hazm al-Andalusi (384-456 AH / 994-1064 CE) was one of the most remarkable and controversial scholars in the history of Islamic civilization. Jurist, theologian, historian, literary critic, philosopher, and poet, he left an intellectual legacy of extraordinary range and depth. His death in 456 AH (1064 CE) brought to a close a life of singular intellectual courage and relentless controversy.

## Life

Ibn Hazm was born in Cordoba, the capital of Umayyad al-Andalus, in 384 AH (994 CE), into an elite family. His father was a senior minister at the Umayyad court. Ibn Hazm grew up in the refined cultural world of Andalusian high society, receiving an excellent education in both the Islamic sciences and the literary arts.

The collapse of the Umayyad Caliphate of Cordoba in the early fifth century AH (early eleventh century CE) was catastrophic for Ibn Hazm's family. In the chaos of the period of petty kingdoms (*taifa* states) that followed, Ibn Hazm lost his family's position, wealth, and security. He was imprisoned multiple times for his support of the Umayyad cause. His personal experience of political upheaval profoundly shaped his intellectual outlook — a tendency toward rigorous analysis of human behavior and a healthy skepticism toward worldly authority.

## The Zahiri School

Ibn Hazm embraced the Zahiri school of Islamic jurisprudence, the most literalist of the traditional schools. The Zahiris, founded by Dawud al-Zahiri (d. 270 AH / 883 CE), held that legal rulings must be derived directly from the literal (zahir) meaning of the Quran and Sunnah, with no recourse to analogy (qiyas), juristic preference (istihsan), or other reasoning techniques accepted by the four main Sunni schools.

This stance put Ibn Hazm in constant conflict with the Maliki school that dominated al-Andalus. His attacks on what he considered the invalid legal reasoning of the Malikis were often delivered with withering polemical force, making enemies throughout the scholarly establishment.

## Major Works

**Al-Muhalla bil-Athar.** Ibn Hazm's masterwork of Zahiri jurisprudence, in eleven volumes. It systematically addresses the major questions of Islamic law, arguing for each ruling from Quran and Sunnah alone and refuting the rulings of other schools. It remains an indispensable reference for Islamic legal scholarship.

**Al-Fasl fil-Milal wal-Ahwa wan-Nihal.** Ibn Hazm's comprehensive comparative study of religions and philosophical schools — Islam, Judaism, Christianity, Zoroastrianism, various pagan traditions, and philosophical systems. It was one of the first works in the history of comparative religion to engage seriously with other traditions' textual arguments, including a detailed critique of the Bible based on internal contradictions.

**Tawq al-Hamamah** (The Ring of the Dove). Written in his youth, this is a literary masterpiece about love — its nature, its stages, and its effects on the soul. It is one of the most celebrated works of Arabic literature from al-Andalus and has been widely read and translated.

**Al-Ihkam fi Usul al-Ahkam.** A treatise on the principles of Islamic jurisprudence from the Zahiri perspective.

**Jamharat Ansab al-Arab.** A comprehensive genealogical work on Arab tribal lineages.

## Controversies

Ibn Hazm's combative style and willingness to attack the positions of respected scholars made him deeply unpopular with the religious establishment of al-Andalus. His books were publicly burned on orders of the Abbadid ruler of Seville, al-Mu'tadid, who had been stung by Ibn Hazm's sharp criticisms. Ibn Hazm reportedly responded that the ruler could burn his papers but not what was in his heart.

He attacked not only Maliki jurists but also Ash'ari and Maturidi theologians, insisting on a rigorously literal reading of divine attributes. He was equally polemical in his critiques of Judaism and Christianity.

Despite the controversy, his encyclopedic knowledge commanded respect even among those who disagreed with him. Ibn Khaldun later counted him among the greatest scholars of al-Andalus.

## Death and Legacy

Ibn Hazm died in Manta Lisham (present-day Niebla in Spain) in Sha'ban 456 AH (August 1064 CE) at the age of approximately 72. He died in relative obscurity, having been driven from the major centers of Andalusian scholarly life by his enemies.

His intellectual legacy, however, was enormous. The Zahiri school survived primarily through his works, and modern scholars who advocate a return to scriptural literalism frequently cite him. His work in comparative religion was pioneering. His literary works established him as a major figure in Arabic letters.

The twentieth-century Egyptian scholar Taha Husayn praised Ibn Hazm as one of the great geniuses of Islamic civilization. Countless modern Muslim scholars have drawn on his legal works, even when disagreeing with his methodology.
