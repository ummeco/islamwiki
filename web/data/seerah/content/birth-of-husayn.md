# The Birth of al-Husayn ibn Ali

## The Second Grandson

Al-Husayn ibn Ali was born approximately one year after his brother Hasan — in Sha'ban 4 AH, the fourth year after the Hijra. Like Hasan, he was born to Ali ibn Abi Talib and Fatimah al-Zahra, the Prophet's ﷺ beloved daughter, making him the second surviving grandson of the Prophet ﷺ and the second of the two children the Prophet ﷺ would call "the masters of the youth of Paradise."

The Prophet ﷺ performed the same birth practices at Husayn's arrival as he had at Hasan's: the adhan in the right ear, the iqamah in the left, the tahnik. He named him Husayn on the seventh day and performed the aqiqah. The name Husayn, like Hasan, was without precedent in the Arab naming tradition of that time. Some narrations indicate both names were divinely suggested to the Prophet ﷺ, paralleling the names used in the Torah for Hasan and Husayn's equivalents in the prophetic lineage of earlier traditions.

## The Prophet's Love for Husayn

The Prophet ﷺ expressed his love for Husayn repeatedly and in ways that the hadith literature has preserved with care. He said: "Husayn is from me, and I am from Husayn. Whoever loves Husayn, Allah loves him." This statement — "Husayn is from me, and I am from Husayn" — is grammatically striking. The first phrase (Husayn is from me) is what one would naturally expect a grandfather to say. The second phrase (I am from Husayn) reverses the relationship, expressing that the Prophet's ﷺ legacy, his continuation in the world, and perhaps the mission's future would come through Husayn and his descendants.

He would call Hasan and Husayn together the sayyids — the masters or leaders — of the youth of Paradise. He would carry them during prayer and hold them against his chest. The companions noted that between the two brothers, Husayn resembled the Prophet ﷺ more in character — his courage, his directness, and his willingness to stand for principle regardless of consequences. Ali, when asked which of his children most resembled the Prophet ﷺ, said: "Hasan in his appearance, Husayn in his courage."

## The Two Fragrant Flowers

The Prophet ﷺ called Hasan and Husayn together "my two fragrant flowers (rayhanatan) in this world." The word rayhanah refers to sweet basil — an intensely fragrant herb that is beautiful but fragile. The metaphor is of the Prophet's ﷺ grandsons as his greatest sensory pleasure in this world — beloved, beautiful, and carrying within them the continuation of everything he had built. This hadith is preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari and is among the most cited descriptions of the Prophet's ﷺ emotional relationship with his grandchildren.

Both boys grew up in the household of Fatimah and Ali under conditions of material simplicity — the household that had no servant when Fatimah asked, the household where the Prophet ﷺ had furnished the room with a mat and a water vessel. The grandchildren of the Prophet ﷺ grew up knowing what it meant to live without excess, in a community that was still building itself and had not yet acquired the wealth that came later.

## Husayn After the Prophet

Al-Husayn survived the Prophet ﷺ by approximately fifty years, dying in 61 AH. He lived through the caliphates of Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali, and through the caliphate of Mu'awiyah and the beginning of Yazid's rule. In 61 AH, he traveled toward Kufa at the invitation of its people, was abandoned by those who had pledged to support him, and was killed at the plain of Karbala on the tenth of Muharram — a day now known across the Muslim world as Ashura.

His death at Karbala is one of the most consequential events in Islamic history in terms of its effect on later Muslim thought, practice, and sectarian division. Sunni tradition mourns Karbala as a great tragedy — the killing of the Prophet's ﷺ grandson by a Muslim army — while acknowledging its complex political context. Shia tradition marks Ashura with deep mourning and regards Husayn's martyrdom as a foundational act of sacrifice for justice in Islam.

The Prophet ﷺ had wept at Husayn's birth, according to some narrations — when Jibril informed him of what would happen to his grandson. Whether or not this narration is accepted, the connection between the joy of Husayn's birth and the grief of his death runs through the Islamic tradition as a reminder that the greatest lives are often the ones most tested — and that the Prophet's ﷺ deepest loves were also the site of his deepest sorrows.

## The Birth Practices as Sunnah

Like Hasan's birth, Husayn's birth provided the community with the model for how to welcome a child into Islam. The practices of adhan, tahnik, naming on the seventh day, aqiqah, and the charitable silver equivalent of the hair's weight — performed twice by the Prophet ﷺ for his two grandsons — were transmitted through the hadith literature and became the normative practice that Islamic jurisprudence prescribes for all Muslim newborns. Every Muslim child whose parents whisper the adhan in their ear at birth is connected by that act to the day in Medina when the Prophet ﷺ bent over his second grandson and did the same. The Prophet's ﷺ weeping at Husayn's birth — when Jibril informed him of the future events at Karbala, in narrations cited by al-Tirmidhi and others — and his overwhelming joy at holding his new grandson represent the two registers of the prophetic heart: a love so deep that knowledge of coming grief did not diminish it, and a grief accepted in advance because the purpose it served was greater than its pain. Husayn's birth was the beginning of a life that would shape Islamic civilization's understanding of martyrdom, justice, and standing for principle for fourteen centuries and beyond.
