# The Conversion of al-Tufayl ibn Amr al-Dawsi

## A Poet Among the Arabs

Al-Tufayl ibn Amr al-Dawsi was a poet of the Daws tribe in southern Arabia — a man of prestige and literary distinction in a culture where eloquence was the highest form of accomplishment. When he traveled to Mecca during the pilgrimage season, the Quraysh knew that a respected poet of his caliber might be moved by Muhammad's ﷺ Quranic recitation, and they made a deliberate effort to prevent him from hearing it.

According to the account preserved by Ibn Hisham, as al-Tufayl approached Mecca, Qurayshi men intercepted him and warned him: "This man is causing division among us — between a father and his son, between a husband and his wife, between a brother and his brother. We fear he will do the same to you. Do not listen to him; plug your ears."

Al-Tufayl, cautious, took the warning to heart. He went to the Ka'bah with cotton stuffed in his ears so he would not accidentally hear anything the Prophet ﷺ might say. Yet even this precaution failed. Watching the Prophet ﷺ pray near the Ka'bah, al-Tufayl — despite the cotton — found himself thinking: "Why am I doing this? I am a man of letters and intelligence. I am capable of distinguishing good speech from bad. Why don't I just listen, and if what he says is good, I'll accept it, and if it is bad, I'll leave it?"

## The Encounter

Al-Tufayl removed the cotton from his ears and approached the Prophet ﷺ. He listened to the Quran. His poetic ear — trained to recognize excellence in language — immediately recognized something unprecedented. He followed the Prophet ﷺ to his home and told him: "Your people have told me about you. By Allah, they frightened me away from you. I stopped my ears, but then thought better of it. I have heard what you say and found it to be beautiful. So lay your affairs before me."

The Prophet ﷺ presented Islam to him — explained tawhid, recited Quran, explained the message. Al-Tufayl accepted Islam immediately, in the same decisive way as Abu Bakr before him. He said the shahada and then said: "O Prophet of Allah, I am a man who is obeyed among my people. I will go back to them and call them to Islam. Pray to Allah that He gives me a sign that will help me in calling them."

## The Sign

The Prophet ﷺ prayed for him. According to Ibn Hisham's narration, a light appeared on al-Tufayl's forehead as he departed. He said: "O Messenger of Allah, I fear that they will say it is a punishment [for having gone to you] and it will deter them." The Prophet ﷺ prayed again, and the light moved to the tip of his whip instead — where it shone as he traveled back to his people.

## The Dawah to the Daws

Al-Tufayl returned to the Daws tribe and began calling his people to Islam. His first target was his own father, who accepted Islam. Then his wife converted. But the wider tribe was resistant; they were deeply attached to their idols and their way of life, and al-Tufayl found that despite his status as a poet and respected man, his call was making slow progress.

He returned to the Prophet ﷺ in Mecca and reported his limited success. He said: "The Daws have disobeyed. Pray to Allah against them." The Prophet ﷺ turned to face the qiblah and lifted his hands. Al-Tufayl feared what was coming. But the Prophet ﷺ prayed: "O Allah, guide the Daws and bring them [to Islam]." Then he told al-Tufayl: "Return to your people, call them [to Islam], and be gentle with them."

## The Return and the Daws' Conversion

Al-Tufayl returned and continued calling his tribe with patience and gentleness. By the time the Prophet ﷺ had migrated to Medina, al-Tufayl had brought a large group of Daws to Islam. He then led approximately seventy or eighty households of Daws — including the young man who would become the greatest hadith narrator in Islamic history, Abu Hurayrah al-Dawsi — on the journey to Medina to join the Prophet ﷺ.

Abu Hurayrah became Muslim through the efforts of al-Tufayl ibn Amr. Had al-Tufayl not returned from his pilgrimage to Mecca as a Muslim, had he not persisted in calling his tribe, had he not brought that caravan of Daws to Medina — the primary transmitter of hadith, the companion who narrated more sayings of the Prophet ﷺ than any other, might never have encountered Islam. The spiritual genealogy of thousands of hadith runs through al-Tufayl's conversion and dawah.

## Significance

The conversion of al-Tufayl ibn Amr illustrates several principles that run through the entire seerah:

The Quran's literary power was a direct instrument of dawah: a trained poet heard a few verses and was immediately moved by their quality, recognizing something that transcended human composition. The Prophet's ﷺ prayer for his companion — providing a tangible sign to assist in his tribe's dawah — demonstrates the divine support that accompanied the mission beyond the Prophet's ﷺ personal efforts. And the instruction to "be gentle with them" in the face of initial resistance established the manner of dawah: patience and softness, not force or coercion.

Al-Tufayl later fought in the battles of the prophetic era and was martyred in 633 CE at the Battle of Yamama — one of the battles of the Ridda (apostasy) wars during the caliphate of Abu Bakr, a final act of devotion from the poet-warrior who had first heard the Quran despite the cotton in his ears.

The chain of consequence from al-Tufayl's conversion is worth tracing to its end. Al-Tufayl heard the Quran. He accepted Islam. He returned to his tribe. He guided Abu Hurayrah رضي الله عنه to Medina. Abu Hurayrah spent three or four years in the Prophet's company ﷺ, memorizing everything he could. He later narrated approximately 5,374 hadiths — more than any other companion — most of which pass through chains that eventually lead back to him. Every time a Muslim hears a hadith "on the authority of Abu Hurayrah," the chain reaches back through him to al-Tufayl ibn Amr al-Dawsi and the day a poet removed cotton from his ears to hear something he had been warned was dangerous.
