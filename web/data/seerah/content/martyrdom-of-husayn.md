# The Martyrdom of Husayn at Karbala: A Tragedy in Islamic History

## The Events That Led to Karbala

The martyrdom of Husayn ibn Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) at Karbala on 10 Muharram, 61 AH / October 680 CE stands as one of the most sorrowful events in Islamic history. To understand it, one must understand the chain of circumstances that led a grandson of the Prophet ﷺ and his small band of family and companions to face death on a plain in Iraq.

When Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan (may Allah be pleased with him) died in 60 AH, he had arranged for his son Yazid ibn Muawiyah to succeed him as caliph — departing from the consultative principles of the earlier caliphate and establishing a hereditary succession. Yazid was a young man. The historical accounts of his personal conduct vary considerably in their reliability, but it is established that he lacked the religious authority and the moral standing of the caliphs who had preceded his father. Husayn ibn Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) — the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, one of the masters of the youth of Paradise as the Prophet ﷺ himself had declared — refused to pledge allegiance to Yazid. He saw Yazid as unqualified and his installation as caliph as a violation of the Islamic principle that leadership should belong to those most fit for it.

Husayn's refusal placed him in immediate danger. Yazid's governors were ordered to obtain his allegiance by whatever means necessary. Husayn left Medina for Mecca, where he remained for months while determining his course.

## The Letters from Kufa

The people of Kufa — the Iraqi city that had been a center of Ali's support throughout the first civil war — responded to news of Husayn's refusal with an outpouring of correspondence. Letters arrived in Mecca in extraordinary numbers, some accounts estimating hundreds or even thousands of letters, all urging Husayn to come to Kufa and lead them. The Kufans promised their allegiance and their support, painting a picture of a population ready to rise in his favor.

Husayn took these letters seriously. He sent his cousin Muslim ibn Aqil to Kufa ahead of him to assess the situation on the ground and report back. Muslim arrived in Kufa and found apparent widespread enthusiasm — he wrote to Husayn that the people were as they described, and that he should come. Husayn made preparations to depart.

But the situation in Kufa deteriorated rapidly. Yazid replaced the lenient governor al-Nu'man ibn Bashir with the harsh Ubaydullah ibn Ziyad, who moved swiftly to crush any organizing activity. Muslim ibn Aqil was captured after the Kufans who had gathered around him melted away under Ibn Ziyad's pressure — the same men who had been writing letters of support and pledging their lives now abandoned him one by one. Muslim was executed by Ibn Ziyad.

By the time news reached Husayn that Kufa had turned, he was already on the road from Mecca with his family and companions. Some of his companions and advisors — including Abdullah ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) and Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) — had urged him before he left not to go, warning him of exactly this outcome: that the Kufans could not be trusted, that they had abandoned his father Ali and then his brother Hasan before. Husayn heard these warnings but pressed on.

## The Interception at Karbala

Near the Euphrates River, at a place called Karbala in Iraq, Husayn's small group was intercepted by Umayyad forces. The size of the force arrayed against him grew as the days passed — what began as a relatively small contingent under al-Hurr ibn Yazid al-Riyahi (who initially only prevented Husayn from proceeding or retreating freely) swelled to a much larger army under the command of Umar ibn Sa'd. Classical accounts give varying figures for the Umayyad forces; the numbers in some later accounts are certainly inflated, but the essential fact is clear: Husayn and his approximately seventy-two fighting companions faced an overwhelmingly superior force.

Al-Hurr ibn Yazid, to his enduring credit, ultimately could not bring himself to participate in the killing of the Prophet's ﷺ grandson. On the morning of Ashura, he broke away from the Umayyad forces and joined Husayn, acknowledging his intention to repent for having blocked him. He fought with Husayn's companions and was killed. His act of courage and contrition has been remembered by Muslim historians as a bright thread in an otherwise dark tapestry.

## The Cut-Off of Water

Three days before the final battle, Ibn Ziyad ordered that Husayn's group be denied access to the water of the Euphrates. The river was close — visible to them — but guarded. For three days, Husayn, his family including women and children, and his companions endured thirst in the heat of the Iraqi plain. This detail of the story has always struck historians, Muslim and non-Muslim alike, as one of its most viscerally unjust elements. Children among the group were dying of thirst.

The accounts in Ibn Kathir's al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya record that Husayn made several attempts to negotiate during these days — offering to be allowed to return to Medina, or to go to a frontier post to fight in jihad, or to present himself directly to Yazid in Damascus. Ibn Ziyad refused all options. The message was clear: only unconditional surrender would be acceptable.

Husayn chose neither surrender nor escape. He understood the reality of his situation and accepted it.

## The Day of Ashura: 10 Muharram, 61 AH

On the morning of Ashura, Husayn delivered a final address to the opposing force, invoking their memory of his grandfather the Prophet ﷺ, his father Ali, and the Prophet's ﷺ love for him and his brother Hasan. Some accounts record that the speech caused weeping even among the Umayyad soldiers, but Ibn Ziyad's commanders would not yield.

The fighting began. Husayn's companions, outnumbered many times over, fought with remarkable courage. They fell one by one through the morning. When the companions were all killed, the battle came to Husayn and the men of his family — his brother Abbas (who was killed trying to reach the river for water for the children), the young men of the Hashimite clan who were with him.

Husayn fought until he could fight no longer. He was struck by arrows and sword blows. According to Ibn Kathir's account, drawing on multiple chains of transmission, he received numerous wounds. He was finally killed — accounts differ on who struck the fatal blow, but the name most often cited in classical sources is Sinan ibn Anas al-Nakha'i, or Shimr ibn Dhil-Jawshan.

His head was severed from his body and taken to Ibn Ziyad in Kufa, and subsequently to Yazid in Damascus. His body, along with those of his companions, was left on the plain of Karbala. The women and children of his family — including his sister Zaynab bint Ali — were taken captive.

When the head of Husayn was placed before Ibn Ziyad, one of those present — Zayd ibn Arqam (may Allah be pleased with him), an elderly Companion of the Prophet ﷺ — wept and said: "Take it away from me. By Allah, I saw the lips of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ upon those lips." He left and could not bear to remain.

## The Ahl us-Sunnah View

The martyrdom of Husayn ibn Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) is, from the Sunni perspective, a genuine historical tragedy and a profound injustice. This is the unambiguous position of classical Sunni scholars.

The killing of Husayn is condemned. Those who killed him bear a grave sin. Ibn Kathir (may Allah have mercy on him), in al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya, narrates these events with evident sorrow and does not hesitate to call the killing of Husayn a catastrophe (musiba). Al-Dhahabi similarly expresses grief at these events. There is no Sunni scholarly voice that excuses the killers of Husayn or minimizes what was done.

Regarding Yazid: the majority of classical Sunni scholars refrain from cursing him by name, while acknowledging the injustice of what occurred under his authority. The scholarly debate on Yazid is long and detailed. Some scholars, including Ibn al-Jawzi, did permit his cursing. Others, including Ibn Taymiyyah (may Allah have mercy on him), held that cursing specific individuals without certain knowledge of their final state is a matter requiring caution — while fully acknowledging the evil of what was done at Karbala. This difference of scholarly opinion should be understood in its proper context: it is a discussion about the bounds of cursing, not a defense of Yazid's actions.

Love for Husayn and for the family of the Prophet ﷺ (Ahl al-Bayt) is a religious obligation for every Muslim. The Prophet ﷺ said: "I remind you of Allah regarding my family; I remind you of Allah regarding my family; I remind you of Allah regarding my family" (Muslim). Husayn is among the most beloved members of that family, and his courage, his principle, and his sacrifice for what he believed to be right are honored.

The day of Ashura — the tenth of Muharram — is a day of fasting in Islam, established by the Prophet ﷺ himself before the events of Karbala, in commemoration of the day Allah saved Musa (peace be upon him) from Pharaoh. The fast of Ashura long predates 61 AH. Mourning rituals — chest-beating, self-flagellation, public processions of grief — are not part of Sunni practice and have no basis in the Prophetic tradition.

The events of Karbala do not alter the Sunni stance on the civil war: we do not curse any Companion or declare them apostates. We grieve the tragedy. We condemn the killers. We honor Husayn. We do not allow grief to become sectarian warfare across the centuries.

## The Legacy

The events of Karbala left a wound in the Muslim community that has never fully healed. They became the founding trauma of the Shia tradition, which developed an elaborate theology of redemptive martyrdom around Husayn's death. For Sunnis, Karbala is a historical tragedy to be remembered with grief and used as a lesson about the dangers of civil conflict, the corruption of power, and the betrayal of those who deserve loyalty.

The most reliable classical Sunni account of Karbala is in Ibn Kathir's al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya (Volume 8), which draws extensively on al-Tabari's earlier account while subjecting the chains of transmission to careful criticism. Al-Tabari's own account (in Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk) remains the primary historical source and is the most detailed treatment of the events from the classical period.
