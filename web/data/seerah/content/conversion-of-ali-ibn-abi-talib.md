# Ali ibn Abi Talib: The First Youth to Accept Islam

## A Child in the House of Prophethood

The story of Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) cannot be understood apart from the household in which he was raised. He was born into the family of Abu Talib ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's uncle and one of the most respected men of the Quraysh. When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was a young man and Abu Talib's family was going through financial hardship, the Prophet ﷺ — moved by compassion and a sense of duty to his family — took Ali into his own home to raise and care for him. This arrangement, which Ibn Hisham and al-Tabari record in detail, meant that Ali grew up not merely near the Prophet ﷺ but in his household, under his direct care and guidance.

Ali was approximately nine or ten years old when the first revelation descended upon the Prophet ﷺ in the Cave of Hira. He was thus not only the youngest person to accept Islam but also the person who perhaps knew the Prophet ﷺ most intimately at the moment the prophetic mission began. He had observed Muhammad ﷺ his entire conscious life — his prayer, his conduct, his honesty, his care for others. The message of Islam, when it came to Ali, did not arrive as something foreign. It arrived in the form of the man he had watched and loved since childhood.

## The Night of the First Prayer

Ibn Hisham, drawing on the account of Ibn Ishaq, records the following: One evening, Ali entered the room where the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her) were engaged in prayer. He observed them bowing and prostrating in a manner he had not seen before among the Arabs. Curious and perhaps startled, Ali asked the Prophet ﷺ: "What is this, O Muhammad?" The Prophet ﷺ explained that he was the Messenger of Allah, that Allah had sent him with the religion of Islam, and that this prayer was the worship due to Allah alone.

The Prophet ﷺ then invited Ali to accept Islam and to worship Allah alone, abandoning the idols. Ali's response, as recorded by Ibn Hisham, was that of a thoughtful boy: "I have not seen this matter before. I cannot decide until I have spoken with Abu Talib." The Prophet ﷺ is reported to have said to him: "If you are not yet ready to accept Islam, then conceal what you have heard." Ali did not sleep that night. By the morning, Allah had opened his heart, and he came to the Prophet ﷺ saying: "What did you present to me last night?" When the Prophet ﷺ repeated the message, Ali accepted Islam. He is reported to have concealed his faith from his father Abu Talib for some time, though he continued to pray with the Prophet ﷺ.

This account, narrated with various chains in the early sirah literature, establishes Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) as among the first three or four people to accept the message of Islam — after Khadijah and before anyone outside the immediate household.

## The Question of "Who Was First"

The scholarly discussion around who was the first Muslim has occupied historians and theologians since the early generations. The traditional Ahl us-Sunnah position, recorded by Ibn Hisham, al-Tabari, and later codified by Ibn Kathir in al-Bidayah wal-Nihayah, distinguishes between categories:

Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with her) was the first person overall — the first to hear the message, the first to confirm it, and the first to pray with the Prophet ﷺ. She is unambiguously the first Muslim. Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) was the first among youth and children (al-sibyan) — those who had not yet reached the age of full adulthood and independent judgment. Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) was the first among free adult men who chose Islam through personal decision and reasoning, without the benefit of living in the Prophet's household. Zayd ibn Harithah (may Allah be pleased with him), the Prophet's freed slave and adopted son, is sometimes mentioned alongside these first converts.

Some early scholars, particularly those of certain tendencies, argued that Ali was the first of all people to accept Islam — or that his conversion, because it came before Abu Bakr's by some accounts, gave him a singular primacy. The mainstream Ahl us-Sunnah position does not accept this formulation. The scholarly consensus, represented by al-Nawawi, Ibn Kathir, and Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, is that the distinctions above are accurate: Khadijah first, Ali first among youth, Abu Bakr first among free adult men.

This does not diminish Ali's status in the slightest. Being the first youth to accept Islam is itself a tremendous distinction — it reflects the purity and clarity of perception that he possessed even in childhood.

## The Unique Position of Living in the House of Revelation

There is something profound in the fact that Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) witnessed the beginning of the prophetic mission from within the household itself. While other early Muslims heard the message and came to it from outside, Ali was present at the very origin. He prayed with the Prophet ﷺ before anyone else outside Khadijah knew. He was formed by the same household that was being transformed by revelation. Al-Hakim records in al-Mustadrak that Ali himself said: "I am the servant of Allah and the brother of His Messenger. I am al-Siddiq al-Akbar (the Greatest Confirmer of Truth). No one will say this after me except a liar. I prayed with the Messenger of Allah seven years before anyone from this ummah prayed."

While the status of this particular narration is debated, the general thrust — that Ali's proximity to the Prophet ﷺ from the first moments of revelation was unique — is confirmed by multiple independent chains.

## His Character and Qualities

Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) grew from that young boy into one of the greatest men the Muslim community has ever known. The Prophet ﷺ recognized in him courage, intelligence, and a depth of understanding that was rare. He gave Ali the flag (the standard of Islam) at multiple battles and said of him in the famous hadith of Khaybar: "Tomorrow I will give the flag to a man who loves Allah and His Messenger, and Allah and His Messenger love him" — then gave it to Ali (Bukhari and Muslim).

He was a formidable warrior. His title Asadullah — the Lion of Allah — was earned in battle after battle: Badr, Uhud, the Trench, Khaybar, and Hunayn. His physical courage was matched by his intellectual depth: he is counted among the greatest jurists and scholars of the early community, and his collected sayings in Nahj al-Balagha (though requiring careful attribution analysis) attest to the breadth of his learning.

The Prophet ﷺ also honored him through the bonds of family. Ali married Fatimah al-Zahra (may Allah be pleased with her), the Prophet's beloved daughter, and from this union came al-Hasan and al-Husayn (may Allah be pleased with them), whom the Prophet ﷺ called "the masters of the youth of Paradise" (al-Tirmidhi, with a hasan isnad per many scholars). The Prophet's love for Ali was thus also expressed through these grandsons who carried Ali's lineage.

## His Status in the Prophetic Hadith

The Prophet ﷺ made numerous statements testifying to Ali's rank. In Sahih Muslim, the Prophet ﷺ said to Ali at the time of the Expedition of Tabuk (when he left Ali to manage Medina in his absence): "Are you not pleased to be to me as Harun was to Musa, except that there is no prophet after me?" This hadith, known as hadith al-manzilah, is one of the clearest textual statements of Ali's unique relationship to the Prophet ﷺ — not as a successor in prophethood, but as a trusted lieutenant of the highest order.

In another well-known tradition, preserved by al-Tirmidhi, the Prophet ﷺ said: "I am the city of knowledge and Ali is its gate." While scholars have debated the degree of this hadith's strength, its meaning is supported by the historical reality of Ali's encyclopedic knowledge of jurisprudence, Quran, and hadith.

The event at Ghadir Khumm, on the Prophet's return from the Farewell Pilgrimage, is also historically established: the Prophet ﷺ took Ali's hand and said: "Whoever takes me as his mawla (master, patron, close friend), let Ali be his mawla." This statement is recorded by al-Tirmidhi and Ahmad ibn Hanbal. The Ahl us-Sunnah understand "mawla" here in its linguistic sense of close companion and beloved — expressing the Prophet's wish that the community should honor and love Ali as he himself loved him.

## His Later Life and the Question of the Caliphate

The Ahl us-Sunnah hold that the caliphate of Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman (may Allah be pleased with them all) was legitimate and that Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) accepted and supported these caliphs. He gave bay'ah to each of them. He served as an advisor to Abu Bakr and Umar, and his opinions were sought and respected. He did not consider himself to have been wronged by their appointments, according to the mainstream Sunni understanding.

When Ali became the fourth Caliph (35-40 AH), the Muslim community faced its most severe internal trials. The murder of Uthman, the Battle of the Camel, and the Battle of Siffin — these were tests that revealed the complexity of the early Muslim polity. Ali navigated these trials with the dignity of a man of faith, and the Ahl us-Sunnah hold all parties in these conflicts in honor, understanding that these were ijtihad-based disagreements among people who were striving sincerely for the truth.

His martyrdom in 40 AH, struck down while praying the Fajr prayer in Kufa by Abd al-Rahman ibn Muljam, was a tragedy that the entire Muslim world mourned. He died as he had lived — in the act of worship, on the prayer mat, in the house of Allah.

## The Ahl us-Sunnah Position on Ali's Status

The proper Ahl us-Sunnah position on Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) is one of profound love and respect, calibrated by the evidence. He is the fourth of the Rightly Guided Caliphs. He is among the ten promised Paradise. He is the cousin and son-in-law of the Prophet ﷺ. He is the first youth to accept Islam. His courage, knowledge, and piety are beyond dispute.

What the Ahl us-Sunnah reject is the elevation of Ali above Abu Bakr and Umar, or the claim that the first three caliphs were usurpers who robbed Ali of a right divinely ordained. Such positions, associated with the Shia traditions, are not supported by the historical evidence and conflict with the ijma (consensus) of the Sahabah themselves. The love of Ali is a mark of true faith; the denigration of other companions in his name is deviation from the straight path.

Ibn Taymiyyah wrote in his Minhaj al-Sunnah al-Nabawiyyah: "The virtues of Ali are among the things that Ahl us-Sunnah and scholars agree upon — and the most correct opinion is that Abu Bakr is better, then Umar, then Uthman, then Ali." This ranking does not diminish Ali; it places him in the company of the three greatest men after the Prophet ﷺ, which is itself a magnificent honor.
