# Treaty with Banu Qurayza

The treaty between the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and Banu Qurayza — the last of the three major Jewish tribes of Medina — was established as part of the constitutional framework created after the hijra in 1 AH (622 CE). Banu Qurayza held to this treaty longer than the other two tribes and maintained their treaty relationship with the Muslim community through the expulsions of Banu Qaynuqa (2 AH) and Banu Nadir (4 AH). The treaty's violation during the Battle of the Trench (5 AH), under pressure from Huyayy ibn Akhtab of Banu Nadir, had catastrophic consequences for the tribe.

## Banu Qurayza's Position

Banu Qurayza occupied a fortified district approximately 5 km south of Medina's main residential area. They were primarily agriculturalists with significant orchards and palm groves. They were allied with the Aws — one of the two major Arab tribal confederations of Medina (the other being the Khazraj, who were allied with Banu Qaynuqa).

Their Aws tribal patrons had interceded for Banu Qaynuqa at Hudaybiyyah (though this occurred with Banu Nadir, not Qaynuqa — the sources indicate the Aws connection was with Banu Qurayza specifically in the context of the later events) and had a long history of mutual support with Banu Qurayza that predated Islam.

## Terms of the Treaty

Banu Qurayza's treaty with the Prophet included the standard provisions of the Constitution of Medina:

**Membership in the covenant community.** "The Jews of Banu Qurayza are one community with the believers, though the Jews have their religion and the Muslims have their religion." This recognized both their political membership in the Medinan covenant and their religious distinctness.

**Blood money and captive ransom.** They shared in the community's blood money obligations, maintaining their own as a tribal group within the broader framework.

**Mutual defense.** They were obligated to contribute to Medina's defense against external attack.

**Non-support of enemies.** They were prohibited from supporting the Quraysh or any external enemy of the covenant community — the term that would prove decisive in 5 AH.

**The Prophet's arbitration.** All disputes were to be referred to the Prophet as supreme arbiter.

## The Relationship Before the Crisis

The accounts in the seerah indicate that Banu Qurayza largely honored their treaty commitments in the period from the hijra to the Battle of the Trench. When the Prophet came to their quarter before the battle in connection with a blood money matter (a different visit from the one involving Banu Nadir), they reportedly received him correctly.

Their chief, Ka'b ibn Asad, was known to the Prophet and had maintained the treaty relationship. When Huyayy ibn Akhtab came to persuade him to break the treaty during the Confederate siege, Ka'b initially refused, saying he had known nothing from the Prophet but faithfulness and honoring of agreements.

## The Violation

The violation was ultimately the work of Huyayy ibn Akhtab's sustained persuasion and the false promises of the Confederate army's strength. Ka'b was eventually convinced that the Confederates represented an overwhelming force that would destroy the Muslim community, making loyalty to the treaty pointless.

When Ka'b tore the treaty document, he committed an act that — under the norms of the time — removed Banu Qurayza's protected status as a treaty partner and made them an active enemy of the Muslim community at the worst possible moment.

## The Aftermath

The violation of the treaty led directly to the siege of Banu Qurayza after the Confederate withdrawal and to the judgment of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh — which Banu Qurayza themselves had accepted as legitimate arbitration. The outcome was the most severe of the three confrontations with Medina's Jewish tribes.

## Historical Reflection

The trajectory of all three tribal treaties — each ending in expulsion or worse — raises questions that Islamic scholars have addressed across the centuries. The pattern was not one of the Prophet seeking confrontation with the Jewish tribes; rather, each confrontation was triggered by specific acts (assassination attempt, marketplace incident, military alliance with besieging enemies) that were treated as treaty violations.

The difference in outcomes — expulsion for Banu Qaynuqa and Banu Nadir, but the harsher judgment for Banu Qurayza — is attributed to the severity of the violation: Banu Qurayza's alliance with the besieging Confederate army at the moment of the Muslim community's greatest military vulnerability was qualitatively different from the earlier confrontations.

## Sources

The treaty terms are preserved in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya* and Abu Ubayd's *Kitab al-Amwal*. The events of 5 AH are documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Ibn Hisham, al-Tabari, and Ibn Saad.
