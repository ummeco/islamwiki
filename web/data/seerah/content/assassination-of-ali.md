# The Martyrdom of Ali ibn Abi Talib: The End of the Rightly-Guided Era

## The Man Who Was Struck Down

Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) — the cousin of the Prophet ﷺ, the husband of his daughter Fatimah, the father of Hasan and Husayn, the fourth Caliph of Islam — was struck with a poison-coated sword as he entered the mosque of Kufa to lead the Fajr prayer in the pre-dawn hours of 17 Ramadan, 40 AH.

He had been warned. In the days before the attack, some accounts record that Ali had received indications of a conspiracy against his life. He reportedly said: "I have searched for this death, but it flees from me." He walked into that mosque with the full awareness that he was a man who had spent his adult life in the service of Allah and was ready to meet Him at any moment.

The attacker was Abd al-Rahman ibn Muljam al-Muradi, a man from the extremist Kharijite sect — those who had seceded from the Muslim community, declaring both Ali and Muawiyah apostates and believing themselves to be the pure vanguard of Islam. Ibn Muljam had made a pact with two companions: on the same night, they would simultaneously assassinate the three most powerful men in the Islamic world — Ali in Kufa, Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan in Damascus, and Amr ibn al-As in Egypt. Only the attack on Ali succeeded. Muawiyah survived a wound to the hip, and the assassin targeting Amr ibn al-As killed the wrong man when Amr ibn al-As happened not to lead the prayer that night.

Ibn Muljam struck Ali with a sword coated in poison, hitting him on the forehead. Ali did not fall immediately. He was carried to his home. He lingered for two days — 17 and 18 Ramadan — before dying on the night of 19 Ramadan (some accounts say 21 Ramadan) 40 AH.

## The Caliphate of Ali: Context of the Fitna

To understand the martyrdom of Ali, one must understand the extraordinary turbulence of his caliphate — a turbulence that he did not cause but was called upon to navigate.

Ali became the fourth Caliph in 35 AH, following the assassination of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) by rebellious soldiers from Egypt and Iraq. The circumstances of Uthman's killing, and the question of who bore responsibility for it, became the defining political issue of the following years.

### The Battle of Jamal (36 AH)

A group of prominent Companions — Talhah ibn Ubaydullah (may Allah be pleased with him), Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (may Allah be pleased with him), and the Prophet's ﷺ wife Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) — raised their concern that the killers of Uthman had not been brought to justice and that Ali was moving too slowly on the matter. They gathered in Basra, not with the intention of waging war against Ali, but to press their case. The situation deteriorated and battle broke out at al-Jamal (36 AH / 656 CE).

Both Talhah and Zubayr died in the fighting — Zubayr was killed after he had already begun to withdraw from the battle, reportedly convinced by Ali himself that fighting was wrong. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), who had been present in a litter on a camel and whose camel became a focal point of the fighting, was unharmed. Ali treated her with the utmost respect, providing her with an escort and sending her back to Medina with honor. The Battle of Jamal ended in Ali's favor militarily, but it left deep wounds in the Muslim community.

### The Battle of Siffin and the Arbitration (37 AH)

The conflict between Ali and Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan — the governor of Syria and a cousin of Uthman — reached its military climax at the Battle of Siffin on the banks of the Euphrates in 37 AH. Muawiyah had refused to give allegiance to Ali, demanding that Uthman's killers be brought to justice first. What followed was months of skirmishing, truces, and diplomatic exchanges before pitched battle commenced.

When Ali's forces were on the verge of a decisive victory, Muawiyah's general Amr ibn al-As instructed the Syrian soldiers to raise copies of the Quran on their spears — signaling that the dispute should be resolved by the Book of Allah rather than by the sword. Ali's soldiers were divided. Many refused to continue fighting. The arbitration of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari (representing Ali) and Amr ibn al-As (representing Muawiyah) failed to resolve the conflict and left Ali politically weakened.

### The Kharijites

The arbitration produced a third party that would prove the most dangerous: the Kharijites (al-Khawarij — those who departed). These were men from Ali's own army who held that by agreeing to human arbitration over a matter they believed was settled by divine law, Ali had committed kufr (disbelief) and thereby forfeited his right to the caliphate. They adopted the slogan "La hukma illa lillah" — "Judgment belongs to Allah alone."

The Kharijites declared both Ali and Muawiyah apostates deserving of death. Ali fought them at the Battle of Nahrawan (38 AH) and largely defeated them, but their ideology survived and their hatred of Ali grew. It was from this remnant that Ibn Muljam emerged — the man who would carry out the assassination.

## The Last Days and Final Words

Ali survived his wound for two days. His last recorded words to his sons Hasan and Husayn capture the quality of his character. He reportedly said: "I advise you both to fear Allah, and not to pursue this world even if it pursues you. Do not grieve over anything that has been taken from you. Say the truth. Act for the reward of Allah. Be an enemy of the wrongdoer and a helper of the oppressed."

When told that his attacker was Ibn Muljam, he said: "If I recover, I will decide what to do with him. If I die, then do not cut off his limb as punishment, and give him only the equivalent of what he gave me — one blow. Do not mutilate him, for I heard the Prophet ﷺ forbid mutilation even against a rabid dog."

This instruction — given by a dying man about the person who had killed him — is one of the most remarkable demonstrations of prophetic morality in Islamic history. Even in death, Ali was concerned not with vengeance but with adherence to the boundaries Allah had set.

He also reportedly said: "Do not fight the Kharijites after me, for one who seeks the truth and errs is not like one who seeks falsehood and finds it." This statement has been extensively discussed by scholars. Ali was not excusing the Kharijites — he had fought them at Nahrawan and would have continued to do so had he lived. He was making a distinction between sincere people who go wrong through misunderstanding and deliberate enemies of the truth. And he was warning his sons not to use the Kharijite threat as a pretext for further civil war.

He died on 19 or 21 Ramadan 40 AH, at an age most scholars estimate between 58 and 63 years.

## The Ahl us-Sunnah View of Ali and the Fitna

The position of Ahl us-Sunnah wal-Jama'ah on Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) and the First Fitna is clear, nuanced, and grounded in classical scholarship.

Love for Ali is obligatory. He is among the greatest Companions of the Prophet ﷺ, the first male to embrace Islam (according to many accounts), the man who slept in the Prophet's ﷺ bed on the night of the Hijra to deceive the Quraysh plotters, the husband of Fatimah (may Allah be pleased with her), the father of Hasan and Husayn, and a Caliph whom the Muslims accepted and pledged allegiance to.

The Prophet ﷺ said about him: "You are to me as Harun was to Musa, except there is no prophet after me" (Bukhari and Muslim). He also said: "Whoever loves me should love Ali. Whoever loves Ali, loves me" (narrated by al-Tirmidhi and others). He said at Ghadir Khumm: "Whoever I am his master (mawla), Ali is also his master" — a hadith whose precise meaning has been debated but whose authenticity is established.

On the civil war: Ahl us-Sunnah hold that both Ali and those who opposed him were sincere Companions of the Prophet ﷺ who disagreed in matters of ijtihad — independent legal judgment. Those who erred in sincere judgment are excused and may even receive reward for their sincerity, as the hadith states: "When a judge makes ijtihad and reaches the correct conclusion, he gets two rewards; if he errs, he gets one" (Bukhari and Muslim). We do not curse any of the Companions, neither those who fought with Ali nor those who fought against him.

The Kharijites who killed Ali are condemned without reservation. Their ideology — declaring Muslims apostates for political actions and making their blood permissible — is rejected in the strongest terms by every major school of Ahl us-Sunnah.

## His Burial

The location of Ali's burial has been a matter of some historical discussion. The majority of classical Sunni scholars, following the account preserved by Ibn Kathir in al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya, hold that he was buried in the area of Kufa — more specifically in the location now called Najaf al-Ashraf in Iraq. Some accounts suggest the location was deliberately concealed during the turbulent years following his death to prevent desecration of the grave.

Ibn Kathir's account in al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya (Volume 7-8) is the most detailed classical Sunni treatment of Ali's caliphate and death, providing the historical narrative with the theological commentary that the Ahl us-Sunnah position requires.

## The End of the Rightly-Guided Caliphate

With the death of Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him), the era of the Khulafa' al-Rashidun — the Rightly-Guided Caliphs — came to an end. The Prophet ﷺ himself had spoken of this era: "The caliphate after me will be for thirty years; then it will become kingship (mulk)" (Abu Dawud, al-Tirmidhi). The thirty years from Abu Bakr's succession to Ali's martyrdom correspond almost precisely to this prophecy.

The caliphate would continue — and would achieve remarkable things under the Umayyads and Abbasids — but the unique character of those first thirty years, when the caliphs were the direct companions and students of the Prophet ﷺ himself, passed from the world with Ali's death in the darkness of Ramadan.
