# Destruction of the Idols Around the Ka'bah

On 20 Ramadan, 8 AH (11 January 630 CE), following the conquest of Mecca, the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) purified the Ka'bah — the house built by Ibrahim (Abraham) and his son Ismail for the worship of the One God — by destroying the 360 idols that the polytheist Arabs had installed within and around it over the preceding centuries. This act of purification restored the Ka'bah to its original monotheistic purpose.

## The Ka'bah Before the Conquest

The Ka'bah had been corrupted over the centuries from its original function as a house of monotheistic worship. By the time of the Prophet's prophethood, it was surrounded by 360 idols representing the pantheon of Arabian polytheism — tribal deities, ancestral figures, and imported religious icons. The major idols included Hubal, a large red carnelian figure who stood inside the Ka'bah and was the primary deity of Quraysh, and al-Lat, al-Uzzah, and Manat — the three daughters of Allah in the pre-Islamic theology of central Arabia.

## The Prophet's Entry

When the Prophet entered Mecca and went to the Masjid al-Haram, he circumambulated the Ka'bah on his camel without dismounting, touching the Black Stone (al-Hajar al-Aswad) with a stick. The Ka'bah was surrounded by idols.

As he completed the circumambulation, he pointed to each idol in succession with his stick and recited the Quranic verse:

*"And say, 'Truth has come and falsehood has perished; falsehood is ever bound to perish.'"* (Quran 17:81)

With each pointing of the stick, an idol toppled and fell. The accounts describe the Prophet going around the Ka'bah and the idols falling as he pointed to them — each one collapsing forward on its face, as the verse says it would.

## The Clearing of the Interior

After the external idols were destroyed, the Prophet ordered the Ka'bah's interior to be opened. He entered and found paintings and images on the interior walls — including reportedly an image of Ibrahim depicted as an old man drawing lots with divination arrows, and images of Mary and Jesus, among others. He ordered all paintings to be erased and covered his hands over the image of Ibrahim, saying: "May Allah destroy them — they depicted our father Ibrahim as drawing lots with arrows. Ibrahim had nothing to do with such things."

Bilal ibn Rabah and Usamah ibn Zayd accompanied the Prophet inside. He prayed inside the Ka'bah before coming out.

## The Role of Ali ibn Abi Talib

Ali ibn Abi Talib played a specific role in the destruction of the idols on the Ka'bah's exterior. The largest idol, Hubal, was on the roof of the Ka'bah (or mounted on high). The Prophet instructed Ali to stand on his shoulders to reach the idol and topple it. This account, preserved in the seerah literature, has significant symbolic weight: the destroyer of Quraysh's chief idol was mounted on the Prophet's own shoulders.

## The Idols Taken to Medina

Some of the Ka'bah's contents and idols were brought to Medina. The famous statue of Manat, the third of the three daughter-goddesses, had been brought to Medina earlier (the destruction of Manat's statue near al-Mushallal was a separate mission commanded by the Prophet).

The famous Black Stone — the al-Hajar al-Aswad — was preserved. The Black Stone predates Islam and is not itself an idol; Muslims do not worship it or believe it has any divine power. The Prophet maintained the practice of touching or kissing it during tawaf, and Umar ibn al-Khattab's famous statement makes this clear: "I know that you are merely a stone; you can neither harm nor benefit. And had I not seen the Messenger of Allah kiss you, I would not have kissed you." The Stone is a marker and a practice, not an object of worship.

## Theological Significance

**The restoration of monotheism at the Ka'bah.** The Ka'bah in Islamic theology is the first house built on earth for the worship of the One God (Quran 3:96). Its purification was not a new act but a restoration — returning the house to its original purpose after centuries of corruption.

**The Quranic verse as the instrument.** The Prophet's method — pointing and reciting Quran — rather than physical force as the primary means of the idols' fall, is understood in Islamic tradition as a miraculous sign. The idols fell at the Quranic word, not merely at the physical gesture.

**The prohibition on idol worship.** The destruction of the idols permanently ended public idol worship at the Ka'bah. The Quran's prohibition on shirk (associating partners with Allah) was given its most visible earthly expression in this act. The Hajj, from that year onward, was conducted without any polytheist participation.

## Sources

The account is in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Hajj and other chapters), Sahih Muslim, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
