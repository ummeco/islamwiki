# The First Pledge of Aqabah

## Six Men from Yathrib

The years of systematic rejection in Mecca had not produced a new community willing to receive and protect the Prophet ﷺ. The journey to Taif had ended in stones and bleeding feet. Tribal delegations at the pilgrimage season had declined without engagement. But in approximately 620 CE — the eleventh year of prophethood — six men from the Khazraj tribe of Yathrib came to Mecca during the pilgrimage season and encountered the Prophet ﷺ at the well of Zamzam or in the hills near Mina.

They were: Asad ibn Zurara, Awf ibn al-Harith, Rafi ibn Malik, Qutbah ibn Amir, Uqbah ibn Amir, and Jabir ibn Abdullah (in the account of Ibn Hisham and Ibn Kathir — other sources vary slightly in names). They were ordinary men of the Khazraj, not the tribal elite.

The Prophet ﷺ presented the message of Islam to them. Their response was different from any other tribal encounter in the Meccan period. The Khazraj of Yathrib had long-standing relationships with Jewish tribes — the Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza — and had heard from their Jewish neighbors repeated references to an expected prophet who would come and whose arrival would give his followers power over their enemies. When these six men heard the Prophet's ﷺ message, they said to each other: "By Allah, this is the prophet the Jews have been warning us about. Let us not let them reach him before us." They accepted Islam on the spot — six men, from a city the Prophet ﷺ had never visited, whose conversion was triggered in part by Jewish prophecy about a coming messenger.

## The Return to Yathrib

The six men returned to Yathrib and spoke about the Prophet ﷺ throughout the city. By the following year's pilgrimage season, twelve men and two women came from Yathrib — a delegation that represented the beginnings of a community, not just individual converts. This group met the Prophet ﷺ at night in the pass of al-Aqabah, near Mina, in the hills outside Mecca.

They pledged an oath to the Prophet ﷺ on terms described in Ibn Hisham as the "women's pledge" (bay'at al-nisa') — so named because the terms were the same as those later required of women at the Conquest of Mecca. The pledge was not a pledge of war — it was a pledge of faith and conduct:

- Not to associate anything with Allah
- Not to steal
- Not to commit fornication
- Not to kill their children
- Not to bring a false accusation
- Not to disobey the Prophet ﷺ in what is right

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever fulfills this pledge, his reward is with Allah. Whoever falls into any of it and is punished in this world, that is his atonement. Whoever falls into any of it and Allah conceals it, his affair is with Allah — if He wills, He pardons; if He wills, He punishes."

## Musab ibn Umayr: The First Ambassador

After the pledge, the Prophet ﷺ sent Musab ibn Umayr with the Yathrib delegation as the first formally appointed teacher and representative of Islam in the city that would become Medina. Musab was from a wealthy Qurayshi family — before his conversion he was known for his fine dress and expensive perfume — but had accepted Islam early, been imprisoned by his family, escaped, and emerged as one of the most knowledgeable and gifted companions. He was young, eloquent, and possessed of a personal magnetism that the Prophet ﷺ recognized as exactly what the nascent Yathribi community needed.

Musab spent approximately a year in Yathrib, living in the house of Asad ibn Zurara, teaching the Quran, leading prayers, and presenting Islam to household after household. His most significant conversion was Usayd ibn Hudayr and Sa'd ibn Muadh — the two leaders of the Banu Abd al-Ashhal clan — who converted after a single conversation. Sa'd ibn Muadh then went to his clan and told them: "Every man and woman of the Banu Abd al-Ashhal — speaking to you is forbidden until you accept Islam." By evening, every member of the clan had converted. This was not unique; Musab's year in Yathrib produced a transformation in the city's religious landscape that prepared the way for the Hijra.

## What the First Pledge Meant

The First Pledge of Aqabah is the pivot point of the entire seerah between Mecca and Medina. The Prophet ﷺ had been preaching in Mecca for eleven years without finding a willing and capable receiving community. In a single encounter at the well of Zamzam or in the hills near Mina, six men from Yathrib changed that. They went home, spoke, brought twelve more, pledged an oath, and took Musab back with them. A year later they would return with seventy-three men and two women and make the Second Pledge of Aqabah — the pledge of war and full political protection that would make the Hijra possible.

The six men at Zamzam are among the most important individuals in Islamic history precisely because of their obscurity: they had no social standing in Mecca, no relationship with the Prophet ﷺ before that encounter, no special position in Yathrib. They were ordinary people who heard the message, recognized it, accepted it, and carried it home. The transformation that followed was entirely the result of that initial recognition — which the Prophet ﷺ later connected explicitly to the Jewish predictions his neighbors had shared with the Khazraj for generations.

## The Role of Jewish Prophecy

The detail that the six Khazraji men connected the Prophet's ﷺ message to Jewish predictions about a coming prophet is one of the most historically significant elements of this event. The Jewish tribes of Yathrib — who had lived alongside the Aws and Khazraj for generations — had for years warned their Arab neighbors in terms that mixed genuine prophetic tradition with the social threat: "A prophet is about to be sent. When he comes, we will follow him and destroy you as 'Ad and Iram were destroyed." The intent of the Jewish community in making these statements was presumably to assert their own expected religious dominance. The effect was to prepare the Khazraji mind to recognize a prophet when they encountered one.

This is one of the clearest examples in the seerah of divine providence operating through unexpected channels. The Jewish communities of Yathrib, in describing the expected prophet to their Arab neighbors, had — without intending to — created a receptive audience for Islam. When the six men heard the Prophet ﷺ, they said to each other: this is the prophet the Jews have been warning us about. Their conversion was, in part, an act of theological recognition across community lines — a recognition that the traditions of the People of the Book and the message of Islam were pointing toward the same truth.

The First Pledge of Aqabah thus carries within it an irony of history: the Jewish communities of Yathrib, who would later become the most significant source of opposition to the Prophet ﷺ in Medina, had unwittingly prepared the ground for his arrival among their Arab neighbors. Their prophecy had served its purpose — carried the truth forward — in a way they did not intend and would not, in most cases, accept when the moment came.
