# Abu Bakr's Campaign of Liberation: Ransoming the Oppressed

Among the defining acts of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) in the Meccan period was his systematic use of personal wealth to purchase freedom for enslaved Muslims who were being tortured for their faith. This was not an impulsive act of charity but a sustained campaign that continued over years, depleting much of his considerable fortune and drawing criticism even from within his own family. It stands as one of the most consequential uses of private wealth in the history of Islam, and the Quran itself commented on it in verses that have been recited in every generation since.

## The Context: Slavery and Persecution in Mecca

Mecca in the years immediately before the Hijra was a city of extreme social stratification. Slave ownership was woven into the fabric of economic and domestic life, and the enslaved had no legal personhood — they were property, and what their owners did to them was, within Meccan norms, entirely their own affair. When some of these enslaved people embraced Islam, their owners had both a social motive (the new religion threatened tribal hierarchy) and a legal cover (discipline of one's property) to torture them without any check.

The torture of enslaved Muslim converts served a dual purpose: it was meant to compel recantation, and it was meant to signal to the free Arab population what the price of following Muhammad ﷺ might be. The Quraysh understood that if the weak could be seen as willing to suffer for this religion, others might be inspired. By making that suffering extreme and public, they hoped to extinguish inspiration before it spread.

Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) had come to Islam early — by most accounts he was among the first free adult males to accept the faith, and through his personal outreach he brought in several of the most influential early Companions. He was a man of substantial commercial wealth, respected in Mecca as a cloth merchant and a genealogist of note. His social standing gave him a degree of protection that the enslaved converts entirely lacked.

## The Slaves He Freed

The classical sources — primarily Ibn Hisham's Seerah and the biographical dictionaries of Ibn Abd al-Barr and Ibn Hajar — record the names of the enslaved Muslims Abu Bakr purchased and freed. The list, while varying slightly across sources, consistently includes the following individuals.

**Bilal ibn Rabah** (may Allah be pleased with him) was perhaps the most famous of those Abu Bakr freed. He was Abyssinian, owned by Umayya ibn Khalaf, a leading Qurayshi merchant and one of the most determined enemies of Islam. When Bilal's conversion was discovered, Umayya began a torture regimen that has become one of the most cited examples of Meccan persecution: Bilal was taken out in the midday heat, laid on the burning sand or on rocks heated by the sun, and a heavy boulder was placed on his chest to prevent movement. He was beaten and threatened. Through all of it he said only one word: "Ahad" — "One," meaning Allah is One. Abu Bakr purchased him and freed him; the price paid varied in different accounts but the purchase was at a premium because Umayya initially refused to sell, relenting only when he saw that Abu Bakr was determined. Bilal later became the first muezzin of Islam.

**Amir ibn Fuhayrah** was Abu Bakr's own servant, a man described as humble and reliable. He accepted Islam quietly and suffered for it. Abu Bakr freed him as part of his broader campaign rather than keeping him in servitude, which was a choice that had economic implications. Amir ibn Fuhayrah later played a role in the Hijra to Medina, tending Abu Bakr's flock and using the sheep to cover the tracks of the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr as they fled Mecca.

**Zinnirah** was a Roman woman who had been enslaved and then converted to Islam. After Abu Bakr purchased and freed her, she lost her sight — and the Quraysh said this was punishment from their gods al-Lat and al-Uzza for abandoning the faith of the Arabs. Zinnirah said she knew her Lord was capable of restoring her sight, and reportedly her sight was later miraculously restored. Her case became a minor point of contention in Qurayshi polemics against the early Muslims.

**An-Nahdiyya and her daughter** were enslaved by a woman of Banu Abd al-Dar. When Abu Bakr came to purchase them, their owner tried to extract an inflated price. Abu Bakr paid what was asked without bargaining — the freeing of the believers took precedence over commercial calculation.

**Umm Ubays** was tortured by her owners with burning coals placed on her body. She endured this with the same quality of patience that characterized the enslaved Muslims generally — a patience that was not passive acquiescence but active trust in Allah's promise. Abu Bakr purchased her freedom as well.

The accounts of others freed include a man named Muammal, whose identity is less clearly established in the sources, and other unnamed individuals mentioned only in passing. The cumulative financial impact of these purchases was significant. Some accounts suggest Abu Bakr spent close to forty thousand dirhams in this cause, though the precise figure varies across sources. What is agreed upon is that his wealth was substantially diminished.

## His Father's Objection

The tension this campaign created within Abu Bakr's own family is preserved in a narration that appears in Ibn Hisham's Seerah and related sources. Abu Quhafa, Abu Bakr's father, witnessed his son spending money to free enslaved Muslims and confronted him about it. His objection was not cruel — it was the voice of practical tribal thinking. He said to Abu Bakr: "Son, I see you freeing weak people. If you are going to do this, why not buy strong men who could defend you and protect you?"

The logic was entirely coherent within the worldview of pre-Islamic Arabia. Tribal power was built on loyal supporters, and purchasing powerful men would be an investment in Abu Bakr's personal security and status. Freeing weak, foreign, enslaved people gave him nothing by those standards.

Abu Bakr's reply to his father has been preserved as a statement of pure theological clarity: "O my father, I am seeking only what is with Allah." This single sentence encapsulates the revolution in values that Islam represented. The criterion for action was not worldly benefit but divine pleasure. The "weak" people he was freeing were not weak in any sense that mattered to him — they were believers, and their freedom was sought for Allah's sake alone.

## The Quranic Verses About Abu Bakr

The scholars of tafsir — including Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, and al-Qurtubi — are in substantial agreement that Surah al-Layl (92:17-21) was revealed in direct reference to Abu Bakr's spending:

"And the righteous one will avoid it — he who gives his wealth to purify himself, not in return for any favor from anyone, but only seeking the Face of his Lord Most High. And he will surely be satisfied."

The "it" in the opening refers to the Hellfire described in earlier verses of the surah. The one who avoids it is described as giving wealth specifically "to purify himself" — the Arabic is "yatazakka," related to the concept of zakat and spiritual purification. He gives not in expectation of reciprocity ("not in return for any favor") but purely for Allah.

This is a remarkable Quranic endorsement of a specific act in the Prophet's lifetime. The divine description of Abu Bakr's motivation — not for social prestige, not for reciprocal obligation, not for political alliance — but only for the Face of Allah Most High — set a standard for charitable giving that has been cited in every subsequent generation. The scholars note that "seeking the Face of Allah" (ibtighaa wajh Allah) is the highest formulation of intention mentioned in the Quran.

## The Theological Significance of This Spending

The freeing of slaves by Abu Bakr illuminates several principles of Islamic ethics that were being established in this early period.

The first is the sanctity of human freedom. Islam did not abolish slavery overnight — the socioeconomic conditions of seventh-century Arabia made an immediate prohibition impractical and potentially destabilizing. But the early emphasis on freeing slaves as a major act of worship, the legal provisions for manumission as expiation for various sins, and the social status accorded to those who freed slaves all pointed in a clear direction. Abu Bakr was not doing something merely praiseworthy — he was enacting the fundamental Islamic principle that a human being's dignity is not contingent on their social position or legal status.

The second is the relationship between wealth and spiritual purification. The Quran describes Abu Bakr's spending as an act of "purification" — the same root word used for zakat. This frames material wealth not as a possession but as a trust, and its proper deployment in the service of the oppressed as a form of cleansing the soul from the spiritual dangers of attachment to worldly goods.

The third is the demonstration that faith requires material expression. Abu Bakr's belief was not a private sentiment held carefully away from the world — it produced action, and that action had costs. The scholars of usul al-fiqh note that among the levels of prohibiting evil (the famous hadith: change it with the hand, or with the tongue, or consider it evil in the heart) — Abu Bakr exercised the most powerful form, taking direct action to remove the oppression.

## The Context Among Other Freeing Acts

Abu Bakr was not the only one who purchased freedom for Muslim slaves, but he was the most systematic and the most prolific. Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) also freed slaves in the early period, as did others with means. But the accounts consistently single out Abu Bakr for the scale of his campaign and the principle he articulated — that the weak believers were worth more to him than strong pagans.

This preference for the weak believer over the powerful pagan reflected a complete inversion of Meccan values. In the tribal world of Arabia, strength, lineage, and wealth determined value. Abu Bakr's actions said something different: the person with the most value is the one with the most sincere faith, regardless of whether they can offer anything in return.

## His Wealth and Its Subsequent Depletion

The historical sources suggest Abu Bakr came to Islam with approximately forty thousand dirhams and emigrated to Medina with approximately five thousand — the bulk having been spent on freeing slaves and supporting the early Muslim community in Mecca. By the time of the Hijra, his commercial wealth had been dramatically reduced, though his social standing as a close companion of the Prophet ﷺ and his intimate involvement in the new community meant he remained a figure of significance.

The Prophet ﷺ acknowledged Abu Bakr's spending explicitly in a hadith reported in Sahih al-Bukhari: "No one's wealth has benefited me as much as Abu Bakr's wealth." This statement — which Abu Bakr received with characteristic humility, saying that he and his wealth belonged entirely to Allah and His Messenger — confirmed the exceptional nature of his generosity in the cause.

## His Legacy

Abu Bakr's campaign of liberation in Mecca stands as a founding act of Islamic civil society: the use of private wealth in the service of the oppressed, motivated purely by faith. In subsequent Islamic jurisprudence, the freeing of slaves was established as one of the most meritorious acts, mentioned in the Quran as expiation for various sins and as a pathway to divine reward. The early example of Abu Bakr gave this teaching its most vivid historical instantiation.

The scholars of Islamic history consistently cite this period as evidence that Abu Bakr's support for the Prophet ﷺ was not merely verbal but material — he put his wealth, his social standing, and ultimately his safety on the line for the faith he had accepted. His reply to his father — "I am seeking only what is with Allah" — remained the defining statement of his motivation.
