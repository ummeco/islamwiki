# Delegation of Jarir ibn Abdullah al-Bajali

The delegation of Jarir ibn Abdullah al-Bajali to Medina in 10 AH was part of the Year of Delegations (Am al-Wufud) — the period in which tribal leaders from across Arabia came to the Prophet to declare their tribes' acceptance of Islam and to establish the terms of their political integration into the Muslim polity.

## Jarir ibn Abdullah

Jarir ibn Abdullah al-Bajali was the chief of the Bajilah tribe — one of the larger tribal confederations of the Hijaz and Yemen region. He was known for his physical beauty: he was described as extraordinarily handsome, and the Prophet reportedly said of him that his face recalled the face of Yusuf (Joseph, peace be upon him). He was also a gifted speaker and tribal leader.

## The Delegation's Arrival and Acceptance of Islam

Jarir came to Medina with a delegation from his tribe. He accepted Islam in Ramadan 10 AH — shortly before or during the Farewell Hajj season. He pledged allegiance to the Prophet and the tribe of Bajilah entered Islam.

## The Mission Against Dhul Khalasah

After accepting Islam, Jarir was given a mission. One of the surviving centers of polytheist worship in the Arabian Peninsula was a temple in Yemen called Dhul Khalasah — sometimes called "the Yemeni Ka'bah." It was an idol-house with significant regional prestige, and its continued existence represented a challenge to the consolidation of monotheism across Arabia.

The Prophet asked Jarir: "Will you relieve me from Dhul Khalasah?" Jarir said yes, though he noted that he was not a confident horseman. The Prophet struck him on the chest and said: "O Allah, make him firm and make him a guide who is well-guided."

Jarir took 150 horsemen from the Bajilah and Ahmas tribes, rode to Dhul Khalasah, destroyed the temple, and burned it. He then sent the news to the Prophet: "By the One who sent you with the truth, I did not leave until it was like a mange-ridden camel."

The Prophet praised the Ahmas tribal contingent for their courage and prayed for them five times.

## Jarir's Relationship with the Prophet

The accounts suggest a warm personal relationship between Jarir and the Prophet. Jarir narrated several hadith from the Prophet directly, and the Prophet treated him with particular warmth. One famous narration: Jarir said "I never saw the Prophet since I accepted Islam but that he smiled at me."

This statement — that the Prophet consistently greeted Jarir with a smile whenever he saw him — is cited by Islamic scholars in discussions of the prophetic character: the deliberate cultivation of warmth in individual relationships, giving each person the sense of being genuinely welcomed.

## Jarir's Later Career

Jarir ibn Abdullah continued as a Muslim after the Prophet's death and participated in the early Islamic conquests. He was in Syria during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab and later participated in the conquest of Iraq. He died approximately in 51-54 AH.

He is remembered as one of the noble Arab chiefs who brought their people into Islam with dignity and as one of the companions who personally received the Prophet's mission to destroy the remnants of polytheism in Arabia.

## The Destruction of Pre-Islamic Shrines

The mission against Dhul Khalasah was part of a systematic policy in the final years of the Prophet's life: the elimination of the major polytheist religious centers across Arabia. Similar missions were conducted against the shrines of al-Lat (at Ta'if — destroyed after the Thaqif accepted Islam), al-Uzzah (destroyed by Khalid ibn al-Walid in 8 AH), Manat (destroyed by Ali ibn Abi Talib in 8 AH), and other local shrines.

This systematic destruction of polytheist worship centers was understood as the physical implementation of the Quranic command to establish monotheism across Arabia and to remove the institutional infrastructure of idolatry.

## Sources

The delegation of Jarir ibn Abdullah al-Bajali and the Dhul Khalasah mission are recorded in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Jihad and Kitab al-Maghazi) and Sahih Muslim, making these among the most reliably documented accounts of the Year of Delegations.
