# The Buwayhids in Baghdad: When Shia Commanders Ruled a Sunni Caliph

## The Decline of Abbasid Power and the Rise of Military Dynasties

The entry of Ahmad ibn Buwayhih into Baghdad in 334 AH / 945 CE, placing the Abbasid Caliph al-Mustakfi under effective house arrest and seizing control of the imperial capital, was not a sudden catastrophe but the culmination of a century-long process of Abbasid political disintegration. Understanding the Buwayhid period requires understanding this process and its causes.

The Abbasid Caliphate, which had reached its zenith under Harun al-Rashid and al-Ma'mun in the late second and early third century AH, was undermined from within by a structural problem that no dynasty had yet solved: how to prevent the military commanders on whom the central authority depended from becoming independent powers in their own right. The Abbasids, like the late Roman empire before them, had solved the problem of an army that was too closely tied to a single ethnic group (Arab) by recruiting from Persians, then from Central Asian Turks. The Turkish military commanders who were brought in to serve the Caliph became, over several generations, the real military force in Baghdad — and real military force translates, with predictable regularity, into real political power.

By the late ninth and early tenth century, Abbasid Caliphs were sometimes reigning for months or even weeks before being blinded and deposed by Turkish commanders who preferred a different candidate. The institution of the caliphate retained its religious legitimacy — the khutba was still read in the Caliph's name, coins were still minted with his title — but effective political and military control had passed to the commanders who surrounded him.

The Buyids (Buwayhids) entered this already-chaotic situation from the west of the Caspian Sea — not as outsiders invading a stable empire but as one among several regional powers competing to control the fragments of Abbasid authority.

## The Buwayhid Origins: Daylamites from the Caspian Mountains

The Buwayhid dynasty takes its name from Buwayhih (or Buyah), the father of the three brothers who founded it: Ali (Imad al-Dawla), Ahmad (Mu'izz al-Dawla), and Hasan (Rukn al-Dawla). They were Daylamites — a people from the mountainous Daylam region on the southwestern shore of the Caspian Sea (modern Gilan province in Iran). The Daylamites were known throughout the medieval Islamic period as fierce mountain warriors who had been among the last peoples in the region to convert to Islam; their conversion was relatively recent, occurring in the ninth century, and their Islam was culturally layered over older Caspian traditions.

The Buwayhids were Shia in their religious orientation — specifically Imami (Twelver) Shia, following the line of twelve Imams descending from Ali ibn Abi Talib. This theological position had significant implications for the dynasty's relationship with the Sunni Abbasid Caliphs they would come to dominate, and for their impact on the religious life of the cities they controlled.

The Buwayhid rise to power was rapid. The three brothers carved out their initial territories in the 320s AH, with Ali taking Fars (centered on Shiraz), Ahmad taking Khuzestan and southern Mesopotamia, and Hasan controlling the Jibal region (Media). By 334 AH, Ahmad had accumulated sufficient power and found the moment propitious: the Abbasid Caliph al-Mustakfi was weak, the Turkish commanders in Baghdad were factionally divided, and Ahmad's forces were large enough to enter the capital without serious resistance.

## The Entry into Baghdad: 334 AH / 945 CE

Ahmad ibn Buwayhih entered Baghdad in Jumada al-Ula, 334 AH. The Caliph al-Mustakfi received him initially with the ceremonial honors accorded a powerful military supporter, apparently hoping to use the Buwayhid arrival to rebalance the power struggle among his existing Turkish commanders. This calculation, if it was one, failed immediately. Within days, Ahmad had the Caliph blinded — the standard caliphal incapacitation — and replaced him with a puppet Caliph, al-Muti'. Ahmad then assumed the title Amir al-Umara (Commander of Commanders), formalizing a position that had existed informally: the effective ruler of the empire.

The political paradox of the next century was striking: a Shia dynasty from the Caspian mountains was now the actual governing power in the seat of the Sunni Abbasid Caliphate, using the Caliph as a legitimizing figurehead while directing all actual policy. The Abbasid Caliph continued to sit on the ceremonial throne, receive foreign embassies, issue formal documents, and have his name recited in the Friday khutba — but he did so at the sufferance of the Buwayhid Amir, who could replace him whenever he chose.

## Shia Practices in a Sunni Capital: The Impact on Baghdad

The Buwayhid occupation of Baghdad had immediate and visible effects on the religious life of the city. For the first time, Shia observances that had previously been either private or subdued became matters of public civic expression.

The commemoration of the tenth of Muharram — Ashura, the day of Husayn ibn Ali's martyrdom at Karbala — was organized as a public civic mourning event in Baghdad under Buwayhid patronage. Large processions, public lamentation, the closing of markets, and public expression of grief for Husayn were organized on a scale that had not previously been possible. The Ghadir Khumm celebration — marking the day when the Prophet ﷺ, according to Shia belief, had designated Ali ibn Abi Talib as his successor — was similarly organized as a public festival.

The effect on the Sunni population of Baghdad, which was the majority, was complex. Some accounts describe tension and episodes of sectarian conflict between Sunni and Shia neighborhoods. The Karkh district of Baghdad had a predominantly Shia population; the east bank was predominantly Sunni. Contemporary accounts in the chronicles of Ibn Kathir and others describe episodes of rioting and violence around the major Shia commemorations, particularly Ashura.

Individual Sunni scholars were sometimes pressured or found their activities constrained under Buwayhid rule. The promotion of scholars who endorsed Shia theological positions, and the public funding of Shia commemorations, represented a shift in the civic environment that the Sunni scholarly establishment had to navigate carefully.

## The Buwayhid Paradox: Patrons of Sunni Scholarship

Yet the Buwayhid period is also, paradoxically, one of the most intellectually productive in the history of Sunni Islamic scholarship. The political structure of Buwayhid rule — multiple competing emirs, no single overwhelming authority, relative freedom of movement for scholars, ongoing need for legitimacy — created conditions that major Sunni scholars used to produce foundational works.

Al-Baqillani (338-403 AH), the greatest early Ash'ari theologian after al-Ash'ari himself, flourished under Buwayhid rule in Baghdad. He served as a court scholar and diplomat for the Buwayhids while producing the most systematic formulations of Ash'ari theology up to his time. The Buwayhids valued scholars as markers of civilized legitimacy; a court that patronized major scholars was a court that claimed to be doing more than wielding raw military power.

The great literary scholar al-Tawhidi (c. 310-414 AH), one of the most brilliant stylists in Arabic literary history, lived and worked in this period. Ibn Abid Dunya (208-281 AH) had already died, but his works on Islamic ethics and piety were widely circulated in this period. The Hanafi legal tradition continued to produce major scholars; the Shafi'i tradition flourished with figures like Ibn Abi al-Fawaris.

The Buwayhid Vizier Sahib ibn Abbad was himself a major literary scholar and patron — a Shia in theology but a supporter of Arabic literary culture and scholarship without sectarian restriction. The cultural flowering of the Buwayhid period in literature, philosophy, and medicine reflects a patronage environment that was, despite its theological orientation, broadly supportive of the intellectual tradition as a whole.

## The Sunni Scholarly Response: Independence and Continuity

The most important response of the Sunni scholarly tradition to the Buwayhid century was simple continuity: scholars continued to teach, transmit hadith, write legal texts and theological refutations, and maintain the institutions of Sunni learning with a degree of independence from the political environment that characterized the best of the Islamic scholarly tradition.

The great hadith scholars of this period — al-Daraqutni (306-385 AH), Ibn 'Adi (277-365 AH), al-Ismaili (277-371 AH) — continued their critical examination and transmission of hadith without reference to whether the political authorities above them were Sunni or Shia. The hadith sciences had their own internal standards, their own institutions (mosques, private study circles, the ijaza system of transmission), and their own community of practitioners. Political disruption at the top did not halt the transmission of prophetic tradition at the level of the scholars.

This pattern — the intellectual and spiritual independence of the Sunni scholarly community from the political vicissitudes of the caliphate — is one of the most important structural features of Islamic civilization's resilience. The Mongol destruction of Baghdad in 656 AH, a century and a half after the Buwayhids, was catastrophic in a way the Buwayhid occupation was not precisely because it eliminated the physical infrastructure of scholarship alongside the political structures. The Buwayhid occupation, for all its disruption, left the mosques standing, the scholars teaching, and the books being copied.

## The Seljuk Restoration: 447 AH / 1055 CE

The Buwayhid period in Baghdad came to an end in 447 AH / 1055 CE when the Seljuk Turk Tughrul Beg entered Baghdad and expelled the last Buwayhid Amir, restoring effective Sunni governance to the seat of the caliphate for the first time in over a century. The Abbasid Caliph al-Qa'im welcomed the Seljuks explicitly as deliverers of Sunni orthodoxy from Shia dominion.

The Seljuks — themselves Hanafi Sunnis — moved quickly to reinstate public Sunni religious observance, end the state patronage of Shia commemorations in Baghdad, and establish Sunni educational institutions (madrasas) under the leadership of the brilliant Nizam al-Mulk. The Nizamiyya madrasas that Nizam al-Mulk founded across the eastern caliphate, including the famous Nizamiyya of Baghdad, became the first state-funded institutions of higher Islamic learning and transformed the landscape of Sunni scholarship.

The Buwayhid century, viewed from the perspective of Islamic intellectual history, was a period in which the Sunni scholarly tradition demonstrated its capacity for institutional independence and intellectual vitality regardless of who held political power. The tradition proved more durable than the dynasty.

## Theological Reflection: Authority, Legitimacy, and Resilience

The Prophet ﷺ said: "There will not cease to be a group from my community manifestly upon the truth, not harmed by those who oppose them, until the Command of Allah comes." The Buwayhid period is historical evidence for the truth of this promise. Despite a century of Shia political dominance in the Abbasid capital, despite the introduction of Shia civic observances, despite pressures on individual Sunni scholars, the Sunni scholarly tradition not only survived but flourished — producing theological, legal, and hadith scholarship of the first rank.

The lesson is not that political power is irrelevant to religious life — it clearly affected individuals and communities in real ways. The lesson is that the preservation of Islamic knowledge is ultimately the responsibility of the scholars and their communities, and that this responsibility does not depend on having coreligionists in political authority. The Quran's promise that Allah would preserve the reminder He sent is not a promise about political outcomes but about the survival of the knowledge itself: "Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder, and indeed, We will be its guardian" (15:9).
