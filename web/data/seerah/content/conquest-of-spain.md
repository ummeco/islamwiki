# Muslim Conquest of Spain (Al-Andalus)

The Muslim conquest of the Iberian Peninsula began in 92 AH (711 CE) when Tariq ibn Ziyad, a Berber commander under the Umayyad governor of North Africa Musa ibn Nusayr, led an army across the Strait of Gibraltar to the southern coast of Spain. This campaign initiated nearly eight centuries of Muslim presence in the Iberian Peninsula, during which al-Andalus became one of the most brilliant civilizations of the medieval world.

## Background

The Visigothic Kingdom that ruled Spain was plagued by internal divisions. King Roderic (Rodrigo) had seized the throne by force, alienating the supporters of the previous royal family. Julian, the governor of Ceuta (a Visigothic outpost in North Africa), invited the Muslims to intervene, either out of personal grievance against Roderic or political calculation.

The Umayyad Caliphate, which had already conquered North Africa, saw an opportunity to extend Islamic rule into Europe.

## The Battle of Guadalete

Tariq ibn Ziyad crossed the strait with approximately 7,000 soldiers, landing at the rock that still bears his name: Jabal Tariq (Gibraltar, from Jabal Tariq). Reinforcements brought his force to approximately 12,000.

The decisive Battle of Guadalete (also known as the Battle of Wadi Lakka) took place in Ramadan 92 AH (July 711 CE). King Roderic assembled an army estimated at 25,000 to 100,000, but a significant portion of his nobles defected during the battle, either due to prior arrangements or loss of confidence. Roderic was killed, and the Visigothic army was destroyed.

Tariq's famous speech to his troops before the battle is one of the most quoted in Islamic military history: "The sea is behind you and the enemy before you. Where can you flee? By Allah, you have nothing but your swords and your resolve."

## Rapid Expansion

Following the victory at Guadalete, Tariq advanced rapidly northward, capturing Toledo, the Visigothic capital, and much of central Spain. Musa ibn Nusayr followed with a larger army the following year, taking Seville, Merida, and other major cities.

Within seven years, virtually the entire Iberian Peninsula was under Muslim control, with only small Christian pockets surviving in the mountainous regions of the far north (Asturias). The conquest was remarkably swift, facilitated by:

- The collapse of the Visigothic state after Roderic's defeat
- Agreements with local populations, particularly Jews who had suffered persecution under Visigothic rule
- Relatively lenient treatment of Christian populations who submitted peacefully

## The Golden Age of Al-Andalus

Under Muslim rule, particularly during the Umayyad Emirate and Caliphate of Cordoba (138-422 AH / 756-1031 CE), al-Andalus became a beacon of learning and culture. Cordoba rivaled Baghdad and Constantinople as one of the great cities of the world:

- The Great Mosque of Cordoba became one of the masterpieces of Islamic architecture
- Libraries in Cordoba held hundreds of thousands of volumes when most European monasteries had a few dozen
- Scholars translated Greek philosophical and scientific works into Arabic, preserving and advancing knowledge that would later spark the European Renaissance
- Agriculture flourished with sophisticated irrigation systems and the introduction of new crops
- Jewish, Christian, and Muslim scholars collaborated in an atmosphere of relative tolerance known as *convivencia*

## Legacy

The Muslim conquest of Spain and the civilization it produced left an indelible mark on European culture. Arabic loan words permeate the Spanish language (algebra, alcove, algorithm), and the architectural heritage of al-Andalus, from the Alhambra of Granada to the Mezquita of Cordoba, remains among the finest artistic achievements in human history.

The Muslim presence in the Iberian Peninsula would last until the fall of Granada in 897 AH (1492 CE), when the last Muslim ruler, Muhammad XII (Boabdil), surrendered to Ferdinand and Isabella.
