# Al-Biruni's Study of India

Abu Rayhan Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Biruni (362-440 AH / 973-1048 CE) was one of the greatest polymaths of the Islamic golden age. His monumental work *Kitab Tahqiq ma lil-Hind* (Book Verifying What Is Said about India), written around 421 AH (1030 CE), stands as the first truly scientific study of another civilization in the history of world scholarship. It represents a landmark of comparative religious and cultural study, combining linguistic mastery, philosophical depth, and rigorous intellectual honesty.

## Life

Al-Biruni was born in Khwarazm (present-day Uzbekistan) in 362 AH (973 CE). He was a contemporary of Ibn Sina (Avicenna), with whom he engaged in famous philosophical correspondence. Al-Biruni mastered mathematics, astronomy, physics, medicine, pharmacology, history, chronology, and linguistics over a lifetime of dedicated study.

His life was significantly shaped by the political upheavals of his time. When Sultan Mahmud of Ghazna conquered Khwarazm in 408 AH (1017 CE), al-Biruni was among the scholars brought — possibly not entirely voluntarily — to the Ghaznavid court. Mahmud's repeated campaigns into the Indian subcontinent gave al-Biruni an extraordinary opportunity: he accompanied or followed these expeditions and spent over a decade living in, studying, and learning to understand India.

## The Method

What distinguished al-Biruni from earlier Arab and Muslim writers on India was his systematic methodology. He did not rely on hearsay, travelers' tales, or hostile polemics. He learned Sanskrit and read Hindu religious, philosophical, and scientific texts in the original language. He engaged directly with Hindu scholars, asking questions and checking his understanding. He compared what he found in India to analogous concepts in Greek philosophy, Persian tradition, and Islamic thought.

He explicitly stated his methodological principles at the beginning of the work: he would describe Hindu beliefs and practices as they actually were, without distortion for polemical purposes, and he would allow readers to compare for themselves.

## Contents of the Work

The *Tahqiq ma lil-Hind* covers an enormous range of topics:

**Religion and Philosophy.** Al-Biruni gave detailed, respectful accounts of Hindu cosmology, the concepts of Brahman, Atman, karma, and transmigration of souls, and the philosophical schools of classical Indian thought. He compared Hindu philosophical monism to Sufi concepts of divine unity and to the Greek philosophy of Plotinus, showing both similarities and differences without passing judgment on their truth.

**Science.** He described Hindu mathematics, including the decimal number system and the concept of zero (which had entered Islamic mathematical tradition via India a century earlier). He engaged seriously with Indian astronomy and compared it to the Ptolemaic system used in Islamic astronomy. He also discussed Indian medicine, the system of Ayurveda.

**Geography.** Al-Biruni provided the most accurate geographical description of India available in the medieval world, including a sophisticated analysis of the curvature of the earth and its mathematical implications for geography.

**Chronology.** He described the complex Hindu calendar systems and historical chronologies, comparing them to Islamic and other chronological systems.

**Social Structure.** He described the caste system with anthropological precision, noting its analogies to social stratification in other societies without endorsing it.

## Intellectual Honesty

What is most remarkable about al-Biruni's work is his intellectual honesty. He acknowledged the limitations of his knowledge. He noted when he was uncertain or when Hindu scholars disagreed among themselves. He distinguished between popular practice and philosophical doctrine. He criticized tendencies toward distortion on both sides — Muslim writers who demonized Hinduism without understanding it, and Indian insularity that refused to share knowledge with outsiders.

In one famous passage, he noted that Hindus tended to assume that whatever exists in India is uniquely excellent and that foreigners have nothing of comparable value — a form of cultural arrogance he also observed in other peoples.

He wrote: "I shall place before the reader the theories of the Hindus exactly as they are, and I shall mention in connection with them similar theories of the Greeks in order to show the relationship existing between them."

## Significance

The *Tahqiq ma lil-Hind* is recognized today as a pioneering work in the history of comparative religion, cultural anthropology, and the sociology of knowledge. It was centuries ahead of its time in its methodological sophistication and its commitment to understanding another culture on its own terms.

Al-Biruni wrote over 150 works across multiple disciplines. His *al-Qanun al-Masudi* on mathematical astronomy, his *al-Athar al-Baqiya* on historical chronology, and his *Kitab al-Saydana* on pharmacology are all major scholarly achievements. He died in Ghazna (present-day Afghanistan) around 440 AH (1048 CE), aged approximately 75.

He is remembered as one of the greatest intellectuals produced by Islamic civilization, a figure whose scope of curiosity and rigor of method have rarely been matched in any tradition.
