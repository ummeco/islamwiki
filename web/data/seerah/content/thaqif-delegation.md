# The Delegation of Thaqif

## The Last Holdouts Come In

The Thaqif of Taif had been among the most implacable opponents of the Muslim community throughout the Medinan period. They had pelted the Prophet ﷺ with stones and driven him from their city in the darkest year of his mission. They had been major participants in the Hawazin-Thaqif confederacy at Hunayn. They had withstood the Muslim siege of Taif in Shawwal 8 AH and watched the Muslim army withdraw without taking the city. After Hunayn and the siege, Taif was isolated: its allies destroyed, the surrounding territory Muslim, trade cut off. But the city had not surrendered.

The Thaqif delegation arrived in Ramadan 9 AH — nearly a year after the siege. The Prophet ﷺ had established a tent for receiving delegations near the mosque, and he received the Thaqif there. The negotiation that followed is remarkable for what the Thaqif asked and what they were refused, and for the Prophet's ﷺ patience in refusing.

## The Negotiation

The Thaqif initially asked to be exempted from the requirement to destroy their idol al-Lat, which was the central object of Thaqifi religious life and a source of tribal pride and income from the pilgrims it attracted. They asked for three years — enough time, they said, for older tribal members to come to terms with the change. The Prophet ﷺ refused. They asked for two years, then one year, then one month. Each request was refused. They asked to be exempt from destroying al-Lat themselves — that the Prophet ﷺ send someone to do it. This too was refused: they would have to participate in its destruction.

They then asked to be exempted from the five daily prayers, arguing that their people were not ready. The Prophet ﷺ refused with one of the most direct statements in the seerah: "There is no good in a religion without prayer." They asked to be exempt from zakat. Refused. They asked to keep their weapons and not have to go on military campaigns. Refused. They asked for a variety of concessions that went to the core obligations of Islamic practice, and each was refused.

What they were granted: the Prophet ﷺ sent Abu Sufyan and Mughirah ibn Shu'ba to Taif to destroy al-Lat. He also agreed to appoint a man from among the Thaqif themselves as their local leader — a recognition of their standing and capability within their own community.

## The Destruction of al-Lat

The destruction of al-Lat was an event of enormous significance for the Thaqif. The idol had been the center of Thaqifi religious and economic life; pilgrims came to it from across the region, and the revenue it generated was substantial. Mughirah ibn Shu'ba was tasked with striking the idol with an axe. Some accounts record that Thaqifi men fainted when the first blows fell — such was the hold the idol had on the community's imagination. But the idol was demolished, and the custodians who had served it were shown — visibly, publicly — that it could not protect itself.

A hadith preserved in the accounts records that when Mughirah prepared to strike, a woman of the Thaqif wailed in grief. The scene of the idol's destruction — in the city where the Prophet ﷺ had been beaten and driven away years earlier — was the visual confirmation that the seerah's arc had reversed completely. The city that had rejected the Prophet ﷺ had come to him, on his terms, with no exemptions, and the idol that had defined its religious life was demolished at his instruction.

## The Significance for the Seerah

The Thaqif delegation is the final chapter of the Taif narrative that had begun with the most painful rejection of the Prophet's ﷺ prophetic mission. Taif was the city where he had gone after the death of Khadijah and Abu Talib — already bereft and isolated — and had been pelted until blood ran to his ankles, the Angel Jibril coming with the mountains and asking for permission to crush the city between them. The Prophet ﷺ had said: I hope Allah will bring from their offspring people who worship Allah alone. The Thaqif delegation in Ramadan 9 AH was that hope's fulfillment — the descendants of those who had thrown stones becoming the people who accepted Islam and destroyed their own idol.

The negotiation's outcome — no concessions on any essential obligation — is also studied in Islamic jurisprudence as establishing that the foundational requirements of Islam cannot be negotiated away on the basis of tribal culture, economic interest, or the convenience of converts. The Prophet ﷺ was generous in many ways — he honored the Thaqif's standing, appointed one of their own as leader, and received them with patience through extended negotiation. But the obligations of prayer, zakat, and the removal of shirk were non-negotiable. This principle has governed Islamic da'wah practice ever since.

## The Thaqif After Submission

After the Thaqif accepted Islam, they became among the more active participants in the Muslim community's subsequent history. Mughirah ibn Shu'ba — the companion who had struck al-Lat — became one of the significant figures of the early caliphate period, serving as governor of Kufa and Bahrain. Uthman ibn Abi al-As al-Thaqafi, whom the Prophet ﷺ appointed as the Thaqif's teacher and leader, became one of the commanders in the early Islamic expansion. The Thaqif's military capability — which had made the siege of Taif so difficult — was redirected into the service of the community they had once opposed. The city that had driven the Prophet ﷺ away became, within a generation, one of the productive centers of early Islamic civilization. The story of the Thaqif is the seerah's most complete account of what happens when patient da'wah, strategic restraint, and divine timing work together — from the bleeding feet at the gates of Taif to the voluntary submission and the demolished idol. The prayer that he had made outside the walls of Taif during the siege — "O Allah, guide the Thaqif and bring them to us" — was answered in every sense.
