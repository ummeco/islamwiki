# Ibn Battuta: The Greatest Traveler in History

## A Hajj That Never Ended

Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Abdullah ibn Muhammad ibn Ibrahim al-Lawati al-Tanji — known to history as Ibn Battuta — was born in Tangier, Morocco on 17 Rajab 703 AH (February 25, 1304 CE). He left Tangier in the summer of 725 AH (June 1325 CE) to perform hajj. He did not return home for 24 years.

In those 24 years, and in subsequent journeys that extended his travels to nearly 29 years total, Ibn Battuta covered approximately 120,000 kilometers — five times the distance traveled by Marco Polo, the European traveler most often cited as a comparable figure. He crossed deserts, seas, and mountains. He served as a judge, a diplomat, and an ambassador. He survived shipwrecks, robbery, civil wars, and the Black Death. He married multiple wives in multiple countries and fathered children he largely left behind. He visited the courts of rulers who ranged from the Mamluk Sultans of Egypt to the Emperor of Mali to the Khan of the Golden Horde to the Sultan of the Delhi Sultanate to the Emperor of China.

His account of these travels, the *Rihla* (Journey), dictated to the scholar Ibn Juzayy in Morocco upon his final return and completed around 756 AH (1355 CE), is one of the most important historical documents produced by any civilization in the medieval period — an incomparable eyewitness description of the 14th-century Islamic world and the cultures it touched.

## The Scholar-Traveler: Formation and Method

Ibn Battuta came from a family of Maliki jurists in Tangier. He received a traditional Islamic education in Arabic language, Quran, hadith, and Maliki fiqh. When he departed for hajj at age 21, he was a trained (if inexperienced) jurist. The hajj itself was a formative experience: he traveled from Morocco through North Africa, Egypt, Syria, and Arabia — his first encounters with the breadth of the Islamic world.

Throughout his travels, his method was consistent. He sought out scholars, saints, and rulers. He attended lectures, obtained *ijazas* (scholarly permissions to transmit texts), and collected books. He observed and recorded religious practices, comparing what he saw to the Maliki standards he had been trained in and noting both praiseworthy conformity and departures from what he considered proper Islamic practice.

This critical dimension is important: Ibn Battuta was not a passive tourist. He was a trained jurist who assessed what he saw against Islamic legal and moral standards. When he found practices he considered improper — women appearing unveiled in Mali, certain customs in the Maldives, specific ceremonial forms in various courts — he said so, often at some personal inconvenience. This gives his account a scholarly reliability that more purely enthusiastic travel narratives lack.

## Egypt and the First East: 725-732 AH

Leaving Morocco, Ibn Battuta traveled through Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya before entering Egypt — a journey that already involved thousands of kilometers. In Egypt, he found the most prosperous and intellectually sophisticated Islamic state of his era: the Mamluk Sultanate at the height of its cultural and political power under al-Nasir Muhammad ibn Qalawun.

In Egypt he performed prayers at al-Azhar mosque, met scholars including the great Sufi shaykh Ibrahim al-Murshidi in the Fayyum region (whom he describes with deep reverence), and was struck by the agricultural wealth of the Nile Delta. His description of Cairo is one of the best we have from any source for the 14th century: a city of extraordinary density, commerce, and learning.

From Egypt he went to Syria, performing pilgrimage in Jerusalem and visiting Damascus — which he would later return to as a resident. He performed hajj in Arabia, visited Medina, and then, rather than returning home, continued east to Iraq and Persia.

## The Broad Islamic World

Over the following years, Ibn Battuta's travels covered the full breadth of the Islamic world as it existed in the early 8th century AH. A summary of his routes conveys the scope:

**East Africa (729-730 AH / 1328-1330 CE)**: He sailed down the East African coast as far as Kilwa (in modern Tanzania), visiting Mogadishu (which he describes as a prosperous Muslim city with a sultan), Mombasa, and the Swahili coast. His account of the East African Muslim city-states is among the earliest detailed Arabic description of this civilization.

**Central Asian Steppes and Constantinople (732-733 AH / 1332-1333 CE)**: He traveled through the Golden Horde territory (modern Ukraine/Russia), visiting the court of Sultan Öz Beg Khan — a devout Muslim whose Islamic state on the Central Asian steppes he describes in remarkable detail. He accompanied one of Öz Beg's wives to visit her father in Constantinople, making him one of the very few medieval Muslim travelers to visit the Byzantine capital before its fall to the Ottomans (857 AH / 1453 CE). His description of Constantinople — the great Hagia Sophia church, the wealth of the Byzantine court, the size and density of the city — is an invaluable historical source.

**India and the Delhi Sultanate (734-746 AH / 1334-1346 CE)**: The longest phase of his journey was spent in India. He was appointed as qadi (Islamic judge) at the court of the Tughlaq Sultan Muhammad ibn Tughlaq in Delhi — a position he held for approximately eight years. His account of the Delhi Sultanate, its ruler, its scholars, the Sufi orders of India, and the diversity of Indian society is one of the richest portions of his *Rihla*. He describes Muhammad ibn Tughlaq as a man of contradictions: generous and learned in some respects, unpredictable and given to severe punishments in others. His service as a qadi in Delhi gave him direct experience of how Islamic law operated in the Indian context.

**The Maldive Islands (746-747 AH / 1345-1346 CE)**: Ibn Battuta was appointed qadi in the Maldives and served there for approximately 18 months. His account of this island Muslim sultanate is the most detailed description of the Maldives in any medieval source. He found a thoroughly Islamic society in terms of identity but with certain practices he considered departures from proper Islamic standards — particularly regarding women's dress, which he attempted (with limited success) to reform through legal rulings.

**Southeast Asia and China (747-749 AH / 1346-1348 CE)**: He visited Sumatra (stopping at Samudera-Pasai, which he describes as a flourishing Islamic sultanate), the Malay Peninsula, and eventually reached China, where he visited Quanzhou, Hangzhou, and Beijing. His account of China is more problematic than other portions of his travels — scholars have debated how much of it is firsthand observation versus interpolated material from other sources — but it contains identifiable accurate elements alongside passages of uncertain origin.

## The Encounter with Ibn Taymiyyah

Among the brief but historically interesting passages in Ibn Battuta's *Rihla* is his account of a fleeting encounter with Ibn Taymiyyah in Damascus. He reports that he saw Ibn Taymiyyah addressing people at the Umayyad Mosque and that the great scholar said something that Ibn Battuta apparently considered theologically problematic — the account is brief and its interpretation has been debated by scholars. What is notable is that Ibn Battuta was in Damascus during the period when Ibn Taymiyyah was active there, confirming the temporal overlap, though the theological disagreement implied in the passage (Ibn Battuta was Ash'ari in his aqeedah, Ibn Taymiyyah was Athari) reflects the genuine scholarly tension of the era.

## West Africa: 752-754 AH / 1352-1353 CE

One of the most significant portions of Ibn Battuta's travels, and one of the most important historical sources we have for medieval West Africa, is his account of his journey to the Mali Empire in 752-754 AH (1352-1353 CE). By this time, Mansa Musa had been dead for approximately fifteen years, and the current ruler was Mansa Sulayman.

Ibn Battuta traveled across the Sahara from Morocco, through the oasis of Sijilmasa, across the great desert to Walata, and then to the Niger River region — a journey of several months. His description of Mali covers its political structure, the character of the ruling class, the condition of merchants, the religious practices of the Muslim community, and the customs of the general population.

His assessment, as noted earlier in relation to the Mali article, is balanced: he praises security, Quranic memorization, and the observation of prayer while criticizing practices he considers departures from Islamic standards. This makes his account both valuable as a historical source and exemplary as a model of engaged Islamic scholarship — applying knowledge of the Quran and Sunnah as a standard of assessment rather than simple cultural relativism.

## The Rihla: A Civilizational Document

The *Rihla* that Ibn Battuta dictated to Ibn Juzayy at the command of the Marinid Sultan Abu Inan Faris in Morocco around 756 AH (1355 CE) is not a simple travel diary but a structured literary work. Ibn Juzayy organized and edited the material, added literary embellishments (some of which scholars have identified as borrowings from earlier travel literature), and produced a text in the cultivated Arabic literary tradition.

The Rihla's value lies precisely in what it documents: the Islamic world of the mid-8th century AH as a functioning civilization with shared institutions, shared language (Arabic), shared legal framework (though administered differently in different regions), and shared religious identity across an enormous geographic range. A Moroccan scholar who could arrive in Cairo, Damascus, Mecca, India, and Mali and find people with whom he could communicate, worship, conduct legal business, and engage in scholarly discourse was living in a genuinely unified civilization.

This *umma* dimension — the real existence of Islamic brotherhood across ethnic, linguistic, and geographical difference — is one of the Rihla's most powerful themes. Ibn Battuta was welcomed, hosted, and employed from Morocco to China not because of ethnic solidarity (he was Moroccan in a world of Arabs, Turks, Persians, Indians, and many others) but because of shared Muslim identity and the framework it provided for trust and cooperation.

## Final Return and Death

Ibn Battuta returned to Morocco in 750 AH (1349 CE), made a brief journey to al-Andalus, then undertook his West African journey (752-754 AH), and finally returned to Morocco around 754-755 AH (1353-1354 CE) — never to travel again. He spent his final years in Morocco, reportedly serving in some judicial capacity. He died around 770-779 AH (1368-1377 CE) — the sources disagree on the precise date — in Morocco, far from the courts and cities he had spent a lifetime visiting.

He is buried in Tangier, the city where his journey began and where it ended — a fitting symmetry for the man who circled the world and returned to where he started.
