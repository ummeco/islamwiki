# Detailed Account of the Siege and Judgment of Banu Qurayza

The siege and judgment of Banu Qurayza in Dhul Qa'dah-Dhul Hijjah, 5 AH (February-March 627 CE) was the most severe event in the early Muslim community's confrontations with the Jewish tribes of Medina. The judgment of their fighting men's execution — carried out according to the sentence of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, whom Banu Qurayza themselves had accepted as arbiter — remains one of the most difficult events in the seerah for both Muslim and non-Muslim readers and has been discussed by Islamic scholars across the centuries.

## Context: The Battle of the Trench

Banu Qurayza's actions during the Battle of the Trench (5 AH) were their decisive violation of the Medina agreements. During the Confederate siege of Medina, Banu Qurayza — persuaded by Huyayy ibn Akhtab — declared their alliance with the Confederates and broke their treaty with the Muslims. This threatened the Muslim community from within, at the moment of its greatest external danger.

The Confederate siege ultimately failed, and when the Confederates withdrew, the Prophet received a divine direction to proceed against Banu Qurayza.

## The Siege

Immediately after the Confederate withdrawal, the Prophet led the Muslim forces to the fortresses of Banu Qurayza. The siege lasted approximately 25 days (some accounts say 21 or 25 days). Banu Qurayza's fortresses were strong and the siege was a war of attrition. Eventually, isolated and with no relief coming from the allies who had made them promises, Banu Qurayza agreed to surrender.

## The Question of the Arbiter

When Banu Qurayza agreed to surrender and face judgment, they were offered the choice of accepting a judgment. The Aws tribe — who were allied with Banu Qurayza and had interceded for Banu Qaynuqa (an earlier Jewish tribe) with the Prophet — requested the right to intercede. The Prophet suggested they accept the judgment of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, the chief of Aws. Banu Qurayza agreed.

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh was seriously wounded during the Battle of the Trench — struck by an arrow — and had asked Allah to keep him alive until he could see the resolution of the Banu Qurayza affair. He was brought on a donkey to give his judgment.

Sa'd's judgment: the fighting men of Banu Qurayza were to be executed, the women and children taken as prisoners, and their wealth distributed.

The Prophet said: "You have judged with the judgment of Allah from above the seven heavens."

## The Execution

The fighting men of Banu Qurayza — the classical sources give their number as between 400 and 900 (most scholars treat 400-700 as the more plausible range based on the size of the tribe) — were executed in the marketplace of Medina. Huyayy ibn Akhtab, who had entered the Banu Qurayza fortress after convincing Ka'b ibn Asad to break the treaty and had promised to share their fate, was also executed.

The Prophet's Jewish wife Rayhanah bint Zayd was among the women captured; some accounts say she became his wife after the battle, others say she remained his slave-concubine.

## Islamic Scholarly Analysis

**The judgment as tribal law.** Sa'd ibn Mu'adh's judgment followed the provisions of the Torah itself — the Pentateuchal law for a city that had refused terms of peace and had continued to fight. Islamic scholars have noted that Sa'd may have been applying the terms of Deuteronomy 20:12-14, which was the law of the community that Banu Qurayza recognized as authoritative. The "judgment of Allah from above the seven heavens" may refer to this Tawraic ruling.

**The treaty violation.** Islamic jurists emphasize that Banu Qurayza's action was not merely a political matter but a treaty violation at the most critical moment of the Muslim community's existence — when an attack from within could have meant the massacre of the entire Muslim community, including non-combatant women and children. The severity of the judgment reflects the severity of the breach.

**The arbiter's authority.** The legitimacy of Sa'd's judgment rests on his being chosen by Banu Qurayza themselves as their arbiter — a recognized Arabian legal principle of voluntary submission to agreed adjudication.

## Contested Aspects

Western scholarship has raised serious questions about:
- The total number killed (the high-end classical figures of 700-900 are doubted by modern historians)
- The precise events of the surrender and the decision-making process
- Whether the execution constitutes a war crime by modern standards

Muslim scholars have responded that: the events must be understood in their 7th-century context; the judgment was applied only to those who had actively engaged in military treachery; and the women, children, and non-combatants were not killed.

## Surah al-Ahzab

The Quran's account of the siege in Surah al-Ahzab (33:26-27) states: "And He brought down those who supported them among the People of the Scripture from their fortresses and cast terror into their hearts; a party you killed, and you took captive a party."

## Sources

The account is in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Sahih Muslim, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*. Modern analysis in W. Montgomery Watt, *Muhammad at Medina*; and Norman Stillman, *The Jews of Arab Lands*.
