# Detailed Account of the Thaqif Delegation

The delegation of Thaqif to Medina in 9 AH was among the most significant of the Year of Delegations (Am al-Wufud) — the period when tribal delegations from across Arabia came to the Prophet to declare their submission to Islam and negotiate the terms of their integration into the Muslim polity. The Thaqif's lengthy negotiations before accepting Islam, and their requests for concessions that the Prophet refused, established important principles about the non-negotiability of Islamic obligations.

## Background: Thaqif After the Siege

The Thaqif tribe of Ta'if had been besieged by the Muslim army after the Battle of Hunayn in Shawwal 8 AH. The siege had failed — the Muslims could not take the strongly fortified city — and the Prophet had withdrawn after approximately 15 to 40 days. His supplication on withdrawing — asking for the Thaqif's guidance rather than their destruction — proved prescient.

The failed siege had demonstrated Thaqif's military capability, but the political landscape was rapidly shifting. As all the surrounding tribes submitted to Islam after the conquest of Mecca, Ta'if became increasingly isolated. In 9 AH, Thaqif sent a delegation to Medina to negotiate.

## The Delegation

The Thaqif delegation arrived in Medina and was housed in the mosque, where they could observe Islamic worship practices. The Prophet appointed Khalid ibn Sa'id ibn al-As to tend to their needs and serve as their liaison.

## The Negotiations

The Thaqif attempted to negotiate exemptions and concessions before accepting Islam. Their requests, as recorded in the seerah sources, included:

**Exemption from prayer.** They asked that they not be required to bow in prayer (some accounts specify they objected to prostration). The Prophet refused: "There is no good in a religion with no prayer."

**The retention of their idol al-Lat for three years.** Al-Lat was one of the three major pre-Islamic Arabian goddesses and had a major temple at Ta'if. The Thaqif asked that the idol's temple be preserved for three years before destruction. The Prophet refused. They successively asked for two years, then one year, then one month — each time refused. Finally they asked that they themselves not have to be the ones to destroy the idol. The Prophet agreed to this point: he would send someone else to destroy the al-Lat temple.

**Exemption from jihad and from zakah.** The Prophet refused these as well.

Despite the refusals, the delegation eventually accepted Islam on the Prophet's terms. When they returned to Ta'if, Abu Sufyan ibn Harb and al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah were sent with them to destroy the al-Lat temple.

## The Destruction of al-Lat

Al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah led the actual destruction of al-Lat's temple. The women of Thaqif reportedly lamented and mourned the idol's destruction. Al-Mughirah himself had previously been a member of a Thaqif delegation that had visited Mecca — his participation in destroying Thaqif's central religious icon was a sign of his Islamic commitment.

The gold and jewelry offerings in the al-Lat temple were distributed as part of the Thaqif's integration into the Muslim community.

## The Principle Established

The Thaqif's negotiations and the Prophet's consistent refusals established a principle of central importance in Islamic jurisprudence: the core obligations of Islam — prayer (salah), zakah, and the prohibition on idol worship — are non-negotiable. A "version" of Islam that exempts its adherents from these obligations is not Islam.

This principle was later applied in the Riddah Wars: when some tribes after the Prophet's death claimed they could keep their Islam but stop paying zakah, Abu Bakr's refusal — echoing the Prophet's refusal to the Thaqif — was grounded in this same logic: the obligations of Islam come as a package; they are not a menu.

## Year of Delegations

The Thaqif delegation was one of many that came to Medina in 9 AH, which is called Am al-Wufud (Year of Delegations) in Islamic history. After the conquest of Mecca, the political reality was clear to tribal Arabia: the Muslim community was the dominant power. Tribal leaders came from across the peninsula to make their pledges of allegiance.

The Prophet received each delegation personally, learned about their tribal customs, and adapted the terms of their integration to their situations while maintaining the core Islamic obligations. The Year of Delegations represents the political consolidation of Islamic Arabia before the Prophet's death in 11 AH.

## Sources

The Thaqif delegation is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and the sunan literature.
