# Uthman ibn Affan: The Man of Two Lights

## Origins and Standing in Mecca

Uthman ibn Affan ibn Abi al-As ibn Umayyah (may Allah be pleased with him) was born approximately six years after the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, making him about thirty years old when the prophetic mission began. He came from the Banu Umayyah clan of the Quraysh — a branch of the tribe that would later become the ruling house of the Islamic empire and, in the eyes of many early Muslims, a source of both governance and controversy. But in those earliest days of Islam, when the message had barely taken root, Uthman's clan affiliation made his conversion all the more remarkable. The Banu Umayyah were among the fiercest opponents of the Prophet ﷺ, led by Abu Sufyan, and their hostility to the new message was consistent and open. For a man of Uthman's standing within that clan to accept Islam was to break with his own family's social and religious position.

Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) was one of the wealthiest merchants of Mecca. He was known for his elegance, his soft manner, and his deep personal modesty — qualities that endeared him to the people of his community even before faith refined them further. He had traveled extensively as a merchant and was familiar with the lands of Syria and the neighboring regions. It was during one such journey, or perhaps shortly before his conversion, that a remarkable event is recorded.

## The Dream That Preceded Faith

Ibn Hisham and Ibn Sa'd record an account that Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) had a dream before his meeting with the Prophet ﷺ. In this dream, he heard a voice calling from the direction of the sky — some accounts describe it as a caller on a rooftop — announcing that Ahmad had appeared among the Quraysh in Mecca. The name Ahmad is one of the Prophet's names, mentioned in the Quran (61:6) as the name by which Isa ibn Maryam (peace be upon him) foretold the coming Prophet.

This dream planted in Uthman a curiosity and an anticipation. When he returned from his travels and learned that Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) was inviting people to Islam, Uthman was already primed to receive the message. When Abu Bakr took him to the Prophet ﷺ, the encounter confirmed what the dream had foreshadowed. Uthman accepted Islam immediately, and the Prophet ﷺ welcomed him warmly.

This account illustrates a pattern seen in the early Muslim community: that Allah prepares hearts through various means before the formal encounter with the message. Dreams, contemplation, and the witnessing of moral excellence in the Prophet ﷺ all served as pathways to the truth. For Uthman, the path began in a dream and was completed in the presence of the Prophet ﷺ himself.

## Persecution from His Own Clan

The conversion of Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) provoked an immediate and harsh response from his family. His uncle, al-Hakam ibn Abi al-As (a man later known for his persistent enmity toward the Prophet ﷺ and the early Muslims), was enraged. He bound Uthman with rope and declared that he would not release him until he renounced this new religion. Al-Hakam reportedly said: "You have abandoned the religion of your forefathers for a new religion. By Allah, I will not release you until you give up this thing you have entered."

Uthman's response was unequivocal. He told his uncle that he would never abandon Islam. He bore the restraint and the pressure with patience and dignity, and al-Hakam eventually released him when it became clear that threats and compulsion would accomplish nothing. This episode is a reminder that the early Muslim community was not composed of the poor and the powerless alone — men of wealth and social standing faced persecution within their own families and accepted it willingly for the sake of their faith.

## The Emigration to Abyssinia

The severity of persecution in Mecca led the Prophet ﷺ to advise his companions to seek refuge in Abyssinia, whose Christian ruler, the Negus (al-Najashi), was known for his justice and his refusal to allow the oppression of those under his protection. Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) was among the first group to make this emigration — the first Hijrah in Islamic history. He traveled with his wife Ruqayyah bint Muhammad (may Allah be pleased with her), the daughter of the Prophet ﷺ.

When news of this emigration reached the Prophet ﷺ, he said — according to a hadith recorded by Ibn Hisham — "They are the first to emigrate in the path of Allah after Lut and Ibrahim." This statement places Uthman and Ruqayyah within a tradition of prophetic sacrifice that begins with the earliest messengers. Their willingness to leave Mecca, their families, and their social networks for the sake of their faith was not a small thing. It was the act of people who had chosen Allah and His Messenger above everything else.

Uthman and Ruqayyah emigrated to Abyssinia twice. The first group returned when they heard — incorrectly, as it turned out — that the situation in Mecca had improved. The second emigration was larger and included many of the early companions. Uthman and Ruqayyah were part of this second wave as well, enduring the hardship of displacement and uncertainty for years before the community finally found its home in Medina.

## Dhul-Nurayn: The Man of Two Lights

The title by which Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) is most commonly known — Dhul-Nurayn, the Man of Two Lights — derives from the extraordinary honor of his marriages to two daughters of the Prophet ﷺ. He first married Ruqayyah bint Muhammad (may Allah be pleased with her), who fell ill during the expedition of Badr and died while Uthman remained by her side in Medina, the Prophet ﷺ having permitted him to stay. When she died, the Prophet ﷺ gave his second daughter Umm Kulthum (may Allah be pleased with her) to Uthman in marriage. When Umm Kulthum also died, the Prophet ﷺ famously said: "If I had a third, I would give her to Uthman" — a testament to his love and trust for this companion.

The title Dhul-Nurayn is thus not merely an honorific. It is a recognition that Uthman was granted a privilege no other man in Islamic history has received: the marriage to two daughters of a Prophet of Allah. This double honor reflected the Prophet's assessment of Uthman's character — his trustworthiness, his gentleness, and his sincere faith.

## Generosity That Armed the Muslim Community

Uthman's wealth was not held for his own comfort. When Islam called for resources, he responded with an open hand and a consistent heart. The most celebrated expression of this generosity came during the preparation for the Expedition of Tabuk, the most difficult military campaign of the Medinan period. The journey was long, the season harsh, and the Muslim army was severely under-equipped. The Prophet ﷺ called upon his companions to contribute what they could, and Uthman's response was extraordinary.

In various narrations recorded by al-Tirmidhi and Ahmad, Uthman donated nine hundred camels and one hundred horses fully equipped for the expedition — some narrations say the total was closer to one thousand. He also donated a vast sum of money in gold and silver. The Prophet ﷺ, upon receiving these donations, raised his hands and prayed: "O Allah, I am pleased with Uthman — be You pleased with him." He repeated this supplication multiple times, a scene the narrators record with evident emotional weight.

This donation gave Uthman the title Jabbar Jaysh al-Usra — the Outfitter of the Army of Hardship. The word "usra" (hardship) was used to describe this expedition because of the difficulty of the conditions under which it was mounted. That Uthman's wealth effectively made the expedition viable placed his name in the memory of the community as one of the great patrons of Islam.

He had earlier purchased the Well of Rumah for the Muslims when they lacked water, paying a substantial price and making it a charitable endowment (waqf) for all Muslims. The Prophet ﷺ had promised that whoever purchased this well would have Paradise — and Uthman purchased it. He also expanded and renovated the Mosque of the Prophet ﷺ at his own expense, both in the time of the Prophet ﷺ and later during his caliphate.

## His Character: Modesty and the Weight of Shame

A distinctive feature of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) was his exceptional modesty and shyness (haya). The Prophet ﷺ recognized this quality explicitly. In a hadith recorded in Sahih Muslim, the Prophet ﷺ said: "The most modest (ashyam) of my community is Uthman." In another narration in al-Tirmidhi, the Prophet said: "The most modest of my ummah is Uthman ibn Affan." This was not a social shyness but a God-consciousness — an awareness of the divine that expressed itself in careful, measured conduct.

The Prophet ﷺ also noted that even the angels showed reverence for Uthman. A well-known hadith in Sahih Muslim records that when Uthman would enter, the Prophet ﷺ would adjust his clothing and sit up properly. When asked about this, the Prophet said: "Should I not be modest before a man before whom even the angels are modest?" This hadith speaks to a quality of spiritual gravity in Uthman that transcended ordinary social interactions.

## The Caliphate and the Compilation of the Quran

Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) became the third Caliph in 23 AH and ruled for twelve years. His caliphate saw the greatest territorial expansion of the early Islamic state. He was also responsible for one of the most consequential acts in Islamic intellectual history: the standardization and distribution of the Quran. When Hudhayfa ibn al-Yaman (may Allah be pleased with him) alerted Uthman to the spread of variant readings that risked causing confusion among Muslims in different regions, Uthman convened a committee under Zayd ibn Thabit to compile a standardized text based on the master copy held by Hafsa (may Allah be pleased with her), the Prophet's wife. Copies were made and sent to the major cities of the Islamic world, and all variant copies were collected and burned.

This action, known as the Uthmanic codification, is one of the great services rendered to the Muslim community. The Quran that Muslims read today in every corner of the world is the Quran of the Uthmanic codex — a testament to the foresight and determination of this companion.

His caliphate ended with his martyrdom in 35 AH, when rebels who objected to his governance besieged his house in Medina and killed him as he was reading the Quran. He died with the Book of Allah in his hands and his blood falling upon the page — a death that the Prophet ﷺ is reported to have foretold and that Uthman had long accepted with equanimity.
