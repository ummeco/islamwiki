# The First Revelation: The Night Everything Changed

## The Night of Power

The first revelation of the Quran to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ occurred in the Cave of Hira on the Mountain of Light (Jabal al-Nur), in the month of Ramadan, when the Prophet ﷺ was approximately forty years old — approximately 610 CE. This event, which Islamic theology calls the beginning of the prophetic mission (nubuwwa), is the most consequential single event in human history after the creation itself: the moment when Allah's final message to humanity was first transmitted, and the man chosen to deliver it received his appointment.

The account of this night is preserved in one of the most foundational hadith in all of Islamic literature — narrated by Aisha and recorded in the opening hadith of Sahih al-Bukhari, the most authoritative collection of prophetic traditions. It is one of the most meticulously documented events in early Islamic history.

## The Encounter with Jibril

The Prophet ﷺ was in the cave during one of his annual Ramadan retreats when Jibril (Gabriel, the Archangel of Revelation) came to him. The encounter was overwhelming. Jibril said: "Iqra'" — Read, or Recite. The Prophet ﷺ responded: "Ma ana bi-qari'" — I am not a reader (or: I cannot read).

Jibril then seized him and compressed him so intensely that the Prophet ﷺ felt he could barely bear it — and then released him. Again Jibril said: "Iqra'." Again the Prophet responded that he was not a reader. Again the same embrace, the same release. A third time: "Iqra'." A third time: "I am not a reader."

Then Jibril recited the first revelation:

*"Read [or: Recite] in the name of your Lord who created — created man from a clinging substance. Read, and your Lord is the Most Generous — who taught by the pen — taught man that which he knew not."*
(Surah al-Alaq, 96:1-5)

These five verses — the first of the Quran to be revealed — contain an extraordinary density of meaning. The command to read/recite (iqra') is addressed to the one who described himself as not a reader, establishing immediately that what was being transmitted was not from the human source but from the divine. The creation of man from a "clinging substance" ('alaq) identifies the human being both physically and symbolically. The attribution of teaching by the pen (al-qalam) places revelation in the tradition of written divine communication that began with the tablets of Moses.

## The Return and Khadijah's Faith

The Prophet ﷺ left the cave and descended the mountain in a state of profound terror and disorientation. He later described feeling that he might be "possessed" (majnun) — a fear that was not theological doubt but the overwhelming experience of an encounter entirely outside his previous frame of reference. He was trembling.

He went directly home to Khadijah. He said: "Cover me, cover me" — and she wrapped him in a cloak. When the trembling subsided and he had recovered enough to speak, he told her everything that had happened and said: "I feared for myself."

Khadijah's response is one of the most celebrated moments in the seerah. Without hesitation, without asking for confirmation, without expressing doubt, she said:

"By Allah, Allah will never humiliate you. You maintain family ties. You speak the truth. You bear others' burdens. You help the destitute. You are generous to your guests. You assist in every righteous cause."

This was the first declaration of faith in the Prophet's prophethood — made before any argument, any miracle demonstration, any theological proof. It was based entirely on character testimony: the certainty that a man of this quality would not be abandoned by God. Khadijah became the first believer, and her faith in that moment provided the Prophet ﷺ with the human support he needed in the most disorienting experience of his life.

## Waraqah ibn Nawfal's Confirmation

Khadijah then took the Prophet ﷺ to her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal — an elderly Christian scholar who had studied the previous scriptures extensively, could read and write in Hebrew, and had a deep knowledge of Biblical prophecy. Waraqah listened to the account and responded with immediate recognition:

"This is al-Namus — the same angel who brought revelation to Moses. I wish I were young and alive when your people drive you out." The Prophet ﷺ asked: "Will they really drive me out?" Waraqah said: "Yes. No man has ever brought what you have brought without being opposed and persecuted. If I live to see that day, I will support you with all my strength."

Waraqah's confirmation came from a lifetime of studying the previous scriptures and their prophecies of the final Prophet. He was, by his own account, recognizing a fulfillment of what he had studied — confirming what Khadijah had discerned through character and what Jibril had enacted through revelation.

Shortly after this, Waraqah died — old and, by some accounts, having gone blind in his final days. He did not live to see the prophetic mission publicly unfold, but his witness at this critical moment is preserved in the foundational hadith.

## The Pause in Revelation: Al-Fatra

After the initial revelation, there was a pause — a period called al-fatra — during which no further revelation came. The length of this pause is disputed in the scholarly tradition: it is recorded variously as a few days to approximately six months. During this period, the Prophet ﷺ experienced significant anxiety and spiritual longing for the return of revelation.

Al-Bukhari preserves an account — attributed to Jabir ibn Abdullah — of the Prophet ﷺ describing this period: he would feel driven toward the edges of mountain peaks in his distress, and Jibril would appear and tell him that he was truly the Prophet of Allah, and the distress would ease. This account, in addition to showing the Prophet's human vulnerability, demonstrates that the pause in revelation was not abandonment but a deliberate divine timing.

The next revelation — Surah al-Muddaththir (74:1-5), addressing the Prophet directly: "O you who wraps himself in his cloak, arise and warn" — came when the Prophet ﷺ was walking and suddenly heard a voice. He looked up and saw Jibril seated on a throne between heaven and earth, and he returned home trembling again, asking Khadijah to cover him. This second revelation was the command to begin the mission. With it, the period of al-fatra ended and the prophetic call began in earnest.

## The Theological Significance

The first revelation event carries multiple layers of theological and historical significance.

It establishes the mode of revelation: not through the Prophet's own thought or inspiration, but through direct transmission from Jibril on behalf of Allah. This transmission model — angel to prophet — is consistent across the Abrahamic prophetic tradition (Jibril brought revelation to previous prophets as well) and places the Quran in the lineage of divine scripture.

It identifies the content of the mission from the beginning: "Read in the name of your Lord" — the entire mission of the Prophet ﷺ would be a matter of conveying what he had received in the name of the One who had sent it. The Prophet ﷺ was not an author; he was a transmitter.

And it establishes the human reality of the Prophet: he was frightened, he needed comfort, he experienced doubt about his own sanity. These reactions — preserved without sanitization in the most authoritative hadith — make the prophetic biography a human story of divine appointment, not a mythology of a super-human figure immune to fear. The man who received this revelation was as human as the people he would be sent to guide — and his humanity was essential to the mission.
