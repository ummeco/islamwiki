# The Death of the Negus

## The King Who Sheltered the First Emigrants

The Negus Ashama ibn Abjar — the Christian king of Abyssinia who had granted the first Muslim emigrants protection when they fled Mecca's persecution — died in Rajab 9 AH. He was not present in the Arabian Peninsula, but his death reached the Prophet ﷺ through the news networks that connected the Muslim community across its expanding geography. The Prophet ﷺ announced the Negus's death to the companions in Medina and led a funeral prayer for him — the salat al-gha'ib, the absent funeral prayer — with four takbeerat, as for any Muslim burial. This was the first recorded instance of the absent funeral prayer in the history of Islam.

The companions were surprised by the prayer. The Negus was a Christian king who had died on the other side of the African continent, with no other Muslims physically present at his death. The Prophet ﷺ prayed for him nonetheless — a declaration that the Negus's faith was real and his standing before Allah warranted the prayer.

## The Negus's Faith

The Negus's relationship to Islam is described in detail in the seerah sources. When the first Muslim emigrants arrived at his court and the Qurayshi delegation of Amr ibn al-As and Abdullah ibn Abi Rabia came to demand their extradition, the Negus heard both sides. Jafar ibn Abi Talib spoke for the Muslims, describing Islam's core teachings and reciting Surah Maryam — the Quranic account of Jesus and Mary. The Negus picked up a small stick and drew a line: "The difference between what you have said about Jesus and what we believe is no greater than this." He refused to hand over the Muslims and returned the gifts the Qurayshi delegation had brought.

In subsequent years, when the Prophet ﷺ sent letters to rulers inviting them to Islam, he sent a letter to the Negus. The narrations indicate that the Negus accepted Islam privately — he acknowledged the Prophet ﷺ as the messenger of Allah and prayed for him — but could not publicly declare it because of the political situation in his kingdom. His son later came to Medina and formally accepted Islam after his father's death. The classical scholars have discussed the nature of the Negus's Islam at length, and the majority position — supported by the Prophet's ﷺ prayer over him — is that he died as a Muslim in his heart, even if his outward declaration was constrained by his circumstances.

## The Absent Funeral Prayer

The salat al-gha'ib for the Negus established the jurisprudential basis for the absent funeral prayer in Islamic fiqh. The three major positions among classical scholars are: that it is valid in all cases where a Muslim dies without having had the funeral prayer performed over them; that it is valid only in exceptional cases as the Prophet ﷺ demonstrated; and that it was specific to the Negus and not generally applicable. The majority of scholars across the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools permit the absent funeral prayer broadly, while the Maliki and Hanafi schools have generally limited it. In practice, it is widely performed across the Muslim world in cases where Muslims die in locations where Islamic funeral rites could not be properly arranged.

## The Companions' Response

The Prophet ﷺ informed the companions of the Negus's death and said: "Your brother has died." The use of "brother" — placing the Negus in the brotherhood of Islam — was itself a declaration. Then he formed the companions into rows and prayed over the absent body. The companions who had been part of the first emigration to Abyssinia — who had lived under the Negus's protection for years and owed their lives to his refusal to extradite them — understood the depth of the honor the Prophet ﷺ was conferring on the man who had sheltered the Muslim community in its most vulnerable moment.

The Negus appears in the seerah as the first external ruler to recognize the truth of the Prophet's ﷺ message and act on that recognition at real political cost — defying his own courtiers, returning expensive gifts, and refusing the demands of a powerful trading partner (the Quraysh controlled significant Red Sea commerce) to hand over a group of refugees. His response to Jafar's recitation of Surah Maryam — weeping, drawing the small line between Islam and Christianity, refusing to hand over the Muslims — is one of the few moments in the seerah in which a non-Muslim ruler responded to the Islamic message with both understanding and courage.

## The Absent Burial

The Negus was buried in Abyssinia. His tomb, in the village of Wukro in present-day Ethiopia, has been maintained for centuries and is visited by Muslims who honor his role in the early history of the Muslim community. The community of Ethiopian Muslims traces its roots directly to the first emigrants who came under his protection — making Abyssinia, in a real sense, the oldest continuous Islamic community outside Arabia.

## The Legacy in Islamic Law

The salat al-gha'ib for the Negus gave Islamic jurisprudence a foundational case for the question of how Muslims honor distant members of their community who die without access to proper Islamic funeral arrangements. The prayer the Prophet ﷺ performed — four takbeerat, rows of companions, in Medina for a king dying in Abyssinia — compressed the geography of the Muslim community into a single prayer and established that the bonds of faith crossed physical distance. The Negus had given the Muslim community its first home in exile; the Prophet ﷺ gave him the honor of a funeral prayer that no distance could interrupt. Every absent funeral prayer performed since 9 AH has its root in this act of the Prophet ﷺ for the man who had sheltered his community at the moment of its greatest vulnerability.
