# Death of Al-Jahiz

Abu Uthman Amr ibn Bahr al-Kinani al-Fuqaymi al-Basri (163-255 AH / 776-869 CE), universally known as al-Jahiz (the Pop-Eyed), was one of the most brilliant, prolific, and entertaining writers in the entire history of Arabic literature. Theologian, literary critic, natural scientist, social observer, and master of prose, he left a body of work that shaped Islamic intellectual culture for centuries.

## Life

Al-Jahiz was born in Basra in approximately 163 AH (776 CE) to a family of African origin (his grandfather was reportedly an African slave). He grew up in poverty but compensated through ferocious intellectual energy, reportedly spending entire nights reading books borrowed from paper merchants, using the shops as reading rooms.

He came of age during the Abbasid golden age, when Baghdad and Basra were the centers of the most vibrant intellectual culture in the world. The Abbasid caliphs supported the translation movement that brought Greek philosophy and science into Arabic, and al-Jahiz was directly shaped by this confluence of traditions.

He spent much of his career in Baghdad, where he became associated with the Mu'tazilite school of rational theology — then favored by the court — and gained considerable fame and patronage. The Caliph al-Mutawakkil reportedly offered him a tutoring position for his sons, but al-Jahiz's reportedly striking appearance (his name, "the Pop-Eyed," refers to his prominent eyes) led to his dismissal after only three days.

He spent his old age in Basra, increasingly afflicted by paralysis, reportedly dying at the age of approximately 92 or 93 when a pile of his beloved books fell on him in his library. This death, whether literally true or apocryphal, seems fitting for a man who had spent his life surrounded by texts.

## The Kitab al-Hayawan

Al-Jahiz's *Kitab al-Hayawan* (Book of Animals), in seven volumes, is one of the most remarkable works of natural history from any tradition. It discusses hundreds of animals with a combination of scientific observation, philosophical reflection, anecdote, poetry, and humor.

What makes the work remarkable from a scientific standpoint is al-Jahiz's genuine observation of animal behavior and his proto-evolutionary ideas. He wrote about the struggle for existence among animals, the adaptation of animals to their environments, and the transformation of species over time through environmental pressures. While he did not develop a systematic evolutionary theory, his observations anticipated certain themes that would not be systematically explored until the nineteenth century.

He also discussed the food chain (animals eat other animals, which in turn are eaten by others), animal intelligence, and the use of animals in human culture.

## The Kitab al-Bayan wal-Tabyin

The *Kitab al-Bayan wal-Tabyin* (Book of Eloquence and Exposition) is one of the foundational works of Arabic literary criticism and rhetoric. Al-Jahiz examined the nature of eloquent speech and writing, the role of gesture and intonation, the relationship between meaning and expression, and the standards by which Arabic writing should be judged.

The work is also a treasure house of anecdotes, sayings, and observations about Arabic society, culture, and intellectual life, assembled with al-Jahiz's characteristic combination of erudition and wit.

## The Kitab al-Bukhala

The *Kitab al-Bukhala* (Book of Misers) is perhaps al-Jahiz's most entertaining work, a collection of anecdotes about miserly characters drawn from all walks of life. The book is a masterpiece of comic characterization and social observation, with misers devising ever more elaborate schemes to avoid spending money. Despite its comic surface, the work contains serious observations about human psychology, social norms, and the relationship between money and character.

## Theological Position

Al-Jahiz was a leading figure of the Mu'tazilite school, the rationalist theological movement that applied Greek logical methods to Islamic theological questions. He defended the Mu'tazilite positions on the createdness of the Quran, divine justice, and human free will, producing theological treatises in addition to his literary and scientific works.

He also wrote works defending the dignity of people of African origin against the racial prejudices of his time, arguing for the equality of all races and the merits of non-Arab cultures — remarkable positions for any period.

## Legacy

Al-Jahiz wrote an estimated 200 to 360 works, of which approximately 30 survive. His influence on Arabic prose style was enormous: his approach — learning worn lightly, wit in service of serious argument, a willingness to engage with any topic from the highest theological question to the habits of the village idiot — set a standard for Arabic literary essay that shaped the tradition for centuries.

He died in approximately 255 AH (869 CE) at an age well past 90, one of the great intellects of the Islamic golden age.
