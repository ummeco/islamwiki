# The Great Mosque of Djenné

The Great Mosque of Djenné (Djinguereber de Djenné) in present-day Mali is the largest mud-brick building in the world and one of the most distinctive examples of the Sudano-Sahelian architectural style. An Islamic place of worship at the heart of one of West Africa's most historic trading and scholarly cities, it represents the remarkable adaptation of Islamic architecture to the materials, climate, and aesthetic traditions of the West African Sahel.

## Djenné: A Trading and Scholarly City

Djenné (Jenne) is located on an island in the Bani River in the inner Niger Delta of present-day Mali. It became one of the most important trading cities in West Africa from the medieval period onward, serving as a terminus of Saharan trade routes connecting sub-Saharan Africa with North Africa and, through North Africa, with the broader Islamic world.

Gold, kola nuts, slaves, and agricultural products moved northward from Djenné; salt, copper, cloth, and other manufactures moved southward from the Sahara. This commerce made Djenné wealthy and gave it a cosmopolitan character.

Djenné Islamized around the twelfth or thirteenth century CE (seventh or eighth century AH), reportedly when its king converted. Within a century it had become a significant center of Islamic scholarship. Ibn Battuta, visiting West Africa in the fourteenth century CE (eighth century AH), described the extensive trade and Muslim scholarly culture of the region.

## The History of the Mosque

According to the Ta'rikh al-Sudan (Chronicle of the Sudan) and Ta'rikh al-Fattash (Chronicle of the Researcher), the first mosque on the site was built by the ruler Koi Komboro (or Kunburu) when he converted to Islam, probably around the thirteenth century CE. The mosque was subsequently rebuilt or significantly modified multiple times.

The Moroccan Saadian dynasty, which conquered the Songhai Empire and took Djenné in 1591 CE (999 AH), reportedly allowed the mosque to fall into disrepair or demolished it as part of the disruption that followed the conquest.

The current structure dates primarily to a major reconstruction in 1907 CE (1325 AH), undertaken by the French colonial administration in collaboration with local builders. The 1907 reconstruction was itself based on an earlier mosque built in 1834 CE (1249 AH) during the Fulani Jihad of Seku Amadu Lobbo, who founded the Dina (Islamic state) of Hamdallahi in the region.

## Architectural Character

The Great Mosque is built entirely of sun-dried mud brick (adobe), coated with banco plaster — a mixture of mud and rice husks that provides a smooth surface. The Sudano-Sahelian style is characterized by:

**Organic forms.** The mud construction creates rounded, sculptural forms rather than the sharp angles of stone or brick construction. The towers, buttresses, and walls have a tactile, hand-made quality.

**Protruding palm-wood beams (torons).** The most distinctive visual element of Sudano-Sahelian mosques is the hundreds of palm-wood beams that protrude from the walls, looking like spines or fingers. These serve both structural and functional purposes: they act as permanent scaffolding, enabling the annual replastering that the mud walls require, and they reduce thermal cracking by anchoring the plaster to the wall.

**Three towers (minarets).** The mosque's facade features three towers, each topped by an ostrich egg — a symbol of fertility and purity in West African Islamic tradition.

**Massive scale.** The prayer hall can accommodate 3,000 worshippers. The roof is supported by 90 columns.

## The Annual Festival of Crepissage

Because mud-brick construction requires regular maintenance in the seasonal rain, the people of Djenné maintain the mosque through an annual festival called the Crepissage de la Grande Mosquée. Each spring, before the rainy season, the entire community gathers to replaster the mosque with fresh banco mud. Groups of young men bring mud from the river while teams of experienced masons apply it. The festival has a festive, communal character that reinforces the relationship between the mosque and the entire community.

## UNESCO World Heritage

Djenné's old city, including the Great Mosque, was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1988 CE. The site faces ongoing challenges from climate change, increasing tourism pressure, and the difficulty of maintaining traditional construction techniques as the pool of skilled craftsmen decreases.

## Legacy

The Great Mosque of Djenné is a symbol of African Islamic civilization — evidence that Islam took root in West Africa not as an import that displaced local culture but as a tradition that adapted to and enriched local building practices, creating something distinctive and magnificent.
