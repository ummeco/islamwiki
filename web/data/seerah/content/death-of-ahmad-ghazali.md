# Death of Ahmad al-Ghazali

Ahmad ibn Muhammad al-Ghazali al-Tusi (d. 520 AH / 1126 CE) was a prominent Sufi master and the younger brother of the renowned theologian Abu Hamid al-Ghazali. Though living in the shadow of his more famous brother's scholarship, Ahmad al-Ghazali was highly regarded in his own right as a spiritual teacher, preacher, and author of one of the most celebrated texts in the Persian Sufi tradition.

## Life and Relationship with His Brother

Ahmad al-Ghazali was born in Tus (in present-day Iran) several years after his brother Abu Hamid (d. 505 AH / 1111 CE). Both received their early education in Tus and studied at the Nizamiyya madrasa in Baghdad. While Abu Hamid became the foremost legal theologian of his generation and the author of the monumental *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, Ahmad took a more exclusively mystical path, becoming a prominent Sufi shaykh.

When Abu Hamid withdrew from his position at the Nizamiyya madrasa in Baghdad in his famous spiritual crisis (488 AH / 1095 CE), Ahmad reportedly played a role in encouraging his retreat. Ahmad also substituted for him at the madrasa for a period during his absence.

Ahmad traveled and taught in Persia and Iraq, gathering disciples and becoming renowned as a preacher. He died in Qazvin in 520 AH (1126 CE).

## Sawanih: Aphorisms for Lovers

Ahmad al-Ghazali's most celebrated work is *Sawanih: Aphorisms for Lovers* (Sawanih al-Ushshaq), a short but profound meditation on love as a spiritual force. Written in Persian, it is considered a masterpiece of Persian mystical prose.

The work uses the framework of human love — the beloved, the lover, longing, union, separation — to explore the soul's relationship to the divine. Ahmad al-Ghazali argued that love, understood properly, is not a distraction from God but a manifestation of divine reality in the human world. The lover's self-annihilation in love for the beloved becomes a symbol of the mystic's annihilation (fana) in divine reality.

The *Sawanih* was highly influential on subsequent Persian Sufi literature, particularly on the Sufi poet Fakhr al-Din Iraqi (d. 688 AH / 1289 CE) and, through the Persian tradition, on Rumi and other major figures of Islamic mysticism.

## Relationship with Divine Love

Ahmad al-Ghazali's theology of love was more radical in its emphasis than that of many other Sufi masters. He pushed the boundaries of conventional piety by insisting that the experience of human love, rightly understood, is a divine gift and a path to God — not something to be suppressed or transcended but to be understood at its deepest level.

This position generated some controversy, as it could be read as endorsing attachment to created beings. Ahmad's defense was that true love involves recognizing the divine beauty that shines through the beloved — love of the manifestation leads to love of the Source.

## Legacy

Though less famous than his brother, Ahmad al-Ghazali was a significant figure in the development of Persian Sufi literature and thought. The *Sawanih* has been translated into multiple languages and remains a standard text in the study of Islamic mysticism. His life illustrated the complementarity of the legal-theological tradition represented by Abu Hamid and the more exclusively mystical tradition he himself embodied — the two streams that fed the comprehensive synthesis of the later *Ihya*.
