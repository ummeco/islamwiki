# The Repentance of the Three Companions

## The Men Who Had No Excuse

Among the consequences of the Expedition of Tabuk was the case of three companions who stayed behind without a legitimate reason. They were not hypocrites — they did not claim to have been sick or unable to travel, they did not spread false reports about the expedition, and they did not encourage others to stay. They had simply failed to go, and they admitted it honestly. Their names were Ka'b ibn Malik, Murarah ibn al-Rabi, and Hilal ibn Umayyah — all three from the Ansar, all three with established standing in the community.

When the Prophet ﷺ returned from Tabuk, the hypocrites came to him with their excuses and oaths, and he accepted their outward declarations while leaving their inner states to Allah. But Ka'b, Murarah, and Hilal told the truth. Ka'b's account is the most detailed — preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari in one of the longest single hadiths in the collection — and it is among the most remarkable narratives of personal crisis and spiritual recovery in Islamic literature.

Ka'b said: "When the Prophet ﷺ returned, I went to him and greeted him. He smiled, but the smile of one who is angry, and said: 'Come here.' I came and sat before him. He said: 'Why did you stay behind? Had you not already bought your mount?' I said: 'By Allah, O Messenger of Allah, if I were sitting before any person of the world other than you, I could speak and argue my way out. But by Allah, I know that if I tell you something false to please you today, Allah will make you displeased with me later. And if I tell you the truth and you become angry with me, I hope for a good outcome from Allah. I had no excuse — I was never stronger or better off than when I stayed behind.' The Prophet ﷺ said: 'As for this man, he has told the truth. Go until Allah judges your case.'"

## Fifty Days of Isolation

The Prophet ﷺ forbade the entire community from speaking to the three men. The boycott was total: their own families were told not to speak with them, only to live in the same household. Ka'b described walking through Medina and greeting companions he knew, watching their eyes avoid him without a word, not knowing if they had answered or not. He described going to the mosque for prayer — the same mosque — and watching the Prophet ﷺ from the corner of his eye without knowing whether the Prophet ﷺ had returned his greeting or not.

After twenty days, an additional instruction came: separate yourselves from your wives. Ka'b's wife went to stay with her parents. Murarah's wife came to the Prophet ﷺ and said her husband was an old man with no servant and asked permission to serve him; the Prophet ﷺ said she could serve him but he was not to approach her. Ka'b was younger and sent his wife to her parents directly.

Ka'b described the experience of the earth narrowing despite its vastness — the Quranic phrase that later described his spiritual state precisely. He went up to the roof of his house one day and looked out over Medina's landscape. A man appeared on the horizon calling out to him from the direction of Mount Sala: a letter had arrived from the king of Ghassan. The letter said: "We have heard your companion has treated you harshly. Come to us and we will honor you." Ka'b burned the letter in the fire: "This too is a test."

On the fiftieth day, the forgiveness came. Ka'b was in his house when he heard a caller proclaiming his forgiveness — people ran to tell him, one man racing on horseback, another running. In the mosque, a companion removed his outer garment and draped it over Ka'b in joy. Ka'b prostrated before Allah in gratitude. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Be glad of the best day that has passed over you since your mother gave birth to you." Ka'b asked: "Is this from you, O Messenger of Allah, or from Allah?" The Prophet ﷺ said: "No — from Allah."

## The Quranic Record

Surah al-Tawbah (9:118) recorded the forgiveness in terms that have been recited in every generation since: "And upon the three who were left behind — until the earth was confining for them despite its vastness, and their very souls were confining to them, and they were certain that there was no refuge from Allah except in Him. Then He turned to them so they could repent. Indeed, Allah is the Accepting of repentance, the Merciful."

The verse is precise: it does not say "sinners" but describes their internal experience — the earth narrowing, the soul narrowing, the certainty that only Allah could be the refuge. This is the Quran's description of sincere tawbah: not a formulaic declaration but an existential narrowing until the only opening is toward Allah. The three companions who stayed behind without excuse, and who told the truth about it, experienced fifty days of the most complete social isolation imaginable — and came through it to a forgiveness that the Quran preserved permanently.

## The Principle Established

The case of the three is studied in Islamic jurisprudence and ethics for several principles: the obligation of truth even when it disadvantages the speaker, the permissibility of the social boycott as a disciplinary measure, the limits of repentance's requirement (patience through the trial rather than flight from it), and the relationship between divine mercy and the honesty that opens the door to it. Ka'b ibn Malik said afterward that among the greatest gifts Allah gave him through the trial was that he had not lied to the Prophet ﷺ — and that he resolved to never tell a lie for as long as he lived. He kept that resolution.
