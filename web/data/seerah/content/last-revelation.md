# The Last Revelation

## The Question of the Final Verse

The question of which Quranic verse was revealed last is among the most carefully studied questions in the sciences of the Quran ('ulum al-Quran). The scholars of Quranic sciences have collected and compared the narrations about the last revelation and found multiple narrations, each presenting a candidate verse with a chain of transmission and contextual argument. The classical scholars generally acknowledge that the question has more than one valid answer depending on the category of inquiry: the last verse revealed addressing a specific topic, the last legislative verse, the last verse to be placed in its surah sequence by the Prophet ﷺ, and the chronologically last revelation.

The most widely cited candidates are:

**Surah al-Ma'idah 5:3** — the Ikmal verse: "Today I have perfected for you your religion." Revealed at Arafat during the Farewell Pilgrimage in Dhul-Hijjah 10 AH. The narration in Sahih al-Bukhari is explicit about the occasion.

**Surah al-Baqarah 2:281** — "And fear a Day when you will be returned to Allah. Then every soul will be compensated for what it earned, and they will not be treated unjustly." This verse was placed at the end of the discussion of riba in Surah al-Baqarah, and a number of narrations hold it to be the last verse revealed.

**Surah al-Baqarah 2:282** — the longest verse in the Quran, dealing with the documentation of debts. Some narrations hold this as the last legislative verse.

**Surah al-Nasr (110)** — the surah about the opening and the multitudes entering Islam. Ibn Abbas is reported to have said this surah was the last surah to be revealed in its entirety.

## Ibn Abbas's Position

Ibn Abbas — the Prophet's ﷺ cousin and one of the most authoritative Quranic scholars among the companions — is reported to have held different positions at different times. One narration has him saying Surah al-Baqarah 2:281 was the last verse and that the Prophet ﷺ lived only nine more nights after it was revealed. Another narration from Ibn Abbas emphasizes Surah al-Nasr as the last complete surah.

The most widely accepted synthesis among classical scholars of tafsir — including al-Suyuti's comprehensive treatment in al-Itqan fi 'Ulum al-Quran — is that Surah al-Ma'idah 5:3 is the last verse in the sense of announcing completion (even if some verses were written after it), and that Surah al-Baqarah 2:281 or 2:282 is the last legislative verse in the strict sense of commanding a new ruling. The two positions are not necessarily contradictory.

## The Collection of the Quran During the Prophet's Lifetime

The final revelation arrived into a Quran that had been carefully ordered, preserved, and transmitted throughout the twenty-three years of prophetic mission. The Prophet ﷺ had dictated the placement of every verse — not just its content but its position within its surah and the surah's position within the whole — to his scribes. The companions Zayd ibn Thabit, Ali ibn Abi Talib, Ubayy ibn Ka'b, Muadh ibn Jabal, and others served as scribes. Thousands of companions had memorized portions or the entirety of the Quran.

In the final Ramadan of his life, the Prophet ﷺ reviewed the entire Quran with Jibril twice — a review that is preserved in the hadith as the final Ramadan review (the Prophet ﷺ would do one complete recitation with Jibril each Ramadan, but in the last Ramadan before his death they reviewed it twice). This was understood by the companions present as a sign that the revelation was complete and that the Prophet ﷺ knew his time was near.

## The Placement and Ordering

A distinction fundamental to Quranic sciences is that the surah order and the verse placement within surahs are not identical to the chronological order of revelation. The Quran as it exists — Surah al-Fatiha through Surah al-Nas, 114 surahs in the sequence Muslims recite today — was placed in that order by the Prophet ﷺ himself under divine guidance. Verses revealed early in the Meccan period appear in surahs that contain later-revealed verses; surahs revealed entirely in Medina appear after surahs revealed in Mecca. The arrangement is thematic and spiritual rather than chronological.

The last revelation thus belongs to two different categories simultaneously: as a historical event, it is a verse or surah that arrived in the Prophet's ﷺ consciousness in approximately Dhul-Hijjah 10 AH or in the weeks before his death; as a textual matter, it was placed within the Quran's existing structure in whatever position the Prophet ﷺ directed. The Quran that Muslims recite today is the text as the Prophet ﷺ arranged it — not a chronological archive of revelations but the Book in the form he authorized.

## What the End of Revelation Meant

For the companions who understood it, the completion of revelation was not simply the conclusion of a literary project. It was the closing of a direct channel between heaven and earth that had been open for twenty-three years. The Prophet ﷺ had received revelation throughout his mission — in Mecca and Medina, during battles and during peace, in joy and in grief, in the middle of a conversation and in seclusion. That channel closed with the final verse. The community that came after would have the Quran entire and the prophetic Sunnah fully documented, but no new verse would come to resolve new questions. That responsibility fell to the scholarship the community would build from the materials the Prophet ﷺ had left — and the scholars understood it as a sacred trust.

## The Final Ramadan Review

The tradition of the Prophet ﷺ reviewing the entire Quran with Jibril every Ramadan culminated in the final Ramadan of his life — 10 AH — when the review was conducted twice rather than once. Zayd ibn Thabit, who was one of the primary scribes, reported that the Prophet ﷺ told him this doubled review indicated that his death was near. The doubled review is understood by classical scholars as a final authorization of the Quran's complete text: every verse, in its place, exactly as it would be transmitted. After the doubled review, the Prophet ﷺ's task with the Quran was complete. He had received it, transmitted it, ordered it, and had it confirmed. The community he left behind would carry it forward in the form he had left — complete, preserved, and ready for the generations to come.
