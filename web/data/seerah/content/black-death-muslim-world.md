# The Black Death in the Muslim World: Plague, Providence, and Patience

## The Great Plague: Arriving from the East

The pandemic known in European history as the Black Death arrived in the Islamic world from Central Asia, traveling along the same trade routes that had carried silk, spices, and ideas across Eurasia for millennia. The precise origin of the plague — caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis* — remains debated among historians and epidemiologists, but the consensus places it in the Central Asian steppe regions in the 740s AH (late 1330s-early 1340s CE), from where it spread westward via the Crimea and the Golden Horde trade networks.

The disease reached the Levant and Egypt in approximately 749 AH (1347-1348 CE), the same year it entered Europe through Messina, Sicily. In the Islamic world it was called *al-ta'un* (the plague) or *al-waba'* (the epidemic), and more specifically this pandemic was called *al-ta'un al-Kabir* (the Great Plague) or *al-ta'un al-Jarif* (the Sweeping Plague). It was recognized immediately as something qualitatively different from the ordinary epidemic diseases that Islamic physicians had long treated.

The scale of mortality was unprecedented in recorded history. Modern epidemiologists estimate that the Black Death killed between one-third and one-half of the total population of Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa over the period 749-756 AH (1347-1356 CE). In Egypt, which may have been among the hardest-hit Islamic regions, the population fell from an estimated 8 million to 3-4 million. Cairo, the greatest city of the Islamic world, lost perhaps half its population in a matter of months. The Syrian cities of Damascus, Aleppo, and others were devastated.

## The Islamic Theological Framework: The Hadith of Plague

The single most important Islamic text for understanding the theological framework within which Muslims interpreted the Black Death is the hadith preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim on the nature of plague and the proper response to it.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "*al-Ta'un* (plague) is a punishment that Allah sends on whom He wills. But Allah has made it a mercy for the believers. There is no person who remains in a land where plague has broken out, with patience and expecting reward from Allah, knowing that nothing will befall him except what Allah has decreed for him, except that he will have the reward of a martyr" (al-Bukhari, Muslim).

This hadith established several principles simultaneously:

**Plague as divine decree**: It is a punishment sent by Allah on those He wills — not a random natural event outside divine sovereignty. This is the Quranic principle that nothing happens in creation without Allah's knowledge and permission.

**Plague as mercy for believers**: The same event that is a punishment for some is a mercy for others — those who die patiently while trusting in Allah receive the reward of martyrdom. The Prophet ﷺ said elsewhere: "The martyr who is killed for the sake of Allah, the martyr who dies of plague, the martyr who dies of drowning, the martyr who dies of a stomach illness — these are all martyrs" (Muslim, Abu Dawud).

**Mandatory patience and trust (*tawakkul*)**: The believer who stays in the plague-affected land (rather than fleeing) while maintaining patience and trust in Allah's decree receives martyrdom reward. This is not a command to be reckless, but a spiritual disposition: accepting what Allah has decreed while taking reasonable precautions.

**The prophylactic command**: The same authentic hadith corpus includes the Prophet's ﷺ saying: "If you hear of plague in a land, do not enter it. If it breaks out in the land where you are, do not leave it, fleeing from it" (al-Bukhari, Muslim). This is a sophisticated public health principle: quarantine of affected areas, prevention of transmission through movement.

## The Scholars Who Died

The Black Death was not merely a demographic catastrophe but a scholarly one. Plague does not discriminate by education or piety, and the 749-756 AH period saw the deaths of an extraordinary number of Islamic scholars across the Islamic world.

**Al-Dhahabi** (d. 748 AH): Died in Damascus just before the peak of the plague, though in its early phase. The greatest biographical scholar in Islamic history, author of *Siyar A'lam al-Nubala* and *Tarikh al-Islam*.

**Ibn al-Wardi** (d. 749 AH): A Syrian historian and literary scholar who wrote a famous eyewitness account of the plague's arrival in Aleppo, titled *Risala al-Naba' fi'l-Waba'* (Epistle of News about the Epidemic). He himself died of the plague shortly after completing this account — one of the most poignant ironies in Islamic literary history. His account describes the progression of symptoms, the rate of death, the paralysis of normal commerce and social life, and the inadequacy of any human response.

**Ibn Abi Hajalah al-Tilimsani** (d. 776 AH): Survived but wrote *Daf' al-Naqima fi al-Salat wal-Salami 'ala Sahib al-Rahma* (Repelling the Calamity through Sending Prayers on the Prophet ﷺ), the most famous Islamic text specifically addressing plague as a spiritual crisis requiring a spiritual response.

Across Egypt, Syria, Iraq, and North Africa, the scholarly infrastructure of Islamic civilization — teachers, students, hadith transmitters, jurists, physicians — was devastated. The loss of scholars represented not merely the death of individuals but the rupture of scholarly chains of transmission: teachers who had not yet transmitted their full knowledge to students, isnads (chains of hadith transmission) broken by simultaneous deaths.

Ibn Battuta, who was traveling in Morocco during the main wave of the plague, later described his return to Morocco to find family members dead. He described the scene in North Africa with evident grief.

## Ibn al-Wardi's Eyewitness Account

Ibn Siraj al-Din Umar ibn al-Wardi's *Risala al-Naba'* is a prose poem describing the plague's arrival and spread in Aleppo in 749 AH (1348-1349 CE). Its literary form — elegant Arabic rhymed prose (*saj'*) — reflects the literary training of its author even as it describes catastrophe. Some scholars have noted the tension between the beauty of the form and the horror of the content.

Ibn al-Wardi describes the plague's symptoms: the swellings in the groin and armpit (*buba*, from the Greek word *bubon*, giving the disease its modern name "bubonic plague"), the rapid progression, the high mortality rate. He describes the streets of Aleppo filling with the dead, the normal processes of washing and burial breaking down under the weight of sheer numbers, the living fearing to touch the dead (even family members).

He describes the public response: prayer, Quran recitation, charitable distribution, gathering in mosques to seek Allah's mercy. He describes the psychological impact — the paralysis of commerce, the abandonment of the city by those who could leave, the collapse of normal social obligation.

He wrote this account and died of the plague himself within weeks.

## The Scholarly Debate: Is Fleeing Permitted?

The hadith's instruction not to flee plague-affected areas generated immediate scholarly debate. Was this an absolute prohibition or a religious recommendation? Could a physician flee to treat patients elsewhere? Could a scholar leave to preserve himself for continued teaching?

The majority of scholars — following the apparent meaning of the hadith — held that leaving a plague-affected region was prohibited. Al-Nawawi (d. 676 AH), writing a century before the Black Death, had already addressed this question and concluded that the prohibition was operative: a Muslim in a plague-affected land should stay.

Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, writing after the Black Death in his *Badhl al-Ma'un fi Fadl al-Ta'un* (Exertion of Effort on the Excellence of Plague), provided the most comprehensive treatment of the jurisprudential questions raised by plague. He examined all the relevant hadith, considered all the scholarly positions, and provided an organized account of the Islamic theology and law of plague. His *Badhl al-Ma'un* is the most important Islamic text on the subject and was widely circulated in the subsequent centuries during later plague outbreaks.

## The Spiritual Response: Collective Prayer and Forgiveness

The Islamic response to the Black Death combined the practical (following the Prophet's ﷺ guidance on quarantine) with the spiritual (intensifying prayer, Quran recitation, seeking forgiveness, and charitable giving).

The principle that calamities are connected to sins — and that sincere repentance and supplication can avert or reduce divine punishment — is established in the Quran: *"Say: You are not going to be punished for what you used to do, I am only a warner for you"* (Surah Saba, 34:44) — and more directly in: *"And whatever strikes you of disaster — it is for what your hands have earned; but He pardons much"* (Surah al-Shura, 42:30).

The Quran also records that the Prophet Yunus (Jonah, peace be upon him) and his people were saved from divine punishment by sincere repentance: *"Was there any town that believed after seeing the punishment and their belief helped them? But people of Yunus — when they believed, We removed from them the punishment of disgrace in worldly life and gave them enjoyment for a time"* (Surah Yunus, 10:98).

This pattern of collective supplication during calamity has deep roots in Islamic practice. The Prophet ﷺ performed the special prayer (*salat al-istisqa'*) for rain during drought, gathering the community for communal supplication. Communities during the Black Death similarly gathered for communal prayer, supplication (*du'a*), and recitation of specific formulas like *Allahu Akbar* and *la ilaha illa Allah*.

## The Long-Term Demographic and Institutional Impact

The Black Death's effects on the Islamic world were profound and long-lasting. Egypt's population, as noted, may have taken over a century to recover. The Mamluk state, which had been at the height of its power before the plague, never fully recovered its demographic and economic base. Agricultural production fell as villages were depopulated. Tax revenues shrank. The scholarly and administrative infrastructure was depleted.

The plague also recurred: major outbreaks hit the Islamic world in approximately 764 AH (1362 CE), 791 AH (1389 CE), and repeatedly through the 9th-10th centuries AH (15th-16th centuries CE). Each recurrence killed populations that had not yet recovered their pre-plague numbers.

Ibn Khaldun, who survived the Black Death as a child (he was born in 732 AH / 1332 CE) and whose family lost many members to it, analyzed the plague's long-term effects in his *al-Muqaddimah*. He used it as a case study in civilizational decline: how demographic catastrophe disrupts the social solidarity (*'asabiyya*) that sustains civilization, how institutions lose the trained personnel needed to function, how the disruption cascades through economic and political systems.

## Trust in Allah: The Core Principle

The Islamic response to the Black Death, at its spiritual core, was an exercise in *tawakkul* (trust in Allah) and *sabr* (patience). The Prophet ﷺ established through multiple hadiths that the believer is never simply a victim of circumstances: every situation — prosperity and adversity alike — is an opportunity for spiritual growth. "How wonderful is the affair of the believer! There is goodness for him in everything, and that is for no one but the believer. If something good happens to him, he is grateful and that is good for him. If something bad happens to him, he bears it with patience and that is good for him" (Muslim).

The Black Death killed a third of the Islamic world's population. It was — by any human measure — a catastrophe of incomprehensible scale. And yet the Islamic scholarly tradition responded not with despair or abandonment of faith, but with deeper trust in Allah's wisdom, renewed commitment to the obligations He had established, and the clear understanding that the believers who died in the plague had received an honor — the rank of martyrdom — that no enemy could confer and no disease could prevent.
