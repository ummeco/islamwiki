# The Birth of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ

## The Most Blessed Birth in Human History

The birth of Muhammad ibn Abdullah ﷺ in Mecca is the most consequential birth in human history — the arrival of the man who would deliver the final revelation from Allah to all of humanity, whose message transformed the world, and whose ummah would number over a billion souls. Yet in the moment of his birth, only a small circle of people in a dusty Arabian city knew that anything extraordinary had occurred. The world-changing nature of this birth would unfold gradually, across decades, until the light he brought illuminated every corner of the earth.

## The Time and Place

The Prophet ﷺ was born in Mecca, in the neighborhood of Banu Hashim, in a house that later tradition identifies as located in what is now the area of al-Mawlid in the old city. The majority of classical scholars — including Ibn Ishaq, Ibn Hisham, and Ibn Kathir — hold that he was born on a Monday in the month of Rabi al-Awwal, in the Year of the Elephant (approximately 570 CE). The Prophet ﷺ himself confirmed the day of the week when asked about fasting on Monday: he said, "That is the day I was born and the day revelation came to me."

The precise date within Rabi al-Awwal has been disputed among scholars since the earliest generations. The twelfth of Rabi al-Awwal has become the most widely cited date in popular tradition. Some scholars, including Ibn Hazm and later researchers who worked backward from astronomical calculations, have argued for the ninth. Still others place it on the second. The disagreement does not affect any matter of religious practice — there is no prescribed observance tied to the birth date — but the scholarly discussion demonstrates the care with which Islamic historians tracked the Prophet's ﷺ biography.

## Signs at the Birth

Islamic tradition records remarkable signs surrounding the Prophet's ﷺ birth. His mother Aminah later reported that at the moment of his birth, a light emerged from her that illuminated the palaces of Busra in Syria — a distance of over a thousand miles. This report was later confirmed by narrations attributed to the Prophet's companion Irbad ibn Sariya and others.

In the wider world, according to narrations in Ibn Kathir and other classical historians, several signs occurred the night of the Prophet's ﷺ birth: fourteen pillars of the palace of the Persian emperor Khosrow (Kisra) cracked and fell; the sacred fire of the Zoroastrian Persians — which had burned without interruption for a thousand years — was extinguished; and the lake of Sawah in Persia dried up. These narrations, taken together, represent the collapse of the old world orders — the Persian and Byzantine empires that had dominated the known world — in mystical anticipation of the new order that this birth would bring.

Aminah is reported to have seen angels who attended to her and heard a voice announcing: "You have given birth to the master of this ummah; name him Muhammad." She named him Muhammad — a name virtually unknown in Arabia before him.

## The Name Muhammad

The name Muhammad — "the Praised One" — was chosen by Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's grandfather, who reportedly heard it in a dream or vision before the birth, or who chose it based on its meaning without knowing the prophecies that attended it. When the Quraysh asked why he had chosen an unfamiliar name rather than a traditional ancestral name, Abd al-Muttalib reportedly said: "I wanted Allah to praise him in heaven and his creation to praise him on earth."

The name proved prophetic beyond any human ability to plan. Muhammad — the praised, the highly praised — is both the Prophet's name and a description of his status in the heavenly and earthly realms. In the Quran, one of his names is Ahmad (also meaning "the Most Praised"), referenced in Surah al-Saff (61:6) as the name by which 'Isa (Jesus) prophesied his coming. The Prophet himself said: "I have five names: I am Muhammad, I am Ahmad, I am al-Mahi through whom Allah erases disbelief, I am al-Hashir at whose feet people will be gathered, and I am al-'Aqib after whom there is no prophet." (Bukhari and Muslim)

## The Orphan Who Arrived

The Prophet ﷺ was born into loss. His father Abdullah had died months earlier in Medina, on his return from a trade journey, leaving Aminah a widow. The Prophet ﷺ was thus born fatherless — an orphan from before birth, in the language of the scholars. This condition is explicitly referenced by Allah in Surah ad-Duha: "Did He not find you an orphan and give you refuge?" (93:6)

Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's grandfather, took immediate responsibility for the child. According to Ibn Hisham's account, Abd al-Muttalib received news of the birth and went to Aminah. He carried the newborn to the Kaaba, prayed, and gave him his name Muhammad. The grandfather's love for this child was remarkable — more devoted than his love for any of his own children, the sources say, because Abd al-Muttalib perceived in the child something extraordinary, even before prophethood.

## The Circumcision and the Aqiqah

Abd al-Muttalib had the Prophet ﷺ circumcised on the seventh day after his birth and performed the aqiqah (sacrifice of animals at the birth of a child). The sacrifice and the naming on the seventh day became the established sunnah practice for newborn Muslims, following the tradition that the Prophet ﷺ later confirmed: "Every child is mortgaged by his aqiqah, which is slaughtered for him on his seventh day, and he is named and his head is shaved." (Abu Dawud, al-Tirmidhi)

## The World That Received Him

The world into which the Prophet ﷺ was born in 570 CE was a world of darkness by the Quran's own description — the age of jahiliyyah (ignorance). Arabia was polytheist, with hundreds of idols clustered around the Kaaba. Girls were buried alive at birth in parts of the peninsula. Slavery, tribal warfare, and social inequality dominated society. The Byzantine and Sassanid empires, the two great powers of the age, were in mutual exhaustion from perpetual warfare. Judaism had lost its prophetic chain, Christianity had fractured into competing sects and had drifted far from the monotheism of 'Isa. The world was spiritually ready for the final divine message — and the bearer of that message had arrived.

Within forty years of this birth, this same Muhammad ﷺ would transform Arabia from a fragmented polytheist society into a unified Muslim ummah. Within a century of his birth, Muslims would carry his message from the Atlantic coast of Spain to the borders of China. The light that Aminah reported at his birth — illuminating the palaces of Syria — was the beginning of an illumination that has never been extinguished.
