# Death of Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni

Abd al-Malik ibn Abdullah ibn Yusuf al-Juwayni (419-478 AH / 1028-1085 CE), known as Imam al-Haramayn (the Imam of the Two Holy Sanctuaries), was the foremost Shafi'i jurist and Ash'ari theologian of his generation and the teacher of al-Ghazali. His death in 478 AH (1085 CE) marked the end of a pivotal career in Islamic jurisprudence and theology that shaped the subsequent development of Sunni scholarship.

## Life

Al-Juwayni was born in the town of Juwain in Khurasan (present-day Iran) in 419 AH (1028 CE). His father, also a distinguished jurist, began his education. When his father died, al-Juwayni was still a young student, and he completed his education under his father's own teachers.

He came to prominence as a jurist and theologian in Nishapur, the great center of Shafi'i and Ash'ari scholarship in Khurasan. When the Ash'ari school faced persecution under the Hanbali-influenced policy of the Seljuk wazir al-Kunduri around 445 AH (1053 CE), al-Juwayni left Nishapur and spent approximately four years in Mecca and Medina — hence the honorific "Imam of the Two Holy Sanctuaries" — before returning when the political climate changed.

Upon his return, under the patronage of the great Seljuk wazir Nizam al-Mulk, he was appointed to head the newly established Nizamiyya madrasa in Nishapur, one of the first great institutional madrasas in Islamic history. He taught there for approximately thirty years until his death.

## Contribution to Jurisprudence

Al-Juwayni's major jurisprudential works include:

**Nihayat al-Matlab fi Dirayat al-Madhhab** (The Ultimate Goal in Understanding the School) — an enormous work of Shafi'i fiqh in approximately 40 volumes in the modern edition, arguably the most comprehensive treatment of the Shafi'i school ever produced.

**Al-Burhan fi Usul al-Fiqh** (The Proof in the Principles of Jurisprudence) — a foundational work on Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh) that introduced significant innovations in how juristic reasoning is conceptualized, particularly the concept of masalih (interests) as a basis for legal reasoning.

**Al-Waraqat** (The Leaflets) — a brief primer on the principles of jurisprudence that became a widely used teaching text, memorized by students across the Islamic world to this day.

## Contribution to Theology

Al-Juwayni was a leading defender and developer of Ash'ari theology. His major theological works include:

**Al-Irshad ila Qawati al-Adilla fi Usul al-Itiqad** (The Guide to the Decisive Proofs in the Principles of Belief) — a systematic exposition of Ash'ari theological positions.

**Al-Shamil fi Usul al-Din** (The Comprehensive Work on the Principles of Religion) — a more advanced theological work.

His theological writing is known for its clarity and its attempt to provide rigorously rational defenses of Ash'ari positions.

## Al-Ghazali's Teacher

Al-Juwayni's most significant contribution to Islamic intellectual history may have been indirect: he was the primary teacher of Abu Hamid al-Ghazali, one of the most influential figures in Islamic history. Al-Ghazali attended al-Juwayni's lectures in Nishapur from his youth and, by al-Juwayni's own admission, surpassed all his other students in ability.

When al-Juwayni died in 478 AH (1085 CE), al-Ghazali moved to the court of Nizam al-Mulk and then to Baghdad, where he would write the *Ihya Ulum al-Din* and transform Islamic spiritual life. The great synthesis of law, theology, and spirituality that characterized al-Ghazali's work was built on the jurisprudential and theological foundations that al-Juwayni had provided.

## Death and Legacy

Al-Juwayni died in Nishapur in Rabi al-Awwal 478 AH (August 1085 CE). Contemporary accounts describe al-Ghazali weeping at his death and covering his face with the mourning scarf.

His works in jurisprudence and theology remained standard references in the Shafi'i school. The *Waraqat* continues to be one of the first texts studied by students of Islamic jurisprudence. His theoretical innovations in Islamic legal reasoning influenced all subsequent development of usul al-fiqh.
