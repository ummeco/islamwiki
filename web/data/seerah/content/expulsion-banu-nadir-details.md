# Detailed Account of the Expulsion of Banu Nadir

The expulsion of Banu Nadir from Medina in Rabi al-Awwal, 4 AH (July 625 CE) was a pivotal moment in the early Muslim community's relationship with the Jewish tribes of Medina, and one of the major events that shaped the political landscape of the Hijaz in the years before the conquest of Mecca. The event is referenced directly in the Quran in Surah al-Hashr (Chapter 59).

## Background: Banu Nadir

Banu Nadir was one of the three major Jewish tribes of Medina, along with Banu Qaynuqa (who had already been expelled in 2 AH) and Banu Qurayza. They lived in a fortified settlement (the banu Nadir quarter) south of Medina's main residential area. They were wealthier and more established than the other tribes, engaged in agriculture and money-lending, and had long-standing relationships with both the Arab tribes of Medina and with the wider Arabian economy.

They were party to the Constitution of Medina and had treaty obligations toward the Muslim community.

## The Alleged Assassination Plot

The immediate cause of the confrontation was a reported assassination plot. The Prophet went to the Banu Nadir quarter in connection with a blood money obligation — seeking their financial contribution as required by the Medina agreements. According to the seerah accounts, while the Prophet was sitting by the wall of a Banu Nadir house, some of them planned to drop a large stone on him from the rooftop. The Prophet received information about this plan through revelation and rose quietly and left.

Upon returning to Medina, he sent a message to Banu Nadir giving them ten days to leave, warning that anyone found remaining in Medina after that time would be killed.

## Abdullah ibn Ubayy's Intervention

Abdullah ibn Ubayy, the Medinan leader who had been passed over for the leadership position that fell to the Prophet's authority, sent a message to Banu Nadir encouraging them to refuse the expulsion order. He promised them support from the hypocrites (munafiqun) and from Banu Qurayza, suggesting that if they stood firm, the combined forces would be sufficient to resist the Prophet.

Banu Nadir, emboldened by this promise, refused to leave and began fortifying their positions.

## The Siege

The Prophet marched to the Banu Nadir quarter and besieged their fortresses. The support promised by Abdullah ibn Ubayy and the other munafiqun did not materialize — they did not send fighters, and their promises proved empty. The Quran comments on this: "Have you not seen those who are hypocrites, saying to their brothers who have disbelieved among the People of the Scripture, 'If you are expelled, we will surely leave with you, and we will never obey anyone against you'..." (Quran 59:11-12)

After a siege of approximately fifteen days (some accounts say six days), Banu Nadir agreed to leave. They were permitted to take with them what they could carry on camels, except weapons. They took their wealth, their household goods, and their families. They demolished their own houses before leaving — taking the wooden beams and doors with them to prevent the Muslims from using them.

## The Destination

The majority of Banu Nadir went to Khaybar — the large oasis settlement approximately 150 km north of Medina that had a significant Jewish population. Some went further, to Syria. Their leaders, including the chief Huyayy ibn Akhtab, settled in Khaybar and became the nucleus of the subsequent political and military opposition to the Muslim community.

It was Huyayy ibn Akhtab who would later broker the alliance with the Quraysh-led Confederate army, and who convinced Banu Qurayza to break their treaty with the Muslims during the Battle of the Trench.

## The Quranic Account: Surah al-Hashr

The expulsion of Banu Nadir is the subject of Surah al-Hashr (Chapter 59 of the Quran). The surah addresses the event directly:

*"It is He who expelled the ones who disbelieved among the People of the Scripture from their homes at the first gathering."* (59:2)

The surah reflects on the swiftness of Banu Nadir's fall — they had fortified positions and allies but could not prevail — and attributes it to Allah's decree. It also contains the famous verse establishing the principle that war spoils taken from the enemy without fighting (fay') go to the Prophet and the general Muslim treasury rather than to individual fighters.

The surah concludes with some of the Quran's most celebrated descriptions of Allah's attributes — passages that became foundational texts for Islamic theology.

## The Distribution of Lands

The lands and property left behind by Banu Nadir were distributed by the Prophet primarily among the Muhajirun (the Meccan emigrants) — men who had left their own property behind in Mecca when they emigrated and had been dependent on the generosity of the Ansar (Medinan Muslims). This distribution gave the Muhajirun an economic footing and reduced the financial burden on the Ansar who had been hosting them.

Two Ansar — Abu Dujanah and Sahl ibn Hunayf — who were genuinely in financial need received shares as well.

## Sources

The expulsion of Banu Nadir is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and the tafsir literature for Surah al-Hashr.
