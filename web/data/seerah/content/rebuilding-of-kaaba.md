# The Rebuilding of the Ka'bah and the Placement of the Black Stone

## The Ka'bah Before the Rebuilding

The Ka'bah — the cubic structure at the center of the Sacred Mosque in Mecca — is understood in Islamic theology to be the first house established for the worship of Allah on earth, originally built by Ibrahim and Ismail as a place of pure monotheism. By the time of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the structure had been rebuilt and altered multiple times across the centuries, and the building the Quraysh maintained was a simplified version of the Ibrahimic original.

Approximately five years before the first revelation — when the Prophet ﷺ was approximately thirty-five years old — the Ka'bah was damaged. The accounts describe a flood that weakened the walls, and a fire that further compromised the structure. The Quraysh decided to tear it down and rebuild it completely. This was a major undertaking: the Ka'bah was the most sacred site in Arabia, and handling it required care, purity, and consensus.

## The Reconstruction

The Quraysh organized the rebuilding by clan: each clan was assigned a section of the wall to construct, distributing both the labor and the honor among the major families of Quraysh. The Prophet ﷺ participated actively in this reconstruction — carrying stones from the mountains surrounding Mecca along with the other men. His uncle al-Abbas is reported to have suggested that he remove his lower garment to cushion the stones on his shoulder, but the Prophet ﷺ was immediately struck with a sense of modesty and did not follow this suggestion.

The stones of the Ka'bah were considered sacred, and there was particular concern about which materials to use. When a Byzantine craftsman offered his skills — the only person with the technical expertise to build the arched ceiling and construct the structure properly — the Quraysh accepted his assistance. The result was a more solidly built Ka'bah than they had previously maintained.

One significant departure from the Ibrahimic design: the Quraysh did not have enough money from what they considered pure sources (no trade money that came from harlotry, usury, or injustice) to complete the Ka'bah to its original Ibrahimic dimensions. They left out the semicircular area on the northwest side — the Hijr Ismail — which was originally part of the Ka'bah's interior but was now enclosed by a low curved wall (al-Hatim) outside the main structure. This is why the Prophet ﷺ would tell Aisha, many years later: "Were it not that your people have recently left idolatry, I would tear down the Ka'bah and rebuild it on the foundations of Ibrahim." He also told her that the Hijr Ismail is part of the Ka'bah, and that praying two rak'ahs inside the Hijr is equivalent to praying inside the Ka'bah.

## The Crisis of the Black Stone

The most dramatic moment of the entire reconstruction was the placement of the Black Stone (al-Hajar al-Aswad). The Black Stone — a dark, irregularly shaped stone set in the eastern corner of the Ka'bah — held unique sacred significance. Its origin is described in the hadith as having descended from Paradise; it was set in the Ka'bah in the time of Ibrahim, and pilgrims had touched and kissed it for generations. Whoever placed it in its position would receive immense honor — and whoever felt cheated of that honor might feel entitled to retaliation.

When the reconstruction reached the point where the Black Stone needed to be placed, the Quraysh fell into a dispute that threatened to become violent. Each clan wanted the honor of placing the stone. Leaders from the major clans — Banu Makhzum, Banu Abd al-Dar, and others — prepared for battle. Some accounts record that they dipped their hands in a bowl of blood, pledging to fight until death rather than yield the honor.

Four or five days of standoff passed. Then one of the elder men of Quraysh proposed a solution: whoever enters through the gate of the mosque next should be their judge. The assembled Quraysh agreed. The first person to enter was Muhammad ibn Abdullah ﷺ — the man they had called al-Amin, the Trustworthy. When they saw who it was, they called out with relief: "This is al-Amin! We accept him as judge!"

## The Prophet's Solution

The Prophet ﷺ did not take the honor for himself or assign it to any single clan. He asked for a large cloth to be brought and laid flat on the ground. He then placed the Black Stone in the center of the cloth himself. He asked the elders of each clan to take hold of a corner or edge of the cloth. Together, they all lifted the cloth and carried the stone to the appropriate height in the Ka'bah's wall. The Prophet ﷺ then took the stone from the cloth and placed it into the wall with his own hands.

Every clan had participated. No clan had been excluded. The honor was shared. The conflict was resolved without violence.

## The Significance

This episode — occurring approximately five years before the first revelation — reveals several qualities that would define the prophetic mission for the next twenty-three years.

First, the instinct for justice that includes all parties: the Prophet ﷺ did not simply assign the honor to the most powerful clan or to his own. He designed a solution in which every party was represented and honored.

Second, the practical creativity: rather than applying an existing formula or tradition, he invented a new mechanism on the spot — using the cloth as a shared carrier. This kind of improvised practical wisdom under pressure would characterize his leadership throughout the prophetic period.

Third, the unanimous trust: the Quraysh — who had been prepared for bloodshed moments earlier — all accepted his judgment immediately upon seeing him enter. The community had already, by this point, designated him as the man whose judgment they would accept without argument. His reputation for trustworthiness was so complete that it dissolved a crisis that had been building toward violence.
