# Detailed Distribution of Hunayn War Spoils

The distribution of the war spoils from the Battle of Hunayn in Shawwal 8 AH was one of the most consequential and controversial administrative acts of the Prophet Muhammad's (peace and blessings be upon him) career — producing a crisis among the Ansar (Medinan Muslims) who felt that their central role in the victory had been overlooked in favor of newly converted Meccans. The Prophet's resolution of the controversy became one of the most celebrated speeches in Islamic biographical literature.

## The Scale of the Spoils

The Battle of Hunayn produced an extraordinary quantity of spoils from the Hawazin and Thaqif tribal confederation:

- **6,000 captives** (men, women, and children)
- **24,000 camels**
- **40,000 sheep and goats**
- **4,000 silver coins (awaqiyah)**

This was the largest single collection of war spoils in the seerah — reflecting both the scale of the Hawazin tribal coalition and the wealth they had brought with them into the battle (their commander Malik ibn Awf had controversially ordered the fighters to march with all their families, livestock, and property).

The Prophet collected the spoils at Ji'irranah, a stopping place between Mecca and Hunayn, where he also distributed them.

## The Distribution

The Prophet gave extraordinarily large shares to the leaders and notables of the tribes and cities that had recently come under Muslim authority — particularly the leaders of Mecca who had accepted Islam just weeks earlier:

- Abu Sufyan ibn Harb received **100 camels and 40 gold pieces**
- His son Muawiyah received a similar large share
- Safwan ibn Umayyah received **100 camels, then another 100, then another 100** (three separate distributions, each time he asked for more and the Prophet gave)
- Hakim ibn Hizam received **100 camels**
- Various other leaders of the Tulaqa (the newly freed Meccans) received large shares

These men were called the "Mu'allifat Qulubuhum" — "those whose hearts were to be reconciled" — a specific category recognized in the Quranic distribution of zakah (Quran 9:60). The large gifts were a political investment: they were designed to consolidate these influential men's commitment to Islam and to prevent them from destabilizing the newly established Muslim authority over Mecca.

The Ansar received no extra shares beyond the standard fighter's portion.

## The Ansar's Grievance

The Ansar — who had borne the greatest risks and sacrifices over years of supporting the Muslim community, who had hosted the Muhajirun for years, and who had fought fiercely at Hunayn when the Meccan converts had fled — received no special allocation. Some Ansar men said among themselves: "By Allah, this is strange. Our swords are still dripping with the blood of Quraysh, yet our spoils are given to them."

When this reached the Prophet, he called the Ansar to a private gathering — excluding everyone else.

## The Prophet's Address to the Ansar

The Prophet said to them:

*"O Ansar, what is this that I hear you say?"*

When they acknowledged the report, he continued:

*"O Ansar, did I not find you astray and Allah guided you through me? Were you not poor and Allah enriched you through me? Were you not enemies and Allah united your hearts through me?"*

They replied: "Yes, to Allah and His Messenger belongs the favor and the grace."

Then he said:

*"Why do you not answer, O Ansar?"*

They replied: "What answer should we give, O Messenger of Allah? The favor and the grace belong to Allah and His Messenger."

He said:

*"By Allah, if you wished, you could say — and you would speak the truth and be believed — 'You came to us rejected and we believed you. You came to us afraid and we sheltered you. You came to us expelled and we gave you a home. You came to us poor and we shared our wealth with you.'*

*O Ansar, are you troubled about worldly things that I used to reconcile people through, because I knew your Islam was firm? Does it not please you, O Ansar, that the people take camels and sheep back to their homes, while you take the Messenger of Allah back to yours?*

*By the One in whose Hand is my soul, if it were not for the hijra I would be a man of Ansar. If all people went one way and the Ansar went another, I would go the Ansar's way.*

O Allah, have mercy on the Ansar, and the children of Ansar, and the grandchildren of Ansar."*

## The Response

The Ansar wept until their beards were wet with tears and said: "We are satisfied with the Messenger of Allah as our portion and our lot."

## Significance

**The concept of Mu'allifat Qulubuhum.** The distribution at Hunayn established the jurisprudential category of "those whose hearts are to be reconciled" — one of the eight categories of zakah recipients specified in Quran 9:60. The Prophet gave not from zakah funds but from war spoils; however, the principle of using material resources to consolidate political and religious commitments of influential individuals became part of Islamic governance theory.

**The primacy of spiritual over material reward.** The Prophet's address to the Ansar inverted the ordinary logic of reward: yes, others received the camels — but the Ansar received the Prophet himself, his permanent residence in their city, and the honor of being his hosts and his people. This redirection from material to spiritual accounting is one of the most celebrated rhetorical moves in the seerah literature.

**The emotional authenticity of the encounter.** The Prophet's speech, his tears (reported in some accounts), and the Ansar's weeping response make this one of the most humanly immediate episodes in the seerah — a moment of genuine emotional communication between a community and its leader.

## Sources

The distribution of Hunayn spoils and the Prophet's address to the Ansar are in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi) and Sahih Muslim (Kitab al-Zakat), and the biographical works of Ibn Hisham, al-Tabari, and Ibn Saad.
