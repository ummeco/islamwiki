# Amr ibn Abasah al-Sulami: The Conversion of a Solitary Seeker

## A Monotheist Before the Message

In the vast expanse of pre-Islamic Arabia, where the Ka'bah had become a house of 360 idols and polytheism was the social fabric of Meccan life, there existed a small category of individuals known as the hunafa (singular: hanif) — monotheists who had rejected idol worship through the force of reason and spiritual intuition alone. These were men and women who had arrived at the recognition of one God without the benefit of revelation, guided only by the fitra (the innate human inclination toward the divine) and their refusal to accept what they could see was false.

Amr ibn Abasah al-Sulami (may Allah be pleased with him) was one of these people. He came from the Banu Sulaym tribe, a powerful Arabian tribal confederation that inhabited the region between the Hijaz and Najd. Before any prophet had appeared, before the word Islam had been spoken in public, before the Prophet ﷺ had received the first word of revelation, Amr had already rejected the idols of his people. He refused to bow to them. He saw in them nothing that deserved worship. He believed that there was a Creator who was one and that the stone figures of his tribe were nothing but human inventions.

This solitary monotheism — held without community, without scripture, without a prophet's guidance — required a kind of intellectual and moral courage that deserves recognition. Amr did not know precisely what he believed in the positive sense. He knew what he rejected. He was a man waiting for the truth to be named.

## The Journey to Mecca

When Amr ibn Abasah heard that a man in Mecca was claiming prophethood and calling people to the worship of one God, he did not wait. He traveled to Mecca alone — no companion, no guide — to investigate for himself. The reports of the Prophet ﷺ had reached even distant tribes through the networks of trade and oral communication that connected the Arabian Peninsula. Amr heard enough to be curious, and curiosity in a man already primed by years of personal monotheism quickly became urgency.

The early Muslim community at this point was small and exposed. The Prophet ﷺ had not yet made his call publicly. He was meeting with people privately, cautiously, aware that the Quraysh's hostility was already forming. A stranger from Banu Sulaym arriving to ask about this new message was therefore not someone the Prophet ﷺ could receive openly.

The detailed account of their meeting is preserved in Sahih Muslim — one of the two most authoritative hadith collections in Sunni Islam. The chain of transmission for this hadith runs through Abu Umamah al-Bahili, who heard Amr narrate it himself in old age. The hadith is remarkable for its length, its detail, and the directness of the theological exchange it records.

## The Conversation with the Prophet

Amr ibn Abasah narrates in Sahih Muslim: "When the Prophet ﷺ appeared, I came to him and said: 'What are you?' He said: 'I am a Prophet.' I said: 'What is a Prophet?' He said: 'I was sent by Allah.' I said: 'Sent with what?' He said: 'Sent with maintaining ties of kinship, breaking idols, and establishing the oneness of Allah without associating anything with Him.'"

Amr then asked: "Who is with you in this?" The Prophet ﷺ said: "A free man and a slave" — meaning Abu Bakr and Bilal. Amr then said: "I follow you." The Prophet ﷺ said: "You cannot do so now. Do you not see the state I am in? Go back to your people, and when you hear that I have prevailed, come to me."

This instruction from the Prophet ﷺ is itself significant. He recognized that Amr's safety in Mecca could not be guaranteed. He sent him back to his tribe — not turning him away from Islam, but protecting him and asking him to wait. Amr returned to his people as a Muslim, but a secret one, living among polytheists while holding the faith in his heart.

## Years of Hidden Faith

Amr ibn Abasah (may Allah be pleased with him) remained with his tribe for years. He was a Muslim during the entire Meccan period — the period of persecution, of the Hijrah to Abyssinia, of the boycott of the Hashimites — without being able to openly practice or speak of his Islam. He was not the only Muslim in this situation; there were others in various parts of Arabia who had accepted the message but remained hidden among their non-Muslim communities. But Amr's case is particularly well documented because of the detailed hadith he narrated in his old age.

When the Prophet ﷺ emigrated to Medina and the Islamic state began to take form, when the message was no longer secret but declared and defended, Amr kept his promise. He came to Medina to join the Prophet ﷺ and the Muslim community. When he arrived, the Prophet ﷺ recognized him and welcomed him.

## The Long Hadith on Prayer and Purification

The most consequential narration attributed to Amr ibn Abasah (may Allah be pleased with him) is a long hadith in Sahih Muslim in which he reports an extended conversation with the Prophet ﷺ about the times of prayer and the spiritual benefits of ritual purification and worship. This hadith is one of the most detailed accounts of the theological and practical dimensions of Islamic prayer preserved in the canonical collections.

In it, the Prophet ﷺ explains to Amr the relationship between each prayer time and its spiritual value. Among the most famous passages: "Is there a man who performs wudu (ablution), performs it well, then prays two rak'ahs directing his face and his heart — except that Paradise becomes obligatory for him?" The Prophet ﷺ also explains the spiritual significance of the pre-dawn hours: "The closest Allah is to His servant is in the latter part of the night. If you are able to be among those who remember Allah at that hour, do so."

The Prophet ﷺ also said in this narration, about the moment of dawn: "The prayer at that time is witnessed and attended [by angels], and it is better to stop reciting the Quran at that point" — referring to the pause between the azan of Fajr and the beginning of the prayer. This guidance about the specific spiritual virtues of different prayer times, as recorded by Amr, has been studied and commented upon by scholars throughout Islamic history.

Al-Nawawi, in his commentary on Sahih Muslim (al-Minhaj), dedicates substantial discussion to this hadith and describes it as one of the most comprehensive records of prophetic teaching on the virtues of prayer. The chain of transmission through Abu Umamah to Amr himself is considered sound (sahih) by the hadith scholars.

## His Status as an Early Convert from Outside Quraysh

Amr ibn Abasah occupies a unique position in the typology of early Muslim converts. Most of the early companions came from within the Quraysh or from the tribes immediately connected to Mecca through commercial and social ties. Amr came from Banu Sulaym — a tribe from a different region entirely, without the direct social proximity to the Prophet ﷺ that characterized the Meccan converts.

His conversion was based entirely on investigation. He heard a report, traveled alone, asked his questions, received his answers, and accepted. There was no clan solidarity, no social network, no economic incentive. There was only the encounter between a sincere mind that had already rejected falsehood and a message that confirmed and completed what that mind had always dimly perceived.

This makes him a significant figure not only in the history of the early Muslim community but in the broader Islamic understanding of how the human heart responds to truth. The fitra — the innate disposition toward monotheism that the Prophet ﷺ described in the famous hadith "every child is born upon the fitra" (Bukhari and Muslim) — had been alive and functioning in Amr long before revelation reached him. When revelation arrived, it found a prepared vessel.

## His Long Life and Continued Transmission

Amr ibn Abasah (may Allah be pleased with him) lived to old age and continued to transmit what he had learned from the Prophet ﷺ to subsequent generations. He settled in Syria after the Islamic conquests and was known there as one of the senior companions whose knowledge of the Prophet's teachings was firsthand.

The hadith scholars note that his transmitted narrations are relatively few in number compared to companions like Abu Hurayrah or Aisha, which is consistent with his biography — he spent years isolated from the main Muslim community, then lived in Syria rather than the major hadith-transmission centers of Medina and Kufa. But the narrations he did transmit, particularly the long hadith in Sahih Muslim about prayer and purification, are among the most substantive in the canonical collections.

Al-Dhahabi mentions him in Siyar A'lam al-Nubala and notes his distinction as one of the very earliest converts from outside the Quraysh and the Meccan social orbit. Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani discusses him in al-Isabah fi Tamyiz al-Sahabah and confirms the historical details of his early conversion.

## Lessons from His Conversion

The story of Amr ibn Abasah carries several lessons that Islamic scholarship has consistently drawn upon. First, it confirms that the search for truth is itself a form of worship — that the person who sincerely rejects falsehood and seeks the One God is in a state of fitra-based conformity with the divine design, even before formal guidance reaches them.

Second, it demonstrates the Prophet's ﷺ pastoral wisdom: he sent Amr back to his people rather than encouraging him to remain in Mecca as a barely-protected secret convert. He protected Amr's life and gave him a framework for practicing his faith in difficult circumstances. This shows that the Prophet ﷺ understood his companions as individuals with specific vulnerabilities and circumstances, not merely as agents to be deployed for the movement's benefit.

Third, the richness of the theological exchange recorded in the Sahih Muslim hadith illustrates the Prophet's capacity for deep, patient teaching. He did not give Amr a brief catechism. He engaged with him at length, explaining the why behind the what, connecting physical acts of purification and prayer to their spiritual significance. This is the model of prophetic pedagogy: meeting the questioner where they are and building understanding from the ground up.

Amr ibn Abasah (may Allah be pleased with him) stands as a reminder that the light of tawhid — the recognition of the oneness of Allah — can burn in a human heart even in the most unlikely circumstances, waiting for the revelation that will name it, confirm it, and complete it.
