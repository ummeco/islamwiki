# The Bosnian Genocide: Muslims in Europe and the Failure of the International Community

## The Return of Genocide to Europe

Between 1992 and 1995 CE (1412-1416 AH), in the heart of Europe, a genocide was carried out against the Bosnian Muslim population — known as Bosniaks — as Yugoslavia dissolved into ethnic and religious conflict. The war in Bosnia-Herzegovina resulted in the deaths of approximately 100,000 people, the displacement of over 2 million, systematic campaigns of mass rape used as a weapon of war, the destruction of hundreds of mosques and Islamic heritage sites, and the Srebrenica massacre of July 1995 — the worst single atrocity on European soil since World War II, in which over 8,000 Bosniak Muslim men and boys were executed by Bosnian Serb forces.

The Bosnian genocide demonstrated that large-scale organized killing of Muslims could occur in the middle of Europe, under the observation of international institutions, while the world debated and delayed. It also revealed the depth of the Muslim world's inability to protect Muslim communities despite the existence of Muslim-majority states, international organizations, and the legacy of decades of pan-Islamic rhetoric.

## Yugoslavia's Dissolution and Bosnia's Position

The Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia — a multiethnic state created after World War II under Josip Broz Tito — held together through communist ideology and Tito's personal authority. After Tito's death in 1980, the federation began to fragment along ethnic and national lines. With the collapse of communism across Eastern Europe in 1989-1991, Yugoslavia's constituent republics moved toward independence.

Bosnia-Herzegovina was the most ethnically mixed of Yugoslavia's republics. Its population at the time of the 1991 census was approximately 44% Bosniak Muslim, 31% Bosnian Serb (Orthodox Christian), and 17% Bosnian Croat (Catholic). The Bosniaks were the descendants of a significant portion of the medieval Bosnian population that had converted to Islam during the Ottoman period (roughly the 15th-19th centuries). They had maintained a distinct Muslim identity for five centuries, marked by Islamic faith, Ottoman-influenced culture, and a tradition of European Sunni Muslim practice.

When Bosnia declared independence in 1992 — recognized by the European Community and the United States — the Bosnian Serb political leadership, backed by the rump Yugoslav Army and the government of Serbia under Slobodan Milosevic, launched a military campaign to partition Bosnia and attach the predominantly Serb-inhabited areas to Serbia. The explicitly stated goal of the Bosnian Serb leadership — including Radovan Karadzic (later convicted of genocide by the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia) and General Ratko Mladic (also convicted of genocide) — was ethnic cleansing: the removal by any means of the Muslim population from areas claimed for a greater Serbia.

## Ethnic Cleansing: The Systematic Campaign

The campaign of ethnic cleansing was systematic and organized. It included:

**Mass killings**: Village-by-village executions of Muslim men, often after separation from women and children. Survivors were expelled; the bodies of those killed were often buried in mass graves (many subsequently discovered during and after the war).

**Mass rape**: The systematic rape of Bosniak women and girls was documented by international investigators as a deliberate military strategy — intended to humiliate, terrorize, and destroy the social fabric of Bosniak communities. Estimates of the number of women raped range from 20,000 to 50,000. The use of rape as a weapon of war was subsequently prosecuted as a crime against humanity and a component of genocide at the International Criminal Tribunal.

**Siege of Sarajevo**: The capital city of Sarajevo, with a mixed population in which Bosniaks formed the majority, was subjected to the longest siege of a capital city in the history of modern warfare — from April 1992 to February 1996. Bosnian Serb forces surrounded the city, shelled it with artillery, and had snipers positioned to shoot civilians crossing open spaces. Approximately 13,952 people were killed in Sarajevo during the siege, including 5,434 civilians. Children were targeted by snipers.

**Destruction of Islamic heritage**: Mosques, Islamic libraries, and cultural centers were deliberately targeted and destroyed. The National and University Library of Sarajevo — containing irreplaceable Ottoman manuscripts and early printed books — was shelled and burned in August 1992. The destruction of cultural and religious heritage was part of the broader attempt to erase the Muslim presence from the region.

**Concentration camps**: Bosniak and Croat men were held in concentration camps by Bosnian Serb forces. The Omarska and Keraterm camps became the sites of systematic torture, starvation, and killing. International journalists who gained access in August 1992 brought images of emaciated prisoners behind barbed wire — photographs that shocked the world and drew uncomfortable comparisons to World War II Nazi camps.

## Srebrenica: The Worst Single Atrocity

In July 1995 CE (Safar 1416 AH), the Srebrenica massacre — designated a "safe area" by the United Nations — became the defining crime of the Bosnian war.

The United Nations had declared Srebrenica a "safe area" in 1993, protected by a Dutch peacekeeping battalion (Dutchbat). The enclave held approximately 40,000 Bosniak civilians who had fled the surrounding countryside. In July 1995, Bosnian Serb forces under General Mladic overran the enclave. The Dutch peacekeepers, outnumbered and under orders that left them unable or unwilling to use force, did not prevent the takeover.

What followed was the systematic murder of approximately 8,000 Bosniak Muslim men and boys — essentially every male between the approximate ages of 12 and 77. They were separated from women and children, transported to execution sites, shot, and buried in mass graves — graves that were subsequently dug up and their contents moved to secondary and tertiary sites in an attempt to conceal the evidence. The International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY) and the International Court of Justice both found that the Srebrenica massacre constituted genocide under the 1948 Genocide Convention.

The response of the Dutch peacekeepers — who handed over Bosniak men seeking refuge at the UN compound — has been the subject of ongoing legal and moral controversy. The Dutch Supreme Court subsequently ruled that the Netherlands bore partial responsibility for the deaths of approximately 350 men handed over by Dutch forces.

## The Muslim World's Response

The Bosnian war provoked extraordinary grief and outrage across the Muslim world. Millions of Muslims watched on television as fellow Muslims were killed, raped, and dispossessed in a country in the heart of Europe — a country that had been part of the European continent for centuries. The contrast between the international community's swift response to Iraq's invasion of Kuwait (which threatened oil supplies) and its years of dithering over Bosnia (which threatened only Bosniak lives) was not lost on Muslim observers.

Muslim volunteers from the Arab world — veterans of the Afghan jihad and others — traveled to Bosnia to fight alongside the Bosnian Army. The Bosnian government, desperate for military assistance that the international arms embargo denied them, accepted these volunteers. Their presence in Bosnia became the subject of intense international discussion; most had returned to their home countries or dispersed by the war's end.

Saudi Arabia, the Gulf states, and Iran all provided financial assistance to Bosnia. The Islamic Development Bank funded reconstruction. After the war, Saudi and Gulf funding helped rebuild mosques — sometimes with architectural styles (including Wahhabi-influenced designs) that differed from traditional Bosnian Ottoman architecture, generating some controversy about cultural appropriation alongside the legitimate solidarity.

## The Dayton Agreement and Aftermath

The war was finally ended by the Dayton Peace Agreement, negotiated in Dayton, Ohio in November 1995 and signed in Paris in December 1995. The agreement divided Bosnia into two entities: the Federation of Bosnia and Herzegovina (Bosniak-Croat) and Republika Srpska (Bosnian Serb). NATO troops were deployed as peacekeepers (IFOR, later SFOR).

The Dayton Agreement ended the killing but essentially rewarded ethnic cleansing by creating a Republika Srpska that was ethnically homogeneous because its Bosniak and Croat populations had been expelled or killed. Displaced persons had a legal right of return; in practice, most could not or did not return. The ethnic geography of Bosnia was fundamentally and permanently altered.

## The Islamic Dimension

The Bosnian genocide has a specific Islamic significance beyond its human catastrophe. The Bosniaks were a European Muslim community with five centuries of continuous Islamic presence. Their destruction would have eliminated the indigenous Muslim community of the western Balkans — a community that had navigated the Ottoman-Christian divide, Austro-Hungarian rule, communist Yugoslavia, and European modernity while maintaining its Islamic identity and contributing to European civilization.

The preservation of the Bosniak community and the Bosnian state, despite the genocide, represents a kind of historical resilience that Islamic history recognizes — the survival of a community against murderous assault. The Quran reminds believers: *"And do not weaken in pursuit of the enemy. If you should be suffering — so are they suffering as you are suffering, but you expect from Allah that which they expect not."* (Quran 4:104)

The lessons of Bosnia for the global Muslim community are multiple: the vulnerability of Muslim minorities in non-Muslim states; the failure of international institutions to protect Muslim lives with the same urgency they apply to other interests; the importance of Islamic solidarity backed by material capability; and the necessity of legal accountability — the ICTY prosecutions of Karadzic and Mladic represent the most important application of international criminal law to anti-Muslim genocide in the modern period.
