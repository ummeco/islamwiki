# Death of Ibn Kathir

## Damascus's Greatest Historian and Mufassir

'Imad al-Din Abu al-Fida' Isma'il ibn 'Umar ibn Kathir al-Busrawi al-Dimashqi died in Damascus on 26 Sha'ban 774 AH (February 19, 1373 CE), having gone blind in his final years but continuing to dictate scholarship to his students until the end. He was approximately 73 years old. With his death, Islamic civilization lost one of its most prolific and influential scholars — a man whose tafsir and universal history would be read, taught, and studied for seven centuries after him and show no signs of declining in use.

Ibn Kathir was born around 701 AH (1300-1301 CE) in the village of Busra (Bosra) in the Hawran region of Syria, the son of a preacher (khatib) who died when he was young. His family moved to Damascus when he was about seven years old, and he spent his entire scholarly life in that city, becoming one of its most prominent scholars by the time he reached his forties.

## Formation: Student of Ibn Taymiyyah

The most important scholarly relationship of Ibn Kathir's life was his study under Ibn Taymiyyah, whom he encountered as a young man in Damascus and whose influence shaped his entire intellectual approach. Like his contemporary Ibn al-Qayyim (who was about a decade older and also a student of Ibn Taymiyyah), Ibn Kathir absorbed the Taymiyyan emphasis on direct engagement with Quran and authentic Sunnah over later jurisprudential elaboration, the commitment to Athari theology, and the method of explaining the Quran primarily through the Quran itself and through authentic hadith rather than through speculative interpretation.

But Ibn Kathir was not simply a disciple. Al-Dhahabi, who was his own teacher, noted that Ibn Kathir had genuine independence of scholarly judgment and would sometimes take positions that differed from Ibn Taymiyyah's. Ibn Kathir was himself a Shafi'i — not a Hanbali like Ibn Taymiyyah — and while he shared Ibn Taymiyyah's theological positions on the divine attributes, he sometimes differed with him on jurisprudential matters. This independence gives his work a scholarly integrity that pure partisan writing lacks.

He also studied extensively under al-Dhahabi himself, inheriting from him a rigorous approach to hadith criticism and biographical scholarship (rijal). The combination of Ibn Taymiyyah's theological method and al-Dhahabi's technical hadith expertise produced a scholar uniquely equipped for the two major projects that would define his legacy.

## Tafsir al-Qur'an al-'Azim: The Most Widely Used Classical Commentary

Ibn Kathir's Tafsir al-Qur'an al-'Azim (Commentary on the Great Quran) is the most widely read and assigned classical Quran commentary in the Sunni world today. This is not a recent development: the tafsir has been in continuous scholarly use since its composition in the 8th century AH. In the era of print, it became accessible to the general Muslim public and its popularity expanded dramatically. Modern printed editions typically appear in 4-8 volumes; the most common Arabic edition runs to about 4,000 pages.

The tafsir's methodology is clearly and consistently applied: Ibn Kathir explains the Quran first by the Quran itself — if one verse illuminates the meaning of another, he cites both. Then he explains by authentic hadith, citing reports from the Prophet ﷺ that explain the verse, with full isnads (chains of transmission) and often with his own assessment of the hadith's authenticity. Then he cites the statements of the Companions of the Prophet ﷺ, then the statements of the Tabi'in (the generation after the Companions). Only after exhausting these transmitted sources does he engage in linguistic or contextual analysis.

This method — tafsir bil-ma'thur (explanation through transmitted reports) as opposed to tafsir bil-ra'y (explanation through personal opinion) — represents the most conservative approach to Quranic interpretation. It privileges the explanations of those closest to the revelation — the Prophet ﷺ himself, then his Companions who witnessed revelation directly, then those who learned from the Companions — over the speculations of later scholars who were further from the original context.

The theological orientation of the tafsir is clearly Athari: on matters of divine attributes, eschatology, and the unseen, Ibn Kathir follows the approach of accepting what the texts say without allegorical reinterpretation. The tafsir is widely used by both Salafi/Athari scholars and by scholars of other theological orientations — a testament to its reliability as a primary source for hadith and Companion statements even when readers may not share every theological premise of its author.

A significant practical virtue of the tafsir is Ibn Kathir's meticulous attention to hadith criticism. He routinely notes when a hadith cited in connection with a verse is weak or fabricated, preventing the reader from relying on spurious reports. This critical vigilance was unusual among classical mufassirun, many of whom cited any hadith that mentioned a verse without evaluating its authenticity. Ibn Kathir's training under al-Dhahabi gave him the technical expertise to do what many others could not.

## Al-Bidayah wan-Nihayah: The Universal History

Al-Bidayah wan-Nihayah fi al-Tarikh (The Beginning and the End in History) is Ibn Kathir's second major achievement — a universal history in 14-15 large volumes that begins with the creation of the world (covering the accounts of the prophets from the Quran and hadith) and continues through the history of Islam to Ibn Kathir's own time. It is one of the most important reference works for Islamic history from the early Islamic period through the 8th century AH.

The structure of Al-Bidayah reflects the same methodological commitment as the tafsir: the historical accounts of the prophets rely primarily on Quranic narrative and authentic hadith, evaluating the Israelite traditions (Isra'iliyyat) that circulated in Islamic historical writing with careful critical attention. Ibn Kathir was notably skeptical of Isra'iliyyat — traditions derived from Jewish and Christian sources that had entered the Islamic historical tradition — and consistently flags them as potentially unreliable when they are not confirmed by the Quran or authentic Sunnah.

The sections on early Islamic history — the life of the Prophet ﷺ, the Rightly Guided Caliphs, the Umayyad and Abbasid eras — draw on the earlier historical sources (Ibn Ishaq, Ibn Sa'd, al-Tabari, Ibn al-Athir) and add Ibn Kathir's critical assessment. For the later periods, particularly the Mamluk era up to his own time, Ibn Kathir is sometimes the primary or near-primary source available to historians.

Al-Bidayah also contains the account of the signs of the Hour (the end of time) — one of the most extensive treatments of eschatological signs in classical Islamic literature, drawing on the relevant hadith in the canonical collections. This section has been widely read and cited across the centuries as a reference for Islamic eschatology.

## The Account of Ibn Taymiyyah's Life and Death

Among the most historically important sections of Al-Bidayah wan-Nihayah is Ibn Kathir's account of his teacher Ibn Taymiyyah's life, imprisonments, and death. As an eyewitness who was present in Damascus during Ibn Taymiyyah's final years, Ibn Kathir provides details unavailable in any other source. His account of Ibn Taymiyyah's death in the Citadel, the refusal to allow him writing materials, his composure in the face of death, and the massive crowd at his funeral is the primary narrative that all later accounts rely on.

Ibn Kathir's admiration for his teacher is evident but does not prevent scholarly independence. He occasionally records positions on which he differs from Ibn Taymiyyah, and his Al-Bidayah is not simply a vehicle for Taymiyyan propaganda but a genuine historical work that represents his own scholarly judgment.

## Jami' al-Masanid wal-Sunan: The Hadith Compilation

Less well known than the tafsir and the history, but significant in its own right, is Ibn Kathir's major hadith compilation Jami' al-Masanid wal-Sunan (Collection of the Musnad Collections and Sunan) — a vast work that brings together the hadiths of the major collections alongside the musnad (narrator-organized) works of the early hadith scholars. The work aimed to produce a comprehensive access point to the prophetic hadith literature, organized both by topic and by narrator.

This project reflects the same impulse as his other major works: comprehensive engagement with the transmitted heritage of Islam, organized and presented with critical awareness of authenticity. The Jami' al-Masanid is less frequently cited in modern scholarship than the tafsir and Al-Bidayah, but it was used by later hadith scholars as a reference work.

## Blindness and Final Years

In his final years, Ibn Kathir lost his sight — a condition that he reportedly bore with characteristic patience and that did not prevent him from continuing to work through dictation to his students. He dictated accounts, explanations, and historical details from the vast reservoir of his memorized knowledge. The condition of blindness in a scholar of his generation was not uncommon; al-Dhahabi had also experienced declining eyesight in his later years, and numerous other scholars of the classical period completed major works after losing their sight entirely.

He died on 26 Sha'ban 774 AH in Damascus and was buried — at his own request, according to some accounts — near the grave of his teacher Ibn Taymiyyah at the Bab al-Saghir cemetery. The choice of burial site was a final act of scholarly loyalty: he had sat at Ibn Taymiyyah's feet as a student, received from him the core of his intellectual method, and now he chose to rest beside him.

## The Seven-Century Legacy

The legacy of Ibn Kathir in Islamic civilization is singular in a specific sense: his tafsir is today read by more Muslims than any other classical Quran commentary, in more languages, across more continents and communities. It has been translated into English, French, Urdu, Turkish, Indonesian, and dozens of other languages. It is assigned in Islamic studies programs worldwide. It is the tafsir that most Sunni Muslims, if they consult one classical commentary, will consult first.

This reach — seven centuries of continuous use — is the result of several factors: the quality of the work itself, the clarity of its method, the reliability of its hadith citations, the accessibility of its language compared to more technically demanding commentaries, and its freedom from the scholastic complications of kalam theology that can make other classical commentaries difficult for non-specialists. Ibn Kathir wrote in classical Arabic of genuine elegance, but his explanations aim for clarity rather than display.

Al-Bidayah wan-Nihayah, though less universally read than the tafsir, is an essential reference for scholars of Islamic history and is regularly cited in academic scholarship on medieval Islamic civilization. Together, the two works constitute a scholarly legacy of the very highest order — works that have served, and continue to serve, the Muslim community's need to understand its scripture and its history.
