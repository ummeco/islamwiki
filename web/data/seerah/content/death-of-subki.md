# Death of Taj al-Din al-Subki

Taj ad-Din Abd al-Wahhab ibn Taqi al-Din Ali al-Subki (727-771 AH / 1327-1370 CE) was one of the most erudite Shafi'i scholars of the eighth century AH. Son of the renowned jurist Taqi al-Din al-Subki (d. 756 AH) and chief judge of Damascus, he continued his family's tradition of distinguished Shafi'i scholarship while adding his own considerable contributions to Islamic jurisprudence, theology, and biography.

## Life

Taj al-Din al-Subki was born in Egypt in 727 AH (1327 CE), where his father Taqi al-Din al-Subki served as chief judge. He grew up in an atmosphere of intense scholarship and received his education primarily from his father and the great scholars of Egypt and Syria. He accompanied his father to Damascus when Taqi al-Din was appointed chief judge there.

Taj al-Din himself eventually became chief judge (qadi al-qudat) of Damascus, a position he held until political complications led to his removal. He died in Damascus in 771 AH (1370 CE) at a relatively young age of approximately 43 or 44 years.

## Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyya al-Kubra

Taj al-Din al-Subki's most important contribution is the *Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyya al-Kubra* (The Major Biographical Dictionary of the Shafi'i School), a monumental biographical work in ten volumes recording the lives and scholarly contributions of Shafi'i scholars from the founding of the school by Imam al-Shafi'i (d. 204 AH / 820 CE) through the eighth century AH.

The work provides indispensable information about hundreds of scholars, including details of their teachers, students, travels, and works. It is a primary source for the history of Islamic jurisprudence and of Shafi'i scholarship in particular. Taj al-Din continued and greatly expanded a project that his father Taqi al-Din had begun.

The biographical entries vary in length from brief notices to extended studies, and many include important theological and jurisprudential discussions as well as biographical data. The entry on al-Ash'ari, for example, is an extended defense of the Ash'ari theological tradition.

## Jam al-Jawami

Another significant work attributed to Taj al-Din al-Subki is the *Jam al-Jawami* (Collection of Collections) in Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh). This text became a standard curriculum work in Shafi'i madrasas and was the subject of numerous commentaries, including the famous commentary by Jalal al-Din al-Mahalli (completed by al-Suyuti as *Sharh Jam al-Jawami*). Its influence on the teaching of Islamic legal theory extended for centuries and is still felt in contemporary Islamic education.

## Theological Positions

Taj al-Din al-Subki was a staunch defender of Ash'ari theology. The eighth century AH was a period of intense theological controversy, with Ibn Taymiyya (d. 728 AH / 1328 CE) — just one generation before the Subkis — having challenged many positions held by the Ash'ari establishment. The Subki family (both Taqi al-Din and Taj al-Din) were among the most vocal scholarly opponents of Ibn Taymiyya's views, writing refutations and using their judicial authority to oppose positions they considered deviant.

This controversy shaped much of Taj al-Din's scholarly output and is reflected in numerous passages in his biographical work, where he defends Ash'ari positions and critiques what he saw as Hanbali literalism.

## Legacy

The *Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyya al-Kubra* and *Jam al-Jawami* secured Taj al-Din al-Subki's place as one of the important scholars of the Shafi'i tradition. His early death at under 44 years of age prevented him from completing what might have been an even larger scholarly contribution. The two major works he did complete, however, remained influential for centuries.
