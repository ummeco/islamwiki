# Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas: The Lion of Islam and First Archer for the Faith

## The Young Man from Banu Zuhra

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him) was born into the Banu Zuhra clan of the Quraysh — the same clan from which the Prophet's mother, Aminah bint Wahb, had come. This kinship gave Sa'd a connection to the Prophet ﷺ through maternal lineage, and the Prophet ﷺ would later acknowledge it explicitly in words that meant more to Sa'd than any worldly honor. He was approximately seventeen years old when he accepted Islam, placing his conversion among the earliest handful of converts — brought to the faith by Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) in the period shortly after the first revelation.

Ibn Sa'd records that Sa'd was the seventeenth person to accept Islam in some accounts, while other narrations place him among the first seven. What is settled is that he was among the earliest converts of Mecca, accepting in the days when the Muslim community had no mosque, no public presence, and no protection beyond the mercy of Allah and the tribal honor of those who sheltered them.

Sa'd was a young man of physical strength and exceptional skill with the bow. His love for his mother was intense and openly acknowledged — a quality the early sources describe with warmth. It was precisely this love for his mother, and the crisis it would create, that would become the defining test of his earliest years as a Muslim.

## The Trial of the Mother

When Sa'd's mother learned that he had accepted Islam, she was devastated. She resolved upon a course of action that was both desperate and, in the context of pre-Islamic Arab culture, socially coercive: she would refuse to eat or drink until Sa'd renounced his new religion. In a society where the dishonor of a parent's death by voluntary starvation would fall upon the child who caused it, this was a form of emotional and social pressure of the highest order.

According to the narrations recorded by al-Tirmidhi and others, Sa'd's mother sat in the sun and refused food and water. She weakened. Sa'd watched her suffer. The love he bore her was real — he was not a callous son, and the sight of his mother in distress was genuinely painful to him.

But Sa'd did not waver.

He said to her — and the accounts preserve his words with precision: "By Allah, O my mother, if you had a hundred souls and they departed one by one, I would not leave this religion for anything. If you wish, eat; and if you wish, do not eat." In some narrations, he added: "I will not renounce my religion." His mother, recognizing that her son was immovable, eventually relented and ate.

## The Quranic Response

The specific trial of Sa'd and his mother was among the experiences that contributed to a revelation that addressed the broader category of parental authority and religious obligation. The Quran addressed this tension directly in Surah Luqman (31:14-15):

"And We have enjoined upon man [care] for his parents. His mother carried him, [increasing her] in weakness upon weakness, and his weaning is in two years. Be grateful to Me and to your parents; to Me is the [final] destination. But if they endeavor to make you associate with Me what you have no knowledge of, do not obey them but accompany them in [this] world with appropriate kindness and follow the way of those who turn back to Me [in repentance]. Then to Me will be your return, and I will inform you about what you used to do."

This verse does not simply resolve the tension in Sa'd's favor. It does something more nuanced: it affirms the deep obligation to parents, commands kindness and companionship toward them even when they demand disobedience to Allah, but establishes that parental authority stops at the boundary of shirk and disbelief. Sa'd honored this balance — he did not abandon his mother, did not treat her with contempt, and continued to care for her — but he would not abandon his Lord.

The narrations in Sahih Muslim (Kitab al-Birr wal-Sila) explicitly connect the revelation of a version of this guidance to Sa'd's situation, and Muslim scholars have consistently cited his story as the primary context (sabab al-nuzul) for this portion of the verse.

## The First Arrow Shot in Defense of Islam

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him) holds a unique honor in the history of Islamic warfare: he was the first person to shoot an arrow in the path of Allah. Ibn Hisham records this in al-Sirah al-Nabawiyyah. During an early period when the Muslims were still relatively few and persecution in Mecca was severe, a small group of companions went out with the Prophet ﷺ or with other companions. An encounter with a group of Qurayshi enemies occurred, and in the resulting confrontation, Sa'd drew his bow and loosed an arrow — the first arrow ever shot in defense of the Muslim community.

This honor, recorded consistently across the sirah and hadith literature, established Sa'd as a figure of martial importance from the very beginning of his Islamic life. He was not a man who came late to the struggle. He was there at the origin.

## "May Both My Parents Be Your Ransom"

The most celebrated statement the Prophet ﷺ made about Sa'd (may Allah be pleased with him) was uttered at the Battle of Uhud. During the difficult fighting that day — when the archers disobeyed their instructions and left their positions, enabling the Qurayshi cavalry to flank the Muslim army — Sa'd distinguished himself with extraordinary skill and courage. The Prophet ﷺ stood near him and called out his name in encouragement: "Shoot, Sa'd! May my father and mother be your ransom!"

The phrase "may my father and mother be your ransom" (fidaka abi wa ummi) is the highest form of praise in the Arabic verbal tradition — to offer one's own parents as ransom for another is the greatest expression of esteem. The scholars of hadith have noted that the Prophet ﷺ was extremely restrained in using this expression. In the narrations of Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, only a very small number of companions are reported to have received this praise from the Prophet ﷺ — and Sa'd is foremost among them.

Al-Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) is reported to have said: "I have not heard the Prophet ﷺ say 'may my father and mother be your ransom' to anyone except Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas." This narration, found in Sahih al-Bukhari, further emphasizes the singular nature of the honor the Prophet ﷺ bestowed upon Sa'd.

## The Conqueror of Persia

Sa'd's role in Islamic history reached its greatest magnitude in the campaigns against the Sasanian Persian Empire. After the death of the Prophet ﷺ, as Abu Bakr and then Umar directed the armies of Islam outward, Sa'd emerged as one of the foremost military commanders of the age. It was Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas who commanded the Muslim forces at the Battle of al-Qadisiyyah (15-16 AH) — the decisive engagement that broke the back of Persian imperial resistance and opened Iraq to the Muslim armies.

The Battle of al-Qadisiyyah is one of the most consequential military encounters in the history of the Near East. The Persian forces, commanded by Rustam Farrukhzad, were substantial, well-equipped, and included war elephants — animals that the Arab horses feared instinctively. Sa'd, ill at the time with a skin condition that prevented him from riding or standing comfortably, directed the battle from a position behind the front line, issuing orders as the fighting raged. The Muslim victory was complete, Rustam was killed, and the road to the Persian heartland was open.

Sa'd went on to found the city of Kufa (al-Kufah), which became one of the great centers of Islamic civilization in Iraq and a major site of hadith transmission, jurisprudence, and later theological development. He served as its first governor and maintained it as a base for further military operations into Persia. By the time the Muslim armies reached Ctesiphon (al-Mada'in), the ancient capital of the Sasanian Empire, Sa'd was the man who had made it possible.

## His Supplication and Its Acceptance

Sa'd (may Allah be pleased with him) was known throughout the community for a remarkable quality: his supplications were answered. The tradition is recorded in both al-Bukhari and Muslim that the Prophet ﷺ specifically prayed for Sa'd's supplications to be accepted: "O Allah, respond to Sa'd's prayer when he prays to You." This gift — the mujab al-da'wah, the one whose prayer is answered — was recognized by the companions in his lifetime and by subsequent generations.

Numerous accounts in the hadith literature record instances where Sa'd made a du'a against someone who wronged him, and that person was afflicted with harm. The most famous of these stories involves a Kufan man who accused Sa'd of not praying correctly. Sa'd prayed against him and his eyesight was affected. Another account involves a man who accused him of not distributing the war spoils fairly. Sa'd's prayer against him became famous in Islamic lore.

This quality was not arrogance or the abuse of a gift. Sa'd was known for restraint and for preferring not to use his du'a against others. When he did so, it was after having been wronged, and after the patience that characterized his general disposition had been exhausted.

## Among the Ten Promised Paradise

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him) is counted among the Ashra Mubashshara — the ten companions to whom the Prophet ﷺ explicitly promised Paradise by name. The hadith recording this is preserved by al-Tirmidhi and others: the Prophet ﷺ listed ten companions and said of each that they would enter Paradise. That Sa'd was among this group — a group that includes the four Caliphs, Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali, alongside al-Zubayr, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, Talhah, Said ibn Zayd, and Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah — reflects his standing in the community.

He was the last of the ten to die, living well into the era of Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan's caliphate. He died in 55 AH, approximately eighty years old. He died at his estate in al-Aqiq, outside Medina, and was carried to the city to be buried in al-Baqi'. His body was brought in wrapped in an old garment — he had requested that he be buried in the same garment he had worn when he fought at Badr, so that he might meet his Lord in the clothes of that first great battle.

The Prophet ﷺ said in a hadith recorded by Ibn Majah and Ahmad: "This is my maternal uncle — let every man show me his uncle." He was addressing the community and pointing to Sa'd, acknowledging the Banu Zuhra kinship and expressing the pride he felt in this early convert, this first archer, this conqueror of Persia, this man whose du'a Allah accepted.
