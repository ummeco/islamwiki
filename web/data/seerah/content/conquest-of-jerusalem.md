# Umar's Entry into Jerusalem: The Covenant That Set the Standard

## The Road to al-Quds

Jerusalem — al-Quds, the Holy City, the Farthest Mosque — fell to the armies of Islam in 16 AH, approximately 637 CE, following a prolonged siege by the Muslim forces under Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah (may Allah be pleased with him), the supreme commander of the Syrian campaign during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him).

The conquest of Jerusalem did not come through a single battle but through a combination of military pressure and negotiation. After the catastrophic Byzantine defeat at the Battle of Yarmouk (15 AH), the armies of Islam moved systematically through Syria, taking city after city. Jerusalem, with its massive stone walls and deep religious significance for Christians, was besieged. Patriarch Sophronius, the elderly and dignified head of the Christian community of Jerusalem and one of the great theological minds of his age, led the defense and the negotiations.

Sophronius made one condition: he would surrender the city peacefully, without bloodshed, but he would hand the keys only to the leader of the Muslims in person. He had learned enough about the Islamic state to know that the Caliph was not a distant figurehead — he was the accountable head of his community. If the terms were to mean anything, they had to be made with the man who bore ultimate authority.

Abu Ubayda sent a messenger to Medina. Umar made the journey.

## Umar's Journey: A Caliph Unlike Any Other

The account of Umar's travel to Jerusalem is preserved by al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir, among others, and it stands as one of the most vivid illustrations of the character of the early caliphate. Umar set out from Medina with a single servant (in some accounts, a young boy from his household) and a single camel. He and his servant took turns riding the camel and walking, so that neither bore the full burden alone. They brought simple provisions and no royal retinue, no guards, no ceremonial dress.

When the Byzantine and Arab delegates came out to receive the Caliph of all the Muslims — the ruler of a state that had just destroyed both the Persian Empire and the Byzantine army in the field — they saw an unassuming man in worn, patched clothing descending from a camel. The contrast with the pomp of Byzantine imperial ceremony was so striking that some accounts record the delegates' astonishment. One story, preserved in Islamic historical tradition, holds that when Umar arrived at the Muslim camp, the soldiers did not recognize him immediately because his clothes were so ordinary.

When Sophronius met Umar and saw the condition of his attire, he reportedly said to those with him: "This is what our scriptures describe — the leader of a people who live as servants even in their authority." Whether or not this exact exchange occurred, the historical reality it points to is undeniable: Umar's deliberate simplicity was not poverty — it was a theological statement about the nature of legitimate authority in Islam.

## The Covenant of Umar: Protecting Jerusalem's Christians

When Sophronius formally surrendered the city, Umar dictated what became known as the Covenant of Umar (al-Ahd al-Umari or al-Kitab al-Umari). The text, preserved in al-Tabari's history and referenced in numerous classical sources, is among the most remarkable religious-political documents of the medieval world.

The covenant guaranteed:

The safety of the persons, property, and churches of the Christians of Jerusalem. Their churches would not be taken from them, nor damaged, nor reduced. None of their property would be confiscated. No one would be harmed on account of their religion. No one would be compelled to abandon their faith.

The specific protection of all houses of worship in the city — "their crosses and their property" — from Muslim appropriation.

The guarantee that those who wished to leave Jerusalem with their possessions and join the Byzantine territories would be given safe passage.

The protection of the Jews of the region, with provisions for their movement to be regulated (consistent with Byzantine practice and later modified in subsequent agreements).

This covenant set a standard for religious protection in conquered lands that the Islamic world would return to repeatedly as a reference point. It was not mere tolerance — it was a formal, legally binding guarantee backed by the full authority of the Caliphate.

## Umar's Prayer at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre

One of the most famous acts of Umar's visit to Jerusalem was his deliberate decision about where to pray. Sophronius invited Umar into the Church of the Holy Sepulchre — the holiest site in Christianity, built over the site where Christians believe Jesus (peace be upon him) was crucified and resurrected. Umar entered, received the tour, and when the time of prayer came, Sophronius offered him the opportunity to pray inside.

Umar declined. He walked outside the church and prayed on the steps — or in the courtyard, depending on which account is followed. When asked why, he gave a reason that has been cited by scholars ever since: he did not want future Muslims, seeing that the Caliph had prayed inside the church, to claim the right to convert it into a mosque.

This was not an act of discourtesy toward the Christians. It was the opposite — it was an act of profound respect for the covenant he had just made. It demonstrated that the protection of Christian holy sites was not a temporary concession but a principle Umar was willing to uphold even at the cost of personal spiritual convenience.

The spot where Umar prayed became, in subsequent centuries, the location of a small mosque named Masjid Umar — built not to claim the site but to commemorate this act of covenantal fidelity.

## Bilal's Adhan in Jerusalem

Among the most moving episodes of the conquest was the adhan of Bilal ibn Rabah (may Allah be pleased with him). Bilal had been the Prophet's ﷺ muadhdhin — the one appointed to call the Muslims to prayer. He had stood on the rooftops of Mecca and Medina and called the ummah to worship with a voice that the Prophet ﷺ himself said he heard in Paradise.

When the Prophet ﷺ died, Bilal was so overcome with grief that he could not give the adhan. He wept during the call and could not complete it. He left Medina, joining the armies in Syria, unwilling to remain where every corner reminded him of the Messenger ﷺ. He had not given adhan since.

In Jerusalem, Umar — who loved Bilal deeply and honored his rank — asked him to give the call to prayer. Classical accounts record that Bilal climbed to a high point in the city and gave the adhan once more. When the Muslims heard his voice — a voice they had not heard since the death of the Prophet ﷺ — they wept openly. The senior Companions present could not contain their emotion. Even those who had been composed in battle broke down.

This single moment captured something essential about the conquest of Jerusalem: it was not merely a political or military achievement. It was the arrival of the ummah at the city of the prophets — the city of Ibrahim, Dawud, Sulayman, Zakariyyah, Yahya, and Isa (peace be upon them all) — and its reclamation as a place where the name of Allah would be called in worship.

## The Theological Status of Jerusalem in Islam

Jerusalem's sanctity in Islam predates the conquest by a millennium. The Prophet ﷺ was taken on the Isra (Night Journey) from the Sacred Mosque in Mecca to the Farthest Mosque (al-Masjid al-Aqsa) in Jerusalem, as the Quran records: "Glory be to the One who took His servant by night from the Sacred Mosque to the Farthest Mosque, whose surroundings We have blessed" (17:1).

From Jerusalem, the Prophet ﷺ ascended through the heavens on the Mi'raj, meeting the previous prophets and being brought before the presence of Allah. Jerusalem was also the first qibla of the Muslims — they prayed toward it from the earliest days in Mecca and for sixteen to seventeen months after the Hijra to Medina, before the qibla was changed to the Ka'bah (2 AH).

The Prophet ﷺ also said: "Do not set out on a journey to visit any mosque except three: al-Masjid al-Haram, my mosque (al-Masjid al-Nabawi), and al-Masjid al-Aqsa" (Bukhari and Muslim). This hadith established Jerusalem as the third holiest city in Islam — a status it has retained without interruption.

Umar, arriving in Jerusalem, was therefore not simply a conqueror claiming a prize. He was a Muslim Caliph arriving at one of the most sacred addresses on earth, fulfilling a historical and spiritual role.

## The Long Significance of the Covenant

The Covenant of Umar became the reference point for Muslim governance of Jerusalem for over a millennium — through Umayyad, Abbasid, and Fatimid rule; through Crusader conquest and Saladin's liberation in 1187 CE; through the Ottoman centuries. When Saladin (may Allah have mercy on him) retook Jerusalem at the Battle of Hattin and the subsequent siege (583 AH / 1187 CE), he explicitly invoked the principles of Umar's covenant in guaranteeing the safety of the city's Christian population and allowing them to depart with their lives and property.

The covenant demonstrated a principle that Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh al-siyar — the law of nations) would articulate systematically in subsequent centuries: that non-Muslim communities living under Muslim governance retain the right to their faith, their houses of worship, and the practice of their religion, and that these rights are not revocable grants of tolerance but binding legal guarantees rooted in Islamic law itself.

Al-Tabari's detailed account of the conquest remains the primary classical source for these events. Ibn Kathir's treatment in al-Bidaya wan-Nihaya provides extensive theological and historical commentary that has shaped how Muslim scholars have understood and narrated this event for seven centuries.
