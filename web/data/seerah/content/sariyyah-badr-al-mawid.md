# Expedition of Badr al-Maw'id (The Promised Badr)

The Expedition of Badr al-Maw'id ("The Promised Badr" or "Second Badr") in Sha'ban 4 AH (January 626 CE) was a unique military operation — an expedition in which the Muslim army marched to the agreed battlefield of Badr for a rematch with the Quraysh that the Meccans ultimately declined to fight. It demonstrated the psychological confidence of the Muslim community and exposed the limits of Qurayshi resolve after Uhud.

## Background: The Promise at Uhud

At the Battle of Uhud (3 AH), when Abu Sufyan was withdrawing his Meccan forces, he called back at the Muslims: "Our meeting will be at Badr next year!" — offering a direct challenge to a rematch. The Prophet responded through one of his companions: "Yes, it is an agreement between us."

This was a formal challenge and response — an accepted convention of Arabian warfare. Both sides had committed, in public, to meet at Badr at the end of the following year.

## The Muslim Preparation

As the appointed time approached, the Prophet organized an expeditionary force of 1,500 fighters and marched to Badr — the site of the Muslims' first major victory (2 AH). They arrived at the watering place of Badr and waited for the Meccan force.

They remained at Badr for eight days, conducting trade at the Badr market (which was held annually at that season) and waiting for the Qurayshi army.

## The Quraysh's Non-Appearance

Abu Sufyan organized a force of 2,000 Qurayshi fighters and marched toward Badr. However, he turned back at Marr al-Zahran (approximately 30 km from Mecca), claiming that the year was one of drought and the soldiers needed nourishment before they could fight effectively. He reportedly said: "This year is a year of drought. We will go back, and next year we will fight them."

His retreat was seen as a significant loss of face. The Arabian code of honor made standing down from an accepted combat challenge a mark of cowardice.

## The Muslim Return

The Prophet and his force returned to Medina after eight days at Badr, having conducted profitable trade and having demonstrated their willingness and readiness to fight. The non-appearance of the Quraysh was understood by both sides as a Muslim psychological victory.

## Significance

**Reversal of the post-Uhud psychological situation.** The Battle of Uhud had been a military setback for the Muslims — the Quraysh had claimed a kind of victory. Badr al-Maw'id reversed the psychological momentum: the Muslims marched to the agreed fighting ground and waited; the Quraysh backed away.

**Economic dimension.** The eight days at Badr were not wasted — the Muslim force conducted trade at the annual Badr market, reportedly making a profit. This practical dimension of the expedition illustrates the Prophet's integration of military and economic objectives.

**The psychological significance of keeping one's word.** The Muslim leadership's insistence on honoring the promise made at Uhud — marching to Badr at the agreed time — reflected the Islamic value placed on fulfilling commitments even when tactically inconvenient. The Quran repeatedly emphasizes the fulfillment of contracts (Quran 5:1, 17:34), and the Muslim leadership's practice exemplified this.

**Abu Sufyan's credibility loss.** The Qurayshi chief's withdrawal from the promised battle damaged his standing in tribal Arabia. Credibility in warfare depended on following through on challenges. His retreat, even with a plausible logistical excuse, was read across Arabia as an indicator of weakened Qurayshi confidence.

## Context in the Seerah

Badr al-Maw'id occurred between the Battle of Uhud (3 AH) and the Battle of the Trench (5 AH) — a period in which the Prophet maintained military initiative through multiple smaller operations while Quraysh assembled its last major direct military challenge. The non-battle at Badr was part of the gradual shifting of the military balance that would culminate in the failed Confederacy siege of Medina.

## Sources

The expedition is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
