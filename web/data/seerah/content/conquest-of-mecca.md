# The Conquest of Mecca (Fath Makkah)

## The Road to Mecca

The Conquest of Mecca on the twentieth of Ramadan 8 AH was one of the most carefully prepared and peacefully executed military campaigns in the seerah. Its immediate trigger was the Quraysh's violation of the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah: Banu Bakr, a Quraysh-allied tribe, attacked the Banu Khuza'ah — who were Muslim allies — with Qurayshi support in breach of the treaty's ceasefire provisions. When news of the attack reached Medina, Abu Sufyan traveled to Medina to negotiate and renew the treaty. He met with the Prophet's ﷺ daughter Fatimah, with Ali, with Abu Bakr, and finally with the Prophet ﷺ himself, who received him but made no commitments. Abu Sufyan returned to Mecca without a renewal.

The Prophet ﷺ began preparations for a march on Mecca, keeping the destination confidential. He asked Allah to blind Qurayshi intelligence. A companion, Hatib ibn Abi Balta'ah — not out of hypocrisy but out of a calculation about protecting his family in Mecca — sent a letter to the Quraysh warning them. The Prophet ﷺ was informed through revelation and sent Ali, al-Zubayr, and al-Miqdad to intercept the messenger. They caught her, found the letter hidden in her hair, and returned it to the Prophet ﷺ. Hatib explained his reasoning; the Prophet ﷺ accepted the explanation and did not punish him. Umar wanted to execute Hatib for treason; the Prophet ﷺ said: "He participated in Badr. Perhaps Allah looked at the people of Badr and said: Do what you will — I have forgiven you."

## The Army of 10,000

The Prophet ﷺ marched from Medina with approximately 10,000 fighters — by far the largest Muslim force assembled to date. Tribes that had aligned with the Muslim community after Hudaybiyyah joined the march. The army moved quickly. On arrival at the outskirts of Mecca, the Prophet ﷺ ordered that fires be lit across the hillsides simultaneously — the camp's light visible to Mecca — a psychological demonstration of overwhelming force. Abu Sufyan, who had ridden out to assess the situation, was captured by one of the Muslim patrols and brought before the Prophet ﷺ. There, under pressure from Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (the Prophet's ﷺ uncle), Abu Sufyan accepted Islam. The Prophet ﷺ announced a general amnesty: anyone who entered Abu Sufyan's house would be safe, anyone who locked their door would be safe, and anyone who entered the mosque would be safe.

## The Entry

The Prophet ﷺ entered Mecca on the twentieth of Ramadan, 8 AH — bowing his head so low in gratitude that his beard nearly touched his camel's saddle. He entered reciting Surah al-Fath. Four columns entered through different approaches: Ali from one direction, Khalid ibn al-Walid from another. Khalid's column encountered armed resistance in one quarter; a brief skirmish left approximately twelve Qurayshi fighters and two Muslims dead. This was the only significant violence of the conquest.

The Prophet ﷺ went to the Kaaba. There were 360 idols around and inside it. He went around the Kaaba on his camel, touching the Black Stone with his staff, and then smashing the idols one by one while reciting: "Truth has come and falsehood has departed — indeed, falsehood is ever departing." (Surah al-Isra, 17:81) Bilal climbed to the top of the Kaaba and called the adhan — the first adhan ever called from the roof of the Kaaba, over the city that had persecuted him and his community for twenty years.

## The General Amnesty

The Prophet ﷺ then gathered the Quraysh before the Kaaba — the people who had beaten and killed his companions, who had boycotted his family, who had organized four major military campaigns against him, who had driven him from his city. He said to them: "What do you think I will do with you?" They said: "Good — a generous brother and the son of a generous brother." He said: "Go — you are free."

Among those specifically pardoned, despite their histories, were: Hind bint Utbah (who had eaten Hamza's liver), Wahshi ibn Harb (who had killed Hamza), Abu Sufyan ibn Harb (who had led three armies against the Prophet ﷺ), Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl (son of the Prophet's ﷺ greatest Meccan enemy), and Safwan ibn Umayyah (who had offered money to have the Prophet ﷺ killed). The general amnesty covered almost all of Mecca. Only a handful of specific individuals — those guilty of particularly heinous crimes — were excluded, and even some of them were eventually pardoned.

The conquest of Mecca was a conquest without revenge. The city that had produced the longest, most bitter, most personal persecution the Prophet ﷺ had experienced — the city he had left weeping, saying "You are the most beloved of Allah's lands to me, and the most beloved of His lands to Allah. Had your people not driven me out of you, I would not have left" — he entered it forgiven, forgiving.

## The Historical Significance

The Conquest of Mecca marks the decisive turning point of the entire seerah: from a persecuted minority seeking survival to a governing community with the power to extend mercy on the largest possible scale. It is the event that gave the seerah its structure — everything from Khadijah's first embrace of the Prophet ﷺ in the cave of Hira, through twenty-one years of Meccan persecution and Medinan warfare, leads to the moment on the twentieth of Ramadan when Bilal stood on the roof of the Kaaba that had once been used to torture him and called the adhan.

The general amnesty is one of the most studied events in Islamic history as a model of political governance. European scholars who have analyzed it — among them W. Montgomery Watt — have noted that the amnesty at Mecca stands in stark contrast to the treatment of conquered populations in virtually every other major military conquest of the ancient world. The Prophet ﷺ did not execute the Qurayshi leadership, did not redistribute property as spoils, did not enslave the population, and did not impose punitive taxes. He said "Go — you are free" — and the majority of Mecca accepted Islam not out of compulsion but out of the recognition, finally, of who he was and what he had always been.
