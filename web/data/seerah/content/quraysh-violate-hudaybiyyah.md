# The Quraysh Violation of the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah

## The Treaty and Its Terms

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, concluded in Dhul-Qa'dah 6 AH, had established a ten-year truce between the Muslim community of Medina and the Quraysh of Mecca. Among its key provisions was an article stipulating that no tribe allied with the Quraysh could attack any tribe allied with the Muslims, and vice versa. This clause allowed the allied tribes to choose their own alignments and made the security of each party's allies a condition of the peace. Banu Khuza'ah, who had long-standing ties with the Hashimites and the Muslim community, aligned with the Prophet ﷺ. Banu Bakr, who had an old blood feud with Banu Khuza'ah, aligned with the Quraysh.

The treaty had held for two years — long enough for conversions to accelerate, for trade to resume, and for the Muslim community to grow in strength and number. Then, in Sha'ban 8 AH, Banu Bakr attacked Banu Khuza'ah at a water source called al-Watir, killing a number of men. The Quraysh were not passive observers: Qurayshi individuals, including members of the Ikrimah and Safwan families, provided weapons and direct combat support to Banu Bakr, themselves joining the killing in the darkness. Some of Banu Khuza'ah fled to the Haram — the sacred precinct of Mecca — but Banu Bakr and their Qurayshi allies pursued them even there, killing inside the sanctuary.

## The Delegation to Medina

Amr ibn Salim al-Khuza'i led a delegation to Medina to report the violation to the Prophet ﷺ. He stood before the Prophet ﷺ and recited verses of Arabic poetry describing the attack — the killing at the water source, the violation of the Haram, the Quraysh's direct participation — and appealed for justice. The Prophet ﷺ said: "You shall be helped, O Amr ibn Salim." The clouds gathered and rain fell — a detail preserved in the narrations as a sign of divine response.

The Prophet ﷺ also received Abu Sufyan himself, who had come to Medina attempting to renew or strengthen the treaty. Abu Sufyan's visit to Medina was a clear acknowledgment that the Quraysh knew they had violated the agreement and were concerned about the consequences. He visited Abu Bakr and requested him to intercede with the Prophet ﷺ; Abu Bakr refused. He visited Umar, who responded with characteristic directness. He went to the Prophet's ﷺ house and addressed the assembly; the Prophet ﷺ did not respond. He turned to Ali ibn Abi Talib for advice; Ali told him that nothing he could do at this point would be sufficient, but suggested he stand in the mosque and call out that he placed himself and the people of Mecca under his own protection — which would at least give him something to report to the Quraysh. Abu Sufyan did so and returned to Mecca.

## The Prophet's Decision

The Prophet ﷺ had been preparing the response to the treaty violation in secret. He gave orders for a military campaign — the largest he had ever assembled — and imposed a communications blackout: no letter was to leave Medina, no traveler was to go ahead. The purpose was surprise. The army would march on Mecca before the Quraysh knew it was coming.

Hatib ibn Abi Balta'ah — a companion with family members in Mecca whom the Quraysh might otherwise retaliate against — secretly sent a letter to the Quraysh warning them. The Prophet ﷺ was informed of the letter through revelation and sent Ali ibn Abi Talib and al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam to intercept the messenger. They caught up with her, found the letter hidden in her hair, and returned it to the Prophet ﷺ. Hatib was summoned and asked why he had done it. He explained that he had no clan in Mecca to protect his family as the other companions did, and had sought to earn Qurayshi goodwill that might protect them — but he had not apostasized and had not intended to harm the Muslim community. Umar asked for permission to execute him. The Prophet ﷺ declined: "He was present at Badr. What will make you know — perhaps Allah has looked upon the people of Badr and said: 'Do as you wish, for I have forgiven you.'"

## The March on Mecca

The army assembled was 10,000 strong — the largest force the Prophet ﷺ had ever commanded to that point. It marched on Mecca in Ramadan 8 AH, keeping the destination secret until the force was already close to the city. The Qurayshi violation of Hudaybiyyah had given the Prophet ﷺ the legal and moral grounds to end the treaty. The ten-year truce had lasted approximately two years. Its violation by the Quraysh — with their direct participation in attacks on the Prophet's ﷺ allies — was the event that ended the era of negotiated peace and opened the era of the Conquest.

The treaty's violation and the subsequent Conquest of Mecca transformed the Quraysh's position from that of equal signatories to a peace agreement to that of a city under the Prophet's ﷺ authority. The Quraysh had held the advantage when the treaty was signed; by the time they violated it, that advantage had been eliminated by two years of Muslim growth. Abu Sufyan recognized this when he came to Medina seeking renewal and found that the Prophet ﷺ had nothing to say to him. The silence was the answer.

The Hatib incident is remembered in Islamic jurisprudence as a case that established the distinction between apostasy and a sinful act committed by a believer under duress or misguided judgment. Hatib had acted wrongly, but his motivation was not the rejection of Islam — it was fear for his family. The Prophet's ﷺ mercy toward him, grounded in his presence at Badr, established the principle that past service and sincere faith weigh in a person's account even when they commit serious errors. The verse of Surah al-Mumtahanah (60:1) was revealed addressing those who take enemies of Allah as allies — but Hatib was pardoned notwithstanding the verse, on the grounds of what the Prophet ﷺ knew of his heart.
