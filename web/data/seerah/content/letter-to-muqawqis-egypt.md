# The Prophet's Letter to al-Muqawqis of Egypt

In 7 AH (628-629 CE), the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) sent Hatib ibn Abi Balta'ah as his messenger to al-Muqawqis — the ruler of Egypt, identified in Arabic sources as the governor or patriarch of Alexandria under Byzantine suzerainty. The letter was one of the series of royal letters sent to world rulers after the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah.

## Al-Muqawqis and His Identity

The identity of "al-Muqawqis" has been debated by historians. Classical Islamic sources consistently use this title for the ruler of Egypt who received the Prophet's letter. Modern historians have proposed various identifications, with Cyrus of Alexandria (the Melkite patriarch and Byzantine governor, also known as al-Muqawqis in some sources) being one candidate. Whatever his precise identity, he was the effective ruler of Egypt under Byzantine authority at the time.

## The Letter

The text of the letter, preserved in classical seerah works:

*"In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate. From Muhammad, the servant and Messenger of Allah, to al-Muqawqis, the leader of the Copts. Peace be upon whoever follows guidance. To proceed: I invite you to the call of Islam. Accept Islam and you will be safe; accept Islam and Allah will give you a double reward. If you turn away, then upon you is the sin of the Copts."*

The letter then cited the Quranic verse inviting People of the Scripture to common ground (Quran 3:64).

## Al-Muqawqis's Response

Al-Muqawqis was, by the accounts, the most diplomatically gracious of the rulers who received the Prophet's letters. He received the messenger with honor, had the letter read and translated, and composed a careful reply that was respectful without accepting the invitation to Islam:

He acknowledged that a prophet was expected, though he said he had expected the prophet to appear in Syria (the biblical lands) rather than Arabia. He declined to accept Islam personally, but sent generous gifts with his reply: two slave women (Maria and Sirin, who were Coptic sisters), a grey mule named Duldul, a donkey named Ya'fur, fine linen garments, and honey.

His letter reportedly said: "I knew a prophet was coming, but I thought he would appear from the land of Syria. I have honored your messenger, and I send you two maidens who are held in high esteem among the Copts, and clothing, and a mule to ride upon."

## Maria al-Qibtiyya

Maria (Mariyah the Copt) became the mother of Ibrahim, the Prophet's youngest child, who was born in 8 AH and died at approximately 17 months. The Prophet's relationship with Maria — who embraced Islam — and the birth and death of Ibrahim are among the most personally moving episodes of the Prophet's final years. Maria's sister Sirin was given to Hassan ibn Thabit, the Prophet's court poet.

Duldul, the grey mule sent by al-Muqawqis, became the Prophet's most frequently used riding animal and was present at major events including the conquest of Mecca. The donkey Ya'fur was also regularly used.

## The Original Letter

In 1858 CE, a letter claimed to be the original letter of the Prophet to al-Muqawqis was found in a monastery in upper Egypt and acquired by a French scholar. The document, which bears the seal "Muhammad Rasul Allah," was brought to Istanbul and later housed in the Topkapi Palace Museum. Scholars have debated its authenticity, with some affirming it as genuine and others skeptical of its age and provenance.

## Significance

**Egypt's position in Islamic history.** Al-Muqawqis's diplomatic response — respectful but non-committal — foreshadowed Egypt's relatively peaceful transition to Arab-Muslim governance. When Amr ibn al-As led the Muslim conquest of Egypt in 20-21 AH, the Copts of Egypt were largely treated as dhimmi (protected non-Muslim subjects) with their rights preserved — partly because of the existing framework established by al-Muqawqis's diplomatic engagement with the Prophet.

**Maria's role.** The gift of Maria al-Qibtiyya was among the most consequential personal outcomes of the letter. Her son Ibrahim, though he died in infancy, was the only child born to the Prophet during his years in Medina. Her presence in the Prophet's household connected the first Islamic community to Coptic Egypt.

**The diplomatic model.** Al-Muqawqis's response demonstrates that non-Muslim rulers who treated the Prophet's messengers respectfully and responded diplomatically, even without accepting Islam, were treated with equivalent courtesy.
