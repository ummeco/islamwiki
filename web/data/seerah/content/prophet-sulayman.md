# Prophet Sulayman (Solomon), Peace Be Upon Him

Prophet Sulayman ibn Dawud (peace be upon him) was given what no human being before or after him has been given: a kingdom encompassing not only men but wind, jinn, and the speech of animals, all subjected to his command by Allah's direct grant. His reign was not merely a political or military achievement but a theological demonstration — a proof of what divine favor looks like when it manifests in the material world without corrupting the one who receives it. The Quran presents Sulayman as a model of gratitude: a king who never forgot that everything he possessed came from One above him, and who spent his life in acknowledgment of that reality.

## The Supplication for an Unmatched Kingdom

Before any account of Sulayman's power, the Quran records his prayer: "My Lord, forgive me and grant me a kingdom such as will not belong to anyone after me. Indeed, You are the Bestower" (Quran 38:35). This supplication is striking in two respects. First, it is entirely transparent — Sulayman asks outright for a kingdom, without dressing the request in more pious language. Second, he asks specifically that it be unmatched by anyone who comes after him. Allah accepted this supplication in full.

Sulayman's reign stands as a unique event in human history: unrepeatable by design. Every subsequent king who has commanded vast territories, every ruler who has bent nations to his will, has done so within the limits of human capacity. Sulayman operated beyond those limits entirely, by explicit divine appointment.

## The Wind and the Jinn

Two of the most remarkable elements of Sulayman's kingdom were his command over the wind and his authority over the jinn.

"And to Sulayman We subjected the wind — its morning course was a month's journey, and its afternoon course was a month's journey" (Quran 34:12). The carpets or craft upon which Sulayman traveled moved with the wind according to his command — a journey of two months completed in a single day. This was not a natural phenomenon he exploited but a subjugation Allah enacted on his behalf.

The jinn served him in two capacities: as builders and as divers. "And from among the jinn were those who worked for him by the permission of his Lord. And whoever of them deviated from Our command — We would make him taste of the punishment of the Blaze. They made for him what he wished of elevated chambers, statues, bowls like reservoirs, and stationary kettles" (Quran 34:12-13). The construction projects of Sulayman's kingdom — including, by the consensus of Islamic scholars, the sacred mosque in Jerusalem (Bayt al-Maqdis) — were built in part through jinn labor. This was not sorcery or forbidden conjuration; it was a direct gift from Allah, as specific to Sulayman as iron was to Dawud.

### A Theological Note

Some people in history sought to access jinn through occult means, claiming to follow the example of Sulayman. The Quran addresses this directly in Surah al-Baqarah, where it notes that it was not Sulayman who disbelieved — commanding jinn through divine authority is not disbelief — but rather those who taught sorcery, which is explicitly condemned (Quran 2:102). The subjugation of jinn to Sulayman was a prophetic miracle specific to him, not a practice available to or sanctioned for ordinary human beings.

## The Language of Animals: The Ant and the Hoopoe

Sulayman was granted the ability to understand the speech of birds and animals. The Quran records a celebrated incident involving an ant:

"Until, when they came upon the valley of the ants, an ant said: 'O ants, enter your dwellings that you not be crushed by Sulayman and his soldiers while they perceive not.' So Sulayman smiled, amused at her speech, and said: 'My Lord, enable me to be grateful for Your favor which You have bestowed upon me and upon my parents, and to do righteousness of which You approve. And admit me by Your mercy into the ranks of Your righteous servants'" (Quran 27:18-19).

The detail here is spiritually precise. Sulayman's immediate response to this astonishing moment — overhearing an ant speak — is not pride, not wonder at his own powers, but a prayer for gratitude. He smiles, and then he asks Allah to help him be worthy of what he has been given.

A bird called the hoopoe (hudhud) served as a scout in Sulayman's kingdom. One day it was absent from its post. Sulayman said: "I will surely punish it with a severe punishment or slaughter it unless it brings me a clear reason." The hoopoe returned and reported that it had discovered a queen in Saba (Sheba) — a woman named Bilqis — who ruled a great kingdom, whose people worshipped the sun rather than Allah (Quran 27:20-26). This report set in motion one of the most remarkable diplomatic episodes in prophetic history.

## The Queen of Sheba

Sulayman sent the hoopoe back with a letter for Bilqis, opening with the words of Allah and calling her to Islam. Her advisors offered her military backing. She was wise enough to recognize that if he were truly a prophet and king of the scale described, war would be futile. She sent a delegation with gifts to test him.

Sulayman dismissed the gifts: "Do you provide me with wealth? What Allah has given me is better than what He has given you" (Quran 27:36). He sent the delegation back and summoned Bilqis herself. Before she arrived, he asked which of his court could bring him her throne. A man who had knowledge of the Book — identified by the majority of classical scholars as either the Angel Jibril, or a righteous human scholar named Asif ibn Barkhiya — said: "I will bring it to you before your glance returns to you." In less than the blink of an eye, the throne was there before him (Quran 27:40).

Sulayman ordered the throne modified slightly, to see whether Bilqis would recognize it. She arrived, was asked if this was her throne, and answered carefully: "It is as though it were." When she entered the palace, she mistook the polished crystal floor for water and lifted her garment. Sulayman informed her that it was a palace of smooth glass. Bilqis, perceiving a power and wisdom entirely beyond the material world, declared her faith: "My Lord, I have wronged myself, and I submit with Sulayman to Allah, Lord of the worlds" (Quran 27:44). Her conversion is presented by the Quran without ambiguity — a queen, through the encounter with genuine prophethood, surrendered to the truth.

## The Death of Sulayman

Sulayman's death is described in the Quran in a passage of profound theological implication. He died while leaning on his staff, and the jinn who were laboring for him continued to work, unaware that he had passed. A wood-boring worm ate through the staff over time, and when it finally gave way, Sulayman fell (Quran 34:14). It was only then that the jinn realized he was dead.

The Quran draws an explicit lesson: "If the jinn had known the unseen, they would not have remained in humiliating punishment" (Quran 34:14). This verse refutes any claim that jinn possess knowledge of the unseen (ghayb), which belongs to Allah alone. Those who attributed special hidden knowledge to the jinn — whether through occult practice or false belief — are corrected by this simple narrative fact: the jinn laboring for Sulayman did not know their master had died. They cannot know what Allah has not shown them.

Sulayman was buried, and the exact location of his tomb is not established with certainty in authenticated Islamic sources.

## His Legacy

Sulayman's kingdom was unprecedented and unrepeatable. His life demonstrates that immense worldly power — over wind, over jinn, over the animal world, over vast human kingdoms — does not corrupt a man when that power comes from Allah and is received with gratitude. At each juncture in the Quran, Sulayman's response to his gifts is to pray, to give thanks, and to seek Allah's further mercy. He never claims the power as his own.

The Temple built by the jinn under his direction — Bayt al-Maqdis in Jerusalem — became one of the holiest sites in Islamic geography, the site from which the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ascended to the heavens on the Night Journey, and the first qibla of the early Muslim community. It remains among the most sacred places on earth, a monument connected to the line of prophets from Ibrahim through Dawud through Sulayman.
