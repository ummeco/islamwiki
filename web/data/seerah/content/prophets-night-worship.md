# The Prophet's Night Worship and Devotional Life

The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) was known throughout his life for exceptional devotion in private worship — particularly his night prayers (qiyam al-layl or tahajjud), which went far beyond what he required of his community. His night worship is documented in extensive detail in the hadith literature, primarily through the narrations of his wives who shared his domestic space and could observe his private practice.

## The Prophet's Night Prayer

The Prophet would regularly wake in the night — often in the last third of the night — for extended prayer. Aisha described his practice: he would sleep for part of the night, then wake, perform ablution, and pray until the call to Fajr. She said: "He would pray so long at night that his feet would swell." When asked about this, he replied: "Should I not be a grateful servant?"

The Quran had initially commanded the night prayer as almost obligatory (Quran 73:1-4), then eased the requirement (73:20). For the Prophet himself, the night prayer remained a near-obligation for his entire life.

He typically prayed eight rak'ahs (units of prayer) in sets of two, then three units of witr (the final odd-numbered prayer of the night). He would sometimes recite very long portions of the Quran in a single rak'ah.

## The Du'a' (Supplication) of the Night

The Prophet's night prayers were accompanied by extensive supplication (du'a'). Many of his night supplications have been preserved in the hadith collections. Among the most famous is the supplication of the night prayer (istiftah al-qiyam):

*"O Allah, to You belongs all praise. You are the Light of the heavens and the earth and whoever is in them. To You belongs all praise. You are the Sustainer of the heavens and the earth and whoever is in them..."*

This extended supplication, preserved in Sahih Bukhari through Ibn Abbas who observed the Prophet's night prayer, reveals the depth and breadth of the Prophet's theological consciousness in moments of private devotion.

## Fasting

The Prophet fasted Mondays and Thursdays as a regular practice — explaining that these were the days on which deeds were presented before Allah, and he wanted to be fasting when his deeds were presented. He also fasted three days of each lunar month (typically the 13th, 14th, and 15th — the bright days, ayyam al-beed). He fasted the month of Ramadan completely and added extra fasts in Sha'ban (the month before Ramadan), about which he said it was a month between Rajab and Ramadan that many people overlooked.

The Prophet was so regular in voluntary fasting that Aisha said: "He would sometimes fast so many days in a row that we would say he will never stop, and sometimes he would not fast so many days that we would say he will never fast again."

## Dhikr (Remembrance of Allah)

The Prophet's dhikr was continuous. Aisha said he remembered Allah at all times — before and after eating, entering and leaving the house, lying down and rising, going to the market and leaving it. He had specific supplications for every activity. His remembrance of Allah was not confined to formal prayer but pervaded all of daily life.

He frequently recited specific formulas: the tasbih (Subhanallah — Glory be to Allah), the tahmid (Alhamdulillah — all praise is for Allah), the tahlil (La ilaha illallah — there is no god but Allah), and the takbir (Allahu Akbar — Allah is the Greatest). He said that reciting these four phrases was more beloved to him than everything the sun rises over.

## The Impact on His Community

The Prophet's private devotional life had a direct impact on his community. When companions visited his house and witnessed his worship, they were inspired and sometimes felt their own worship was inadequate. In a famous incident, a group of companions came to ask about the Prophet's worship and, when told of it, felt they should do more — one said he would pray all night, another that he would fast every day without break, a third that he would never marry. When the Prophet heard this, he said: "I am the most God-fearing among you, but I fast and break the fast, I pray and I sleep, and I marry women. Whoever turns away from my Sunnah is not of me."

This established the principle that the Islamic path is a middle way — intense devotion in its proper forms, but not asceticism that denies the body, the family, and the community their rights.

## Sources

The Prophet's devotional life is documented across all major hadith collections, especially in Kitab al-Salah and Kitab al-Dhikr sections of Sahih Bukhari and Sahih Muslim. The primary narrators are Aisha bint Abi Bakr and Ibn Abbas.
