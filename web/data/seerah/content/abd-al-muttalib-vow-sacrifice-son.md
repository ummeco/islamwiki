# Abd al-Muttalib's Vow and the Ransom of Abdullah

## Abd al-Muttalib: The Patriarch of Mecca

Abd al-Muttalib ibn Hashim was the Prophet's ﷺ paternal grandfather and, in the decades preceding the Prophet's birth, the most honored and influential man in Mecca. His real name was Shaybah — meaning "the grey-haired one," because he was born with a streak of white hair — and he was called "Abd al-Muttalib" (slave of al-Muttalib) only because when his uncle al-Muttalib first brought him from Medina to Mecca as a boy, those who saw the child riding behind al-Muttalib assumed he was a slave. The name stuck, though al-Muttalib himself corrected the mistake at the time.

Abd al-Muttalib inherited and then greatly expanded the honor of the Hashimite clan. He rediscovered and excavated the Zamzam well — a story of immense importance in Meccan history, involving a dream in which he was commanded to dig in a specific location, and a process that required extraordinary persistence against tribal opposition. When the well was rediscovered and its waters proved abundant, his prestige reached its zenith. He became the undisputed chieftain of Mecca, responsible for providing water to pilgrims through the sacred well and for the general leadership of the Qurayshi community.

It was in this context — the discovery of Zamzam, the gratitude to Allah, and the responsibilities of leadership — that Abd al-Muttalib made the vow that would directly determine the life or death of Abdullah, the Prophet's father.

## The Vow and Its Context

The classical sources differ slightly on the precise occasion of the vow, but the majority account — preserved in detail by Ibn Hisham in his Sirah and referenced by Ibn Kathir in Al-Bidaya wa'l-Nihaya — connects the vow to the excavation of Zamzam. The story is approximately 58 years before the Hijra.

When Abd al-Muttalib set out to excavate the Zamzam well (guided by a dream commanding him to dig between the idols Isaf and Nailah), he had only one son to help him — al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib. This relative isolation at a moment of great labor and some danger (since digging near the sacred precinct was a matter of tribal dispute) caused Abd al-Muttalib to make a solemn vow to Allah: if He blessed him with ten sons who survived to manhood, he would sacrifice one of them in gratitude at the Ka'bah.

The vow reflects the pre-Islamic understanding of sacred obligation. The Arabs of that period, even those without a complete prophetic religion, retained a sense of covenant with divine power — particularly in matters of great request or great gratitude. A vow to sacrifice a child in fulfillment of a covenant was not unknown in the Near Eastern world (the story of Ibrahim's near-sacrifice of his son Ismail was part of their collective heritage), and Abd al-Muttalib understood his vow in that tradition of absolute commitment to what had been promised before God.

Allah answered his prayer. Zamzam was discovered and its waters flowed. Over the following years, Abd al-Muttalib was blessed with ten sons who survived to adulthood: al-Harith, al-Zubayr, Abu Talib, Abdullah, Hamzah, Abu Lahab (Abd al-Uzzah), al-Ghaydaq, al-Muqawwim, Safar, and al-Abbas. When the tenth son reached maturity, Abd al-Muttalib remembered his vow and resolved to fulfill it.

## Drawing the Lots

The mechanism by which the vow would be fulfilled was the casting of lots — the ancient Arabian practice of using arrows (azlam) to determine divine will or apportion decisions. Abd al-Muttalib gathered his ten sons, explained the vow he had made, and announced that he intended to honor it. According to Ibn Hisham, all ten sons accepted the situation and told their father to proceed — an act of remarkable collective submission to the father's covenant with the divine.

Abd al-Muttalib took the lots to the sanctuary of Hubal, the chief idol of the Ka'bah, where the custodian of the lots managed such consultations. Each son's name was written on a lot (arrow shaft), and the lots were cast. When the arrows were drawn, the lot indicating sacrifice fell upon Abdullah — Abd al-Muttalib's youngest and most beloved son, described in the classical sources as exceptionally beautiful and of outstanding character.

The sources note that Abdullah was Abd al-Muttalib's dearest child. Ibn Hisham records that when the lot fell on Abdullah, Abd al-Muttalib wept. But his resolve did not waver. He took Abdullah by the hand and led him toward the Ka'bah with a knife prepared for the sacrifice.

## Quraysh Intervenes

What happened next demonstrates how the entire subsequent history of the Muslim ummah — and indeed of humanity — turned on a single intervention. The Quraysh, watching as Abd al-Muttalib led his beloved son to slaughter, were horrified. The women of Banu Makhzum, kinswomen of Abdullah's mother, wept and tried to intercede. The men of Quraysh gathered and pleaded with Abd al-Muttalib to find an alternative.

Several of Abd al-Muttalib's daughters and much of Quraysh urged him to redeem Abdullah with camels. Abd al-Muttalib, unwilling to simply break his vow but also recognizing the weight of their concern, agreed to seek guidance. They told him to consult a wise woman — described in the sources as a kāhina (prophetess or soothsayer) or a woman of great wisdom — who lived in Medina (Yathrib). She was said to have a familiar spirit (this was the pre-Islamic era) that could help determine matters of sacred consequence.

Abd al-Muttalib, accompanied by some of the Quraysh, traveled to Medina. The woman they consulted (Ibn Hisham gives different versions with some uncertainty about whether the consultation was in Medina or with a wise man elsewhere) instructed them to cast lots between Abdullah and a group of camels. The standard blood-money of the time was ten camels for a man's life. She — or the process she described — indicated that if the lot fell on the camels, Abdullah was redeemed; if it fell on Abdullah again, they should add more camels and repeat.

## The Lots of Ten Camels

Abd al-Muttalib returned to Mecca and performed the lot-casting at the sanctuary. He put Abdullah's name against ten camels. The lot fell on Abdullah. He added ten more camels — twenty against Abdullah's name — and cast again. Again the lot fell on Abdullah. This process continued, with Abd al-Muttalib adding ten camels each round and the lot persistently falling on Abdullah, until the camels numbered one hundred.

On the tenth round — one hundred camels against Abdullah's name — the lot finally fell on the camels.

The crowd that had gathered cried out that Abd al-Muttalib was now free of his vow. But Abd al-Muttalib, a man of scrupulous conscience, was not satisfied with a single result. He repeated the casting two more times, and both times the lot fell on the camels. Only then did he accept that the divine decree had spoken clearly: Abdullah was to live, and one hundred camels were to be slaughtered in his place.

## The Hundred Camels and the Law of Blood Money

Abd al-Muttalib slaughtered all one hundred camels at the Ka'bah, and the meat was distributed to the people of Mecca — human and animal alike, with no one turned away. Ibn Hisham notes that even the wild birds and beasts were allowed to eat. This act of total distribution, holding nothing back, was characteristic of the Hashimite tradition of generosity.

From this event, the Islamic scholars note, emerged the standard of one hundred camels as the blood-money (diyya) for a human life. The Prophet ﷺ himself later confirmed this standard in Islamic law. Ibn Hisham records that the Prophet ﷺ said: "I am the son of the two who were to be slaughtered" — referring both to Abdullah (ransomed by camels) and to his ancestor Ismail (ransomed by the ram when Ibrahim was commanded to sacrifice him). Al-Hakim and others record this in their collections.

The classical jurists — Ibn Qudamah in al-Mughni, al-Nawawi in al-Majmu', and others — acknowledge that the blood-money standard of 100 camels, though it predates Islam, was confirmed by the Prophet ﷺ and incorporated into Islamic law. The ransom of Abdullah thus had a direct legislative legacy in the Shariah.

## Abdullah Marries Aminah

Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib, having been redeemed from sacrifice, went on to marry Aminah bint Wahb — a woman of the Banu Zuhra clan of Quraysh, described in the sources as the most honorable woman of Quraysh in terms of lineage and character. The marriage was arranged by Abd al-Muttalib, and it was from this union that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was born, in the Year of the Elephant, approximately one year after the marriage.

The sources note that Abdullah was young and exceptionally handsome at the time of his marriage. There is a famous account that on the day Abd al-Muttalib was leading Abdullah to the Ka'bah for what the crowd thought would be a sacrifice, women who saw Abdullah wept out of love for him — he was that striking in appearance. Aminah bint Wahb, his wife, was said to have experienced a blessed pregnancy, free of the difficulties that ordinarily attended childbirth in that era.

Abdullah died before the Prophet ﷺ was born — or according to some accounts, when the Prophet ﷺ was a very young infant — while on a trading journey to Medina. He was approximately twenty-five years old. His death left the Prophet ﷺ an orphan from birth, a condition that Allah later referenced in Surah ad-Duha (93:6): "Did He not find you an orphan and give you shelter?"

## Reflections on Divine Providence

The story of Abd al-Muttalib's vow and Abdullah's ransom is read by the scholars of Islamic history as a luminous example of divine providence operating through the choices of individuals who did not yet have complete prophetic guidance. Abd al-Muttalib was not a Muslim in the Islamic sense — he lived in the era of Jahiliyyah, before the revelation was sent to his grandson. Yet he possessed a profound sense of covenant with Allah, a willingness to honor his promises at any personal cost, and a generosity of spirit that expressed itself in how he distributed the ransom camels to every creature that could eat.

The Prophet ﷺ spoke of Abd al-Muttalib with respect, acknowledging his grandfather's role in protecting and supporting him. While the ultimate fate of those who died before Islam's revelation is a matter of scholarly discussion, what is clear is that Abd al-Muttalib's life was shaped by qualities — truthfulness, courage, generous hospitality, and a deep sense of obligation to the divine — that presaged and prepared the environment into which the Prophet ﷺ would be born and raised.

The lot that fell on the camels on the tenth round was not mere chance. It was the divine decree ensuring that the father of the Seal of the Prophets would live, would marry, and would beget the one for whose sake, in the deepest theological sense, the universe itself was created.
