# The Prophet's Letter to al-Mundhir ibn Sawa of Bahrain

Among the letters dispatched by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) to regional rulers in 7 AH was a letter to al-Mundhir ibn Sawa al-Tamimi — the governor or chief ruler of Bahrain (which in this period referred to the eastern Arabian coastal region, not the modern island state). Unlike the letters to the major world empires, the letter to al-Mundhir yielded one of the most positive and substantive responses: his acceptance of Islam and the integration of Bahrain into the Muslim polity.

## Al-Mundhir ibn Sawa

Al-Mundhir ibn Sawa was the Arab ruler of the Bahrayn region — the eastern coast of the Arabian Peninsula including the area around present-day Qatif, al-Ahsa, and the Bahrain islands. The region was religiously mixed: Arab tribes of various religious orientations, including significant communities of Zoroastrians, Jews, and Christians, particularly from the Persian-influenced populations.

Al-Mundhir had previously had correspondence with the Prophet, and there was an existing relationship. Some accounts suggest he was already positively disposed toward the Prophet's message.

## The Letter

The Prophet sent al-Ala ibn al-Hadrami as his messenger to Bahrain, carrying the letter of invitation. The letter followed the established format of the royal letters: inviting the ruler to Islam, warning of the consequences of refusal, and citing the Quranic invitation to People of the Scripture.

## Al-Mundhir's Response

Al-Mundhir's response was among the most positive and detailed of any ruler who received one of these letters. He wrote back accepting Islam and reporting that the Muslims of Bahrain and the surrounding region had also accepted. He also noted the presence of Zoroastrian (Magi) and Jewish communities in the region and asked for guidance on how to deal with them.

The Prophet wrote back with instructions that became an important reference in Islamic jurisprudence for the treatment of non-Muslim minorities in Muslim-governed territories:

- The Jewish and Zoroastrian communities were to be offered the choice of accepting Islam or remaining in their faith and paying jizyah (the tax on non-Muslim subjects in exchange for protection)
- Those who chose to remain in their faith were to be protected, their religious practices were not to be interfered with, and they were not to be compelled toward Islam
- Their business dealings and financial arrangements, including those involving ribā (usury), which was common in Zoroastrian trade practice, were to be tolerated between themselves (i.e., the Muslim state would not police the internal financial practices of non-Muslim communities)

## Bahrain's Integration

Bahrain's integration into the Muslim polity through al-Mundhir's acceptance of Islam was peaceful and voluntary — a contrast to some of the other regions that were brought under Muslim governance through military campaigns. Al-Ala ibn al-Hadrami remained in Bahrain as the Prophet's governor.

After the Prophet's death, some of the tribes of Bahrain participated in the Riddah movements, but the region was relatively quickly restabilized under Abu Bakr.

## Significance

**Voluntary acceptance.** The Bahrain case is one of the clearest examples of a regional ruler voluntarily accepting Islam and aligning his territory with the Muslim state through correspondence rather than military compulsion.

**The jizyah precedent.** The Prophet's instructions to al-Mundhir about the treatment of Zoroastrian and Jewish communities in Bahrain are among the foundational references for the dhimma system in Islamic jurisprudence. The extension of dhimma status to Zoroastrians (Majus) in particular was a significant legal development: while the Quran explicitly identifies Jews and Christians as People of the Scripture (Ahl al-Kitab), the status of Zoroastrians was debated. The Prophet's instruction that they be treated like the People of the Scripture settled the question for subsequent jurists.

## Sources

The account of the letter and al-Mundhir's response is preserved in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*. The Prophet's response to al-Mundhir's questions is in the Muwatta of Imam Malik and the Sunan literature.
