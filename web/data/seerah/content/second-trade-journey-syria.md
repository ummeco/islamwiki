# The Prophet's Trade Journey to Syria for Khadijah

## Khadijah bint Khuwaylid: The Businesswoman of Mecca

To understand the trade journey to Syria, one must first understand the woman who commissioned it. Khadijah bint Khuwaylid ibn Asad ibn Abd al-Uzzah was, by all accounts in the classical sources, the most prominent businesswoman in Mecca — and indeed one of the most successful traders in Arabia. She was a widow who had been married twice before and who, after the death of her second husband, had taken charge of her own considerable fortune and continued expanding the trading enterprise herself, hiring agents and investing in caravans.

Ibn Hisham describes her as a woman of nobility, intelligence, and substantial wealth. The classical sources emphasize that she was known in Mecca for the quality of the merchandise she sent, for the trustworthiness of her commercial arrangements, and for her ability to identify and hire the best trade representatives. She paid her agents well and had a reputation for fair dealing — qualities that placed her in a distinct position in Meccan commercial life.

She was also, the sources tell us, known as Khadijah al-Kubra — "Khadijah the Great" — a title that spoke to her standing in the community, which encompassed not only her wealth but her character, her lineage (from the honorable Asad clan of Quraysh), and her wisdom.

When Khadijah heard reports about the young man from Banu Hashim — Muhammad ibn Abdullah, known throughout Mecca as Al-Amin — she recognized an opportunity. He had already made one trading journey to Syria, reportedly at a younger age, accompanying his uncle Abu Talib. He had returned from that journey with accounts of the northern markets, the Byzantine trade networks, and the routes through the Levant. His reputation for honesty in commercial dealings was impeccable. And Khadijah, who understood commerce, understood that an honest agent who returned with full accounts and no losses was worth more than any percentage of profit.

## The Proposal of Employment

Khadijah's approach to hiring the Prophet ﷺ was, according to Ibn Hisham's account, facilitated through intermediaries — specifically through a woman named Nafisa bint Munabbih (also recorded in some sources as Nafisa bint Munya), who was a friend and confidante of Khadijah. Nafisa first sounded out the Prophet ﷺ about his interest in leading a caravan to Syria on Khadijah's behalf.

The Prophet ﷺ, who was approximately twenty-five years old at this time (approximately 28 years before the Hijra), agreed to the arrangement. Khadijah offered him terms that were more generous than what she offered her other agents — double the share — reflecting her confidence in his abilities and the premium she placed on reliability. She also sent with him her servant Maysara, who would serve as a traveling companion, assistant, and, as events transpired, a witness to everything that occurred on the journey.

The caravan was substantial. Khadijah's trading operations were among the largest in Mecca, and a summer journey to Syria — the region of Busra (Bostra) in what is now southern Syria/northern Jordan, a major Byzantine commercial hub — would have involved significant capital, a large train of loaded camels, and multiple personnel. The Prophet ﷺ was the principal agent, responsible for the commercial decisions: what to sell, to whom, at what price, what to buy for the return journey, and how to navigate the commercial relationships in the Syrian markets.

## The Journey North

The route from Mecca to Busra passed through the Hijaz northward, skirting the desert, passing through or near Medina (Yathrib), continuing through the territory of various tribes whose goodwill or at least neutrality was secured by the trade agreements the Quraysh maintained under the ilaf system established by Hashim ibn Abd Manaf. The journey was physically demanding — long distances in desert heat, managing a large caravan of camels, men, and merchandise — and commercially complex, requiring negotiation at multiple points along the road.

The classical sources record that the Prophet ﷺ conducted the entire journey with the same qualities that had earned him the title Al-Amin in Mecca: absolute honesty in transactions, patient and fair dealing with all parties, and a dignified bearing that commanded respect from those he encountered.

## The Monk at Busra: Bahira's Successor

When the caravan reached Busra, something occurred that would become one of the most discussed details of this journey in the Seerah literature. Ibn Hisham records — drawing on Ibn Ishaq — that a Christian monk in Busra, named Nestorius in some accounts (and referred to in other versions simply as a monk at a particular monastery), saw the caravan approach and specifically asked Maysara who the young man leading it was. He then made a remarkable statement: that this young man was a prophet (or would be a prophet), and that he recognized in him signs that the scriptures had described.

This encounter echoes — and is clearly connected to — the earlier story of Bahira the monk, who had recognized signs of prophethood in the young Muhammad ﷺ when he traveled to Syria as a child with his uncle Abu Talib. In that earlier encounter (approximately at age twelve), Bahira had noticed that a cloud seemed to shade the Prophet ﷺ and that the tree he sat under bent its branches over him. He had examined the physical signs described in the scriptures and declared to Abu Talib that this child would be a prophet and should be protected from those who might harm him.

The Busra encounter on the second journey repeats this motif. A man with knowledge of the scriptures — Christian, versed in Syriac and in the inherited religious learning of the Near East — recognized what Mecca did not yet know. The scholars of Seerah note that these encounters with knowledgeable Christian monks who recognized prophetic signs are part of a pattern of divine foreshadowing (irhas) — the signs and portents that preceded the formal prophethood and confirmed, to those with eyes to see, that something extraordinary was unfolding.

## Maysara's Observations

Maysara ibn Maymun (al-Khadam), Khadijah's freed servant who accompanied the Prophet ﷺ on the journey, was an attentive observer throughout. When they returned to Mecca, his account to Khadijah of what he had witnessed would prove decisive in changing the course of both their lives.

Maysara reported two remarkable things. First, the commercial success of the journey had been exceptional — the Prophet ﷺ had returned with double the profit that other agents typically achieved with equivalent capital. His commercial acumen, his honest dealing with counterparts, and his skill in negotiating favorable terms had produced outstanding results. For a businesswoman like Khadijah, this was significant information about the quality of the agent she had hired.

But second, and more striking to Maysara, were his observations of the Prophet ﷺ during the journey. He reported that throughout the heat of the desert journey, a cloud had seemed to shade the Prophet ﷺ — following him even as he moved. He reported the monk's statement at Busra. And he reported a general quality of character and bearing that had distinguished the young Hashimite merchant from every other person Maysara had ever accompanied or observed.

Al-Suhayli and Ibn Hisham both preserve the substance of Maysara's report to Khadijah as she sat hearing his account of the journey. She listened to the testimony of a man she trusted completely, a man who had no motive to embellish, a man who was describing what he had seen with his own eyes across weeks of travel.

## The Return and Its Consequences

The caravan returned to Mecca, and the Prophet ﷺ delivered full accounting to Khadijah of everything that had been bought, sold, and earned. The accounts were complete and honest. The profit was remarkable. Khadijah, who had been an astute observer of men throughout a career of hiring and managing agents, was deeply impressed.

But what she felt was more than professional respect. Nafisa bint Munabbih, who served as the intermediary once again, reports that Khadijah confided that she was inclined toward this man — not merely as a business partner but as a husband. She was approximately forty years old; he was approximately twenty-five. She was wealthy and from an honored family; he was honorable but not wealthy. By the social calculations of Meccan aristocracy, the proposal would seem to favor him. But Khadijah's judgment was otherwise: she recognized that what she had in front of her was a man of extraordinary character, and that character was worth more than any fortune.

Nafisa was sent to sound out the Prophet ﷺ on the matter. He consulted with his uncle Abu Talib. And the marriage was arranged — a marriage that would prove to be the most significant human relationship of the Prophet's life, the relationship that would sustain him through the first devastating years of prophethood, and which he would continue to honor decades after Khadijah's death.

## The Trade Journey as Preparation

The second trade journey to Syria was, in retrospect, a turning point in the Seerah on multiple levels.

Practically, it established the Prophet ﷺ as a man of commercial competence and financial reliability — qualities that would matter when he later conducted the affairs of the young Muslim community in Mecca and Medina. He understood trade, markets, and the management of resources. The Muslim community's later engagements in commerce, including the trade caravans that were a source of both livelihood and political tension in the Medinan period, were managed by a Prophet who knew the reality of commercial life from direct experience.

Spiritually, the journey through Syria — through the lands of ancient prophets, past monasteries preserving fragments of earlier revelation, along routes that generations of pilgrims and prophets had traveled — was a final survey of the world he was about to be commissioned to transform. He passed through the territories of Sham, which the Quran would later describe with the phrase "the land We have blessed" (al-Anbiya 21:71, al-A'raf 7:137), the land from which so many prophets had come and where so much prophetic history had unfolded.

And personally, the journey led directly to his marriage with Khadijah — the first Muslim, the first to believe, the woman who would say to him on the night the first revelation descended: "By Allah, Allah will never disgrace you. You keep good relations with your kith and kin, help the poor and the destitute, serve your guests generously and assist those who are afflicted with calamities." Her testimony of his character on that night of terror was the testimony of a woman who had seen him tested in the hardest arena — the commercial arena of the ancient world — and found him unimpeachable.

## Syria in the Prophetic Narrative

It is worth noting that Syria (al-Sham) occupies a privileged place in the prophetic geography. The Prophet ﷺ would later describe al-Sham as a blessed land and speak of the importance of having Muslims there in the end times. His personal connections to Syria — through the trade journeys of his youth, through the encounter with Bahira as a child, through the second journey and the monk at Busra — gave him a lived relationship with the land that would later feature in his prophetic warnings and blessings.

The trade journey to Syria was the Prophet's ﷺ last extended time outside the Hijaz before his prophethood. It was his farewell, in a sense, to the ordinary life of a Meccan merchant — the life he was about to leave behind entirely for a different kind of journey altogether.
