# Hashim ibn Abd Manaf and the Foundation of Quraysh's Trade Empire

## Who Was Hashim?

Hashim ibn Abd Manaf ibn Qusayy ibn Kilab was the great-great-grandfather of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and one of the most consequential figures in the history of pre-Islamic Arabia. He lived approximately five generations before the Prophet ﷺ, flourishing in the period roughly 123 years before the Hijra. His full name was Amr ibn Abd Manaf, but he became universally known as "Hashim" — a name earned through an act of generosity that would define his legacy and the identity of his clan for all time.

The name Hashim derives from the Arabic root meaning "to crush" or "to break apart." According to Ibn Hisham in his Sirah, during a year of severe famine in Mecca, Hashim slaughtered camels, baked bread, crumbled it into a broth (tharid), and fed the hungry pilgrims and the people of Quraysh. It was said: "Hashim crushed the bread (hashama al-khubz) for his people." From this act of breaking bread for the hungry, he received the epithet that his descendants would carry as a badge of honor — Banu Hashim, the Hashimites, the clan into which the Seal of the Prophets would be born.

## Quraysh Before Hashim

To understand Hashim's contribution, one must first understand what Quraysh was before him. The tribe of Quraysh had settled in Mecca under the leadership of Qusayy ibn Kilab, Hashim's grandfather, who had united the scattered Qurayshi clans and established their control over the Haram, the Sacred Precinct around the Ka'bah. Qusayy organized the key functions of the sanctuary — the provision of water to pilgrims (siqaya), the feeding of pilgrims (rifada), the custodianship of the Ka'bah (hijaba), and the authority of the tribal assembly (nadwa).

However, while Quraysh had political and religious prestige, their economic foundation remained fragile. Mecca sits in a barren valley with no agriculture, no rivers, and no natural resources. The city depended entirely on trade and on the pilgrimage. It was Hashim who transformed this dependency into an empire.

## Establishing the Trade Routes

Hashim ibn Abd Manaf personally undertook the diplomatic groundwork that established Quraysh's bilateral trade routes — the two great annual journeys that would be immortalized in the Quran. He traveled to Syria and secured agreements with the Byzantine emperor (or his regional governors) granting Qurayshi merchants safe passage and trading rights in the Levant. He traveled to Yemen and secured similar agreements with the Himyarite kings of the south. He sent his brothers to negotiate with other regional powers: Naufal secured trading rights with Persia and Iraq, Abd Shams secured agreements with Abyssinia (Ethiopia), and al-Muttalib secured agreements with other Arabian tribes.

These were not mere commercial arrangements. They were formal compacts (ilaf, from which the name of Surah Quraysh is sometimes derived) — agreements of mutual security, safe conduct, and commercial privilege. By establishing these networks, Hashim transformed Mecca from a local pilgrimage center into the commercial hub of Arabia, sitting at the crossroads between the Byzantine north, the Sassanid east, the Himyarite south, and the Abyssinian west.

The caravans that resulted from these agreements were enormous by the standards of the age. According to classical sources, the summer caravan to Syria could consist of hundreds of loaded camels, carrying leather goods, raisins, silver, and spices northward, returning with grain, olive oil, cloth, and Byzantine manufactured goods. The winter caravan to Yemen carried similar volumes in the opposite direction, trading for incense, spices, and luxury goods from the Indian Ocean trade network.

## The Verse of Quraysh

Allah, the Most High, honored these very trade journeys with a mention in the Quran. Surah Quraysh (106:1-4) reads:

*"For the accustomed security of the Quraysh — their accustomed security in the journey of winter and summer — let them worship the Lord of this House, Who has fed them against hunger and made them safe from fear."*

This surah is remarkable for several reasons. It is the only surah of the Quran named after a single tribe. It connects Quraysh's material blessings — their safe journeys, their commerce, their food security — directly to an obligation: gratitude expressed through worship of the Lord of the Ka'bah. The classical commentators, including Ibn Kathir in his Tafsir, explain that the "journey of winter" refers to the caravan to Yemen (in the cooler southern winter months) and the "journey of summer" refers to the caravan to Syria (undertaken in summer).

Al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir, and others trace these journeys directly to Hashim's foundational diplomacy. The blessings described in the verse — security from fear, protection from hunger — were the practical results of the ilaf agreements Hashim negotiated. When the Prophet ﷺ was born into this world, he was born into a family that had, for generations, been the custodians of both the Sacred House and the commercial arteries of Arabia. This background is not incidental to his prophethood; it is context that Allah Himself saw fit to preserve in the eternal record of revelation.

## The Siqaya and Rifada

Beyond trade, Hashim also institutionalized two critical services for pilgrims visiting the Ka'bah. The siqaya was the provision of water for pilgrims — Hashim organized the drawing and distribution of water from the wells of Mecca for the thousands who came for Hajj and Umrah. The rifada was the feeding of pilgrims — Hashim established a practice of levying a contribution from Qurayshi merchants each year to fund communal meals for pilgrims who could not afford food during their stay.

These were not acts of mere charity in the tribal sense; they were institutionalized obligations that Hashim made into defining characteristics of his clan. Long after his death, the Banu Hashim continued to hold the siqaya as a hereditary honor. Abd al-Muttalib, Hashim's son and the Prophet's grandfather, would later rediscover the Zamzam well and restore the water supply to Mecca, continuing his father's tradition. And when Mecca was conquered in the eighth year after the Hijra, the Prophet ﷺ confirmed that the keys of the Ka'bah and the siqaya would remain with the Banu Hashim and related clans — a continuity stretching back to Hashim himself.

## His Death in Gaza

Hashim's life was consumed by the very trade routes he built. He died not in Mecca but in Gaza — the ancient city at the Mediterranean end of the Arabian trade road, which connects to Syria and the Levant. This fact, preserved in the classical sources, tells us something profound about the man: he died on the road, in the service of the commerce he had established, far from his home in the Hijaz.

He left behind a wife in Medina (then called Yathrib) — a woman of the Banu Najjar clan named Salma bint Amr. From this marriage was born Shaybah ibn Hashim, who would later be brought to Mecca by his uncle al-Muttalib and become known as Abd al-Muttalib — the grandfather of the Prophet ﷺ. The story of how Abd al-Muttalib came to be called by that name (al-Muttalib's "slave," because he was seen riding behind his uncle and people assumed he was a servant) is a famous episode in the Seerah.

## The Hashimite Legacy

Hashim died without seeing the full fruit of what he had planted. But the clan he founded — the Banu Hashim — became the most honored of Quraysh's sub-clans, a status that survived into the Islamic era and beyond. The Prophet ﷺ said, as recorded by Muslim: "Allah chose Kinanah from the sons of Ismail, and chose Quraysh from Kinanah, and chose Banu Hashim from Quraysh, and chose me from Banu Hashim." This chain of divine selection traces back to Hashim's founding of the clan.

The Hashimite legacy did not end with the Prophet ﷺ. The descendants of Hashim through Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) and through al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (may Allah be pleased with him) continued as distinguished families throughout Islamic history. The Abbasid caliphate that ruled from Baghdad for five centuries descended from al-Abbas. The Sharifs of Mecca, who held custodianship of the Holy Cities for a thousand years, descended from the Prophet ﷺ through Ali (may Allah be pleased with him). The current royal families of Jordan and other regions trace their lineage to this same origin.

## Hashim in Prophetic Genealogy

The Prophet's full genealogical chain runs: Muhammad ibn Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib ibn Hashim ibn Abd Manaf ibn Qusayy ibn Kilab ibn Murrah ibn Ka'b ibn Lu'ayy ibn Ghalib ibn Fihr ibn Malik ibn al-Nadr ibn Kinanah — and so back through Adnan to Ismail (Ishmael), the son of Ibrahim (Abraham), peace be upon them both.

In this chain, Hashim stands four generations before the Prophet ﷺ. He is the man who turned the tribal prestige that Qusayy had established into economic power. He is the man who ensured that the family of the Prophet ﷺ would be, for generations before his birth, the preeminent family of Arabia in terms of both honor and sustenance. When Allah says in the Quran (Ad-Duha, 93:8): "And He found you in need and made you self-sufficient," this self-sufficiency had, in part, a material lineage — it was Hashim who built the commercial foundation upon which the family of the Prophet ﷺ stood.

## Theological Reflection

The story of Hashim ibn Abd Manaf invites reflection on how Allah prepares the world for His prophets before those prophets arrive. The trade routes, the siqaya, the rifada, the honored status of Banu Hashim — all of this was a preparation. Mecca needed to be a city of consequence for it to be the birthplace of the final Messenger. The Quraysh needed to be a tribe of authority for the Prophet ﷺ to be heard when he first called them to Islam. The Ka'bah needed to be the center of Arabian life for it to become the Qibla of a global religion.

Hashim did not know he was preparing the world for a prophet. But the classical scholars of Islam understood his significance. Ibn Kathir in his Al-Bidaya wa'l-Nihaya and Ibn Hisham in his Sirah both give Hashim substantial treatment not merely as a historical figure but as a link in the prophetic chain — a man whose life and legacy were, in the language of Islamic theology, part of the divine preparation (tamhid) for the greatest blessing ever bestowed on humanity.
