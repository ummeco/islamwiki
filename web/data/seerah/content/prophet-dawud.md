# Prophet Dawud (David), Peace Be Upon Him

Prophet Dawud ibn Isha (peace be upon him) stands among the greatest figures in Islamic prophetology — a shepherd who became a king, a warrior who became a judge, and a man whose worship was so extraordinary that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ described it as the most beloved voluntary worship to Allah. He was given the Zabur, one of the four major revealed scriptures, and was the father of the Prophet Sulayman (peace be upon him). His life demonstrates that kingship and prophethood, when united in one man, can elevate an entire nation.

## The Shepherd from the Children of Israel

Dawud grew up among the Children of Israel during the time of Talut — the king known in the Bible as Saul — whom Allah had appointed over them despite their initial objections to his lack of wealth. The Children of Israel had asked Allah for a king who could lead them in battle to reclaim their land. Talut was chosen, and one of the signs of his appointment was the miraculous return of the Ark of the Covenant (Tabut) to them (Quran 2:246-248).

Dawud at this time was a young shepherd, not a soldier. He accompanied Talut's army in some capacity — the early commentators differ on whether he was serving his older brothers or was among the fighting men — but what is unambiguous is his extraordinary courage. The Quran describes Dawud as emerging from among the believers who understood that a small, steadfast group could overcome a large army by Allah's permission (Quran 2:249).

## Slaying Jalut and the Rise to Kingship

The turning point of Dawud's life came in his confrontation with Jalut (Goliath), the enormous warrior-king of the opposing army. Jalut was the champion of the enemy forces, and no man among the Israelites could match him. Dawud stepped forward with a sling and a smooth stone. He cast it with precision, and Jalut fell.

"And Dawud killed Jalut, and Allah gave him the kingship and prophethood and taught him from that which He willed" (Quran 2:251). With this single sentence, the Quran compresses the astonishing trajectory of one man's life: a shepherd boy becomes the decisive figure in a war, and Allah then grants him both the authority of a king and the light of prophecy. These are two gifts that, when combined, place their recipient among the rarest of human beings.

Allah gave Dawud the Zabur — the Psalms — as his revealed scripture. It contained prayers, praises, and wisdom. The mountains and birds were commanded to join him in his glorification of Allah: "We subjected the mountains to exalt Us, along with him, at nightfall and at sunrise, and the birds assembled, all joining him in devotion" (Quran 38:18-19). This was not metaphorical. The created world responded to the voice of Dawud as he glorified his Lord.

## The Gift of Craft and the Softening of Iron

Among the miracles Allah bestowed upon Dawud was the softening of iron in his hands. He did not need forge, hammer, or great heat — iron became pliable for him as if it were clay. Allah taught him the craft of making coats of mail (armor): "And We made iron soft for him, saying: 'Make full coats of mail and measure the links with care'" (Quran 34:10-11). This was not merely a supernatural spectacle but a practical gift — the armor Dawud made protected soldiers in battle and represented the first systematic production of defensive equipment among his people. The Quran adds a note of gratitude: "And work righteousness, O family of Dawud. Indeed, I am All-Seeing of what you do" (Quran 34:13).

## His Worship: The Fast of Dawud

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ described Dawud's worship in terms that have been transmitted in the most authenticated collections of hadith. He fasted every other day — one day fasting, one day breaking fast — and this was the most that any human body could sustain with consistency. He slept the first half of the night, stood in prayer for a third, then slept again for a sixth (Bukhari, Muslim). This schedule of worship was so balanced and so comprehensive that the Prophet ﷺ said: "The most beloved fasting to Allah is the fasting of Dawud. The most beloved prayer to Allah is the prayer of Dawud. He used to sleep half the night, then pray a third of it, then sleep a sixth of it" (Sahih al-Bukhari, Kitab al-Anbiya).

When a Companion asked whether he should fast every day, the Prophet ﷺ counseled him toward moderation, saying: "Fast the fast of Dawud, for it is the best fast." This single prophetic endorsement has enshrined the alternating fast in Islamic piety for fourteen centuries.

## The Two Litigants: A Test of Judgment

The Quran describes a striking episode in Dawud's life that has been the subject of extensive scholarly commentary. Two men climbed over the wall of his prayer chamber while he was in private devotion — an unusual and startling intrusion. One man said: "This is my brother. He has ninety-nine ewes and I have one ewe, and he said: 'Entrust her to me,' and he overcame me in speech" (Quran 38:23).

Dawud, without hearing from the second man, immediately pronounced: "He has certainly wronged you in demanding your one ewe to add to his ewes." He then recognized — the Quran says Allah made him realize — that he had been tested. He sought forgiveness from his Lord, prostrated himself, and repented (Quran 38:24-25).

The scholars of tafsir — among them Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, and al-Qurtubi — offer varying explanations for why this was considered an error warranting repentance. The dominant and most defensible scholarly view is that Dawud made a hasty judgment, pronouncing one party wrong before fully hearing the other. This is a principle of justice that applies to all judges: no ruling may be made until both parties have been heard. The severity of Dawud's remorse was proportional to his station — the higher the rank before Allah, the greater the sensitivity to any shortcoming in conduct. His immediate recognition and prostration demonstrate the quality of the prophets: they do not need to be corrected twice.

### Note on Isra'iliyyat

A number of reports in early Islamic literature (drawn from Jewish and Christian traditions, known as Isra'iliyyat) attach additional narrative details to this episode. Classical scholars such as Ibn Kathir explicitly flag these accounts as unreliable Isra'iliyyat that should not be repeated as established Islamic history. The Quran's account is the standard; what is not affirmed by it or authentic hadith cannot be attributed to Dawud.

## Father of Sulayman

Dawud's son Sulayman (peace be upon him) was also made a prophet and king — a dynasty of prophethood unique in the Quran. The Quran records that both Dawud and Sulayman judged a case together involving a field that sheep had entered and grazed at night: Sulayman's judgment was confirmed as the more precise one, and both prophets are praised — "to each We gave judgment and knowledge" (Quran 21:79). This episode shows the relationship between them: mutual respect, shared wisdom, and the continuity of divine guidance across generations.

## His Legacy

Dawud's life carries lessons that cut across every era. He demonstrated that physical courage and spiritual humility are not opposites — the man who slew Goliath with a stone was the same man who wept before Allah in the depths of the night. He built a kingdom and used it for justice. He received a scripture and used it for glorification. And when he was tested through an error of judgment, he did not rationalize or make excuses. He prostrated, he wept, and he returned.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ placed Dawud among the prophets he encountered during the Night Journey, describing him as a man of dignity and stature. His example remains embedded in the daily practice of Muslims through the fast that bears his name — alternating days of hunger and nourishment, a rhythm that mirrors the life of the man himself: always in motion, always returning.
