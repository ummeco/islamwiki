# Death of Ibn al-Jawzi

Abu al-Faraj Abd al-Rahman ibn Ali ibn Muhammad al-Jawzi (510-597 AH / 1116-1201 CE) was one of the greatest Hanbali scholars, preachers, and historians of the medieval period. Prolific to an extraordinary degree, deeply influential as a popular preacher, and a forceful defender of Sunni orthodoxy against Ash'arism, Sufism, and Shia doctrines, he spent nearly eight decades engaged in scholarship, preaching, and writing. His death in Baghdad in 597 AH (1201 CE) marked the end of one of the most productive scholarly careers in Islamic history.

## Life

Ibn al-Jawzi was born in Baghdad in approximately 510 AH (1116 CE). He was orphaned at a young age and was raised and educated by an aunt who took him to study with major scholars of Baghdad. He showed extraordinary intellectual ability from childhood and began preaching publicly as a young man.

He became the leading figure of the Hanbali school in Baghdad during the Abbasid Caliphate's period of partial restoration. The Caliph al-Muqtafi and his successors patronized Ibn al-Jawzi, who gave hugely popular sermon sessions attended by tens of thousands of people. Contemporary accounts describe his preaching style as riveting — combining deep learning with emotional intensity in a way that moved audiences to tears and to repentance.

His career suffered a significant interruption in 590 AH (1194 CE) when Caliph al-Nasir, influenced by opponents, had him imprisoned at Wasit for approximately five years. He was released after the Caliph who had imprisoned him died, and he returned to Baghdad, resuming his preaching and scholarship until his death in 597 AH (1201 CE).

## Prodigious Output

Ibn al-Jawzi's literary output was extraordinary. He claimed to have written 20,000 pages per year for 60 years and to have used the shavings from his reed pens to heat his water for washing for the dead. Modern estimates credit him with between 300 and 700 works across a remarkable range of topics. Of these, approximately 100 survive.

His works include:

**History:** *al-Muntazam fi Tarikh al-Muluk wal-Umam* — a monumental world history in 17 volumes, organized by year from the creation to his own time. It is a primary source for Abbasid history. *Sifat al-Safwa* — biographical dictionary of pious individuals. *Manaqib Ahmad ibn Hanbal* — biography of the founder of the Hanbali school.

**Hadith:** *al-Mawduat* (Fabricated Hadith) — a pioneering encyclopedic work on hadith criticism, listing thousands of hadiths he judged to be fabricated and explaining why.

**Tafsir:** *Zad al-Masir fi Ilm al-Tafsir* — a comprehensive Quranic commentary.

**Ethics and Preaching:** *Talbis Iblis* (The Devil's Deception) — his most famous polemical work, attacking what he considered the errors of the Sufis, philosophers, Ash'arites, and various popular religious practices. *Sayd al-Khatir* (Capturing the Fleeting Thought) — a miscellany of reflections on religious and personal life. *Dhamm al-Hawa* (The Condemnation of Passion) — on controlling worldly desires.

**Medicine and Natural History:** Works on the humoral system, dreams, and natural phenomena.

## The Critique of Sufism and Ash'arism

Among Ibn al-Jawzi's most controversial contributions was his sustained critique of what he considered deviations from pure Hanbali Sunni orthodoxy. *Talbis Iblis* contains extended attacks on Sufi practices he considered innovations (bid'a): listening to music, mystical dancing, antinomianism, and what he saw as the abandonment of the religious law in the name of mystical experience.

His critique of Ash'ari theology from the Hanbali position, and his defence of the Hanbali approach to divine attributes (following a strict literalism that he argued was the approach of the Salaf), made him a central figure in the ongoing debates between the major theological schools.

His grandson was Ibn al-Jawzi the Younger (Sibt ibn al-Jawzi), who paradoxically became a Shia Muslim in addition to being a distinguished historian.

## Legacy

Ibn al-Jawzi's legacy is primarily that of the great Hanbali scholar-preacher. His historical works, particularly al-Muntazam, are invaluable primary sources. His work on fabricated hadiths established important standards for hadith criticism. His preaching tradition influenced subsequent Hanbali scholars, including Ibn Taymiyya (d. 728 AH / 1328 CE), who engaged extensively with Ibn al-Jawzi's works.

He died in Baghdad in Ramadan 597 AH (June 1201 CE). His death was mourned by the people of Baghdad, and the Caliph al-Nasir himself expressed regret over having imprisoned him.
