# Detailed Account of the Expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa

The expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa from Medina in Shawwal 2 AH (March 624 CE) — just weeks after the victory at Badr — was the first major confrontation between the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and one of the Jewish tribes of Medina. It established a precedent for how treaty violations by the Medinan Jewish tribes would be handled and set the pattern for subsequent confrontations with Banu Nadir and Banu Qurayza.

## Banu Qaynuqa

Banu Qaynuqa was one of the three major Jewish tribes of Medina. They were settled in a quarter of their own and were primarily craftsmen and goldsmiths — artisans rather than farmers. They had a strong fortified position within Medina itself. They were party to the Constitution of Medina and its mutual obligations.

Unlike the other two Jewish tribes, Banu Qaynuqa were allied with the Khazraj — one of the two major Arab tribal confederations of Medina. The chief of Khazraj, Abdullah ibn Ubayy (who had outwardly accepted Islam but was the leader of the munafiqun, the hypocrites), was their strongest political patron.

## The Incident in the Marketplace

The triggering incident involved a Muslim woman who came to the Banu Qaynuqa marketplace. A goldsmith reportedly played a trick on her, fastening her garment so that when she stood up, she was exposed. When a Muslim man witnessed this and reacted by killing the goldsmith, he was in turn killed by Banu Qaynuqa members.

The Islamic sources present this as an attack on a Muslim woman's honor and a subsequent killing of a Muslim man — a serious violation of the Medina covenant.

## The Prophet's Address and Their Response

The Prophet gathered Banu Qaynuqa and addressed them, warning them against provoking a conflict similar to what the Quraysh had experienced at Badr (which had just occurred). He invited them to accept Islam or to honor their treaty obligations.

Banu Qaynuqa reportedly responded with arrogance, saying that the Muslim victory at Badr was against men who had no military experience and that if it came to a war with them, the Muslims would learn who was truly capable of fighting. This statement was understood as a repudiation of the treaty.

The Prophet laid siege to Banu Qaynuqa in their fortified quarter. The siege lasted fifteen days. Without external support materializing, Banu Qaynuqa surrendered.

## Abdullah ibn Ubayy's Intervention

The Khazraji tribal connection gave Abdullah ibn Ubayy a basis for intervening. He came to the Prophet and reportedly grabbed the Prophet's collar — a physical act of tribal importuning — demanding that the Banu Qaynuqa be released. The Prophet's face reportedly darkened with anger. He told Abdullah ibn Ubayy to release him.

Abdullah repeated his demand, invoking his client relationship with Banu Qaynuqa and his own honor as their tribal patron. The Prophet eventually said: "They are yours" — meaning he would release them from the death that might otherwise be their penalty — but they were to be expelled from Medina.

## The Expulsion

Banu Qaynuqa left Medina and went initially to Wadi al-Qura (a region north of Medina) and then further north. Their ultimate destination was the Balqa' region in the Syrian steppe, north of Arabia.

They were allowed to take their movable property. Their land and fixed assets remained behind.

## Significance

**The first major confrontation.** The expulsion of Banu Qaynuqa established the precedent that treaty violation by the Medinan Jewish tribes would result in expulsion. The subsequent expulsions of Banu Nadir (4 AH) and the harsher outcome for Banu Qurayza (5 AH) followed the same basic pattern of: trigger event, accusation of treaty violation, siege, surrender, judgment.

**The role of Abdullah ibn Ubayy.** His intervention on behalf of Banu Qaynuqa and the Prophet's accommodation of it (expulsion rather than harsher treatment) illustrates the political complexities of early Medinan society. The Prophet navigated tribal loyalties and political relationships while maintaining the principle that treaty violations could not be ignored.

**The Quranic context.** Verses from Surah al-Ma'idah (5:51) and al-Baqarah (2:100-101) are often linked by commentators to this period of confrontations with the Medinan Jewish tribes, though the specific referents of these verses are debated among exegetes.

## Sources

The account is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*.
