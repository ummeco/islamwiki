# Dispatching of Usamah ibn Zayd's Army

One of the final military preparations made by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) before his death was the organization and dispatch of an army under the command of Usamah ibn Zayd ibn Haritha — a young man of about 17 or 18 years — to campaign in the Byzantine frontier of al-Balqa' (in present-day Jordan). The army's eventual dispatch under Abu Bakr, against considerable opposition within the community, became one of the defining acts of the first Caliphate.

## Background: The Prophet's Appointment

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) organized the expedition in the final days of his illness. He designated Usamah ibn Zayd as commander — a choice that raised eyebrows among some companions, who felt that the young man's youth and relative lack of experience made him an unusual choice to lead senior companions.

The Prophet was aware of the murmuring and addressed it directly. He had previously appointed Usamah's father, Zayd ibn Haritha, to command major expeditions — including the Battle of Mu'tah (8 AH), where Zayd died as a martyr. The Prophet's love for Zayd and his son Usamah was well known; he had adopted Zayd before the Quranic prohibition on adoption in the full legal sense. Usamah had grown up in the Prophet's household.

The expedition was intended to campaign in the region of Ubna in al-Balqa', avenging the Muslim casualties at Mu'tah and reasserting Muslim presence at the Byzantine frontier.

## The Delay

The army had assembled and was camped at Jurf, just outside Medina, when the Prophet's illness worsened sharply. The army could not depart while the Prophet was dying. The delay continued through the political crisis of the Prophet's death and the question of succession. During this period, Usamah himself returned to Medina briefly.

## Abu Bakr's Insistence

When Abu Bakr became Caliph, the internal crisis was acute. The Riddah Wars were beginning — tribes across Arabia were renouncing Islam or refusing to pay zakah. Many advisors, including Umar ibn al-Khattab, urged Abu Bakr to delay or recall Usamah's army, arguing that Medina itself was vulnerable and that sending so large a force away would be dangerous.

Abu Bakr refused categorically. He said:

*"By Him in Whose Hand is my soul, I would not undo a knot that the Messenger of Allah had tied, even if birds and beasts were to carry off the flesh of the people of Medina and wolves were to prowl around it."*

He added: "Even if I am left alone, I will dispatch Usamah's army as the Prophet ordered."

Abu Bakr then walked alongside Usamah's horse personally to see the army off, and Usamah offered to let the Caliph ride while he walked. Abu Bakr replied: "By Allah, you shall ride and I shall walk, for I do not think it too much to give every step I take in the path of Allah."

## The Campaign and Return

Usamah's army carried out its mission successfully, raiding the target region and returning to Medina after approximately 40 days. The successful campaign had a stabilizing effect: it demonstrated that the Muslim state was strong enough to project military force beyond Arabia even during internal turmoil, and it helped deter some tribes from rebellion.

## Significance

The episode illustrates several principles that Abu Bakr's caliphate established:

**The inviolability of the Prophet's orders.** Abu Bakr treated the Prophet's commands as binding, even after his death, even when circumstances appeared to argue against following them.

**The legitimacy of young commanders.** The appointment of Usamah over older companions established a precedent that military competence and the Prophet's designation mattered more than age or seniority.

**Steadiness under pressure.** The decision to dispatch the army while Medina was threatened internally is one of the clearest examples of Abu Bakr's strategic nerve. The short-term risk he accepted prevented the longer-term danger of appearing weak.

## Sources

The account of Usamah's army is preserved in Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, the Muwatta of Imam Malik, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
