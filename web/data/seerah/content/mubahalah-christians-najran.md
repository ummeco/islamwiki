# The Mubahalah with the Christians of Najran

In 10 AH (631-632 CE), a delegation of Christian scholars and leaders from Najran — a large Christian community in southern Arabia — came to Medina to engage the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) in theological dialogue about the nature of Jesus (Isa, peace be upon him) and the truth of the prophetic message. The encounter culminated in a proposal for mubahalah — a formal invocation of divine judgment through mutual cursing — that the Najranis ultimately declined to accept.

## The Delegation

Najran was home to a significant Christian population, organized along tribal lines and including both scholars (learned in scripture) and political leaders. Their delegation to Medina numbered approximately sixty riders, including their bishop, several scholars, and tribal chiefs. They came to investigate the claims of the Prophet and to determine the community's relationship to the new religion spreading across Arabia.

The Prophet received them with courtesy and allowed them to pray according to their own rites in the mosque — an act of religious accommodation notable in the context of a newly established monotheistic state. This precedent was later referenced by Islamic jurists in discussions of the status of non-Muslims in Muslim-majority territories.

## The Theological Dispute

The central dispute concerned the nature of Jesus. The Quran's position is that Jesus was a prophet and servant of Allah, born miraculously of a virgin (Maryam), without a father, but not divine — neither God nor the Son of God in the theological sense. The Najrani Christians held the Trinitarian position: that Jesus was divine, the Son of God, part of the divine nature.

The Quran was explicit about this dispute: "Indeed, the example of Jesus to Allah is like that of Adam. He created him from dust, then He said to him, 'Be,' and he was." (Quran 3:59) — this verse was revealed in the context of the Najran delegation. The comparison to Adam, who was also created without a father, was the Quranic rebuttal to the claim that Jesus's miraculous birth implied divine nature.

The dialogue extended over several days and touched on Quranic passages about Jesus's birth, miracles, and prophetic status.

## The Mubahalah Proposal

When the theological discussion reached an impasse, Allah revealed the mubahalah verse:

*"Then whoever argues with you about it after [this] knowledge has come to you — say, 'Come, let us call our sons and your sons, our women and your women, ourselves and yourselves, then supplicate earnestly [together] and invoke the curse of Allah upon the liars [among us].'"* (Quran 3:61)

The mubahalah (from Arabic: to invoke curses upon oneself if one is lying) was a recognized form of solemn oath in Arabian tradition — a way of placing the question before divine judgment. The Prophet accepted the Quran's direction and prepared to proceed.

## The Prophet's Preparations

According to the accounts preserved in Sahih Muslim and the seerah literature, the Prophet came out the following morning wrapped in a black cloak, holding the hand of al-Hasan ibn Ali (his grandson), carrying al-Husayn ibn Ali in his arms, with Fatimah al-Zahra (his daughter) walking behind him, and Ali ibn Abi Talib following. He said: "O Allah, these are my family (ahl)."

This gathering is of immense significance in Islamic tradition, particularly for Shia Muslims, who see it as the Quranic identification of the Ahl al-Bayt. The verse of purification (33:33) and the mubahalah are both referenced in Shia theology as definitive identifications of who the Prophet's family is.

## The Najrani Decision

The Najrani leaders consulted among themselves. Their bishop reportedly said: "By God, you know that he is a Prophet, and that whoever curses a Prophet is destroyed, along with his family." The delegation declined the mubahalah.

They chose instead to negotiate a treaty (dhimma) arrangement with the Prophet, agreeing to pay the jizyah (a tax on non-Muslim subjects of the Muslim state) in exchange for protection of their persons, property, churches, and religious practices. The Prophet accepted.

## The Treaty

The treaty with the Christians of Najran is one of the earliest documented examples of an Islamic dhimma treaty and has been extensively analyzed by Islamic jurists. It guaranteed:
- Protection of life and property
- Freedom to practice their religion
- Protection of their churches
- No imposition of conversion

The Najranis were required to provide a contribution of cattle, clothing, and financial support during times of military need. The treaty was one of the most generous early examples of Islamic governance toward religious minorities.

## Significance

The mubahalah episode demonstrates several principles that have been important in Islamic thought:

**The Quranic approach to interfaith dialogue.** The Quran engaged Christian theology directly, on its own terms, rather than dismissing it. The mubahalah itself represents a serious theological challenge: bring your families, invoke the divine, and let Allah judge between us.

**The Islamic position on Jesus.** The Quran's identification of Jesus as a prophet and servant of Allah, not divine, is definitively restated in the context of this encounter. The verses of Surah Al-Imran (3:59-61) are among the clearest Quranic statements on the subject.

**Interfaith coexistence in the Islamic state.** The Najran treaty became a model for how Muslim states should treat non-Muslim minorities: protection, autonomy, and legal status in exchange for a financial contribution and acknowledgment of Muslim political authority.

## Sources

The Najran delegation and mubahalah are recorded in Sahih Muslim (Kitab al-Fada'il), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and the tafsir literature for Surah Al-Imran verses 59-61.
