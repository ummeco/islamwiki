# Assassination of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf

Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf was a leader of Banu Nadir — a Jewish tribe of Medina — and a poet who became one of the most active opponents of the Prophet and the Muslim community in the early Medinan period. His assassination in 3 AH, ordered by the Prophet, has been a subject of sustained discussion in Islamic and Western scholarship on the ethics of the early Muslim state's treatment of political opponents.

## Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf

Ka'b was the son of a man from the Arab tribe of Tayy and a woman of Banu Nadir. Through his mother's lineage he was a member of Banu Nadir and held a position of leadership and cultural prominence. He was also a poet — and in Arabian tribal culture, the poet was a warrior of a different kind. Satirical or mourning poetry could shame a tribe, galvanize opposition, or celebrate enemies in ways that carried real political weight.

## The Escalation

When the Battle of Badr (2 AH) ended in a decisive Muslim victory, Ka'b was deeply disturbed. The Quraysh dead included men of standing, and he responded with elegies and satirical poetry that publicly mourned them and excoriated the Muslims. He then traveled to Mecca and composed poetry inciting Quraysh to take vengeance for the Badr dead.

The Islamic sources record that he also composed sexually provocative poetry about Muslim women — a form of attack in Arabian culture known as nasibi — which was understood as a direct assault on the honor of Muslim families.

Upon his return to Medina, he continued his poetic campaign, using his cultural position to undermine Muslim morale and encourage opposition. The Prophet reportedly asked his companions: "Who will deal with Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf, for he has harmed Allah and His Messenger?"

Muhammad ibn Maslamah volunteered, along with Abu Naila (Ka'b's foster brother), Abbad ibn Bishr, al-Harith ibn Aws, and Abu Abs ibn Jabr.

## The Operation

The operation involved deception: the men approached Ka'b under the pretext of personal business dealings, gaining access to him. They had been authorized by the Prophet to say what was necessary to gain access. Over a period of meetings — building Ka'b's trust — they eventually lured him out of his fortress at night, and he was killed.

## Scholarly Discussion

The assassination of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf has been the subject of extensive discussion in Islamic jurisprudence and in Western scholarship:

**The Islamic position:** Classical Islamic scholars hold that Ka'b had violated the constitutional arrangements of Medina (the Sahifat al-Madinah — the Constitution of Medina), which had established a political community with obligations. His hostile poetry, his travel to Mecca to incite warfare against the Muslims, and his role in the political and cultural opposition to the Muslim community constituted acts of hostility that moved him from the category of a protected non-Muslim resident (dhimmi) to an active combatant in the political sense. The Prophet ordered his removal as a political-military measure.

**The deception aspect:** Islamic scholars have noted that the use of deception to gain access is permitted in warfare. The men were authorized to misrepresent their intentions in the context of an operation against an active enemy of the community. This is distinguished from ordinary lying, which is prohibited.

## Legal Precedent

The case of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf is cited in Islamic jurisprudence in discussions of:
- The limits of the protection afforded to non-Muslim residents of a Muslim-governed community
- The circumstances under which leaders of actively hostile groups may be targeted
- The permissibility of tactical deception in military operations

It is a case that requires care in presentation: it occurred in a specific political context (the early Muslim community's existential conflict with Mecca and its allies), and the Islamic scholarly tradition has analyzed it carefully within that context rather than as a general rule.

## Sources

The account of Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi and Kitab al-Jihad), Sahih Muslim, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
