# The Treaty with Banu Qaynuqa

Banu Qaynuqa were one of the three major Jewish tribes of Medina at the time of the Hijrah. Along with Banu Nadir and Banu Qurayza, they formed a significant part of Medina's population and controlled considerable economic power, particularly through their role as goldsmiths, blacksmiths, and traders in the city's central marketplace.

## Background: Who Were Banu Qaynuqa?

Banu Qaynuqa occupied a quarter of Medina adjacent to the central market and were renowned for their craftsmanship. They were the most militarily bold of the three Jewish tribes and had the closest social ties with the Khazraj tribe among the Ansar — a relationship that would later become politically significant.

They were, by the pre-Islamic tribal standards of Arabia, allies of the Khazraj, and this alliance created a web of mutual obligations that complicated the post-Hijrah political settlement of Medina.

## The Constitutional Framework

When the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) arrived in Medina and established what scholars have called the Constitution of Medina (Sahifat al-Madinah), Banu Qaynuqa were included — along with Banu Nadir, Banu Qurayza, and several smaller Jewish clans — within the broader political community centered on the Prophet's authority.

The key provisions that applied to Banu Qaynuqa under the constitutional framework included:

**Mutual security.** The document established that all signatory groups — Muslim and Jewish — formed a single community (ummah) for purposes of defense against external attack. The Jews would defend alongside the Muslims if Medina were attacked, and the Muslims would reciprocate.

**Internal legal autonomy.** Banu Qaynuqa retained their own religious law (din) and their own legal structure for internal matters. The constitution explicitly stated: "The Jews have their religion and the Muslims have their religion." This principle of legal pluralism — each community governing its internal affairs by its own law — was a foundational feature of the Medinan settlement.

**The Prophet as final arbiter.** In disputes between communities — between Jews and Muslims, or matters affecting the collective — the Prophet was designated as the final authority. This was the critical provision that established Medinan sovereignty: not by imposing a single legal code, but by concentrating dispute resolution authority in a single recognized figure.

**Prohibition of separate alliances with Quraysh.** No signatory group was permitted to make a unilateral alliance with Quraysh or their confederates against the Medinan community. This provision was designed to prevent the fragmentation of Medina's collective defense and to ensure that Quraysh could not pick off allied groups one by one.

**Financial obligations.** In cases of warfare involving Muslim parties, the Jewish tribes were not obligated to contribute to the war effort against their traditional enemies — but the reciprocal obligations of mutual defense still applied for defense of Medina itself.

## The Significance of the Treaty

The treaty with Banu Qaynuqa was significant for several reasons that went beyond its immediate political utility.

**It established the dhimma principle in nascent form.** The constitutional recognition of Jewish legal autonomy in exchange for political loyalty to the Medinan community prefigures the later, more formally developed dhimma (protected status) institution of Islamic law. The Medinan constitution did not yet use the term dhimma, but the underlying arrangement — non-Muslim communities retaining their religious and legal identity within a Muslim-governed polity — was the same.

**It tested the limits of the constitutional order.** The framework depended on all parties maintaining their treaty obligations. When Banu Qaynuqa eventually violated the agreement — by provoking a violent incident in the marketplace (reported as involving the harassment of a Muslim woman, which led to a killing) — the constitutional framework was activated: the Prophet besieged them, and after their surrender, Abdullah ibn Ubayy's intervention led to their expulsion rather than execution. The episode clarified that the constitutional protections were conditional on treaty compliance.

**It demonstrated the Prophet's preference for negotiated settlement.** The Prophet's approach to Medina's complex multi-community environment was consistently to formalize relationships, establish clear mutual obligations, and hold signatories to those obligations rather than pursuing preemptive suppression. The constitutional settlement with Banu Qaynuqa reflected a political philosophy that sought maximum inclusion within a framework of enforceable commitments.

## Sources

The treaty provisions are reconstructed primarily from Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, which preserves the text of the Medinan constitutional document. Scholarly analysis of the document — including debate over whether it was a single document or multiple agreements — is extensive; the most thorough classical treatment is in al-Tabari's *Tarikh*. Modern scholarship includes W. Montgomery Watt's analysis in *Muhammad at Medina* and Uri Rubin's work on the document's authenticity.
