# Timbuktu: Center of Islamic Learning in West Africa

## A City Born at the Intersection of Desert and River

Timbuktu stands at one of the most strategically significant geographic points in West Africa: the northernmost bend of the Niger River, where the great waterway passes closest to the Sahara Desert. This location made it, from its earliest history, the natural meeting point between the trans-Saharan trade routes running north to the Maghreb and the river-based commerce of the West African interior. Goods moved through Timbuktu in both directions — gold and kola nuts moving north; salt, copper, and cloth moving south. The wealth this commerce generated, accumulated over generations, was what made Timbuktu's flowering as a center of Islamic scholarship possible.

The city's name appears in various forms in Arabic sources — Tanbuktu, Tunbuktu — and its foundation is traditionally dated to the 6th century AH (12th century CE), when Tuareg nomads established a seasonal camp that became a permanent settlement. The founding narrative, preserved in the local chronicle Tarikh al-Sudan, attributes the name to a Tuareg woman named Buktu who was left in charge of the camp while the men went to graze their herds. By the time the Malian empire incorporated the city in the 7th century AH (13th century CE), it had already grown into a significant trading center.

## The Malian Period: First Flowering

Timbuktu came to wide Islamic attention through Mansa Musa I of Mali, whose extraordinary hajj of 724 AH (1324 CE) brought him and his immense entourage — tens of thousands of people, hundreds of camels loaded with gold — through Cairo and across Arabia to Mecca, astonishing the Islamic world with the wealth of West Africa. On his return journey, Mansa Musa brought with him the Andalusian architect Ibrahim al-Sahili (Abu Ishaq al-Sahili), who built the first proper mosque structure in Timbuktu and began the architectural tradition that would define the city.

Mansa Musa's patronage transformed Timbuktu from a prosperous trading city into a center with genuine scholarly ambition. He built the Djinguereber Mosque (Masjid Jingara Ber) — still standing today, and still in use — which became the first institutional anchor for Islamic learning in the city. He subsidized scholars and encouraged the Malian court's engagement with Islamic intellectual life. Al-Sahili, who settled in Timbuktu and died there, became the first in a succession of scholars from the broader Islamic world who chose to make the city their home.

The mosque Ibn Battuta visited during his West African journey of around 754 AH (1353 CE) was already an established institution. He noted in his Rihla that Timbuktu's inhabitants were Muslim who observed the prayers, that the sultan of Mali (Mansa Suleiman at the time of his visit) supported scholars, and that the country was generally safe for travelers. He was less enthusiastic about some local customs he found inconsistent with Islamic standards, but his account establishes Timbuktu as an Islamic city of substance in the mid-8th century AH.

## The Songhai Ascendancy and the Golden Age

The golden age of Timbuktu is associated with the Songhai Empire, which displaced Malian hegemony in West Africa during the 9th century AH (15th century CE). Under the Sunni (Sonni) dynasty and then the Askiya dynasty, Timbuktu reached the apex of its wealth and scholarship.

Askiya Muhammad I (also known as Askia the Great), who came to power in 898 AH (1493 CE) and ruled until 935 AH (1528 CE), was the most significant patron of Timbuktu's scholarly life. He had himself a serious Muslim who performed his obligation of hajj in 902 AH (1497 CE), meeting in Cairo with the great Egyptian scholar Jalal al-Din al-Suyuti (d. 911 AH), who issued him a fatwa endorsing his authority and appointed him "Caliph of the Blacks" — a recognition of both his political power and his religious legitimacy. Askiya Muhammad returned from hajj with renewed commitment to supporting Islamic scholarship in his empire.

Under his patronage, three institutions in particular defined Timbuktu's scholarly identity.

The Djinguereber Mosque had been in existence since Mansa Musa's time, but its associated teaching activities expanded greatly under Askiya rule. Scholars gave lessons in the mosque's courtyard and in attached halls, following the traditional Islamic pattern of halqas — circles of students around a teacher.

The Sankore Mosque, originally built as a neighborhood mosque in the Sankore quarter, became under the Askiya period the most intellectually distinguished of Timbuktu's educational institutions. Unlike a formal madrasa with a fixed curriculum and paid faculty on a state salary, Sankore's "university" operated through individual scholar-teachers who attracted students to their circles. At its height, around 25,000 students studied in Timbuktu — a remarkable figure for any premodern city — though scholars debate whether all of these were in formal study or included the broader population of literate Muslims engaged in part-time learning.

The Sidi Yahia Mosque, built in the early 9th century AH (15th century CE) and associated with the scholar and saint Sidi Yahia al-Tadilsi, completed the triad of major mosques that gave Timbuktu its architectural and scholarly character.

## The Curriculum and the Books

What was taught in Timbuktu's mosques and scholarly circles covered the full range of classical Islamic sciences. Ibn Battuta had noted that the qadi (judge) of Mali was a learned man; by the Askiya period, Timbuktu had multiple scholars capable of teaching at the highest levels of each discipline.

Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) was central, following the Maliki madhab that predominated across North and West Africa. Timbuktu's scholars studied and taught al-Mudawwana (the foundational Maliki legal text compiled by Sahnun in the 3rd century AH), Ibn Rushd al-Jadd's Al-Bayyan, Khalil ibn Ishaq's Mukhtasar — the standard Maliki legal manual used from Morocco to West Africa — and the major commentaries.

Quranic sciences, hadith, theology ('aqeedah following the Ash'ari tradition predominant in Maliki West Africa), logic, rhetoric, history, and mathematics were all taught. The library collections of Timbuktu's scholarly families contained not only Islamic religious texts but also mathematics, astronomy, medicine, and philosophy in the tradition of the classical Islamic sciences that integrated these fields.

The manuscripts that survive — the famous "Timbuktu manuscripts," estimated variously between 300,000 and 700,000 documents preserved in private family collections — testify to the scope of this culture. The number remains debated: comprehensive surveys have not been completed, and the manuscripts are distributed across many private collections whose owners have not always permitted full access. But even conservative estimates place the collection among the largest repositories of pre-modern Islamic manuscripts in the world. The texts range from complete books to fragments, from original compositions to student notes, covering every discipline of classical Islamic learning as well as documents of local history, administration, and commerce.

## Ahmad Baba al-Timbukti: The Greatest Scholar of Timbuktu's Tradition

The scholar who most clearly represents the intellectual tradition of Timbuktu at its highest is Ahmad Baba ibn Ahmad ibn Umar al-Sudani al-Timbukti, born in Timbuktu in 963 AH (1556 CE) and died there in 1036 AH (1627 CE). He was the student of scholars who had themselves studied in the great tradition of Timbuktu's scholarship across multiple generations, and his productive period — before the Moroccan invasion — was the final flowering of that tradition.

Ahmad Baba was a Maliki scholar of the highest rank, also educated in hadith, Quranic commentary, logic, and the history of Islamic scholarship. He was appointed to teach at Sankore and became the most respected scholar in Timbuktu before the invasion of 1591. He wrote on Maliki jurisprudence, on hadith, and — most significantly — composed Nayl al-Ibtihaj bi-Tatriz al-Dibaj, a biographical dictionary of Maliki scholars that supplements and updates Ibn Farhun's earlier Al-Dibaj al-Mudhahhab. This work is the most important source for West African Islamic scholarly genealogies and continues to be used by historians of Islam in West Africa today.

Ahmad Baba also composed a response to a query from northern Mali about the permissibility of enslaving certain West African peoples — a fatwa that argued against the enslavement of Muslims from named West African regions regardless of their ethnicity, distinguishing between those of confirmed Muslim identity (who could not be enslaved) and non-Muslims (for whom the standard rules applied). This fatwa is a significant document in the history of Islamic scholarship on slavery and has been extensively discussed in modern scholarship.

## The Moroccan Invasion of 1591

The Sa'dian dynasty of Morocco, under Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur al-Dhahabi, had long coveted the gold and salt wealth of the West African Sahel. In 999 AH (1591 CE), he sent an army of approximately 4,000 men under the command of Judar Pasha — a Spanish-born convert to Islam who had risen in Moroccan service — across the Sahara in a crossing that even contemporaries regarded as extraordinary. The army brought firearms; the Songhai Empire, despite its size, had no comparable weaponry.

The Battle of Tondibi (18 Jumada al-Akhira 999 AH / March 12, 1591 CE) was decided almost as quickly as the First Battle of Panipat had been decided sixty-five years earlier in India. The Songhai army, vastly larger but armed with traditional weapons, was broken by firearms. The Songhai Askiya, Ishaq II, fled. Timbuktu fell to Moroccan forces shortly afterward.

The Moroccan soldiers entered Timbuktu's scholarly district and treated the scholars as a political threat. Ahmad Baba had refused to acknowledge Moroccan authority. In 1001 AH (1593 CE), Ahmad Baba and several other leading scholars were arrested, their libraries confiscated, and they were deported to Marrakesh. Ahmad Baba was kept under arrest in Marrakesh for fourteen years, forbidden to teach publicly at first, then later permitted restricted teaching.

In Marrakesh, Ahmad Baba attracted students despite his circumstances. He completed Nayl al-Ibtihaj during this period. He wrote letters demanding the return of his confiscated books — one famous letter lists over 1,600 volumes that were taken from him, a window into the scale of Timbuktu's private scholarly libraries. He was never adequately compensated.

He returned to Timbuktu in 1016 AH (1607 CE) after Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur's death and spent the last two decades of his life there, teaching and writing. He died in Timbuktu in 1036 AH (1627 CE) and is buried in a tomb that remains a site of reverence in the city today. A research institute in Timbuktu was named after him in the 20th century — a recognition that he represents the pinnacle of the tradition the city produced.

## The Aftermath: Pashalik and Decline

The Sa'dian conquest did not establish a stable successor to the Songhai Empire. The Moroccan forces became the Arma — a term for the locally-born descendants of the Moroccan soldiers who had mixed with the local population — a ruling caste that controlled Timbuktu under nominal Moroccan suzerainty as the "Pashalik of Timbuktu." The Pashalik was increasingly autonomous as Morocco's own Sa'dian dynasty collapsed into civil war after Ahmad al-Mansur's death.

The Arma ruled Timbuktu for the next two centuries but never replicated the prosperity of the Askiya period. The trans-Saharan trade routes, which had sustained Timbuktu's wealth, shifted as Atlantic trade routes developed and as West African political instability made overland commerce more dangerous. The scholarly class survived — families continued to teach, copy manuscripts, and produce scholarship — but at a diminished level. The institutional patronage of the Askiya period was gone.

The 19th century brought further disruption: the Fulani jihad movements, the Tuareg revival, and ultimately French colonial conquest in 1893 CE. The French established administrative control over Timbuktu and the surrounding region, integrating it into French West Africa.

## The Manuscripts and Their Survival

The most tangible legacy of Timbuktu's golden age is the manuscripts. Through all the political disruptions — the Moroccan invasion, the Arma period, the 19th-century upheavals, and colonial rule — Timbuktu's scholarly families preserved their book collections. Books were hidden during times of danger, buried, carried by camel to distant relatives, wrapped in animal skins, stored in sealed rooms. The instinct to preserve was apparently stronger than the forces of destruction.

The modern effort to catalogue and preserve the Timbuktu manuscripts began in earnest in the late 20th century. The government of Mali, working with international organizations and private donors, established the Ahmed Baba Institute (now IHERIAB — Institut des Hautes Etudes et de Recherches Islamiques Ahmed Baba) to digitize and catalogue the collections. The crisis came in 2012 CE when jihadist groups (Ansar Dine and MUJAO) captured Timbuktu and threatened to destroy the manuscripts. Timbuktu scholars and librarians undertook a remarkable operation, smuggling the most important manuscripts out of the city in grain sacks and other containers, distributing them to Bamako and other safe locations. When the jihadists burned the Ahmed Baba Institute before fleeing the city in January 2013, they found largely empty shelves. The most significant portion of the manuscripts had been saved.

Their significance — as testimony to the depth and breadth of Islamic scholarly civilization in West Africa, to the global reach of the classical Islamic intellectual tradition, and to the capacity of one city to sustain serious scholarship over centuries — remains to be fully appreciated. The work of cataloguing and studying them continues.
