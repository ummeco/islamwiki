# From Rejection to Resolve: The Aftermath of Ta'if

The journey to Ta'if and its aftermath stand as perhaps the most painful episode in the entire Meccan period — and one of the most theologically revealing. Within a span of months, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ lost the two people who had been most essential to his human support: Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), his wife of twenty-five years, and Abu Talib, his uncle and protector. Bereft of both personal and political shelter, he traveled to Ta'if to seek a new base of support. What he received instead was rejection, mockery, and a rain of stones that left him bleeding. What followed that rejection reveals the quality of the Prophet's ﷺ character, the nature of his mission, and the divine management of a plan that would lead, within a few years, to the establishment of an Islamic state in Medina.

## The Year of Grief

The Islamic tradition designates the tenth year before the Hijra as "Aam al-Huzn" — the Year of Grief. The name alone tells the story. Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her) died in this year, after approximately fifteen years of marriage, during which she had been the Prophet's ﷺ first believer, his financial supporter, his emotional anchor, and the mother of his surviving children. The Prophet ﷺ never ceased to honor her memory; years later in Medina, when Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) once expressed slight jealousy of her memory, the Prophet ﷺ said: "She believed in me when others disbelieved. She supported me with her wealth when others withheld. And Allah blessed me with children through her."

Abu Talib died in the same year, within months of Khadijah. Whatever his personal theological position, his protection had been a practical and political reality that prevented the Quraysh from directly harming the Prophet ﷺ for over a decade. With his death, that protection expired. The Prophet ﷺ needed a new protector to re-enter Mecca's public life.

It was in this context — doubly bereaved, politically exposed — that the decision to travel to Ta'if was made.

## The Journey to Ta'if and the Thaqif's Response

Ta'if was approximately sixty miles from Mecca, a prosperous city known for its cooler climate, vineyards, and the powerful Thaqif tribe. The Thaqif were significant enough to be potential allies; if they could be brought to support the Islamic mission, it would dramatically change the political landscape.

The Prophet ﷺ traveled to Ta'if, some accounts say with Zayd ibn Haritha (may Allah be pleased with him) as his companion. He met with the three leading men of the Thaqif tribe — the sons of Amr ibn Umayr: Abd Yalayl, Masud, and Habib. He presented Islam to them and asked for their support and protection.

Their responses were contemptuous. The first said: "I would tear down the curtains of the Ka'bah if Allah had truly sent you as a prophet." The second said: "Couldn't Allah find anyone better than you to send?" The third said: "By Allah, I will never speak to you. If you are truly a prophet, you are too great for me to argue with; and if you are lying about Allah, you are too low for me to speak to."

The Prophet ﷺ asked them, before leaving, to at least keep his visit secret — if they would not help, he asked them not to add to his difficulties by publicizing his rejection. They refused even this. They set their servants and street boys on him, creating two rows through which the Prophet ﷺ had to walk while they pelted him with stones and jeered. The stoning continued until his sandals were soaked in blood. Zayd ibn Haritha tried to shield him, receiving wounds on his head.

## The Garden of Utbah and Shaybah: The Prayer of Ta'if

The Prophet ﷺ finally found refuge at the wall of a garden belonging to Utbah ibn Rabi'a and his brother Shaybah — ironic names to find in this moment of the seerah, given that Utbah ibn Rabi'a was also the name of the Qurayshi elder who had offered the Prophet ﷺ kingship. Whether it is the same man or a different Utbah is debated in the sources.

Bleeding and exhausted, the Prophet ﷺ sat against the garden wall and made a du'a that has been preserved as one of the most moving supplications in the Islamic tradition. The full Arabic text is reported in Ibn Hisham and other sources:

"O Allah, to You I complain of my weakness, my scarcity of resources, and the humiliation I have been subjected to by people. O Most Merciful of those who show mercy — You are the Lord of the weak, and You are my Lord. To whom will You leave me? To a distant stranger who will mistreat me? Or to an enemy to whom You have given power over me? If You are not angry with me, I do not care — but Your well-being is more spacious for me. I seek refuge in the light of Your Face by which darkness is illuminated and the affairs of this world and the next are set right — lest Your anger descend upon me or Your displeasure befall me. To You belongs the right to blame until You are pleased. There is no power and no strength except through You."

The scholars of Islamic spirituality — Ibn al-Qayyim, al-Ghazali, and others — have analyzed this du'a extensively. Several elements are notable. The Prophet ﷺ began by acknowledging his own weakness and limitation — not out of despair but out of true 'ubudiyya (servitude). He then oriented himself to Allah's mercy: "You are the Lord of the weak." He expressed a hierarchy of concerns: not "don't let me suffer" but "if You are not angry with me, I don't care" — meaning the divine pleasure was the primary concern, his worldly suffering secondary. He sought refuge in the divine light itself — one of the most intimate formulations of tawakkul (reliance on Allah) in the entire prophetic legacy.

## The Angel of Mountains

While the Prophet ﷺ was in the garden, Jibril (peace be upon him) appeared along with another angel — described in the narration as "the angel of the mountains." Jibril said: "Allah has heard what your people have said to you and how they have responded to you. He has sent you the angel of the mountains to do with them what you wish."

The angel of mountains greeted the Prophet ﷺ and offered: "O Muhammad, command me as you wish. If you want, I will cause the two mountains [surrounding Ta'if] to crush these people between them."

The Prophet's ﷺ response was immediate and unambiguous: "No. Rather, I hope that Allah will bring forth from their loins people who worship Allah alone and associate nothing with Him."

This response has been cited across Islamic scholarship as one of the defining statements about the Prophet's ﷺ character and the nature of his mission. He had just been physically attacked, bleeding, rejected by people he had come to with the best of intentions. The opportunity to end the threat entirely was placed before him. He declined it — not because he was weak or had no power, but because he saw through the temporal offense to the eternal opportunity. The descendants of those who stoned him might be the believers of the next generation.

His prophecy was accurate. The Thaqif tribe did eventually embrace Islam — during the Year of Delegations (9 AH), just two years before the Prophet's death, they sent a delegation to Medina and became Muslim. The city of Ta'if, which had expelled him, became a Muslim city.

## Addas the Christian Servant

In the garden at Ta'if, a servant of Utbah and Shaybah brought the Prophet ﷺ a plate of grapes. The servant's name was Addas, and he was a Christian from Nineveh in Iraq. As he presented the grapes, the Prophet ﷺ said: "Bismillah" — "In the name of Allah."

Addas, surprised, asked: "This is something no one in this land says. What is your religion?" The Prophet ﷺ replied: "Which land are you from?" When Addas said Nineveh, the Prophet ﷺ said: "From the city of the righteous man Yunus [Jonah] ibn Matta?" Addas, astonished at the knowledge of his city and the prophet associated with it, asked how the Prophet ﷺ knew of Yunus. The Prophet said: "He was my brother — a prophet, and I am a prophet."

Addas knelt before the Prophet ﷺ and began kissing his feet. His masters, watching from afar, were concerned that their servant was being influenced. When Addas returned, they asked what had happened. He told them: "There is no one on earth better than this man. He told me something only a prophet would know." His masters warned him against abandoning Christianity, but the narrations suggest Addas's conviction was shaken irreversibly.

This brief encounter is theologically significant: even in the lowest moment of the Ta'if journey, the Prophet ﷺ made a convert. His ability to reach the sincere — even a servant from Nineveh in a Meccan garden — remained intact regardless of the surrounding rejection.

## The Problem of Re-Entry to Mecca

The Hijra to Medina was still years in the future, and the Prophet ﷺ needed to return to Mecca. But without tribal protection — without a chief willing to publicly declare him under his protection — he could not simply walk back into Mecca without risking immediate attack. Abu Talib was dead, and his role as protector had died with him.

The Prophet ﷺ sent messengers to several tribal chiefs asking for jiwar — the pre-Islamic custom of tribal protection, in which a chief's honor was pledged to protect the person under his care. This was not a religious act but a social one; any chief could extend it regardless of their religious position.

He was turned down by Akhnas ibn Shuraiq, who said he was an ally of the Quraysh and could not give protection against them. He was turned down by Suhayel ibn Amr, who offered to help in some unspecified limited way but not full protection. He was turned down by al-Mut'im ibn Adi first — but in the end Mut'im ibn Adi accepted.

Mut'im ibn Adi came to the Ka'bah the next morning with his sons and nephews, all armed, and declared publicly that Muhammad ﷺ was under his protection. He told the Prophet ﷺ to perform tawaf, which the Prophet did while Mut'im's armed family stood guard. When Abu Jahl demanded to know whether Mut'im was "following" Muhammad ﷺ or simply giving protection, Mut'im said he was giving protection — not following. The distinction mattered: as a protector rather than a follower, his protection was valid under tribal custom even though he personally rejected Islam.

## The Spiritual Lessons

The Ta'if episode and its aftermath contain a set of lessons that Islamic scholars have returned to in every generation.

The first is the relationship between worldly suffering and divine plan. The Ta'if journey looked like a failure by every external measure: he went seeking support, was rejected and attacked, and left bleeding and without an alliance. But the journey produced the Ta'if prayer, the encounter with the angel of mountains, the conversion of Addas, the arrangement of Mut'im's protection, and the passage through Nakhla where the jinn heard the Quran. What appeared as a series of defeats was a divinely orchestrated series of events leading toward an outcome the Prophet ﷺ could not yet see.

The second is the standard of prophetic mercy. When offered the destruction of Ta'if's population, the Prophet ﷺ declined in favor of the hope for their descendants' guidance. This is the prophetic mercy that the Quran describes: "We did not send you except as a mercy to all the worlds" (21:107). That mercy extended even to those who had just thrown stones at him.

The third is the power of the du'a made in absolute helplessness. The Ta'if prayer is considered by scholars one of the most profound examples of turning to Allah when all worldly means have been exhausted. The scholars note that such moments — when human agency has been stripped away — are among the moments of greatest nearness to Allah for the believers.
