# Death of Ibn al-Qayyim al-Jawziyyah

## The Scholar Who Shaped an Age

Shams al-Din Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr ibn Ayyub ibn Sa'd ibn Hurayz al-Zar'i al-Dimashqi, known to history as Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah, died in Damascus on the night of 13 Rajab 751 AH (September 23, 1350 CE). He was approximately sixty years old. His death came in the aftermath of the Black Death, which had devastated Syria and Egypt in 749-750 AH, in an era of demographic collapse and scholarly disruption that made his passing all the more significant for the tradition he represented.

The name "Ibn al-Qayyim al-Jawziyyah" is a patronymic of sorts: his father Abi Bakr was the qayyim — the overseer or administrator — of the Jawziyyah madrasa in Damascus, a school founded in the 7th century AH for the Hanbali tradition. Ibn Qayyim thus grew up in a scholarly household attached to one of Damascus's important educational institutions, an environment that shaped his early formation and gave him the rigorous Hanbali legal training that underpinned everything he later wrote.

## Formation and the Encounter with Ibn Taymiyyah

Ibn al-Qayyim was born in 691 AH (1291 CE) in Damascus. He studied with multiple teachers in the Hanbali tradition, mastering the established legal sciences alongside Arabic grammar, Quranic commentary, and hadith before the encounter that would define his intellectual life. Sometime in his youth he became the student of Ibn Taymiyyah — and not merely a student in the conventional sense of attending lectures, but the devoted companion and intellectual heir of the greatest Hanbali scholar of the era.

The relationship between Ibn al-Qayyim and Ibn Taymiyyah was one of the most significant in Islamic scholarly history. Ibn Taymiyyah was the generator of ideas — the scholar whose mind ranged across every discipline with intimidating breadth, who challenged the established positions of the schools where he found them insufficiently grounded in Quran and Sunnah, and who attracted both devoted followers and powerful enemies. Ibn al-Qayyim was the consolidator, the refiner, the scholar who took the core insights of his teacher's method and developed them into systematic works of extraordinary depth.

Al-Dhahabi, who knew both men, wrote that Ibn al-Qayyim was "devout, humble, fond of lengthy prayer, devoted to worship, a lover of knowledge" — qualities that complemented rather than duplicated Ibn Taymiyyah's more combative public persona. Where Ibn Taymiyyah was inclined to confront, Ibn al-Qayyim was inclined to elaborate. Where Ibn Taymiyyah wrote polemics, Ibn al-Qayyim wrote sustained intellectual syntheses. Together, they formed one of Islamic scholarship's most productive teacher-student partnerships.

## Imprisonment and Proximity in the Citadel

When Ibn Taymiyyah was imprisoned in the Citadel of Damascus in 726 AH — his final and fatal imprisonment — Ibn al-Qayyim did not abandon him. He too was imprisoned in the Citadel, suffering confinement alongside his teacher for an extended period. The biographical sources indicate that this imprisonment, far from interrupting his scholarship, deepened it: the sustained conversation between teacher and student under the intense conditions of confinement produced intellectual exchanges that shaped Ibn al-Qayyim's subsequent writing in ways that would not have been possible in ordinary circumstances.

When Ibn Taymiyyah died in the Citadel on 20 Dhul Qa'da 728 AH, Ibn al-Qayyim was eventually released. He emerged from the imprisonment as the primary surviving heir of the Taymiyyan tradition — the scholar best equipped to continue, develop, and transmit what his teacher had taught. The subsequent twenty-three years of his life (728-751 AH) were spent in exactly this enterprise: writing, teaching, and developing the intellectual legacy he had received.

## Zad al-Ma'ad: The Major Achievement

The most important work Ibn al-Qayyim produced after his teacher's death — and arguably the most important work associated with the Taymiyyan tradition as a whole — is Zad al-Ma'ad fi Hady Khayr al-'Ibad (Provisions for the Afterlife from the Guidance of the Best of Mankind). This is a comprehensive account of the Prophet's ﷺ life organized not as conventional biography but as a guide to living according to the Prophetic example in every domain of life.

Zad al-Ma'ad is organized around the key dimensions of the Prophet's ﷺ practice: his worship, his dealings, his military campaigns, his medical remedies, his rulings, his treatment of his family and Companions, his character and manners. For each domain, Ibn al-Qayyim traces the prophetic guidance from the authentic hadith literature, analyzes its jurisprudential implications in light of the four madhabs, and draws the practical lessons for the believer. The result is a work that is simultaneously Prophetic biography, jurisprudential analysis, and practical guide to Islamic living.

In five large volumes, Zad al-Ma'ad covers the full range of human experience with a scholarly depth that few Islamic works have matched. Its section on prophetic medicine (*al-tibb al-nabawi*) is one of the most thorough treatments in classical Islamic literature. Its analysis of the Prophet's ﷺ military campaigns illuminates the jurisprudential principles of jihad and treaty-making. Its treatment of the Prophet's ﷺ worship and private life conveys with extraordinary vividness the human reality of the man who was the last Messenger of Allah.

## I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in: The Methodology of Fatwa

I'lam al-Muwaqqi'in 'an Rabb al-'Alamin (Informing the Signatories on Behalf of the Lord of the Worlds) is Ibn al-Qayyim's major work on fatwa methodology and the theory of Islamic jurisprudence. The title refers to the mujtahids and muftis — those who "sign" (give formal rulings) on behalf of Allah — and the work examines the foundations, methods, and responsibilities of Islamic legal reasoning.

The work's central argument is that the purpose of Islamic law is the realization of human welfare (maslaha) in accordance with divine wisdom, and that any legal interpretation that produces hardship, harm, or obvious injustice requires re-examination. This does not mean that Ibn al-Qayyim advocated for legal flexibility at the expense of textual commitment; rather, he argued that the careful, comprehensive reading of the Quran, Sunnah, and the practice of the Companions will in every case reveal a ruling that is wise, just, and conducive to human flourishing. The apparent conflicts between Islamic law and human welfare arise from misunderstanding either the law or the welfare, not from genuine tension between divine guidance and human good.

This optimistic synthesis — the Quran and Sunnah contain within them all the wisdom needed for every human situation, properly understood — is one of Ibn al-Qayyim's most enduring contributions. It stands against both the rigid formalism that finds no flexibility in any circumstance and the liberal dissolution that subordinates textual rulings to individual judgment.

## Madarij al-Salikin: Spiritual Stations Grounded in Sunnah

Madarij al-Salikin bayn Manazil Iyyaka Na'budu wa-Iyyaka Nasta'in (Stations of Travelers between the Two Stations of "You alone we worship and You alone we seek help from") is Ibn al-Qayyim's most extensive treatment of Islamic spirituality. It is a commentary on the famous Sufi manual Manazil al-Sa'irin (Stations of the Travelers) by the Hanbali Sufi Khwaja Abdallah al-Ansari al-Harawi (d. 481 AH), covering the stations of the spiritual path from the beginning of repentance to the highest states of proximity to Allah.

The Madarij occupies a distinctive place in the history of Islamic spirituality precisely because of its method: it grounds every spiritual station in the Quran and authentic Sunnah, evaluating al-Harawi's formulations against scriptural evidence and correcting or clarifying where Ibn al-Qayyim considers them to have been imprecise. The result is a work that takes the Sufi tradition of spiritual psychology seriously — affirming its profound insights into the human soul, the nature of love and longing for Allah, the mechanics of spiritual progress — while insisting that every spiritual claim must be accountable to revelation.

This approach — neither dismissing the wisdom accumulated in the Sufi tradition nor accepting it uncritically — represents Ibn al-Qayyim's characteristic method of engaging with the Islamic intellectual heritage. He refused the two simplistic positions: blanket condemnation of all spiritual practice, or uncritical acceptance of everything in the name of tolerance. He applied scholarly scrutiny, and the Madarij is the most thorough product of that scrutiny applied to spiritual experience.

## Al-Wabil al-Sayyib: Rain of Blessings Through Dhikr

Al-Wabil al-Sayyib min al-Kalim al-Tayyib (The Heavy Rain of Blessings from the Pure Words) is Ibn al-Qayyim's treatment of dhikr (remembrance of Allah) — its virtues, its forms, its spiritual effects, and its relationship to the heart's health. The book draws on his comprehensive knowledge of the hadith literature to present the full range of prophetic remembrances and their benefits.

The work is an extended meditation on the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ: "There is a polish for everything that takes away rust, and the polish of the heart is the remembrance of Allah" (Bayhaqi). Ibn al-Qayyim develops this prophetic metaphor into a comprehensive spiritual psychology: the heart is naturally inclined toward Allah (*fitra*), but it accumulates rust through sin and heedlessness, and only the sustained practice of dhikr — properly performed, with presence of heart — restores it to its original clarity.

## His Death and the Twilight of the Classical Age

Ibn al-Qayyim died on the night of 13 Rajab 751 AH in Damascus. Ibn Kathir, his contemporary who lived another twenty-three years, wrote in his account of his death in Al-Bidayah wan-Nihayah: "He was learned in the Arabic language, hadith, jurisprudence, the principles of the law, and the interpretation of the Quran. He was the imam of his era in these sciences." His funeral prayers were performed at the Umayyad Mosque and at the Jami' Jarrah, and large crowds attended.

His death, coming so shortly after the Black Death and just over twenty years after his teacher Ibn Taymiyyah's death, marked the end of the formative period of what would later be called the "Taymiyyan tradition." The intellectual legacy he left — in hadith, jurisprudence, Quranic interpretation, and spiritual psychology — was vast. His books continued to be copied and transmitted across the Islamic world, though his influence varied by region: in Egypt and Syria, where the Ash'ari tradition was strongest, his works were sometimes controversial; in the Arabian Peninsula, where the Hanbali tradition maintained a stronger presence, they were central to scholarly education.

The influence of Ibn al-Qayyim's work on later Islamic reform movements — particularly the 18th-century movements associated with Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab and Shah Waliullah Dehlawi — was immense. Both reformers drew heavily on the Taymiyyan tradition as Ibn al-Qayyim had transmitted and developed it. The modern "Salafi" intellectual tradition owes more to Ibn al-Qayyim's elaboration of Ibn Taymiyyah's principles than to any other single classical source.
