# Islam in the Hausa States

The Islamization of the Hausa city-states of West Africa (in present-day northern Nigeria and southern Niger) was a gradual process spanning several centuries, beginning around the fourteenth century CE (eighth century AH) and deepening significantly through the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. The Hausa states became important centers of Islamic learning, commerce, and culture in the Sahel region, linking West Africa to the broader Islamic world through trade routes across the Sahara.

## The Hausa States

The Hausa were a major agricultural and commercial people of the central Sahel. Their city-states — including Kano, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir, Daura, Biram, and Rano — were known collectively as the Hausa Bakwai (the Seven Hausa States), though in practice more than seven existed. These cities were centers of trade, craft production, and political organization.

## The Entry of Islam

Islam arrived in the Hausa states primarily through the Saharan trade networks connecting the region to North Africa. Muslim traders, particularly Wangara and Dyula merchants from Mali, introduced Islam through commercial contacts. Tuareg nomads of the Sahara also played a role in transmission.

Kano and Katsina appear to have had Muslim rulers and significant Muslim populations by the early ninth century AH (mid-fifteenth century CE). The chronicles of Kano (*Kano Chronicle*), compiled in the nineteenth century but preserving older traditions, record Islamic influence beginning with the rule of Ali Yaji (r. 803-828 AH / 1349-1385 CE), who reportedly accepted Islam under the influence of Muslim clerics from Mali.

## Centers of Islamic Learning

**Katsina** became the most important center of Islamic scholarship in the region by the late sixteenth and seventeenth centuries CE (tenth and eleventh centuries AH). Muslim scholars from North Africa settled there, and Katsina ulema studied in North Africa and the Middle East, bringing back books and knowledge. The city's scholarly culture attracted students from across the region.

**Kano** was also an important center of commerce and Islamic practice. Its Friday mosque and madrasa system supported a learned class of scholars.

The Hausa states participated in the broader networks of Islamic scholarship: Hausa scholars corresponded with scholars in Timbuktu (in Mali), in the Maghreb, and in Egypt. Some made the Hajj and returned with direct knowledge of the Islamic heartlands.

## The Fulani Jihad

In 1218 AH (1804 CE), Usman dan Fodio (1754-1817 CE), a Fulani Islamic scholar, launched a major reform movement and jihad that transformed the Hausa states. Dan Fodio had long criticized the Hausa rulers for mixing Islamic governance with pre-Islamic practices, charging jizya inappropriately, enslaving Muslims, and failing to implement Sharia properly.

His movement rapidly overthrew the Hausa kings and established the Sokoto Caliphate — a confederation of emirates governing most of what is now northern Nigeria. The Sokoto Caliphate brought a more thoroughgoing implementation of Sharia and significantly deepened Islamic identity and practice in the region.

## Legacy

The Islamization of the Hausa states established one of the most durably Islamic regions in Africa. Northern Nigeria today has approximately 50 million Muslim inhabitants, and the Hausa-Fulani are among the largest Muslim ethnic groups in Africa. The scholarly tradition that developed in Katsina and Kano fed into the reform movements of the nineteenth century and continues in the major Islamic universities of northern Nigeria.

The Hausa language itself, written in the Arabic script (Ajami) from at least the seventeenth century CE, became a vehicle for Islamic learning and literature across the Sahel region.
