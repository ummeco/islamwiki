# Ibn Arabi: The Greatest Sheikh and the Most Contested Mystic

## Life of a Spiritual Prodigy

Muhyi al-Din Muhammad ibn Ali ibn Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Abd Allah al-Hatimi al-Ta'i — known to history simply as Ibn Arabi — was born in Murcia, al-Andalus (Islamic Spain) on 17 Ramadan 560 AH (July 28, 1165 CE). He died in Damascus on 22 Rabi al-Thani 638 AH (November 8, 1240 CE) at approximately 76 years of age and was buried in the Salihiyya district at the foot of Mount Qasiyun.

His family was of Arab origin — the Banu Tayy tribe — and his father reportedly had a close relationship with Ibn Rushd (Averroes), whom the young Ibn Arabi encountered as a boy. By his own account, he was spiritually precocious from childhood, experiencing visions and spiritual encounters at an early age. He received a traditional Islamic education in al-Andalus, studying hadith, fiqh, and the Islamic sciences under the scholars of Seville. He also studied under several women teachers of hadith, which he documented and which reflects the Islamic tradition's historical respect for female scholarship.

He spent decades traveling: from al-Andalus to Morocco to Tunis, eastward to Egypt, then Arabia (where he performed hajj and lived in Mecca for extended periods), Iraq, Turkey, and finally settled in Damascus under the patronage of the Ayyubid ruler. The city of Damascus was his home for the last 17 years of his life.

## The "al-Shaykh al-Akbar": His Works and Their Scope

Ibn Arabi earned the title *al-Shaykh al-Akbar* (the Greatest Sheikh) from his followers — a designation that reveals the extraordinary esteem in which he was held by Sufi circles for centuries. The scope of his writing is genuinely staggering: he reportedly composed over 300 works, though many are short treatises.

His two major works define his legacy and his controversy:

**al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya** (The Meccan Openings/Revelations): An encyclopedic work of approximately 2,700 pages in its printed edition, divided into 560 chapters covering cosmology, Sufi spiritual stations, Islamic sciences, metaphysics, and esoteric interpretation. He began composing it during his time in Mecca, claiming the work came to him through spiritual unveiling (*kashf*) rather than ordinary rational composition. It is a vast and often labyrinthine text that rewards patient scholarly reading but also contains passages that have generated significant theological controversy.

**Fusus al-Hikam** (The Bezels of Wisdom / The Ringstones of Wisdom): Composed near the end of his life (627 AH / 1229 CE) in Damascus, this shorter but even more theologically dense work contains 27 chapters, each devoted to a Prophet and the particular divine wisdom (*hikma*) embodied in that Prophet's mission. It is in this work that the most theologically controversial of Ibn Arabi's formulations appear.

## The Doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud

Ibn Arabi did not himself use the term *Wahdat al-Wujud* (Unity of Being / Oneness of Existence) — this term was coined by later commentators to describe what they understood to be his essential metaphysical position. The doctrine as typically explained holds that there is ultimately only one Reality, one Being, which is Allah; all that appears to be created existence is in reality a manifestation or self-disclosure (*tajalli*) of this single Reality. The created universe does not have independent existence separate from the Divine Being — it is, in some sense, the self-expression of Allah.

This doctrine has been interpreted in multiple ways by subsequent scholars:

**The sympathetic reading**: Ibn Arabi's Wahdat al-Wujud is not pantheism (the doctrine that God is the universe) but rather a form of existential dependence — the recognition that created things have no existence of their own apart from Allah's sustaining power. This reading aligns it with the Islamic principle that *la hawla wa la quwwata illa billah* — there is no power or motion except through Allah. On this reading, Ibn Arabi is expressing in highly technical philosophical language what any Muslim would affirm: that Allah alone has necessary existence and all creation depends absolutely upon Him.

**The critical reading**: Ibn Arabi's formulations, particularly in the Fusus al-Hikam and parts of the Futuhat, go beyond this standard theological affirmation and collapse the distinction between Creator and creation in ways that cannot be squared with Islamic monotheism. The Quran is clear that Allah is distinct from His creation: *"He is the First and the Last and the Manifest and the Hidden, and He is All-Knower of everything"* (Surah al-Hadid, 57:3). The Athari theological tradition firmly maintains the absolute distinction between al-Khaliq (the Creator) and al-makhluq (the created). Any doctrine that blurs this line — regardless of the sophistication of its formulation — departs from the Quranic teaching.

## The Specific Controversy: Pharaoh and Redemption

Perhaps the most debated passage in the Fusus al-Hikam appears in the chapter on Moses. Ibn Arabi argues there that Pharaoh (Fir'awn) — who drowned in the Red Sea pursuing the Israelites — died in a state of belief because his drowning itself was an act of witnessing divine Reality. He connects this to Quran 10:90, where Pharaoh, as he was drowning, declared: "I believe that there is no god except He in whom the Children of Israel believe, and I am of those who submit [in Islam]."

The Quranic text itself immediately follows this declaration with the divine response: *"Now? And you had disobeyed [Him] before and were of the corrupters? So today We will save your body so that you may be to those who succeed you a sign"* (Surah Yunus, 10:91-92). Classical commentators from the Companions onward have understood this passage to mean that Pharaoh's last-moment declaration was rejected — it came too late (the rule against deathbed repentance when death is certain is established in Islamic law from Surah al-Nisa 4:18) and Allah preserved his body as a warning, not as a mercy.

Ibn Taymiyyah (may Allah have mercy on him) identified this passage in the Fusus as among the clearest examples of theological deviation in Ibn Arabi's work — a position that contradicts explicit Quranic text and the consensus of commentators.

## The Scholarly Debate: Orthodox Responses

The debate over Ibn Arabi in the Islamic scholarly tradition has been intense for nearly eight centuries and has not fully resolved. Several positions can be identified:

**Complete acceptance** (prevalent in later Ottoman scholarship, certain Ash'ari and Sufi circles): Ibn Arabi was a *wali* (saint) of the highest rank; his writings must be interpreted charitably; apparent theological problems arise from misreading or from ordinary minds failing to grasp the depth of his meaning. Scholars like Mulla Ali al-Qari (1014 AH / 1606 CE) initially engaged critically but later moderated his position.

**Qualified acceptance** (some Ash'ari scholars): Acknowledge the beauty and spiritual depth of much of his writing while noting that certain formulations require allegorical interpretation or are simply beyond what the ordinary believer should engage.

**Critical rejection** (Ibn Taymiyyah, Ibn al-Qayyim, Ibn Kathir, al-Dhahabi, and generally the Hanbali-Athari tradition): Some of Ibn Arabi's explicit statements in the Fusus al-Hikam cannot be reconciled with Quranic theology even with charitable interpretation; they represent genuine theological error that should be clearly identified and corrected.

Al-Dhahabi, who is usually measured in his assessments, wrote in *Tanbih al-Ghabi ila Takfir Ibn Arabi* a careful critique — though even al-Dhahabi was cautious about making personal judgments on Ibn Arabi's eternal state. The concern was not his sincerity or his spirituality but the content of specific theological formulations.

## What the Ahl us-Sunnah Position Requires

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging several things simultaneously:

First, Ibn Arabi was a genuine scholar with broad and deep knowledge of the Islamic sciences. His early works, including his collection of biographies of Andalusian scholars (*Ruh al-Quds*), demonstrate conventional Islamic learning. His love for Allah and the Prophet ﷺ is evident throughout his writing. He was not an atheist or a cynical manipulator of religious symbolism.

Second, the doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud as formulated in the Fusus al-Hikam contains statements that cannot be harmonized with the clear Quranic distinction between Allah and His creation. The interpretation that reads these formulations charitably as merely expressing existential dependence may rescue the doctrine philosophically, but the plain meaning of certain passages resists this rescue.

Third, his death in Damascus (638 AH) and burial there, where his tomb at the foot of Mount Qasiyun became a site of pilgrimage for centuries, raises its own issues. From the Athari perspective, excessive veneration of graves — regardless of whose grave it is — falls under the prohibition the Prophet ﷺ established when he said: "Do not make my grave a site of festival (*'id*)" and "May Allah curse the Jews and Christians who took the graves of their prophets as places of worship" (Bukhari, Muslim).

The appropriate Ahl us-Sunnah approach to Ibn Arabi is neither uncritical acceptance nor wholesale denunciation, but careful scholarly engagement: acknowledging the spiritual depth and sincerity of much of his writing, identifying the specific theological formulations that are problematic, and maintaining the Quranic foundations — the absolute distinction between Creator and creation — as the non-negotiable criterion.

## Legacy

Ibn Arabi's influence on the subsequent Sufi tradition was immense. Virtually every major Sufi order after the 7th century AH engaged with his ideas, whether to embrace, adapt, or refute them. His student Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi (d. 673 AH) systematized the Akbarian school and transmitted it to later generations. Through al-Qunawi, his influence reached Jalal al-Din al-Rumi and other major figures of the Konya intellectual scene.

He died in Damascus on 22 Rabi al-Thani 638 AH, having spent his last years in Damascus under Ayyubid patronage, composing, teaching, and reportedly still receiving the spiritual visions he claimed throughout his life. Whatever the theological assessment of his specific formulations, he represents one of the most extraordinary and complex minds in Islamic intellectual history — a figure who cannot be dismissed but must be engaged with scholarly rigor and theological clarity.
