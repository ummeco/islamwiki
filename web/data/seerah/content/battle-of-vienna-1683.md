# Battle of Vienna (1683)

The Battle of Vienna (Ramadan 1094 AH / 12 September 1683 CE) was the decisive engagement that ended the second and final Ottoman siege of the Habsburg capital. A massive relief force led by King Jan III Sobieski of Poland routed the Ottoman besieging army under Grand Vizier Kara Mustafa Pasha. The battle marked the beginning of Ottoman territorial retreat in Europe and the start of the Great Turkish War (1683-1699 CE).

## Background

### Ottoman Resurgence Under the Köprülüs

The Ottoman Empire experienced a revival in the second half of the seventeenth century under the Köprülü family of grand viziers. Mehmed IV (r. 1058-1099 AH / 1648-1687 CE) delegated effective power to these capable administrators, who reformed the military and resumed aggressive expansion.

By 1683, the Ottoman Empire had recovered from earlier setbacks and the Grand Vizier Kara Mustafa Pasha was eager for a major campaign. Hungarian Protestant nobles, chafing under Habsburg Catholic rule, appealed to the Ottomans for assistance, providing a pretext for intervention.

### The Decision to March on Vienna

Kara Mustafa Pasha assembled one of the largest Ottoman armies ever fielded, estimated at 150,000 to 200,000 men including auxiliaries and camp followers, though the effective combat strength was perhaps 90,000 to 120,000. The army included Janissaries, sipahi cavalry, Crimean Tatar horsemen, Hungarian rebel forces, and contingents from vassal states across the empire.

## The Siege

The Ottoman army arrived before Vienna on 14 July 1683 CE. The city was defended by a garrison of approximately 11,000 soldiers and 5,000 militia under Count Ernst Rüdiger von Starhemberg. Emperor Leopold I had fled the city, leaving Starhemberg in charge.

The Ottomans established an extensive siege camp and began systematic mining and bombardment of the city walls. Over the next two months, the Ottomans made steady progress. Multiple mines were detonated under the walls, creating breaches that the defenders barely managed to seal each time. Disease, exhaustion, and dwindling supplies weakened the garrison steadily.

By early September, the situation was critical. The Ottoman miners had advanced close to the main bastion, and a final major mine was being prepared that could have created a breach too large to defend. The city was days away from falling.

### The Holy League Response

Pope Innocent XI helped broker a defensive alliance between the Habsburgs and Poland. King Jan III Sobieski of Poland personally led his army south to relieve Vienna. Combined with Austrian and German reinforcements from Bavaria, Saxony, Franconia, and Swabia, the relief force totaled approximately 70,000 to 85,000 soldiers.

## The Battle

On 12 September 1683, the relief army attacked from the heights of the Kahlenberg and the Vienna Woods northwest of the city. The battle unfolded in three phases:

**Morning.** Austrian and German infantry descended from the hills and engaged Ottoman positions on the left and center. The fighting was difficult in the wooded and broken terrain.

**Afternoon.** As the infantry battle ground on, the allied forces gradually pushed the Ottoman defensive positions back toward the plain. Kara Mustafa attempted to organize a counterattack but was hampered by the terrain and by the need to maintain the siege lines against a potential sortie from the city garrison.

**The Cavalry Charge.** In the late afternoon, the Polish heavy cavalry, led by Sobieski himself and including the famous Winged Hussars, launched one of the largest cavalry charges in recorded history. Approximately 18,000 horsemen descended from the heights onto the Ottoman camp in a devastating charge that shattered the Ottoman lines.

The Ottoman army broke. Kara Mustafa's forces abandoned their siege works, camp, artillery, and vast quantities of supplies in a disorganized retreat. The garrison of Vienna sortied from the city and joined the rout. By nightfall, the siege was over.

## Aftermath

### Kara Mustafa's Fate

Kara Mustafa Pasha retreated to Belgrade, where he attempted to reorganize. However, Sultan Mehmed IV, furious at the catastrophic defeat, sent the executioner. On 25 December 1683, Kara Mustafa was strangled with a silk cord, the traditional Ottoman method of executing high-ranking officials.

### The Great Turkish War (1683-1699)

The defeat at Vienna triggered a general European counteroffensive against the Ottoman Empire. The Holy League, now including Austria, Poland, Venice, and later Russia, launched coordinated campaigns across multiple fronts. Over the next sixteen years, the Ottomans lost:

- **Hungary** (including Buda, which fell in 1686 after 145 years of Ottoman rule)
- **Transylvania**
- **Slavonia and Croatia**
- **The Peloponnese** (temporarily, to Venice)
- **Podolia** (to Poland)

The war ended with the Treaty of Karlowitz in 1110 AH (1699 CE), the first major treaty in which the Ottoman Empire ceded significant European territory. It marked the definitive end of Ottoman expansion in Europe and the beginning of the long Ottoman retreat.

## Significance

The Battle of Vienna in 1683 is widely regarded as a turning point in the history of the Ottoman Empire and of Europe. It ended the Ottoman threat to Central Europe and initiated a shift in the balance of power that would continue over the next two centuries.

For the Muslim world, the defeat signaled the beginning of a period of military and political decline relative to the rising European powers. The technological and organizational advantages that the Ottomans had long enjoyed over their European adversaries were diminishing as European states modernized their armies and economies.

The enormous Ottoman camp left behind at Vienna included vast stores of coffee, which, according to popular tradition, contributed to the establishment of Vienna's famous coffeehouse culture.
