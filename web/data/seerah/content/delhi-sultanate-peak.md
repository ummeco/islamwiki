# The Delhi Sultanate: Islam's Gateway to the Indian Subcontinent

## Islam Enters India

The Delhi Sultanate (602-932 AH / 1206-1526 CE) was the first major Muslim state in the Indian subcontinent and the political framework within which Islam established deep and permanent roots in one of the world's most populous regions. Its story is one of conquest, adaptation, intellectual achievement, and the long, slow process by which a religion carried by foreign armies became native to tens of millions of people whose descendants today constitute the Muslim populations of India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh — over 500 million people.

Islam had reached the subcontinent well before the Delhi Sultanate: Arab merchants had traded in the ports of Malabar and Gujarat from the early centuries of Islam; the Arab general Muhammad ibn Qasim had conquered Sindh for the Umayyad Caliphate in 93 AH (712 CE); and Mahmud of Ghazni's raids into northern India in the 4th century AH (10th-11th centuries CE) had extended Muslim political power into the Punjab. But it was the conquests of Muhammad ibn Sam (Ghori) and the subsequent establishment of the Delhi Sultanate by his slave-general Qutb al-Din Aybak that created a permanent Muslim-ruled state at the heart of the subcontinent.

## The Five Dynasties

The Delhi Sultanate is conventionally divided into five ruling dynasties:

**The Mamluk (Slave) Dynasty (602-686 AH / 1206-1290 CE)**: Founded by Qutb al-Din Aybak (d. 607 AH / 1210 CE), this first dynasty consisted of Turkish slave-soldiers who had served Muhammad of Ghor. The greatest ruler of this dynasty was Shams al-Din Iltutmish (r. 607-633 AH / 1211-1236 CE), who consolidated the sultanate, established it as a proper Islamic state, and received formal investiture from the Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad — an important legitimizing act. His daughter Raziyya al-Din (r. 634-637 AH) briefly ruled as sultana, a unique instance in medieval Islamic history of a woman ruling from the throne of a major Islamic state.

**The Khalji Dynasty (686-720 AH / 1290-1320 CE)**: The Khaljis were of Afghan-Turkish origin and represented a shift in the ethnic composition of the ruling elite. The most important Khalji ruler was Alauddin Khalji (r. 695-715 AH / 1296-1316 CE), whose reign represents the Delhi Sultanate's greatest territorial extent and military achievement.

**The Tughlaq Dynasty (720-816 AH / 1320-1413 CE)**: Founded by Ghiyath al-Din Tughlaq, this dynasty produced one of the most ambitious and controversial rulers of Islamic India in Muhammad ibn Tughlaq (r. 724-752 AH / 1325-1351 CE), whose experiments with monetary policy and capital relocation were discussed by Ibn Battuta who served as a qadi at his court.

**The Sayyid Dynasty (816-855 AH / 1414-1451 CE)**: A period of diminished power and territorial contraction.

**The Lodi Dynasty (855-932 AH / 1451-1526 CE)**: The last dynasty of the Sultanate, of Afghan Pashtun origin, ended at the First Battle of Panipat (932 AH / 1526 CE) when Babur's Mughal forces defeated the last Lodi Sultan Ibrahim.

## The Qutb Minar and the Architecture of Faith

The material legacy of the early Delhi Sultanate is most visibly expressed in the monumental complex constructed in and around Delhi during the Mamluk Dynasty. The Quwwat al-Islam (Might of Islam) Mosque, begun by Qutb al-Din Aybak on the site of a demolished Hindu temple using some of its stonework, was the first mosque built in Delhi and one of the first in India — a symbolic statement of Islamic presence in the heart of the subcontinent.

The Qutb Minar, attached to this mosque complex, was the tallest minaret ever built at the time of its completion (approximately 607-631 AH / 1193-1233 CE) — 72.5 meters, with five stories of exquisite stone carving in interlocking geometric and calligraphic patterns. It served the functional purpose of providing a visible minaret for the adhan (call to prayer), but its scale made it a declaration: Islam had arrived in India and was not leaving.

These structures survive today in southern Delhi as a UNESCO World Heritage Site — a direct, tangible connection to the founding moment of the Muslim presence in India.

## Alauddin Khalji: The Defender and the Conqueror

Alauddin Khalji (r. 695-715 AH / 1296-1316 CE) was the Delhi Sultanate's most militarily capable ruler and the one who brought it closest to pan-Indian dominance. His reign is also one of the most studied in terms of Islamic governance theory, because he implemented price controls, market regulations, and administrative centralization on a scale unprecedented in the subcontinent.

His greatest military achievement, from the perspective of Islamic history, was the repeated defeat of Mongol invasions of northern India. The Mongols under various commanders invaded India six times during Alauddin's reign (1297-1306 CE) with forces numbering in the hundreds of thousands. Alauddin defeated every one of these invasions — at Jalandhar (1298 CE), at Kili (1299 CE), at Amroha (1305 CE), and at Ravi (1306 CE). The last major Mongol invasion of India was repelled so decisively that the Mongols never again attempted to conquer the subcontinent.

This achievement earned Alauddin the informal title "Second Alexander" (*Sikandar al-Thani*) and the less flattering but historically accurate description of having saved the subcontinent's Muslim population from the Mongol fate that had befallen Khorasan and Iraq. Ibn Battuta later noted that the stability and prosperity of the Delhi Sultanate that he witnessed under Muhammad ibn Tughlaq was built on the foundations Alauddin had established.

## The Sufi Orders and Grassroots Conversion

The political history of the Delhi Sultanate is only one dimension of Islam's establishment in India. The deeper and more lasting process was the work of the Sufi orders — the *silsilas* (chains of spiritual transmission) that brought Islamic teaching and personal spiritual example directly to the Indian population.

The **Chishti Order** was the most important in India. Its founder in the subcontinent was Khwaja Mu'in al-Din Chishti (d. 627 AH / 1230 CE) of Ajmer — a scholar-saint who reportedly converted hundreds of thousands of Indians to Islam through personal spiritual example and teaching. His tomb in Ajmer became (and remains) one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in South Asia. The Chishti order under Qutb al-Din Bakhtiyar Kaki and then Farid al-Din Ganj-i-Shakar (Baba Farid, d. 664 AH / 1265 CE) and Nizam al-Din Awliya (d. 725 AH / 1325 CE) penetrated deep into Indian society, making Islam accessible to people across caste lines and social strata.

The **Suhrawardi Order** was active in Sindh and Punjab. The **Naqshbandi Order** came later (Mughal period) but had lasting influence.

These Sufi teachers operated largely outside the formal political structures of the Sultanate. They taught, healed, fed the poor, and lived lives of personal austerity and devotion that earned the respect even of non-Muslims. Their tombs became centers of popular devotion that continue to this day — though the practice of visiting saints' tombs for spiritual intercession is itself a matter of scholarly debate within Islam.

## Amir Khusraw: The Poet Who Built Urdu

Among the figures who emerged from the Delhi Sultanate milieu, none is more culturally significant than Khusraw Dihlavi (known as Amir Khusraw, 651-725 AH / 1253-1325 CE) — a disciple of Nizam al-Din Awliya, a court poet of extraordinary skill who wrote in both Persian and Hindi, and the figure credited with laying the foundations of Hindustani classical music and the Urdu language.

Amir Khusraw was of Turkic origin (his father was a Turkic noble from Transoxiana) but was born in India and identified wholly with his Indian homeland. He is called "the Parrot of India" and "the Father of Urdu Literature." He composed in multiple Persian genres with exceptional skill — ghazals, masnavis, qasiyas, and riddles — while simultaneously creating a distinctive Indo-Persian literary tradition. His synthesis of Persian literary culture with Indian (particularly Braj Bhasha Hindi) linguistic and musical traditions created the foundations of what would become Hindustani and Urdu culture.

Ibn Battuta, who visited Delhi and met the scholars of the Tughlaq court, provides important observations on the intellectual and religious life of the sultanate at its political midpoint. He served as qadi (judge applying Islamic law) under Muhammad ibn Tughlaq for approximately eight years — a position that gave him direct experience of how Islamic jurisprudence operated in the Indian context.

## The Dhimmi Framework

The Delhi Sultanate governed a population that was overwhelmingly non-Muslim throughout its existence. Hindus, Jains, and Buddhists constituted the vast majority of the Indian population. The Islamic legal framework for such situations is the dhimmi system: non-Muslims under Islamic rule are protected communities who pay the jizya (a per-capita tax in lieu of military service, from which they are exempt) and whose religious practice is legally protected.

In practice, the application of the dhimmi framework in the Delhi Sultanate was uneven. Some rulers — particularly Alauddin Khalji and Firuz Shah Tughlaq — imposed measures that some historians describe as harsh; others were more permissive. The broader Indian population was not forced to convert: conversion was individual and voluntary, driven by conviction, social mobility, or the spiritual influence of Sufi teachers.

The long-term result — that over 30% of the subcontinent's population converted to Islam over the 320 years of the Sultanate's existence and the subsequent Mughal period — reflects genuine spiritual transformation rather than coerced acceptance.

## The End and the Legacy

The Delhi Sultanate ended at the First Battle of Panipat in 932 AH (April 1526 CE), when Babur ibn Umar Shaykh Mirza, a Timurid prince from Central Asia, defeated and killed the last Lodi Sultan Ibrahim II. Babur founded the Mughal Empire, which would govern most of India for the next three centuries.

The Mughal Empire inherited all the institutional, cultural, and intellectual foundations that the Delhi Sultanate had built: the administrative machinery, the mosque-madrasa network, the Sufi order infrastructure, the Persian literary tradition, and the Muslim population that had grown over more than three centuries. The Taj Mahal, the great Mughal mosques of Agra, Delhi, and Lahore, the Mughal miniature painting tradition — all rest on foundations laid during the Delhi Sultanate.
