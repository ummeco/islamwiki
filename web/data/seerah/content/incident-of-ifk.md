# The Incident of the Ifk

## The Accusation Against Aishah

The ifk — the slander — was one of the most painful episodes in the Prophet's ﷺ personal life during the Medinan period. It occurred on the return journey from the expedition against Banu al-Mustaliq in 5 AH. Aishah bint Abi Bakr, the Prophet's ﷺ wife, had been left behind when the army departed camp without realizing she had stepped away to look for a lost necklace. Safwan ibn al-Mu'attal, a companion who had been assigned to travel at the rear of the army and collect anything left behind, found her, recognized her, offered his camel, and walked her back to Medina on foot while she rode. They arrived after the army had already reached Medina.

This situation — a young woman arriving separately, alone with a man — became the subject of a widespread and damaging rumor spread through the Muslim community. Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul, the hypocrite leader, was the chief instigator in spreading the accusation of adultery. Others, including some sincere Muslims who accepted and transmitted the story without full verification, contributed to its spread. The rumor reached the Prophet ﷺ, reached Abu Bakr's household, and caused enormous pain. Aishah was unaware for weeks that she was the subject of any rumor until she was informed by another woman. When she learned what was being said, she wept so severely that she could not stop.

## A Month of Silence

For approximately a month, the Prophet ﷺ received no revelation clarifying the situation. He was visibly distressed — his relationship with Aishah was noticeably affected, and his usual warmth toward her was withdrawn during this period. He consulted companions about the matter. Ali ibn Abi Talib gave practical advice, suggesting that women were plentiful and that he could replace her easily — an answer that Aishah never forgot and that created lasting tension between her and Ali. Usama ibn Zayd, on the other hand, defended Aishah's character unreservedly, saying he knew nothing of her but good.

Aishah herself requested to go to her parents' home, where she wept continuously. When the Prophet ﷺ came to her there and told her that if she had been innocent, Allah would clear her, and if she had been guilty, she should repent, she said — in a response that the hadith literature preserves with precision: "By Allah, I will not repent to Allah for something I did not do. If I say I did it while Allah knows I did not, I would be admitting to something I never committed. And if I deny it, you will not believe me." Then she turned to the wall and said she would trust in the patience of Yaqub (Jacob), who said: "Patience is most befitting" when his sons came to him with his son's bloodied shirt.

## The Revelation

The Prophet ﷺ was still sitting in the house when the revelation descended. He fell into the state that accompanied Quranic revelation — described by the companions as a kind of heaviness or trance. When it lifted, he was smiling. He told Aishah: "Allah has declared you innocent." Surah al-Nur (24:11-20) was revealed, directly and unambiguously clearing Aishah of the accusation and establishing permanent legal and moral principles for the Muslim community.

The verses are among the most powerful in the Quran for their combination of legal instruction and direct condemnation of those who had spread the slander: "Indeed, those who came with the falsehood are a group among you. Do not think it bad for you; rather it is good for you. For every person among them is what he has earned of sin, and he among them who had the greater share — his is a great punishment." (24:11) The verses then establish the rule for qadhf — accusing another of adultery — requiring four witnesses and imposing eighty lashes on those who make the accusation without sufficient proof. This legal standard directly refutes the basis on which the accusers had operated.

## The Consequences

Three men who had participated in spreading the accusation — Mistah ibn Uthathah, Hassan ibn Thabit, and Hamnah bint Jahsh — received the hadd punishment of eighty lashes for qadhf (false accusation of adultery). Abdullah ibn Ubayy, the primary instigator, was not formally punished, though the Quran identified those among the accusers who bore the greater share of the sin. He died shortly after.

The Prophet ﷺ came to Aishah's parents' house to share the news of her exoneration. Her mother told her to go to the Prophet ﷺ; she said: "I will not go to him and praise no one but Allah." Abu Bakr, sitting in the corner, wept from both joy and emotion. Aishah's response — refusing to give praise even to the Prophet ﷺ in the moment of her vindication, directing all gratitude to Allah alone — was a statement of theological clarity and personal dignity that the hadith literature preserves as a mark of her character.

The incident permanently established in Islamic law the principle that accusation of sexual impropriety requires four eyewitnesses to the act itself — a standard so demanding that it effectively cannot be met through rumor, circumstantial evidence, or personal suspicion. The legal legacy of the ifk is that it makes false accusations of this kind extremely difficult and their consequences (eighty lashes) severe, protecting the honor of the accused rather than the presumption of the accuser. The ifk is also, in the Islamic tradition, one of the most powerful demonstrations of Aishah's character — her refusal to confess to what she had not done, her appeal to divine clarity over human judgment, and her turning to the patience of Yaqub as her model in an impossible moment. These qualities made her not only innocent but, in the seerah's telling, distinguished: a woman whose honor was defended by revelation itself.
