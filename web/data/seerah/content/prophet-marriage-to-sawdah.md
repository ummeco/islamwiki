# Sawdah bint Zam'ah: The Prophet's First Marriage After Khadijah

The death of Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her) left the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in a situation that combined personal grief with practical difficulty. For approximately fifteen years she had been his wife, his first believer, his confidante, and the manager of his household. Her death in the Year of Grief — coinciding with the death of Abu Talib — came when the Muslim community was most vulnerable and the Prophet ﷺ most burdened. His first marriage after her death, to Sawdah bint Zam'ah (may Allah be pleased with her), was arranged through the suggestion of a compassionate Companion and carried within it the qualities that characterized many of the Prophet's subsequent marriages: compassion for a vulnerable woman, practical household need, and a relationship built on genuine affection rather than passion.

## The Context of the Marriage

Following Khadijah's death, the Prophet ﷺ was left with his daughters still at home — Fatima was young, and the household required management that the Prophet ﷺ could not provide while engaged in the demands of the growing Muslim mission. The Prophet ﷺ was a man in his early fifties dealing with the grief of losing a life partner of twenty-five years, the political vulnerability created by Abu Talib's death, and the daily demands of leading a community under sustained persecution.

Khawla bint Hakim, the wife of Uthman ibn Maz'un (may Allah be pleased with them), noticed the Prophet's ﷺ state and came to him with a practical suggestion. She asked: "Would you not marry?" The Prophet ﷺ replied: "Whom?" She offered two options: Sawdah bint Zam'ah, a widow, or Aisha, the daughter of Abu Bakr, through a formal betrothal arrangement.

For the immediate practical matter of household management and the care of his children, Sawdah was the more immediately appropriate choice. The betrothal to Aisha was made at this time but the marriage itself came later, after the Hijra to Medina. It was Sawdah who entered the Prophet's ﷺ household in Mecca, and it was she who managed the home and cared for the children in the difficult remaining years before the Hijra.

## Who Was Sawdah?

Sawdah bint Zam'ah al-Amiriyya was from the Banu Amir ibn Lu'ayy branch of the Quraysh. She was not young at the time of her marriage to the Prophet ﷺ — the classical sources suggest she was in her fifties, though exact ages in the seerah are often approximate. She was a widow; her previous husband was Sakran ibn Amr al-Amiri.

Sawdah and Sakran had been among the first Muslims and had participated in the second emigration to Abyssinia. The Abyssinian emigration (Hijra to Abyssinia) was one of the first tests of the early Muslim community — a group of believers who sought refuge at the court of the Negus (Ashama ibn Abjar), the Christian king of Abyssinia, because he was known as a just ruler who would not oppress the wronged. Sawdah and her husband Sakran were among those who made this sacrifice, leaving Mecca and everything familiar for the unknown.

The accounts differ on the exact circumstances of Sakran's death. Some narrations say he died in Abyssinia and was buried there. Others say he returned to Mecca with the emigrants and died there. What is consistent is that his death left Sawdah a widow, returned to Mecca without her husband's protection, in a city where the Muslim community was still under threat.

Her situation — a widow of one of the early emigrants, alone in Mecca — illustrates the particular vulnerability that widowhood created in pre-Islamic and early Islamic society. A woman without a husband lacked the primary protector that tribal society provided through marriage. For Sawdah, who had sacrificed the comfort of Mecca for the sake of the faith and then lost her husband in the process, the situation was especially precarious.

## The Marriage and Its Character

Khawla went to Sawdah's father Zam'ah ibn Qays to propose the marriage on the Prophet's ﷺ behalf. She then returned to the Prophet ﷺ with the acceptance. The marriage was contracted with a mahr (dowry) and Sawdah entered the Prophet's ﷺ household.

The sources that describe Sawdah consistently give the same impression: she was a woman of good humor, generous character, and physical presence (she is described as a large woman, physically robust). She was warm, occasionally comic, and genuinely devoted to the Prophet ﷺ. There are narrations in the Sahih collections that record the Prophet ﷺ smiling at her observations and gentle jokes.

Her role in the household was substantial and essential. She managed the domestic life that allowed the Prophet ﷺ to continue the mission. She cared for the children, particularly Fatima, who was still young. She maintained the home during the final difficult years in Mecca and then through the enormously challenging Hijra to Medina.

Her relationship with the Prophet ﷺ was affectionate. This was not a marriage of cold convenience — the later narrations make clear that the Prophet ﷺ cared for Sawdah genuinely and that their household was warm. The famous episode that tests and reveals this warmth came years later, after additional wives had joined the household and the dynamics had changed.

## The Episode of Her Day

After the Prophet ﷺ had married additional wives, the system of taking turns among wives (the principle of nawm, or the night and day assigned to each wife) became operationally significant. Each wife had a designated time with the Prophet ﷺ, and equity among them was an obligation.

As Sawdah grew older, a revelation came that gave the Prophet ﷺ a specific permission: if any of his wives wished to give up their designated turn to another wife, this was permitted. The verse is in Surah al-Ahzab (33:51): "You may put aside whom you will of them and take to yourself whom you will. And whoever you desire of those you had set aside — there is no blame upon you. That is more suitable that their eyes may be cooled and they not grieve and that they will be satisfied with what you have given them — all of them."

When this verse was revealed, Sawdah made a choice that has been cited across Islamic literature as a portrait of sincerity and wisdom. She gave her designated day and night to Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her). Her stated motivation is preserved in the sources: "I have no desire for men. I only want to be raised on the Day of Judgment as one of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ."

The implications of the verse — that the Prophet ﷺ could divorce some wives if he chose — were not lost on Sawdah. She was aware that she might be among those set aside. Her response was not to compete or argue but to hold fast to what mattered most to her: the status of being a wife of the Prophet ﷺ in this life and the next. She did not want the dunya (this world) — she wanted the akhira (the next world).

The Prophet ﷺ chose not to divorce Sawdah. By the narrations in Abu Dawud and other collections, he kept all of his wives. Sawdah remained in his household until his death, her status as a wife of the Prophet ﷺ secured — which was exactly what she had asked for.

## The Prophet's Marriages: A Scholarly Framework

The scholarly tradition across all four Sunni madhabs has consistently understood the Prophet's ﷺ multiple marriages in a framework very different from the popular caricature sometimes offered by critics. Several principles guide the scholarly analysis.

First, the marriages were not motivated by physical desire. The Prophet ﷺ was monogamous — married only to Khadijah — for the first twenty-five years of his marriage life, from approximately age twenty-five to fifty. His subsequent marriages in the Meccan and Medinan period had documented specific motivations.

Second, many of the marriages served social and political functions that were understood within their historical context. Marriage in tribal Arabia was a primary mechanism for alliance-building, for honoring widows of fallen Companions, for extending tribal relationships that served the mission. The marriage to Sawdah fit the pattern of honoring a Muslim widow who had made significant personal sacrifices.

Third, several of the Prophet's ﷺ later marriages specifically honored women who had been left vulnerable — widows of Companions killed in battle, women whose tribes came to Islam and who needed the dignity of a marital connection to the community's leader. The Prophet's ﷺ household became, in effect, a place of refuge and honor for some of the early community's most vulnerable members.

Fourth, the marriages produced the Mothers of the Believers — women who transmitted a vast portion of the hadith literature, particularly regarding the Prophet's ﷺ private life, prayers, domestic habits, and intimate teachings. Without the marriages, and without the multiple women from different backgrounds who entered the household, the community would have lost access to enormous quantities of prophetic knowledge. Sawdah's transmission of hadith is modest compared to Aisha's but she is recorded in the collections as a transmitter of knowledge about the Prophet ﷺ.

## Her Later Life and Death

Sawdah survived the Prophet ﷺ and lived in Medina as one of the Mothers of the Believers — a title of honor given by the Quran itself to all the Prophet's wives (Surah al-Ahzab 33:6: "The Prophet is more worthy of the believers than themselves, and his wives are their mothers"). The Mothers of the Believers were treated with particular honor by the early Muslim community; their testimony in religious matters was given great weight, and their households were places of religious learning.

Sawdah is reported to have died during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), approximately 54 AH in some accounts, though the exact date is uncertain. Her generosity was remembered in the community — narrations suggest she gave away much of her household wealth in charity, consistent with the Prophet's ﷺ teaching about the limits of worldly attachment.

Her simple request — to be raised as a wife of the Prophet ﷺ on the Day of Judgment — stands as one of the most focused expressions of otherworldly aspiration in the companion literature. She did not ask for wealth, children, social status, or power. She asked for a specific spiritual standing that the world could not give her but that her faith had shown her was available through sincere devotion.

## Her Significance in Islamic Memory

Sawdah bint Zam'ah holds a particular place in the seerah not for dramatic deeds in battle or spectacular intellectual achievement but for the qualities she embodied: loyalty, practicality, generosity, humor, and a deeply grounded relationship with what mattered in the afterlife. She was the wife who stepped into the void left by Khadijah's death, who managed the household, who gave her day to a co-wife rather than fight for it, and who asked for nothing except to remain connected to the Prophet ﷺ in both worlds.

These are not qualities that generate famous narrations or historical debates. But they are qualities that sustained the early Muslim community in ways that the more dramatic deeds depended on.
