# Imam al-Nawawi: The Scholar Whose Works Will Never Be Forgotten

## A Life of Astonishing Brevity and Output

Yahya ibn Sharaf ibn Murri al-Nawawi was born in the village of Nawa, south of Damascus in the Hauran region of Syria, in 631 AH (1233 CE). He died in the same village on 24 Rajab 676 AH (December 1277 CE) at the age of 45. In forty-five years — barely a normal scholarly career's beginning — he produced a body of work that has never ceased to be read, taught, and relied upon in mosques and madrasas across the entire Islamic world.

Al-Nawawi is the proof that the quality of a scholar's contribution cannot be measured by longevity alone. Scholars who lived twice as long left far smaller marks on the tradition. His *Arba'in* (Forty Hadiths) is likely the most memorized hadith collection in Islamic history; his *Riyadh al-Salihin* is perhaps the most widely distributed book of Islamic learning after the Quran and the major hadith collections; his commentary on Sahih Muslim remains the standard reference; his fiqh manuals are taught in Shafi'i institutions from Morocco to Indonesia.

## Coming to Damascus

Al-Nawawi came from a modest family in the countryside of Syria. The accounts of his early life emphasize his unusual spiritual precocity: he reportedly was not like other children in his attachment to play and distraction, preferring instead to recite Quran and engage in religious study from a young age. When he was 19, his father brought him to Damascus to study, and the young man never really returned.

Damascus in the 7th century AH was one of the great intellectual centers of the Islamic world, particularly in the aftermath of Baghdad's destruction by the Mongols (656 AH). The Mamluk Sultanate, established in Egypt in 648 AH, had made Syria a part of a state that valued and patronized Islamic scholarship. The Diyar mosque complex (the Dar al-Hadith al-Ashrafiyya) was a major center of hadith studies, and al-Nawawi became associated with it early in his student life.

He reportedly studied under more than twenty teachers of note, and he studied in multiple institutions simultaneously, covering a vast syllabus of Islamic sciences with a speed that even his contemporaries found remarkable. He was appointed as a teacher (*mudarris*) at the Dar al-Hadith al-Ashrafiyya and served there for the remainder of his academic life.

## The Life of the Ascetic: Extraordinary Personal Piety

Al-Nawawi's personal life was a study in voluntary austerity. He reportedly owned only two garments throughout his scholarly life in Damascus. He slept only when overcome by fatigue — a minimal amount, perhaps two to three hours, in order to maximize his time for study and worship. He ate once a day, at most twice, and the food was simple. Al-Dhahabi, his biographer, records that he never married and that he gave away any gifts or additional items he received to those in need.

He reportedly spent years without eating the famous Damascus fruits — the apricots, figs, and other produce for which the region was known — because he became convinced that much of the Damascus orchards at the time were improperly owned (disputed ownership due to endowment complications) and he therefore avoided them out of scrupulous piety (*wara'*). This level of legal conscientiousness (*wara'*) — avoiding not just the forbidden but anything doubtful — was a hallmark of his character.

Ibn Khallikan, in *Wafayat al-A'yan*, describes al-Nawawi as someone whose time was entirely occupied with worship and scholarship — the two poles of his existence alternated with no gap between them. When not studying or writing, he was praying or reciting Quran.

## The Works: A Library in Forty-Five Years

The scope of al-Nawawi's written production given his lifespan and his simultaneous teaching duties is difficult to comprehend. His major works include:

**Riyadh al-Salihin** (Gardens of the Righteous): A thematic compilation of Quranic verses and hadiths arranged according to virtues, spiritual states, and practical guidance for Muslim life. It is organized so that a reader can turn to chapters on topics like truthfulness, patience, reliance on Allah, treatment of parents, and the etiquette of eating, and find the relevant Quran and hadith texts. It has been translated into dozens of languages and is distributed in extraordinary quantities. Generations of Muslims have read it in family circles after the evening prayer.

**al-Arba'in al-Nawawiyya** (The Forty Hadiths of al-Nawawi): A selection of 42 hadiths (the "forty" is traditional; al-Nawawi himself added two beyond the round number) that al-Nawawi considered foundations of the religion. They begin with the hadith of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) on intentions: "Actions are by intentions" (*Bukhari, Muslim*). The collection is memorized by millions of Muslims as a foundation of Islamic learning. Ibn Rajab al-Hanbali later wrote a famous and extensive commentary on it.

**Sharh Sahih Muslim** (Commentary on Sahih Muslim): An 18-volume commentary on the second most important hadith collection in Sunni Islam, after Sahih al-Bukhari. Al-Nawawi's commentary explains the meaning of each hadith, addresses issues of chain authentication, discusses the legal rulings derived from each hadith (often across different madhabs), and addresses theological points that arise in the texts. It remains the standard scholarly commentary on Sahih Muslim.

**Rawdat al-Talibin** (The Garden of the Seekers): A 12-volume encyclopedia of Shafi'i fiqh — one of the most comprehensive works ever written on the Shafi'i school, abridged from the massive *al-Aziz Sharh al-Wajiz* of al-Rafi'i and revised by al-Nawawi himself.

**Minhaj al-Talibin** (The Path of the Seekers): A condensed fiqh manual based on *al-Muharrar* of al-Rafi'i, providing rulings across all areas of Islamic law. This work became one of the most important teaching texts of Shafi'i jurisprudence in the world and generated hundreds of commentaries and super-commentaries. It remains a standard text in traditional Islamic education in Southeast Asia, South Asia, and the Arab world.

**Tahdhib al-Asma wal-Lughat** (Refinement of Proper Names and Languages): A biographical dictionary and linguistic resource covering Companions, Tabi'un, scholars, and key terms in Islamic sciences.

**al-Adhkar** (The Remembrances): A comprehensive collection of Prophetic prayers (*du'a*) and formulas of remembrance (*dhikr*) for every situation in Muslim life — entering and leaving the home, at meals, at prayer, at times of hardship, at sleep, at illness. It is a practical manual of the Prophetic Sunnah in du'a and remains widely used.

**Etiquette of the Scholar and the Student**: His brief works on the proper conduct of scholarship, including *al-Tibyan fi Adab Hamlat al-Quran* (on the etiquette of those who carry the Quran), demonstrate that he understood scholarship as a moral and spiritual practice, not merely an intellectual one.

## His Theological Position

Al-Nawawi followed the Ash'ari school in theological matters (*aqeedah*) — the dominant school of Sunni theology in the Shafi'i tradition of his time. In fiqh he was Shafi'i. His approach to Sufism was positive but measured: he accepted spiritual practices that were within the bounds of Shafi'i jurisprudence and encouraged the cultivation of spiritual stations (tawakkul, zuhd, ikhlas) as integral to Islamic life, while being careful to ground such practices in recognizable Prophetic precedent.

This combination — Ash'ari aqeedah, Shafi'i fiqh, measured Sufi spirituality — represents a coherent and historically influential strand of Sunni scholarship. It should be noted that his theological positions, like those of all scholars, should be evaluated against the Quran and Sunnah rather than accepted uncritically by virtue of his authority alone. The great majority of his work is accepted across the Sunni schools.

## Standing Up to Power

Al-Nawawi's integrity was not merely personal asceticism — it extended to his dealings with political authority. He is known to have returned a gift from the Mamluk Sultan Baybars (the powerful and generally just sultan who defeated the Mongols and expelled the Crusaders). He refused the gift on grounds that he could not be certain of its legitimacy.

He also wrote a letter of protest to the same Sultan regarding a proposed tax collection that he considered unjust. When warned that the Sultan might expel him from Damascus as punishment for such opposition, he reportedly said that he would return to his hometown of Nawa and live there — the implication being that the Sultan's threat held no terror for him. Whether this exchange is reported accurately or has been embellished by later hagiographers, it reflects the tradition's understanding of al-Nawawi: a scholar who placed his duty to truth above personal safety and comfort.

He left Damascus for Nawa in the final year of his life and died there shortly after. Some accounts suggest the departure itself was connected to political circumstances, though the details are not entirely clear.

## The Durability of His Legacy

Al-Nawawi has been dead for over seven centuries. His works have been in continuous use for every one of those centuries. The *Arba'in* is recited in mosques; the *Riyadh al-Salihin* is read in homes; the *Minhaj al-Talibin* is taught in Islamic universities; the commentary on Sahih Muslim is consulted by scholars. His *al-Adhkar* accompanies Muslims through the routines of daily life.

Al-Dhahabi, in his *Siyar A'lam al-Nubala*, wrote of al-Nawawi: "He was the Shaykh al-Islam, the leader of the later scholars, the preserver of the hadith, the jurist, the Sufi." This enumeration of qualities captures something important: al-Nawawi was not merely technically skilled but spiritually integrated — his scholarship was the expression of his character.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "When a human being dies, his deeds come to an end except for three things: a continuing charity, knowledge that benefits others, or a righteous child who prays for him" (Muslim). Imam al-Nawawi's knowledge has been benefiting others for 750 years without interruption. By any measure, it is among the most durable charitable endowments in Islamic history.
