# The Meeting at Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah

Among the most consequential events in Islamic history, the deliberations at the Saqifah (covered hall) of Banu Sa'idah in Medina on the day of the Prophet's death established the institution of the Caliphate and shaped the political structure of the Muslim community for centuries. The events at Saqifah remain one of the defining moments in early Islamic political thought — analyzed exhaustively by Muslim historians, jurists, and theologians across the centuries.

## The Crisis of Succession

The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) died on 12 Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH, without leaving explicit written instructions about who should lead the community after him. The question of leadership was thus immediately urgent.

While the Prophet's household and close companions prepared his body for burial, a separate gathering had already formed. The Ansar (the Muslims of Medina who had hosted the emigrant community) assembled at the Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah — a large covered communal space — to deliberate on the question of leadership. Their leading candidate was Sa'd ibn Ubadah, chief of the Khazraj tribe, one of the two major Medinan tribal confederations.

## The Ansar's Case

The Ansar's position was grounded in their historical role. They had sheltered the early Muslim community, sacrificed their wealth and safety, and provided the base from which Islam had grown into a state. Their spokesman argued that if any group deserved leadership, it was they. Some proposed a dual arrangement: one leader from the Muhajirun (Meccan emigrants) and one from the Ansar.

## The Arrival of Abu Bakr and Umar

When Abu Bakr and Umar ibn al-Khattab learned of the Ansar's gathering, they went at once to the Saqifah. Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah accompanied them. The situation was tense: a rapid consolidation of Ansar power around Sa'd ibn Ubadah could have fractured the community before the Prophet was even buried.

Abu Bakr addressed the assembly. He acknowledged the Ansar's immense contribution and their status. But he argued that the Arabs would not accept leadership from anyone other than the Quraysh — the Prophet's own tribe and the guardians of the Ka'bah — and that the Muhajirun were the first to embrace Islam, the tribe of the Prophet himself. He then offered the assembled a choice: "We are the rulers (umara), and you are the ministers (wuzara)."

## The Bay'ah (Pledge of Allegiance)

Umar ibn al-Khattab then acted decisively. He took Abu Bakr's hand and pledged allegiance to him, declaring: "Who has better qualities than this man, who was the second of the two in the cave?" — a reference to the hijra, when the Prophet and Abu Bakr hid together in the Cave of Thawr.

The other Muhajirun present followed Umar. Members of the Ansar then gave their pledges one by one, though Sa'd ibn Ubadah himself refused to pledge allegiance to Abu Bakr and maintained his position until his death. The majority of the Ansar, however, accepted the outcome.

The next morning, a general pledge of allegiance was made in the mosque of the Prophet, formalizing Abu Bakr's position as the first Caliph (Khalifah, "successor") of the Prophet.

## What Saqifah Established

The Saqifah deliberations established several principles that shaped Islamic political theory:

**The Caliphate as a consultative institution.** Leadership was determined by the consent of the leading figures of the community (ahl al-hall wal-aqd, "those who loosen and bind"), not by hereditary succession or divine designation.

**Qurayshi lineage as a condition.** Abu Bakr's argument that the Caliph should be from Quraysh became a normative position in Sunni political thought, though later scholars debated whether it was a strict requirement or a pragmatic consideration.

**The primacy of the Muhajirun and Ansar.** Both groups' contributions were recognized, even if the Muhajirun ultimately prevailed. The Ansar were accorded honored status in Islamic tradition precisely because of their role.

## Shia Perspective

Shia Muslims view the events at Saqifah as a usurpation. They hold that the Prophet had designated Ali ibn Abi Talib as his successor at Ghadir Khumm during the Farewell Pilgrimage and that the Saqifah gathering bypassed this designation. In the Shia understanding, the Saqifah was an illegitimate seizure of power that diverted leadership from the Ahl al-Bayt (the Prophet's household). This fundamental disagreement about Saqifah is one of the root causes of the Sunni-Shia split.

From the Sunni perspective, the Ghadir Khumm hadith refers to the merits of Ali rather than a formal political appointment, and the Saqifah process, while imperfect under the circumstances, represented a legitimate exercise of communal decision-making.

## Historical Significance

The Saqifah remains, fourteen centuries later, one of the most studied events in Islamic history. The political theology that emerged from it — the nature of the Caliphate, who was eligible, how it was to be determined, and what obligations it placed on the community — animated centuries of Islamic jurisprudence, political theory, and sectarian conflict.
