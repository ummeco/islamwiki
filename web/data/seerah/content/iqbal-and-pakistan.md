# Iqbal and the Vision of Pakistan

Muhammad Iqbal (1294-1357 AH / 1877-1938 CE) was the most influential Muslim poet-philosopher of the twentieth century and the intellectual architect of the idea of a separate Muslim homeland in South Asia that eventually became Pakistan. His philosophical poetry in Urdu and Persian, his political vision, and his advocacy for a revitalized Islamic identity made him the spiritual father of Pakistan — though he died a decade before the state was actually established.

## Life

Muhammad Iqbal was born in Sialkot (in present-day Pakistan) in 1877 CE to a Kashmiri family of modest means. He received an excellent traditional Islamic education alongside modern British colonial education, ultimately studying at Government College in Lahore before pursuing advanced studies in Europe.

He completed a law degree at Lincoln's Inn in London and a doctorate in philosophy at the Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich, where his dissertation focused on the development of metaphysics in Persia. His time in Europe deepened his understanding of Western philosophy — Hegel, Nietzsche, Bergson — while also crystallizing his conviction that the Muslim world needed a creative internal revival rather than mere imitation of the West.

He returned to India in 1908 CE and spent the rest of his life in Lahore as a lawyer, poet, philosopher, and political thinker.

## Philosophical Poetry

Iqbal's poetry, primarily in Persian and Urdu, developed a distinctive philosophical vision centered on the concept of khudi (selfhood, ego). Against what he saw as the passivity and fatalism that had afflicted Muslim thought, he championed the dynamic self: the human being who realizes the divine trust (amanah) by developing his or her God-given capacities to their fullest.

His major works include:

**Asrar-i Khudi** (Secrets of the Self, 1915 CE) — his first major philosophical poem in Persian, presenting his philosophy of khudi. The work was translated into English by Reynold Nicholson.

**Rumuz-i Bekhudi** (Mysteries of Selflessness) — the companion volume, treating the relation between individual and community.

**Bang-i Dara** (The Call of the Caravan Bell) — his major Urdu collection.

**Javid Nama** (The Book of Eternity) — modeled on Dante's Divine Comedy, a spiritual journey through the heavens in conversation with great figures of history.

His poetic dialogue with the spirit of Rumi runs through much of his work — Rumi as the great guide of Islamic spirituality and revival.

## The Allahabad Address (1930)

Iqbal's most politically consequential contribution was his presidential address to the All India Muslim League annual session in Allahabad in 1930 CE. In this address, he articulated for the first time a vision of a consolidated Muslim state in northwestern India:

"I would like to see the Punjab, North-West Frontier Province, Sind and Baluchistan amalgamated into a single State. Self-government within the British Empire, or without the British Empire, the formation of a consolidated North-West Indian Muslim State appears to me to be the final destiny of the Muslims, at least of North-West India."

This was not yet a demand for full independence — Iqbal was at this point still imagining an autonomous Muslim state within an Indian federation. But the vision became the seed of the Pakistan movement.

## Relationship with Jinnah

Iqbal engaged in significant correspondence with Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the leader of the Muslim League, urging him to return from London to lead the Muslim political cause in India. His letters to Jinnah articulated his belief that only a separate Muslim state could protect Islamic civilization in the subcontinent.

## Legacy

Iqbal died in Lahore in April 1938 CE, nearly a decade before Pakistan came into existence in 1947 CE. He did not live to see the realization of the vision he had helped articulate. Pakistan officially designates him as its national poet (Shair-i-Mashriq, the Poet of the East), and his birthday (9 November / 21 April in the Islamic calendar) is a national holiday.

Beyond Pakistan, Iqbal's influence on Islamic intellectual thought in the twentieth century was profound. His call for the "Reconstruction of Religious Thought in Islam" — also the title of his major philosophical prose work — influenced generations of Muslim thinkers grappling with how to engage modernity from within an Islamic framework.
