# Death of Abu al-Faraj al-Isfahani

Abu al-Faraj Ali ibn al-Husayn al-Qurashi al-Isfahani (284-356 AH / 897-967 CE) was an Abbasid-era scholar and literary anthologist whose massive work *Kitab al-Aghani* (Book of Songs) is one of the most valuable documents of early Arabic culture, preserving information about poets, musicians, singers, historical events, and social customs that would otherwise have been lost.

## Life

Abu al-Faraj was born in Isfahan but grew up and spent most of his career in Baghdad. He claimed descent from Marwan II, the last Umayyad Caliph — an unusual lineage to claim in Abbasid-ruled Baghdad. He was Sunni in his religious affiliation despite his claim to Umayyad descent.

He was a figure of contradictions: a scholar of encyclopedic learning who was also known for his enjoyment of wine and social pleasures, and who wrote about them with evident relish alongside his scholarly material. He lived and worked at the court of the Hamdanid ruler Sayf al-Dawla in Aleppo for some years, in one of the most refined cultural courts of the tenth century CE. He died in 356 AH (967 CE) in Baghdad.

## Kitab al-Aghani

The *Book of Songs* is Abu al-Faraj's masterwork, compiled over fifty years and reportedly presented to Sayf al-Dawla's library. It runs to over 20 volumes in modern editions and is organized around songs — primarily poems set to music — that were important in the musical and literary culture of the Arab world.

For each song, Abu al-Faraj provides:
- The poem's text
- Information about the musician who set it to music
- Biographical material about the poet
- Historical anecdotes about the circumstances of composition
- Information about related historical events

This organizational structure — the song as the entry point for a vast web of historical and biographical information — makes the *Aghani* an extraordinarily rich document. It preserves material about pre-Islamic Arabian society, the early caliphs, the Umayyad court, the early Abbasid period, and the cultural life of the tenth century — material that exists in no other source.

The work is particularly valuable for the history of Arabic music, which is poorly documented elsewhere. It also preserves hundreds of otherwise unknown poems from early Islamic and pre-Islamic periods.

Abu al-Faraj supplemented the Aghani with *Maqatil al-Talibiyyin* (The Killings of the Talibids) — a compilation of accounts of members of the Prophet's family who were killed unjustly, from Husayn ibn Ali onward. This work is a primary source for early Islamic political history.

## Scholarly Character

Abu al-Faraj was a meticulous compiler who cited his sources carefully, enabling modern scholars to trace the chains of transmission for individual traditions. He was also willing to record multiple conflicting versions of the same story rather than imposing false consistency.

His work shows deep familiarity with earlier literary and historical compilations, and he preserved substantial excerpts from works that no longer survive independently. The *Aghani* is thus a palimpsest of earlier scholarship.

Despite his personal reputation for unconventional behavior, his scholarly reputation was generally high among his contemporaries and successors.

## Legacy

The *Kitab al-Aghani* is indispensable for any serious study of Arabic poetry, music, and the cultural history of the first three centuries of Islamic civilization. Ibn Khaldun praised it as one of the great monuments of Arabic literature. Modern scholars of Arabic literature, Islamic history, and the history of music rely on it as a primary source.
