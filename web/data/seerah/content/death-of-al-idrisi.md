# Death of Al-Idrisi the Geographer

Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Abdullah ibn Idris al-Qurtubi al-Hasani al-Idrisi (493-560 AH / 1100-1165 CE), universally known as al-Idrisi, was the greatest geographer of the medieval world. His monumental geographical work, commissioned by the Norman King Roger II of Sicily, combined Islamic geographical knowledge with information gathered across the known world to produce the most accurate and comprehensive world map and geographical description of the twelfth century CE.

## Life

Al-Idrisi was born in Ceuta (Sabta), a city on the northern tip of Morocco, in 493 AH (1100 CE). He was a descendant of the Idrisid dynasty that had ruled Morocco in the early Islamic period, and his full name reflects his Hasani (descended from al-Hasan ibn Ali) lineage.

He studied in Cordoba and traveled extensively across the Mediterranean world — Morocco, al-Andalus, Portugal, France, England, Anatolia, and the Near East — before accepting an invitation from Roger II of Sicily to come to his court in Palermo. Roger II's Norman court was one of the most cosmopolitan in medieval Europe: a Christian king who used Arabic as a court language, surrounded by Arab administrators, physicians, and scholars, and deeply interested in learning from the Islamic world's superior scientific tradition.

Al-Idrisi spent approximately fifteen years in Palermo working on the commission. He died around 560 AH (1165 CE), though accounts of his later life are uncertain.

## The Book of Roger

The work commissioned by Roger II is formally titled *Nuzhat al-Mushtaq fi Ikhtiraq al-Afaq* (The Pleasure of He Who Longs to Cross the Horizons), commonly known as the *Book of Roger* (Kitab Rujjar) or the *Tabula Rogeriana*.

The project combined several components:
- A world map engraved on a silver disk, approximately 3.5 meters by 1.5 meters (now lost, but described in contemporary sources)
- 70 detailed regional maps covering the entire known world
- An extensive geographical text describing each region's climate, topography, cities, economies, and peoples

Al-Idrisi organized his work on the classical Islamic geographical principle of seven climate zones, dividing each into ten sections from west to east, producing a systematic 70-part coverage of the world.

## Content and Significance

The *Tabula Rogeriana* was the most accurate map of the world produced anywhere up to that time. It incorporated information from:
- The classical Islamic geographical tradition (al-Khwarizmi, Ibn Hawqal, al-Muqaddasi, al-Biruni)
- Direct observation from al-Idrisi's own travels
- Reports from travelers and merchants gathered through questioning at the Sicilian court
- Knowledge from classical Greek geography (Ptolemy, Strabo), filtered through Islamic scholarship

Al-Idrisi depicted Africa, Asia, and Europe with a precision that was not surpassed in European cartography until the early modern period. He provided detailed descriptions of sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and the Far East based on trade route information unavailable to earlier geographers.

Following the convention of Islamic cartographers of his time, his maps were oriented with south at the top — opposite to the modern convention. When viewed in modern orientation (north up), they are recognizable as depictions of the continents in their correct general shapes.

## Significance for the History of Geography

The *Tabula Rogeriana* circulated widely in the Islamic world after Roger's death and influenced subsequent Islamic geographical writing. Its significance in European geographical history was recognized only gradually, as Latin translations were made in later centuries.

Al-Idrisi's work demonstrates the remarkable intellectual exchange that characterized Roger II's Sicily — a meeting point between the Islamic, Byzantine, and Latin Christian worlds where knowledge from across the known world could be synthesized by a Muslim scholar working in the service of a Christian king.
