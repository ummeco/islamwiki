# Prophet Isa (Jesus), Peace Be Upon Him

Prophet Isa ibn Maryam (peace be upon him) is one of the greatest messengers in the history of prophethood. He is mentioned by name in the Quran twenty-five times, and an entire surah — Maryam — bears the name of his mother. He is described as a Word from Allah, a Spirit from Him, the Messiah, and a prophet and messenger sent specifically to the Children of Israel. Muslims love Isa deeply, revere him as a prophet of the highest rank, and believe his second coming is among the established signs before the Day of Judgment.

The Islamic position on Isa differs fundamentally from mainstream Christian theology. He is a created human being and prophet of Allah — not divine, not the son of God in any literal sense, not part of a trinity. The Quran addresses this distinction directly and repeatedly, not to demean Isa, but to protect his true status from the distortions that entered his tradition after him.

## His Birth: The Word "Be"

Isa was born to Maryam bint Imran, a woman of exceptional piety who had been dedicated to the service of the sacred precinct from before her birth. She had no husband, had never been touched by a man, and was in a state of spiritual retreat when the Angel Jibril appeared to her. He announced that Allah would grant her a righteous son. She asked: "How can I have a child when no man has touched me and I have not been unchaste?" Jibril responded: "Thus it is. Your Lord says: 'It is easy for Me, and We will make him a sign for the people and a mercy from Us. And it is a matter already decreed'" (Quran 19:20-21).

The Quran provides its own theological framework for the miracle of Isa's birth: "The likeness of Isa before Allah is as the likeness of Adam. He created him from dust, then said to him: 'Be,' and he was" (Quran 3:59). Adam had no father and no mother. Isa had a mother but no father. Both came into existence through the direct creative command of Allah. The miracle of Isa's birth, in Islamic theology, is not evidence of divinity — it is evidence of Allah's unlimited creative power. To conclude divinity from a miraculous birth would require the same conclusion about Adam, which no monotheist has ever argued.

Maryam gave birth alone, beneath a date palm tree, and Allah caused a stream to appear beneath her and dates to fall from the tree (Quran 19:24-25). She returned to her people carrying the infant. When they accused her, she pointed to the child. The infant Isa spoke from the cradle:

"Indeed, I am the servant of Allah. He has given me the Scripture and made me a prophet. And He has made me blessed wherever I am and has enjoined upon me prayer and zakah as long as I remain alive, and dutiful to my mother. And He has not made me a wretched tyrant. And peace is on me the day I was born and the day I will die and the day I am raised alive" (Quran 19:30-33).

His first words were a declaration of servitude to Allah — not a claim to divinity, but a repudiation of it.

## His Miracles by Allah's Permission

Isa performed miracles that the Quran records with the consistent qualifier "by Allah's permission" — a phrase that appears explicitly in the text and carries theological weight. Miracles belong to Allah; prophets are instruments through whom He makes them manifest.

He fashioned the shape of a bird from clay and breathed into it by Allah's command, and it became a living bird (Quran 3:49). He healed those born blind. He cured lepers. He raised the dead (Quran 3:49; 5:110). He announced to people what they had eaten and what they had stored in their homes — knowledge beyond ordinary human perception, granted to him by his Lord.

The disciples of Isa asked him to bring down a table spread with food from the sky, as a sign and a source of celebration. After Isa prayed for it, Allah descended the table: "Indeed, I will send it down to you, but whoever disbelieves afterwards from among you — then indeed will I punish him with a punishment by which I have not punished anyone among the worlds" (Quran 5:115). The surah in which this account appears — al-Ma'idah, the Table Spread — takes its name from this miracle.

## The Injeel: His Revealed Scripture

Isa was given the Injeel — a revelation from Allah containing guidance and light (Quran 5:46). The Injeel of Isa was his own distinct revelation, as the Torah was for Musa and the Quran for Muhammad (peace be upon all of them). It confirmed what came before it in the Torah and announced what would come after.

The Gospels as currently extant in Christian tradition are not identical to the Injeel of Isa as described in the Quran. They are accounts written by later followers, in languages other than what Isa spoke, transmitted through chains of transmission that do not meet Islamic standards of authentication. The original Injeel — the direct word revealed to Isa — is not preserved in an accessible form recognized by Islamic scholarship. This is not a point of polemic but a factual distinction: the Quran describes the Injeel as a revealed text given to Isa, not as a biographical account of him written after his departure.

## His Call to Tawhid

The central message of Isa, as recorded in the Quran, was the worship of Allah alone. He never claimed to be God or to be worshipped. The Quran records what Isa will say on the Day of Judgment when Allah asks him: "Did you say to the people: 'Take me and my mother as deities besides Allah?'" Isa will respond:

"Exalted are You! It was not for me to say that to which I have no right. If I had said it, You would have known it. You know what is within myself, and I do not know what is within Yourself. Indeed, it is You who is Knower of the unseen. I said not to them except what You commanded me — to worship Allah, my Lord and your Lord" (Quran 5:116-117).

This exchange, which the Quran describes as taking place before Allah on the Day of Judgment, is one of the most moving passages in the entire text. Isa disavows any claim to divinity before the throne of Allah — not because he was stripped of a status he once held, but because he never held it and never claimed it.

## He Was Not Crucified

The most consequential point of difference between Islamic and mainstream Christian belief concerns the crucifixion. The Quran is explicit: "And for their saying: 'Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Isa the son of Maryam, the messenger of Allah.' And they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but it was made to resemble him to them. And indeed, those who differ over it are in doubt about it. They have no knowledge of it except the following of assumption. And they did not kill him, for certain" (Quran 4:157).

The Islamic consensus, accepted by all four major schools of jurisprudence and by Ahl us-Sunnah wal-Jama'ah broadly, is that Isa was not killed. He was raised bodily by Allah to the heavens before those who sought to kill him could do so. The man who was crucified resembled him in some way, but Isa himself was protected. "Rather, Allah raised him to Himself" (Quran 4:158).

Who was crucified in his place, and precisely how the resemblance occurred, is not resolved with certainty in authentic Islamic sources. Scholars mention various views, but none carry the authority of a hadith from the Prophet ﷺ that names the individual. The point that is certain — and that the Quran settles definitively — is that Isa himself was not killed.

## His Return Before the Day of Judgment

The return of Isa is among the established, mutawatir signs of the Hour. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: "By the One in whose hand is my soul, the son of Maryam will soon descend among you as a just ruler. He will break the cross, kill the swine, and abolish the jizyah" (Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim). This means he will descend as a Muslim, implementing Islamic law, and correcting the distortions that accumulated in his name after his departure.

According to the hadith transmitted in Sahih Muslim, Isa will descend near the white minaret east of Damascus, dressed in garments of a yellowish hue, his hands resting on the wings of two angels. He will join the Muslim community and will pray behind the Mahdi — or, in other narrations, will lead the prayer after the Mahdi steps aside. The details of the sequence are discussed among scholars, but the core facts are agreed upon: he descends, he leads a just reign, he kills the Dajjal (the Antichrist), and he lives for a time among the believers.

After this period, Isa will die a natural death, and he will be buried near the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in Medina — in the space reportedly reserved beside the Prophet's tomb for this purpose.

## One of the Five Ulul Azm

Isa is one of the five Messengers of Firm Resolve (Ulul Azm) — the prophets who were given the most demanding missions, the greatest scriptures, and the largest communities to address. The Quran identifies them: "And when We took from the prophets their covenant and from you, and from Nuh, Ibrahim, Musa, and Isa the son of Maryam" (Quran 33:7). The five are: Nuh, Ibrahim, Musa, Isa, and Muhammad (peace be upon all of them). Each brought a universal message. Each carried his mission through conditions of severe opposition. Each left a legacy that shaped the course of human civilization.

To deny the prophethood of Isa, to insult him, or to disbelieve in him would itself constitute disbelief for a Muslim. He is part of the chain of guidance that culminated in the final prophethood of Muhammad ﷺ. The Muslim position is not one of rejection of Isa but of recovery — an affirmation of who he truly was, before the distortions of later centuries.
