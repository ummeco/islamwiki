# Three Years of Isolation: The Boycott of Banu Hashim

Among the most severe episodes of the Meccan period was a formal, written boycott imposed by the Quraysh against the entire clans of Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib. Lasting approximately three years, the boycott subjected these two clans — Muslims and non-Muslims alike — to conditions of economic strangulation and social isolation that reduced them to eating leaves from trees. Its sudden end, discovered through a miracle reported by the Prophet ﷺ before it was confirmed, marked one of the clearest divine interventions of the Meccan period.

## The Decision and the Document

The boycott was a collective decision of the Quraysh leadership, taken in response to the failure of all other methods to suppress the Islamic movement. The negotiations with Abu Talib had failed; the persecution of individual Muslims had not caused mass apostasy; the Prophet ﷺ continued to preach. The Quraysh needed a more systematic approach.

Their solution was a formal written agreement, recorded on a parchment (sahifa) that was then hung inside the Ka'bah as a solemn public document. The location was significant: by placing it in the Ka'bah, Quraysh bound themselves to its terms with religious solemnity and made it part of the central institution of Meccan life.

The terms of the document, as recorded across the seerah sources, included: no Qurayshi would buy from or sell to the two clans; no one would intermarry with them (neither give daughters to them nor take their daughters in marriage); no food or supplies would be permitted to reach them; no social interaction was permitted; and all this would continue until Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib surrendered Muhammad ﷺ to face punishment.

The scholar Ibn Hisham notes that the document was written by Mansur ibn Ikrimah — or in some accounts by Baghid ibn Amir ibn Hashim — and that the Prophet ﷺ prayed against the writer, whose hand is reported to have become paralyzed afterward.

## The Clans in the Boycott

An important and often overlooked detail is that the boycott applied to Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib as tribal entities — not only to the Muslims within them. Abu Talib, who was not a Muslim, withdrew with the clan into the Shi'b (the valley or mountain pass associated with the clan) because the clan's honor required solidarity in the face of an external threat. This meant that non-Muslim members of the two clans also endured the hardship of the boycott.

The only prominent exception was Abu Lahab ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's ﷺ uncle and one of his most active persecutors. He crossed clan lines and sided with the boycotting Quraysh, reportedly supporting the boycott's aims. For this he was deeply dishonored in the memory of the clan, and his name is forever associated with the divine condemnation of Surah al-Masad.

## The Confinement to the Shi'b

The two clans retreated to the Shi'b Abi Talib — a narrow valley or defile in the hills above Mecca that provided a defensible location. Here they gathered together: men, women, children, the elderly, Muslims and polytheists of the clans side by side, all subject to the same blockade.

The conditions deteriorated rapidly. The economic strangulation was effective: with no ability to buy food in Mecca's markets and no caravans permitted to trade with them, the clans had to survive on whatever they had stored and whatever they could obtain through the few clandestine channels that remained available.

The narrations preserved in the seerah sources are harrowing in their specificity. People ate leaves from trees — the scrub vegetation of the rocky Meccan hillside, not a food source under ordinary circumstances. The sound of children crying from hunger could be heard by Quraysh in the valley below. Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her) spent much of her substantial wealth during this period buying food and supplies to sustain the community, a generosity that — combined with her earlier support of the Prophet ﷺ with her own resources — depleted her fortune dramatically by the time of her death.

The period falls during what is sometimes called "the worst years" of the early Islamic community — years when the pressure was sustained and seemingly without relief.

## The Clandestine Supply Lines

Despite the thoroughness of the boycott, a few individuals of conscience among the Quraysh organized secret supply runs. The most notable of these was Hakim ibn Hizam (may Allah be pleased with him), a nephew of Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), who would bring supplies secretly at night, slipping past the blockade through the darkness.

His efforts were not without cost. On one occasion, as he was attempting to bring grain to the Shi'b, he was spotted by Abu Jahl, who confronted him. A third man — Abu al-Bukhtari ibn Hisham — intervened and allowed Hakim to pass, telling Abu Jahl bluntly that a man bringing food to his own family members could not be stopped. This intervention by Abu al-Bukhtari is recorded as one of the reasons the Prophet ﷺ made an exception at the Battle of Badr: he instructed that Abu al-Bukhtari not be killed if found alive, because of this earlier act of mercy.

## The Five Who Ended the Boycott

After approximately three years, the boycott was brought to an end through the actions of five men within the Quraysh who had concluded that the collective punishment had gone too far. The classical sources name them consistently: Hisham ibn Amr ibn Rabi'a al-Amiri, who is credited with the initiative; Zuhayr ibn Abi Umayyah al-Makhzumi (nephew of Khadijah and cousin of the Prophet ﷺ through his mother Atika bint Abd al-Muttalib); Abu al-Bukhtari ibn Hisham; Zam'ah ibn al-Aswad; and Mut'im ibn Adi al-Nawfali.

Each of these men had some personal connection to the boycotted clans — kinship ties that made the extended starvation of their relatives personally painful. Mut'im ibn Adi in particular is noted for his pivotal role. Ibn Hisham records that he coordinated among the five, ensuring they would all speak together when they presented their position publicly.

The process of ending the boycott required going to the Ka'bah to retrieve the parchment document. But when they arrived and opened the Ka'bah, they found the document in a condition that shocked them: it had been almost entirely eaten by worms or insects, leaving nothing readable except the words "Bismika Allahumma" — "In Your name, O Allah" — which had formed the document's opening invocation.

## The Prophetic Foreknowledge

The discovery of the eaten parchment was not a surprise to Abu Talib. The Prophet ﷺ had come to his uncle during the boycott and told him: "O uncle, Allah has sent against the sheet of your people's boycott a creature that has eaten everything from it except the mention of His name." Abu Talib went to the Quraysh and passed on this information, saying: "Your nephew claims that the worm has eaten your sheet of boycott, leaving nothing except the name of Allah." He challenged them: if he is right, you must reconsider; if he is wrong, we will hand him over to you.

The Quraysh went to the Ka'bah, opened the document, and found exactly what the Prophet ﷺ had described. This event — the prophetic advance knowledge of the document's state, confirmed by the Quraysh's own inspection — had a significant impact on some of those present. It was another in the series of verifiable statements the Prophet ﷺ had made that proved accurate.

The five men proceeded with their plan, publicly tearing up the agreement in the assembly of Quraysh and declaring the boycott ended. Not all of the Quraysh leadership agreed — Abu Jahl opposed ending it — but the combination of the public challenge and the shock of the parchment's condition shifted the mood.

## Mut'im ibn Adi's Special Role

Mut'im ibn Adi's role in ending the boycott earned him a specific mention in the Prophet's ﷺ later statements. When the Prophet ﷺ was returning from Ta'if after the rejection there, he needed a patron to provide protection for entry into Mecca (since he had lost Abu Talib's protection through his uncle's recent death). He sent messengers to several tribal chiefs asking for jiwar (tribal protection). Most refused. Mut'im ibn Adi accepted.

Mut'im came with his sons and nephews, armed, to escort the Prophet ﷺ into the Ka'bah and publicly declare his protection. This was an act of tribal honor and personal courage — siding with someone the entire Quraysh leadership was against.

Mut'im died before the Battle of Badr (he was not a Muslim). But the Prophet ﷺ said, in a narration preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari: "If Mut'im ibn Adi were alive and interceded with me for these [the Qurayshi prisoners], I would have released them all for his sake." This statement — a direct acknowledgment of personal debt from the Prophet ﷺ — is one of the most striking examples in the seerah of the Prophet honoring those who had shown genuine humanity regardless of their religious status.

## The Theological Significance

The boycott period is significant in Islamic thought for several reasons.

First, it demonstrates the depth of the early Muslims' commitment. They endured three years of systematic starvation and isolation rather than surrender the Prophet ﷺ or abandon the faith. The option to recant and end the suffering was always available; the fact that it was not taken shows the quality of conviction in the community.

Second, the eaten parchment is treated in the scholarly tradition as a clear sign: the document through which the Quraysh tried to enforce their will against the divine plan was physically nullified by the divine will, leaving only the invocation of Allah. The symbolism — what remained was only His name — was understood by the Companions and subsequent scholars as divine commentary on the boycott's ultimate futility.

Third, the event illustrates how non-Muslims can be involved in actions that serve the divine plan without being aware of it. The five men who ended the boycott did so for personal and social reasons, not for religious ones. Yet their action served the preservation of the Muslim community through one of its most dangerous periods. The scholars note this as an example of divine management of events through various human agents.

## The Human Cost

The three years left permanent marks on the early Muslim community. Khadijah's death within months of the boycott's end may have been partly a result of the deprivation she had endured. Abu Talib's death followed within months as well. The Prophet ﷺ named this year — the year both died — the "Year of Grief" (Aam al-Huzn). The boycott did not break the community spiritually, but it broke it physically in ways that took years to recover from.

The resilience shown through the boycott became part of the community's foundational identity. Those who endured it were remembered as people who had been tested at the most fundamental level — not their property or their safety but the food required to keep their children alive — and had held.
