# Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz: The Reviver of the Sunnah

## The Man Before the Caliphate

To understand what Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz (may Allah be pleased with him) achieved as Caliph, one must first understand who he was before he became one. He was born approximately 61 AH in Medina — the year of the martyrdom of Husayn at Karbala — and grew up in the city of the Prophet ﷺ, where he studied under great scholars including the Companion Anas ibn Malik (may Allah be pleased with him) and the eminent scholar of Medina, Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib.

He was, by blood, both Umayyad and Hashimite: his father was Abd al-Aziz ibn Marwan, an Umayyad prince and governor of Egypt; his mother was Umm Asim bint Asim ibn Umar ibn al-Khattab — a granddaughter of the second Caliph. This combination made him exceptional: he carried Umayyad blood on his father's side and was the great-grandson of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) on his mother's side. Some classical scholars saw in this lineage a kind of providential preparation for the role he would play.

Before his caliphate, Umar served as governor of Medina (from 87 AH) under the Caliph al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik. His governorship of Medina was distinguished by piety and justice — he convened a council of ten senior scholars to advise him on every matter, something no governor had done before. He was known for his fine clothes, his perfume, and his dignified bearing — a prince in every sense of the word. And yet there was already in him a quality of religious seriousness that set him apart from others of his class.

## The Day Everything Changed

When the Caliph Sulayman ibn Abd al-Malik (who had succeeded al-Walid) lay dying in 99 AH without a clear plan for succession, he took the advice of the scholar Raja' ibn Haywa and designated Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz as his successor — passing over the Umayyad princes who expected one of their own.

The moment Sulayman died and Umar became Caliph, something remarkable happened. He did not move into the royal palace. He did not accept the perquisites of the caliphate as they had accumulated under his predecessors. On the very first day of his caliphate, he sent away the royal horses. He returned to the Bayt al-Mal (state treasury) all the gifts that the caliphs had accumulated — lands, properties, jewelry, precious items that had been given to caliphs personally and absorbed into dynastic wealth. He did this with his own wife Fatimah bint Abd al-Malik, a princess by birth and accustomed to extraordinary luxury. When he told her that everything must go back to the treasury, she agreed without hesitation and handed over her jewelry.

He wrote to his governors across the empire ordering them to stop accepting bribes, to treat the people with justice, to stop the practice of beating people to extract taxes, and to ensure that no one was imprisoned without legal cause. He reduced his own stipend from the state to a level he considered appropriate — accounts differ on the exact amount, but it was a fraction of what his predecessors had taken.

## The Theological Transformation

Umar's transformation of the caliphate was not merely administrative. It was profoundly theological. He understood his role as Caliph to be the same as the Prophet's ﷺ Companions had understood theirs: a trust (amanah) from Allah, not a privilege to be enjoyed.

One of his first symbolic acts was to abolish the practice, established under the Umayyads, of cursing Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) from the pulpits of the Friday prayer. This practice — begun under Muawiyah as a political measure during the civil war and continued as a dynastic habit — was a stain on the Islamic public worship that pious scholars had long condemned. Umar ended it immediately and replaced it with the recitation of Quranic verses urging justice and giving.

He also mandated that jizya (the tax paid by non-Muslim subjects of the Islamic state) not be imposed on those who had converted to Islam but were still being taxed by governors who did not want to lose revenue. Earlier Umayyad governors had, in some regions, continued to tax converts as if they had not embraced Islam — a practice that was both unjust and contrary to Islamic law. Umar's order was clear: whoever embraces Islam is free from the jizya, regardless of what that costs the treasury.

## The Practice of His Personal Life

The accounts of Umar's personal life as Caliph are striking in their consistency across classical sources. He ate dry bread with olive oil. He had a single lamp in his official quarters — and when state business was concluded, he extinguished the state lamp and used a personal one to read, unwilling to use public resources for private purposes. His clothes were worn and patched. Visitors who came to the court of the Caliph of the greatest empire on earth found a man who looked like a traveling scholar from a modest background.

One famous account preserved by Ibn Kathir: his wife Fatimah — a princess, raised in palaces — came to him one evening and found him weeping in prayer after the people had gone. She asked him what troubled him. He said: "Fatimah, I have been entrusted with the affairs of this ummah — the orphan, the widow, the poor, the imprisoned, the one wronged in the East and in the West — and I know that Allah will ask me about every one of them. And I fear that I will not be able to answer."

This was not performance. The classical biographical sources — Ibn Kathir, al-Dhahabi, Ibn Sa'd — are unanimous that Umar's transformation was genuine, deep, and consistent until his death.

## The Administrative and Judicial Reforms

Umar's reforms were not merely personal — they restructured the relationship between the state and the people it governed.

He established a principle that had not been consistently applied under the earlier Umayyads: that the ruler is accountable to the ruled. He received petitions personally, responded to complaints against governors (and removed governors who were found guilty), and created channels through which ordinary subjects could reach the Caliph directly.

He ordered that scholars be sent to newly converted regions to teach Islam — not just to establish administrative control but to genuinely transmit the religion. He understood that the spread of Islam could not be maintained by force alone; it required education, scholarship, and the living example of just governance.

On the question of the dhimmis (non-Muslim subjects), he instructed his governors to honor the covenants made with them and protect their rights, while also ensuring that the conditions of the dhimma were upheld by both parties. He was firm that non-Muslims should not be mistreated and that any contract the Islamic state had entered into must be honored.

## The Hadith of the Mujaddid

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Verily, Allah will raise for this ummah at the beginning of every century someone who will renew (yujaddid) its religion for it" (Abu Dawud, authenticated by al-Albani and others).

Classical Muslim scholars, beginning with Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him) himself, identified Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz as the mujaddid (reviver/renewer) of the second Islamic century. Al-Shafi'i reportedly said: "The scholars have agreed that Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz is the mujaddid at the head of the second century." This identification has been sustained by virtually every subsequent classical scholar who has written on the subject.

What made Umar the mujaddid? Not military conquest — he actually halted some expansionary campaigns to focus on consolidating and governing justly what had already been conquered. Not new scholarship — he was a learned man but not the greatest jurist of his age. What made him the mujaddid was his revival of the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ in governance itself — his demonstration that Islamic rule could be just, simple, and God-fearing, and that this was not merely an ideal from the distant past but a living possibility in the present.

## His Death

Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz died in 101 AH, less than two and a half years after becoming Caliph. He was approximately forty years old. Classical sources consistently record that he was poisoned — his death came suddenly after a period of good health. The suspected culprits were Umayyad relatives who feared that his reforms, if continued, would permanently undermine the privileges and accumulated wealth of the dynasty.

He died in a small village in the Homs region of Syria. He had refused to be buried in any of the state properties, insisting that he be buried in a simple plot of private land. His death was mourned across the Islamic world — not with the ceremonial mourning of a political ruler, but with the grief of people who had seen, for two and a half years, what Islamic governance could be, and who feared they would not see it again.

Al-Dhahabi, in Siyar A'lam al-Nubala, writes of him: "He is the imam, the example, the scholar, the just, the pious — the Fifth Rightly-Guided Caliph, and the Mujaddid of the Second Century."

## Legacy

The brief caliphate of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz has functioned in Islamic civilization as a perpetual standard and reproach — a reminder that the principles of the early caliphate were not impossible ideals but achievable realities. Every Muslim ruler who came after him was measured against what he demonstrated in 99-101 AH.

His reforms in the treatment of non-Arab Muslims laid foundations for the more fully multicultural civilization that would flower under the Abbasids. His insistence on the primacy of scholarship and consultation shaped the relationship between rulers and scholars in subsequent centuries. His personal example of simplicity in high office remains, fourteen centuries later, one of the most compelling arguments in Islamic history for the idea that power and piety are not contradictory.
