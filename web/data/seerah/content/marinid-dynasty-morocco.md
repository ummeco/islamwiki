# Marinid Dynasty in Morocco

The Marinid dynasty (c. 614-869 AH / 1217-1465 CE) was a Berber dynasty that ruled Morocco for nearly two and a half centuries, following the decline of the Almohads. Though the Marinids lacked the theological program of their predecessors, they became significant patrons of Islamic architecture and scholarship, constructing some of the most beautiful madrasas in the Islamic world and providing refuge and patronage for scholars, jurists, and craftsmen who fled the destruction of the Mongol invasions in the East.

## Rise

The Marinids were a Zanata Berber tribe from the region of the high Moulouya valley in eastern Morocco. They emerged as a political force during the decline of the Almohad Caliphate in the early seventh century AH. Abu Yahya ibn Abd al-Haqq founded the dynasty around 614 AH (1217 CE), and the Marinids gradually expanded their control over northern Morocco, capturing Fez in 645 AH (1248 CE) and establishing it as their capital.

The final Almohad ruler was deposed in 668 AH (1269 CE), and the Marinids became the dominant power in Morocco.

## Patronage of Learning

The Marinids became notable patrons of Islamic education, constructing a network of madrasas throughout their territory that rank among the finest examples of Islamic architecture in the Maghreb:

**The Attarine Madrasa in Fez** (725 AH / 1325 CE) — adjacent to the Qarawiyyin mosque, featuring intricate zellij tilework, carved stucco, and cedar woodwork.

**The Bou Inania Madrasa in Fez** (755 AH / 1355 CE) — the largest and grandest Marinid madrasa, also functioning as a Friday mosque, featuring an ornate minaret and elaborate decoration.

**The Bou Inania Madrasa in Meknes** — a smaller version of the same design.

**The Qarawiyyin complex** — the Marinids expanded and enhanced the Qarawiyyin mosque, one of the oldest universities in the world, which remained the center of Islamic learning in the Maghreb.

These madrasas drew students from across North Africa and al-Andalus and maintained a high level of scholarship in Maliki jurisprudence, hadith, and Arabic literature.

## Refuge for Andalusian Muslims

As the Christian Reconquista progressively reduced Muslim territory in the Iberian Peninsula, significant numbers of Andalusian Muslims sought refuge in Marinid Morocco. Scholars, artisans, and craftsmen brought their traditions with them, enriching Moroccan culture and architecture. The distinctive Andalusian architectural style — incorporating elements like the horseshoe arch, elaborate geometric tilework, and muqarnas (honeycomb vaulting) — became deeply embedded in Moroccan architectural culture through this migration.

## Military Campaigns in al-Andalus

The Marinids also attempted to reverse the Reconquista by sending armies to assist the Nasrid Emirate of Granada. Several Marinid expeditions crossed to the Iberian Peninsula in the seventh and eighth centuries AH (thirteenth and fourteenth centuries CE), winning some victories before being repulsed. The Marinids held the city of Algeciras (al-Jazira al-Khadra) for some time, giving them a European bridgehead.

The Battle of Rio Salado (741 AH / 1340 CE), in which the combined Marinid and Nasrid forces were defeated by the Castilians and Portuguese, marked the end of effective Marinid military involvement in Iberia.

## Decline

The Marinid dynasty declined in the late eighth and early ninth centuries AH due to internal disputes among the ruling family, challenges from powerful tribal confederations, and the growing strength of the Wattasid family (Marinid viziers who eventually supplanted them as the ruling dynasty around 869 AH / 1465 CE).

## Legacy

The architectural legacy of the Marinid period is among the most admired in the Islamic world. The madrasas of Fez are UNESCO World Heritage Sites and among the most visited monuments in Morocco. The scholarly tradition maintained in the Marinid madrasas contributed to the continuity of Islamic learning in the Maghreb through subsequent political changes.
