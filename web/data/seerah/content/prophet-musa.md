# Prophet Musa (Moses), Peace Be Upon Him

Prophet Musa ibn Imran (peace be upon him) is the most frequently mentioned prophet in the Quran, named in 136 places across the Book of Allah. He is one of the five Ulul Azm — the Messengers of Firm Resolve — and holds the distinction of "Kalimullah," the one to whom Allah spoke directly without an intermediary angel. His life is a sustained testimony to the power of divine protection, the patience required of those who carry a message, and the ultimate defeat of tyranny before the truth.

## Birth and Early Life in Egypt

The Children of Israel had long been enslaved in Egypt under Pharaoh (Fir'awn), who had been warned by his soothsayers that a boy born among the Israelites would one day bring about the destruction of his kingdom. In response, Fir'awn ordered the systematic killing of every male child born to the Israelites in a given year. It was into this climate of terror that Musa was born.

Allah inspired his mother: "Suckle him, and when you fear for him, cast him into the river, and do not fear and do not grieve. Indeed, We will return him to you and will make him one of the messengers" (Quran 28:7). His mother placed the infant in a sealed chest and set it upon the Nile. The chest was discovered by the household of Fir'awn himself, and his wife — whom the Quran names as a woman of true faith — prevailed upon Fir'awn to spare the child and adopt him. "Perhaps he will benefit us, or we will take him as a son," she said (Quran 28:9).

Musa's sister had followed the chest at a distance. When Fir'awn's household sought a wet nurse, she stepped forward and directed them to a woman who could nurse the child — his own mother. In this way, Allah fulfilled His promise and returned him to her before he was even weaned, as a mercy and so that she would not grieve (Quran 28:13).

## Fleeing to Madyan

Musa grew to manhood in the palace of Fir'awn, nurtured in power and learning. When he reached full maturity, he entered the city to find two men fighting — one from the Children of Israel, one from the Egyptians. Musa struck the Egyptian a single blow to defend the Israelite, and the man died. "This is from the work of Satan," Musa said immediately, recognizing his own error (Quran 28:15). He sought forgiveness from Allah, who forgave him.

The following day, warned that Fir'awn's men were seeking to arrest him, Musa left Egypt in haste, walking alone toward Madyan, a journey across the Sinai Peninsula. He arrived exhausted at a well where shepherds were watering their flocks, and found two young women standing apart, waiting. They explained that they could not water their flock until the men had finished, as their father was an old man. Musa watered their flocks for them, then sat in the shade and prayed: "My Lord, I am in need of whatever good You might send down to me" (Quran 28:24).

One of the women returned with a message: her father — identified by most commentators as the Prophet Shu'ayb (peace be upon him) — invited Musa to his home as a reward for his help. Shu'ayb offered one of his daughters in marriage in exchange for Musa working for him for eight years, with the option to extend to ten. Musa accepted, and in Madyan he found safety, a home, and a family.

## The Burning Bush: Receiving Prophethood

After completing the agreed term of service — and the Quran notes he completed the fuller ten years, as the more generous option — Musa set out with his family. On the journey, in the darkness, he saw a fire on the slope of Mount Sinai (at-Tur). He told his family to wait while he went ahead to bring them a coal or to find guidance. As he approached, he heard a voice:

"O Musa, indeed I am your Lord, so remove your sandals. Indeed, you are in the sacred valley of Tuwa. And I have chosen you, so listen to what is revealed to you. Indeed, I am Allah. There is no deity except Me, so worship Me and establish prayer for My remembrance" (Quran 20:11-14).

Allah showed him the two signs he would carry to Fir'awn: his staff, which became a living serpent when cast down, and his hand, which became white as light when drawn from his garment. Musa asked that his brother Harun be granted prophethood and sent with him as a helper in speech, for Harun was more eloquent. Allah granted this request (Quran 20:29-36).

## Confronting Fir'awn

Musa and Harun returned to Egypt and presented themselves before Fir'awn with the message of tawhid and the demand to free the Children of Israel. Fir'awn refused. He arranged a public contest between Musa and his court magicians. The magicians cast their ropes and staffs, which appeared to the audience as moving serpents through their craft. Musa's staff swallowed them all. The magicians, who recognized in this a power no human craft could produce, fell to the ground in prostration and declared their belief in the Lord of Musa and Harun. Fir'awn threatened them with torture and death. They did not retract (Quran 26:45-51).

Allah sent a series of plagues upon Egypt: years of drought and crop failure, floods, locusts, lice, frogs, and blood (Quran 7:130-133). Each time, Fir'awn's people begged Musa to pray for relief, promising to believe and release the Israelites. Each time, when the affliction was lifted, they returned to their arrogance. Fir'awn's heart was sealed.

## The Exodus and the Parting of the Sea

Allah commanded Musa to lead the Children of Israel out of Egypt by night. Fir'awn, enraged upon discovering their departure, mobilized his army in pursuit. When the Israelites reached the sea and saw Fir'awn's forces closing behind them, they cried out in despair. Musa said: "No. Indeed, with me is my Lord; He will guide me" (Quran 26:62).

Allah commanded Musa to strike the sea with his staff. The sea split into twelve dry paths — one for each tribe — with walls of water on either side. The Israelites crossed. When Fir'awn and his army followed them onto the paths, the waters collapsed upon them. Every soldier drowned.

Allah preserved Fir'awn's body as a sign for those who came after: "So today We will save you in body that you may be to those who succeed you a sign" (Quran 10:92). This verse was revealed fourteen centuries before the mummies of Egypt were rediscovered.

## The Torah and the Forty Nights

Musa left Harun in charge of the Children of Israel and ascended Mount Sinai for forty nights to receive the Torah. In his absence, a man named Samiri fashioned a golden calf from the jewels the Israelites had borrowed from the Egyptians before departing. He animated it with a handful of dust he claimed to have taken from the hoofprint of the Angel Jibril's horse, and it produced a lowing sound. Many of the Israelites fell into worshipping it.

When Musa descended with the Tablets and saw what had happened, he was overcome with grief and anger. He took Harun by the beard — his brother — and Harun explained that he had tried to stop them but they had threatened to kill him. Musa turned on Samiri and banished him. He melted the calf and scattered its ashes into the sea (Quran 20:83-98).

## Forty Years in the Wilderness

The Israelites were commanded to enter the holy land, but they refused. "Go, you and your Lord, and fight. We will remain here," they said (Quran 5:24). As a consequence, they were made to wander in the wilderness for forty years, lost in the desert. During this time, Allah provided them with manna and quails as sustenance. When Musa struck a rock with his staff at Allah's command, twelve springs burst forth — one for each tribe — so they would not quarrel over water (Quran 2:60).

## His Status and the Night Journey

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ encountered Musa on the Night Journey (al-Isra wal-Mi'raj). Musa was in the sixth heaven. When the Prophet ﷺ descended after receiving the command of fifty daily prayers, it was Musa who repeatedly urged him to return to Allah and ask for a reduction, citing his own experience with the Children of Israel. The prayers were reduced in stages until they reached five, their present number. Musa still said: "Return and ask for a further reduction." The Prophet ﷺ said: "I am too shy before my Lord. I am satisfied and I submit" (Bukhari, Muslim).

This account shows the tender concern Musa holds for the Muslim ummah even after his death — and the closeness the two prophets share across time and revelation.
