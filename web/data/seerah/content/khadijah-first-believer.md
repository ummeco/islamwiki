# Khadijah: The First Believer

## The Immediate Response

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ returned from Cave Hira trembling and overwhelmed after his encounter with Jibril, he went directly to Khadijah. "Cover me, cover me," he said — and she wrapped him in a cloak. She held him until the trembling eased. When he recovered enough to speak, he told her everything: the angel, the embrace, the words, the terror.

Then he said: "I fear for myself."

Khadijah's response, recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari as part of the hadith that opens the entire collection, is one of the most significant declarations in Islamic history:

"By Allah, Allah will never humiliate you. You maintain family ties. You speak the truth. You bear others' burdens. You help the destitute. You are generous to your guests. You assist in every righteous cause."

This was not a theological argument. It was not a reasoned analysis of whether the encounter in the cave was genuine revelation. It was a declaration of absolute faith based on direct knowledge of the Prophet's character — a testimony that a man of this quality, a man whose entire life had been oriented toward goodness, would not be abandoned or humiliated by God. It was the first declaration of Islam, and it was made by a woman whose faith was built on the evidence of who the Prophet ﷺ was as a human being.

## The Visit to Waraqah

After calming the Prophet ﷺ and giving her declaration of faith, Khadijah took action. She led him to her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal, who was elderly, had become Christian, could write in Hebrew, and had extensive knowledge of the previous scriptures. Waraqah listened to the account of what had happened in the cave and responded immediately: "This is al-Namus — the same angel who was sent to Moses."

He then made a prediction: the Prophet ﷺ would be driven out of Mecca by his people. "No man has ever brought what you have brought without being opposed and persecuted." He pledged that if he lived long enough to witness that day, he would support the Prophet ﷺ with everything he had. Waraqah died shortly after — old and having gone blind — without living to see the public mission begin. But his confirmation at this earliest moment represented something significant: recognition from a scholar of the previous scriptures that what was happening was continuous with what had come before.

Khadijah's decision to take the Prophet ﷺ to Waraqah was not passive. She was not simply accompanying him as support; she was taking a decisive step to obtain expert confirmation of what she had already accepted on faith. Her move from faith to active support — seeking and acquiring theological validation from the most qualified person she knew — foreshadowed the role she would play throughout the years of persecution.

## Her Status in Islamic Tradition

Khadijah bint Khuwaylid holds a unique and exalted place in Islamic tradition. The Prophet ﷺ is recorded as having said: "The best of the women of Paradise are Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, Fatimah bint Muhammad, Maryam bint Imran, and Asiya bint Muzahim, the wife of Pharaoh." (Ahmad and others) In another narration: "Khadijah is one of the four greatest women of Paradise."

During her lifetime, Allah sent her His salaam (peace) through Jibril, with the additional news of a house prepared for her in Paradise made of pearl — with no noise and no fatigue. The Prophet ﷺ conveyed this divine greeting to Khadijah before her death, and she replied: "Allah is al-Salam (the Source of Peace), and upon Jibril be peace, and upon you, O Messenger of Allah, be peace and Allah's mercy."

The Prophet ﷺ remembered Khadijah with extraordinary love throughout the remainder of his life. His later wife Aisha reported: "I was never jealous of any of the Prophet's wives as I was jealous of Khadijah — though I never met her — because of how often the Prophet mentioned her." When the Prophet ﷺ slaughtered a sheep, he would send portions of it specifically to Khadijah's old friends as an act of loyalty to her memory. When a woman identified herself as Khadijah's old friend, the Prophet ﷺ would stand up to receive her and treat her with great honor.

## The Particular Honor of Being First

Being the first believer carries a specific weight in Islamic theology. The Prophet ﷺ himself said: "I am told that Khadijah ﷲ was the first of this ummah to believe in me and to testify that I am the Messenger of Allah." (Ahmad) This precedence — being the very first — is described in the hadith tradition as carrying a permanent spiritual distinction.

The companions understood this. Abu Bakr, known as the greatest of the male companions, was consistently described as "the first of the men to believe" — not the first absolutely, since Khadijah (a woman), Ali ibn Abi Talib (a child), and Zayd ibn Haritha (a freed slave) all preceded him. The distinction of first among the free adult men carried honor. But the distinction of being the first of all — man, woman, child, and slave — belonged to Khadijah.

The combination of her precedence in faith, her material support during the most difficult years, and the exceptional quality of her character made her, in the Prophet's ﷺ own understanding and expression, the most significant person in the foundation of the prophetic mission. Without her immediate faith, the most devastating moment of self-doubt the Prophet ﷺ ever experienced might have been far more damaging. Without her wealth, the early Muslims' survival would have been far more precarious. Without her love and steadfast support, the Prophet ﷺ would have faced the hostility of Mecca without the one person who knew him most completely.
