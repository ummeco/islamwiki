# The Trade Journey to Syria for Khadijah

## Khadijah bint Khuwaylid: A Businesswoman of Mecca

Before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ received revelation, he was known throughout Mecca as al-Amin — the Trustworthy. This reputation was not abstract; it was earned through years of conduct in the close-knit mercantile world of Quraysh, where every transaction was a test of character and every broken promise had consequences. Among those who had observed this reputation and decided to act on it was Khadijah bint Khuwaylid al-Asadiyyah — a wealthy widow who ran her own trading enterprise.

Khadijah was among the most respected and wealthy people in Mecca, regardless of gender. She had been widowed twice — first from Atiq ibn A'idh al-Makhzumi, and then from Abu Hala al-Tamimi (also recorded as Hind ibn al-Nabbash) — and had continued to operate her trading business after each husband's death, employing men from Quraysh to travel with her caravans in exchange for wages or a share of the profits. Her trading instincts were sharp, and she was selective about whom she trusted with her goods.

## The Proposal and the Journey

When Khadijah heard of Muhammad ibn Abdullah's reputation for honesty and good judgment, she approached him through an intermediary — her close friend Nafisah bint Munabbih — asking whether he would be willing to travel to Syria (Bilad al-Sham) with her trade goods. She offered to pay him double what she would ordinarily pay other men and to provide additional provisions. The Prophet ﷺ agreed.

This journey — when he was approximately twenty-five years old — was his second significant trading expedition to Syria, the first having been the childhood journey with Abu Talib when he encountered the monk Bahira. This time he traveled as a representative of a merchant, responsible for her goods, her financial interests, and the conduct of the caravan in her name. The level of trust this represented was significant: Khadijah was sending a man she had not personally dealt with before to handle a substantial portion of her wealth.

He traveled with Khadijah's servant Maysarah, who accompanied him both as a practical assistant and as Khadijah's eyes and ears on the journey. Maysarah would later be the one to report back to Khadijah everything he had observed — and his observations would prove decisive.

## The Journey and Its Signs

The journey followed the established Meccan trade route north through the Hejaz, into the Jordanian highlands, and on to the Byzantine trading centers of Syria. At a stopping-point during the journey, Maysarah observed something that remained with him: when the Prophet ﷺ stopped to rest under a tree near a monastery, the monk who lived there came out and told Maysarah: "No one has ever rested under this tree except a prophet." He also observed on the journey that two angels shaded the Prophet ﷺ from the sun as he rode — a cloud that moved to cover him but not others in the caravan.

These observations by Maysarah are preserved in Ibn Hisham's Sirah and corroborated in al-Bayhaqi's Dala'il al-Nubuwwa. Whether interpreted as miraculous or as Maysarah's heightened attention to an extraordinary man, they point to the same reality: even before prophethood, those who traveled with the Prophet ﷺ noticed something different about him.

Commercially, the journey was a success. The goods were sold at excellent prices. The Prophet ﷺ exercised his business judgment — buying and selling as Khadijah had authorized — and returned with substantially more profit than usual.

## Maysarah's Report

When the caravan returned to Mecca and the Prophet ﷺ delivered the accounts and proceeds to Khadijah, Maysarah gave his private report. He described the man's conduct throughout the journey: his honesty in every transaction, his gentleness in every interaction, his extraordinary composure — and the signs that Maysarah had personally witnessed. He described the monk's statement. He described the angels and the cloud of shade.

Khadijah was a woman of intelligence and discernment. She had sent Maysarah specifically to observe. What she received back was a portrait of a man who combined commercial competence with exceptional moral character and — in Maysarah's telling — possible signs of prophetic distinction. She had also, through this indirect interaction, formed her own assessment: the accounts were accurate, the profit was real, and the conduct of the journey was exactly what she had hoped for.

What followed the report was Khadijah's decision to propose marriage.

## The Significance of the Journey

This trading journey to Syria is often treated as merely the prelude to the marriage — the commercial errand that brought Khadijah and the Prophet into contact. But it deserves recognition on its own terms as a formative episode in the prophetic biography.

It demonstrated the Prophet's ﷺ exceptional competence in the world of trade — not a small matter in a society where commerce was the primary economy and honesty in trade was the primary social virtue. His management of Khadijah's caravan was not the performance of a hired hand but of someone who brought intelligence, integrity, and judgment to every decision along the way.

It also represented his last significant period as a working trader before the events of the next few years — the marriage to Khadijah, the years in her household with its financial security, the retreat in Cave Hira, and finally the revelation — would permanently redirect the course of his life. The journey to Syria for Khadijah was, in a sense, the final chapter of the Prophet's life as a Meccan merchant, and the beginning of what would become the most consequential marriage in human history.
