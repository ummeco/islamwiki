# Expedition of Abdullah ibn Jahsh to Nakhla

The expedition of Abdullah ibn Jahsh to Nakhla in Rajab, 2 AH (January 624 CE) is one of the most discussed early Muslim military expeditions — significant not for its military outcome but for the legal and theological questions it raised about the permissibility of fighting in a sacred month and the use of war spoils.

## Background

The Prophet sent Abdullah ibn Jahsh al-Asadi with a small party of eight Muhajirun (emigrants from Mecca) on a mission. He gave Abdullah a sealed letter with instructions not to open it until he had traveled for two days, and then to proceed according to the instructions but without compelling any of his companions to follow if they chose not to.

When Abdullah opened the letter, it contained instructions to go to Nakhla — a valley between Mecca and Ta'if — and watch the movements of the Quraysh and report on them.

## The Encounter

At Nakhla, the party encountered a small Meccan trading caravan returning from Yemen with leather goods and raisins. The men in Abdullah's party deliberated: should they attack the caravan? The problem was that they were in Rajab — one of the four sacred months of the Arabian calendar in which fighting was traditionally prohibited by pre-Islamic custom.

Some said: "We are in the sacred month. If we attack them, we violate the sacred month. If we wait until they reach the Haram (sacred precinct of Mecca), they will be beyond our reach."

Others argued that the month of Rajab was ending and Sha'ban was beginning — or perhaps it had already ended — and that the situation was ambiguous.

They decided to attack. They killed one man (Amr ibn al-Hadrami), captured two men, and took the caravan's goods.

## The Controversy in Medina

When Abdullah ibn Jahsh and his party returned to Medina, the Prophet did not accept the caravan or the prisoners. He said: "I did not order you to fight in the sacred month." He placed the caravan and the captives in holding without acting on them.

The Quraysh immediately seized on the incident as propaganda: the Muslims had violated the sacred month, a universal taboo among the Arabs. This was politically damaging and the Prophet was disturbed by the controversy.

## The Quranic Response

Then a Quranic revelation came that addressed the matter directly:

*"They ask you about the sacred month — about fighting therein. Say, 'Fighting therein is great [sin], but averting [people] from the way of Allah and disbelief in Him and [preventing access to] al-Masjid al-Haram and the expulsion of its people therefrom are greater [evil] in the sight of Allah. And fitnah is greater than killing...'"* (Quran 2:217)

The verse contextualized the expedition's fighting within the broader moral picture: yes, fighting in the sacred month was wrong, but the Quraysh had been preventing Muslims from worshipping at the Masjid al-Haram, persecuting and torturing early Muslims, and expelling them from Mecca — acts that in Islamic moral reckoning were greater violations than the technical sacred-month prohibition.

The verse did not retroactively justify the action as fully correct — the expedition's fighters had acted on their own judgment about a genuinely ambiguous situation. But it relativized the prohibition: the Quraysh's continuous greater violations of Islamic moral principles could not be overlooked by pointing only at the Muslims' lesser violation.

## Resolution

After the Quranic response, the Prophet accepted the expedition's outcome. The prisoners were ransomed, the goods became war spoils, and Abdullah ibn Jahsh's mission — while controversial in its method — was incorporated into the ongoing conflict with Quraysh.

## Significance

**The sacred months in Islamic law.** The Nakhla expedition raised the question of fighting in the sacred months (Dhul Qa'dah, Dhul Hijjah, Muharram, and Rajab). The Quran's response established that the prohibition was not absolute when weighed against the ongoing persecution of Muslims. Later Islamic jurisprudence generally holds that if an enemy does not respect the sacred months, the defensive-military response is not bound by them either.

**The limits of prophetic authorization.** Abdullah ibn Jahsh acted beyond his explicit instructions — he was sent to scout, not to raid. The Prophet's initial refusal to accept the spoils reflects the principle that military actions not authorized by the Prophet were not automatically legitimate.

**The first war spoils.** Nakhla yielded what are considered the first war spoils (ghanimah) in Islamic history — the property taken from the Quraysh caravan. The rules for distributing war spoils, later codified in Quran 8:41, developed from this context.

## Sources

The expedition of Nakhla is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*. The Quranic verse (2:217) is addressed in all major tafsir works.
