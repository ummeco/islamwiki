# The Prophet's Treatment of Servants

The Prophet Muhammad's (peace and blessings be upon him) treatment of servants — enslaved people in his household and those who served him — is one of the most revealing aspects of his character in the hadith literature. His practice challenged the brutal norms of 7th-century Arabian slavery and established principles that Islamic scholars cite as the foundation of Islam's ameliorative approach to the institution.

## Anas ibn Malik's Testimony

The most extensive first-person account of the Prophet's treatment of a servant comes from Anas ibn Malik, who entered the Prophet's service in Medina at approximately age ten and served him for ten years until the Prophet's death. Anas became one of the most prolific transmitters of hadith, narrating over 2,000 narrations. His assessment of the Prophet as a master is direct and consistent:

"I served the Messenger of Allah for ten years. He never said 'uff' to me (an Arabic expression of annoyance), and he never said about something I did 'Why did you do that?' and about something I left undone 'Why did you not do it?'"

In another narration: "He was the best of people in character. He never beat a servant or a woman with his hand."

Anas's testimony — from someone who knew the Prophet's domestic life intimately over a decade — carries unusual evidential weight. He was describing not public behavior but private, domestic treatment of someone in a vulnerable position.

## The General Principle

The Prophet said: "Your servants are your brothers, whom Allah has placed under your hands. Whoever has his brother under his hand, let him feed him from what he eats, clothe him from what he wears, and do not burden him with what overwhelms him. If you do burden him, then help him." (Sahih Bukhari and Muslim)

This hadith revolutionized the conceptual framework of servitude. The servant was not a sub-human category but a "brother" — a full human being whose relationship to his master was accidental and circumstantial rather than ontological. The master's obligations to the servant included food, clothing, and limits on workload.

## Abu Dharr's Correction

One of the most famous incidents about the Prophet's standards for servant treatment involves Abu Dharr al-Ghifari. The Prophet heard that Abu Dharr had called his servant "son of a black woman" in a moment of anger — using racial insult. The Prophet said to him: "Did you revile him by mentioning his mother? You still have some jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance) in you."

This statement was a direct theological assertion: racial insult was a residue of pre-Islamic ignorance that had no place in the character of a Muslim. The implication was clear — the person who insulted his servant on the basis of race had not fully embraced Islam's equal valuation of all human beings.

Abu Dharr was so stricken by the Prophet's words that he placed his cheek on the ground and asked his servant to step on it.

## Freeing of Enslaved People

The Prophet consistently encouraged and rewarded the freeing of enslaved people. He specified that freeing a slave was among the acts that expiate certain sins. He freed many enslaved people in his own life. His freed slave Zayd ibn Haritha was so dear to him that the Prophet adopted him (before the Quranic prohibition on legal adoption) and Zayd was known in Medina as "Zayd ibn Muhammad" until the adoption's legal status was changed.

Bilal ibn Rabah — the Ethiopian slave who had been tortured for his Islam by his Meccan owner Umayyah ibn Khalaf — was purchased and freed by Abu Bakr on the Prophet's direction. Bilal then became the first muadhdhin (caller to prayer) in Islam, his voice calling the community to prayer five times a day.

## The Theological Grounding

The Prophet's treatment of servants was grounded in explicit Quranic principles. The Quran directly addressed the owners of enslaved people, urging kindness and the freeing of slaves as an act of piety (Quran 90:13, 2:177, 24:33). The Prophet's practice gave these Quranic values concrete expression.

His statement that "the best of you is the best to his family, and I am the best of you to my family" established the domestic sphere — including relationships with servants and enslaved members of the household — as a primary arena for the expression of Islamic character.

## Legacy in Islamic Law

The Prophet's practice became the foundation of Islamic law on the treatment of enslaved people:
- Enslaved people had legal rights enforceable by courts
- Torture and mutilation were prohibited
- Enslaved people could not be forced into sin
- The freeing of enslaved people was one of the most meritorious acts in Islamic law
- Children of enslaved mothers and free fathers were born free in the Maliki and Shafi'i schools

Islamic slavery was theoretically ameliorative in its normative framing — even while the institution itself persisted across centuries of Islamic civilization.

## Sources

The accounts of the Prophet's treatment of servants are in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Adab, Kitab al-Itq), Sahih Muslim, and the Sunan collections. Anas ibn Malik's narrations are the most extensive, followed by those of Abu Dharr al-Ghifari and Aisha bint Abi Bakr.
