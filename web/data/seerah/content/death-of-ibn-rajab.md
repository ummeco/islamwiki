# Death of Ibn Rajab al-Hanbali

Zayn ad-Din Abd al-Rahman ibn Ahmad ibn Rajab al-Hanbali (736-795 AH / 1335-1393 CE) was one of the most esteemed Hanbali scholars of the eighth century AH and a master of hadith science. Known for the depth of his piety, the breadth of his scholarship, and the clarity and spiritual depth of his writing, he left a body of work that has remained widely read and studied by Muslims across the centuries.

## Life

Ibn Rajab was born in Baghdad in 736 AH (1335 CE) to a family originally from Baghdad. His family moved when he was young, and he grew up and was educated primarily in Damascus. His father, who was also a scholar, took him to study under the leading hadith scholars of the age, traveling to Mecca, Medina, Cairo, Jerusalem, and Damascus to sit at the feet of the great teachers of his time.

He became a student of Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya's students and was deeply shaped by the spiritual and theological tradition of Ibn Taymiyya, whose influence ran through his Hanbali teachers. He spent his mature years in Damascus, teaching in the Umayyad Mosque and leading a life of scholarly devotion, prayer, and worship.

He died in Damascus in 795 AH (1393 CE). His funeral was attended by a large gathering, and he was buried in the Bab al-Saghir cemetery.

## Major Works

**Jami al-Ulum wal-Hikam** (The Collection of Knowledge and Wisdom) is considered his masterwork. It is a commentary on the famous collection of forty hadiths compiled by Imam al-Nawawi, with Ibn Rajab adding an additional eight hadiths to bring the total to fifty. Each hadith is treated with remarkable depth — the linguistic analysis, the legal rulings derived from it, the spiritual lessons it contains, and the relevant scholarly discussions. The work is notable for combining legal and spiritual dimensions in a way that reflects the best of the Hanbali tradition.

**Sharh Ilal al-Tirmidhi** (Commentary on the Legal Defects of al-Tirmidhi's Hadith Collection) is a technical work of hadith criticism, examining the chains of narration in al-Tirmidhi's collection and discussing which narrations have defects (ilal) that affect their authenticity. This work demonstrates Ibn Rajab's mastery of the technical sciences of hadith.

**Lata'if al-Maarif** (The Subtleties of Knowledge) is a calendar of the sacred seasons of the Islamic year — Ramadan, Muharram, Sha'ban, and others — explaining their virtues, the recommended acts of worship during them, and the relevant scholarly discussions. It remains widely read, particularly during Ramadan.

**al-Qawa'id al-Fiqhiyya** (The Legal Principles) is a collection of foundational Hanbali legal maxims with explanations.

**Dhayl Tabaqat al-Hanabila** (Continuation of the Biographical Dictionary of the Hanbali School) provides biographies of Hanbali scholars from after the period covered by Ibn Abi Ya'la's original work.

## Spiritual Character

Ibn Rajab was noted for his piety and spiritual depth as much as his scholarship. He reportedly devoted extensive time to worship, especially at night, and maintained a simple lifestyle despite his scholarly reputation. His writings reflect a constant concern with the spiritual dimensions of Islamic practice — the inner realities of worship, the dangers of ostentation and worldliness, and the cultivation of sincere devotion.

He lived during a difficult period: the Black Death (749-750 AH / 1348-1349 CE) had swept through the Islamic world during his youth, killing enormous numbers, including many scholars; the Mongol threat had receded but the political fragmentation of the Islamic world continued; and the Ottoman state was still consolidating its power in Anatolia. His spiritual emphasis reflects the concerns of a period of great uncertainty.

## Legacy

Ibn Rajab's works, particularly *Jami al-Ulum wal-Hikam* and *Lata'if al-Maarif*, have been continuously read and studied since his death. Modern readers find in them a combination of scholarly rigor and spiritual depth that is characteristic of the best of the classical tradition. He represents the continuation of the tradition of Ibn Taymiyya and Ibn al-Qayyim into the next generation — maintaining the Hanbali commitment to the Quran and Sunnah while expressing it with spiritual warmth and practical wisdom.
