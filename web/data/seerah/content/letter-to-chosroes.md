# The Prophet's Letter to Chosroes II, Emperor of Persia

Among the letters sent by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) to the rulers of the world's major powers in 7 AH, the letter to Khosrow II (also rendered as Chosroes, Kisra in Arabic) — the Shahanshah (King of Kings) of the Sassanid Persian Empire — stands apart for the dramatic and ominous manner in which it was received, and for the prophetic statement the Prophet made upon learning of its fate.

## The Messenger

The Prophet sent Abdullah ibn Hudhafah al-Sahmi as his messenger to the Persian court. Hudhafah traveled to the Sassanid capital — at the time, likely Ctesiphon (al-Madain, near modern Baghdad). He delivered the letter to Khosrow II.

## The Letter

The letter followed the format of the other royal letters:

*"In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate. From Muhammad, the Messenger of Allah, to Kisra, the great leader of Persia. Peace be upon whoever follows guidance, believes in Allah and His Messenger, and testifies that there is no god but Allah alone with no partner, and that Muhammad is His servant and His Messenger. I invite you to the call of Allah, for I am the Messenger of Allah to all people, to warn whoever is living and to establish the proof against the disbelievers. Accept Islam and be safe. If you refuse, then upon you is the sin of the Magi (Majus)."*

## Khosrow's Reaction

According to Islamic historical sources, Khosrow II received the letter and tore it apart in contempt — a deliberate insult to the Prophet and his message. He then sent a letter to his governor in Yemen, Badhan, ordering him to send two strong men to the Hijaz to bring Muhammad back to him.

When the Prophet heard that Khosrow had torn his letter, he reportedly said: "May Allah tear up his kingdom" — or in another narration: "Even so will Allah tear his kingdom to shreds."

## The Prophetic Fulfillment

The Islamic historical tradition records this statement as one of the fulfilled prophecies of the Prophet. Within less than a decade, the Sassanid Empire — which had stood for four centuries as one of the greatest powers in the ancient world — began a catastrophic collapse. In 628 CE (the same year as the letters), Khosrow II was killed by his own son Shiruya (Kavadh II), who then died of plague. The empire was plunged into civil war, with multiple rulers following one another in rapid succession. By 636-651 CE, the Arab-Muslim armies under the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab had conquered the entire Sassanid Empire.

Khosrow II's empire — which had controlled Persia, Iraq, and significant parts of the Near East — was absorbed into the Islamic state within two decades of his tearing the Prophet's letter.

## The Yemen Governor's Response

Badhan, the Persian governor of Yemen, had sent two men to Medina as Khosrow instructed. When they arrived and delivered Khosrow's demand, the Prophet told them to return to Yemen and informed them that Allah had set upon Khosrow his own son Shiruya, who had killed him — something that had not yet occurred in Arabia but which soon proved true.

When news arrived confirming Khosrow's death at his son's hands, Badhan accepted Islam and Yemen came under Muslim governance.

## Significance

**The contrast with Heraclius.** The contrasting receptions of the Prophet's letters by Heraclius and Khosrow II have been noted by Muslim historians and theologians. Heraclius conducted a respectful inquiry; Khosrow treated the letter with contempt. Both empires fell to the Arab-Muslim expansion — but Khosrow's fell far more completely and rapidly.

**The prophetic statement.** The Prophet's response to news of the torn letter — "May Allah tear his kingdom" — is recorded in multiple hadith collections and is one of the fulfilled prophecies cited in Islamic apologetics (ilm al-dala'il wa al-mu'jizat, the science of prophetic proofs and miracles).

**The letter to kings as da'wah.** The dispatch of letters to the world's rulers established a prophetic precedent for Islamic da'wah (invitation to Islam) at the highest political levels. The principle that Muslim leaders should communicate the message of Islam to non-Muslim rulers has been referenced throughout Islamic history.

## Sources

The letter to Khosrow and the account of its reception are recorded in Sahih Bukhari, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
