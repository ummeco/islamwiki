# The Ottoman Conquest of Constantinople

The conquest of Constantinople by the Ottoman Sultan Mehmed II (Muhammad al-Fatih) on the 20th of Jumada al-Ula, 857 AH (May 29, 1453 CE) fulfilled a prophecy of the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and marked the end of the Byzantine Empire, the last remnant of the Roman Empire that had endured for over a thousand years.

## The Prophetic Tradition

The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "You will certainly conquer Constantinople. What an excellent leader its conqueror will be, and what an excellent army that army will be." (Reported by Ahmad and al-Hakim, authenticated by al-Dhahabi)

This hadith inspired generation after generation of Muslim commanders to attempt the conquest. The first Muslim siege of Constantinople occurred during the caliphate of Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan in 49 AH (669 CE). Subsequent attempts were made by the Umayyads and Abbasids, but the massive Theodosian Walls and the Byzantine mastery of naval warfare, including the devastating Greek Fire, repelled all attackers.

## Mehmed's Preparation

Sultan Mehmed II, who ascended the throne at the age of nineteen, made the conquest of Constantinople his primary objective. His preparations were meticulous:

- He commissioned the construction of Rumeli Hisari, a fortress on the European shore of the Bosphorus, cutting off Constantinople's access to the Black Sea
- He assembled an army of approximately 80,000 soldiers, including the elite Janissary corps
- He commissioned the Hungarian engineer Orban to cast massive cannons, including the legendary "Basilica," one of the largest cannons ever built at that time, capable of hurling stone balls weighing over 600 kilograms
- He built a fleet of over 100 ships to blockade the city from the sea

## The Siege

The siege began on April 6, 1453. The Byzantine Emperor Constantine XI Palaiologos commanded a garrison of approximately 7,000 defenders, supplemented by Genoese and Venetian volunteers. The Theodosian Walls, which had protected the city for a thousand years, were subjected to sustained bombardment from the Ottoman cannons.

One of the most remarkable episodes of the siege occurred when Mehmed ordered his ships to be transported overland across the Galata peninsula on greased wooden rails, bypassing the chain barrier that blocked the Golden Horn. This feat of engineering caught the defenders by surprise and opened a new front.

After 53 days of siege, multiple assaults, and intense fighting, the final assault came in the early hours of May 29. The Ottoman forces breached the walls at several points. Emperor Constantine XI died fighting, reportedly charging into the oncoming Ottoman soldiers.

## Mehmed's Entry

When Mehmed entered the city, he rode to the Hagia Sophia, the greatest church in Christendom, and ordered it converted into a mosque. He performed two rak'ahs of prayer there. However, he also issued orders that gave significant protections to the Christian population:

- The Greek Orthodox Patriarch was confirmed in his position with authority over the Christian community
- Christians were guaranteed freedom of worship
- The city's infrastructure was to be preserved, not destroyed

Mehmed declared Constantinople the new capital of the Ottoman Empire, renaming it Islambol (later Istanbul). He repopulated the city by encouraging settlement from across the empire, including Muslims, Christians, and Jews.

## Impact

The conquest sent shockwaves across Europe and was celebrated throughout the Muslim world. It gave the Ottoman Empire control of the strategic crossroads between Europe and Asia, securing their position as the dominant power in the region for centuries.

Constantinople, now Istanbul, became one of the great cities of the Islamic world. The Ottomans built magnificent mosques, including the Sultanahmet (Blue Mosque) and the Suleymaniye, transforming the cityscape while preserving much of its Byzantine heritage.

## Legacy

Mehmed II earned the title al-Fatih (the Conqueror) and is celebrated in Islamic tradition as the fulfiller of the Prophet's prophecy. The conquest of Constantinople marked the definitive end of the Byzantine Empire and the beginning of a new era in which the Ottoman Empire would serve as the preeminent Muslim power for over four centuries.
