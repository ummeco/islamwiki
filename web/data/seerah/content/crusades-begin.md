# The First Crusade and the Fall of Jerusalem

The First Crusade (490-492 AH / 1096-1099 CE) was a military expedition launched by Western European Christians to capture Jerusalem and the Holy Land from Muslim rule. It culminated in the fall of Jerusalem on the 22nd of Sha'ban, 492 AH (July 15, 1099 CE), an event that sent shockwaves through the Muslim world and initiated two centuries of intermittent warfare known as the Crusades.

## Background

In 463 AH (1071 CE), the Seljuk Turks defeated the Byzantine Empire at the Battle of Manzikert, opening Anatolia to Turkish settlement. The Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos subsequently appealed to Pope Urban II for military assistance. At the Council of Clermont in 1095 CE, the Pope called for a holy war to liberate Jerusalem from Muslim control, promising spiritual rewards to participants.

The Muslim world at this time was deeply fragmented. The Abbasid Caliphate held only nominal authority. The Seljuk sultanate had splintered into rival principalities. The Fatimid Caliphate in Egypt controlled Jerusalem but was militarily weak. Local Muslim rulers were often more concerned with fighting each other than with the distant European threat.

## The March East

Multiple armies from France, the Low Countries, southern Italy, and other parts of Europe converged on Constantinople before marching through Anatolia. Along the way, they captured the city of Antioch after a brutal eight-month siege in 1098 CE. The march was marked by atrocities against both Muslim and Jewish populations in the cities they passed through.

## The Fall of Jerusalem

The Crusaders reached Jerusalem in June 1099 CE and laid siege to the city, which was defended by a Fatimid garrison. On July 15, after five weeks of siege, the walls were breached. What followed was one of the most notorious massacres of the medieval period.

Contemporary accounts, both Muslim and Christian, describe horrific scenes:

- The Muslim population was slaughtered in the streets and in the Al-Aqsa Mosque
- The Jewish community, who had taken refuge in their synagogue, were burned alive
- Even Eastern Christians were not spared in the initial violence
- Estimates of the dead range from tens of thousands to the entire population of the city

The chronicler Ibn al-Athir wrote that the Crusaders killed more than 70,000 people in the Aqsa Mosque alone, including scholars and worshippers.

## Muslim Response

The initial Muslim reaction was one of shock and disbelief, but it was slow to translate into organized resistance. The political fragmentation of the Muslim world meant that no single ruler could mount an effective counterattack.

The first significant Muslim response came from Imad al-Din Zengi, the ruler of Mosul and Aleppo, who captured the Crusader County of Edessa in 539 AH (1144 CE). His son Nur al-Din continued the effort, uniting Syria under his rule and promoting the ideology of jihad against the Crusaders.

The ultimate response came from Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi (Saladin), who recaptured Jerusalem in 583 AH (1187 CE) at the Battle of Hattin, treating the defeated Christians with a mercy that stood in stark contrast to the Crusaders' behavior in 1099.

## Legacy

The Crusades left deep scars on Muslim collective memory. The fall of Jerusalem demonstrated the consequences of Muslim disunity and became a rallying point for calls to solidarity and resistance. The eventual Muslim reconquest under Saladin became a symbol of what could be achieved through unity and righteous leadership.

The Crusader presence in the Levant lasted approximately two centuries, ending with the fall of Acre in 690 AH (1291 CE) to the Mamluk Sultan al-Ashraf Khalil.
