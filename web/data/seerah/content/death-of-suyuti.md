# Imam al-Suyuti: The Last of the Great Polymath Scholars

## A Prodigy of Extraordinary Breadth

When Jalal al-Din Abd al-Rahman ibn Abi Bakr al-Suyuti died in Cairo in 911 AH (May 1505 CE), Islamic scholarship lost the most prolific author in its history. He had reportedly composed over 600 works — more than any other single Muslim scholar before or after. He was sixty-one years old, and for the last twenty years of his life he had largely withdrawn from public society, refusing to meet sultans, judges, or distinguished visitors, devoting himself entirely to scholarship in a simple room on the Nile island of Rawda near Cairo.

Al-Suyuti was born in Cairo in 849 AH (1445 CE), though his family had origins in Asyut (Suyut) in Upper Egypt — hence his name. His father, who was a scholar of some standing, died when al-Suyuti was only five years old. Thereafter he was raised among books: as a child, he reportedly memorized the Quran by age six or seven, then moved through the basic texts of Arabic grammar and fiqh with remarkable speed. By his own account, he had memorized approximately 200,000 hadiths by the time he reached scholarly maturity — a claim that his contemporaries regarded as plausible given the depth of his subsequent citation of hadith literature.

He studied under over 150 scholars, taking knowledge from every significant teacher available in Cairo during his formative years. His primary area was the hadith sciences, but his learning encompassed Arabic language and grammar (he was a major grammarian in the Suyuti tradition), Shafi'i fiqh, Quranic sciences, history, and what he called "rational sciences" — logic and Sufi doctrine.

## The Works That Defined a Career

Al-Suyuti's productivity was not merely quantitative. His most significant works represent genuine intellectual contributions that have served Islamic scholarship for over five centuries.

Al-Itqan fi Ulum al-Quran (Mastery in the Sciences of the Quran) is generally considered his masterwork in terms of theoretical importance. An encyclopedic treatment in eighty chapters covering every aspect of Quranic sciences — the revelation's circumstances, its collection and transmission, its variant readings, its internal organization, its linguistic features, its interpretation — al-Itqan became the standard reference work for the field. Anyone studying Quranic sciences seriously reads al-Itqan as a foundational text. It synthesizes all previous scholarship on the subject and adds al-Suyuti's own analysis and organization, presenting a comprehensive map of a vast field.

Tafsir al-Jalalayn (The Commentary of the Two Jalals) occupies a unique place in Islamic education. Jalal al-Din al-Mahalli (d. 864 AH) had begun a commentary on the Quran but died after completing only half. Al-Suyuti completed the other half, reportedly in forty days, and the combined work became the most widely used Quran commentary in the Islamic world for teaching purposes. It is concise — typically one page of commentary for one page of Quran — written in clear classical Arabic that itself serves as a teaching text for Arabic students. It has been memorized, in whole or in part, by hundreds of thousands of students across the centuries.

His hadith collections — al-Jami' al-Saghir (The Small Collection) and al-Jami' al-Kabir (The Large Collection) — arranged prophetic hadiths alphabetically by opening word, making them convenient reference works for scholars checking citations. Al-Jami' al-Saghir became the basis for the important commentary Fayd al-Qadir by al-Munawi (d. 1031 AH), and together they constitute a major strand of accessible hadith scholarship.

Tadrib al-Rawi fi Sharh Taqrib al-Nawawi (The Training of the Transmitter in Explanation of al-Nawawi's Taqrib) is his comprehensive manual on hadith methodology — one of the most complete treatments of the science of hadith classification, transmission rules, and narrator evaluation available in accessible form. It remains a standard textbook in Islamic studies curricula.

His historical works include Tarikh al-Khulafa (History of the Caliphs), a comprehensive account of Islamic caliphs from Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) through the Mamluk-era shadow caliphs, and Husn al-Muhadara fi Tarikh Misr wal-Qahira (Beautiful Discourse on the History of Egypt and Cairo), which is the primary source for the intellectual and institutional history of Mamluk Egypt.

## The Claim to Mujaddidhood

One of the most discussed aspects of al-Suyuti's biography is his claim to be the mujaddid (reviver) of the 10th Islamic century. This claim is based on the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ: "Allah will raise for this Ummah at the head of every hundred years someone who will renew (yujaddid) its religion for it." (Narrated by Abu Dawud and authenticated by scholars.)

Al-Suyuti calculated that he was born at the head of the 10th Islamic century (849 AH is close enough to 900 AH for his argument, and his scholarship reached its mature phase around the turn of the century) and that his prolific output was evidence of this special divine appointment. He stated this claim in writing, in works explicitly dedicated to defending it.

His contemporaries were divided. Some accepted the claim with respect; others — including some senior scholars — thought it reflected excessive self-regard for a Muslim scholar. The Ahl us-Sunnah understanding is that the office of mujaddid is a description, not a self-designation: one cannot reliably know whether one is the mujaddid any more than one can reliably know one's own spiritual station. That said, the substance of al-Suyuti's claim — that his scholarship represented a significant renewal and systematization of Islamic knowledge at the turn of the 10th century — has a reasonable basis in the historical record.

## Withdrawal from Public Life

In approximately 876 AH (1472 CE), when al-Suyuti was in his late twenties, he had already established himself as a major Shafi'i scholar and hadith expert. His public career had included the prestigious position of teaching hadith at the Baybarsiyya madrasa in Cairo, one of the most significant scholarly appointments in the Mamluk capital.

But around 906 AH (1500 CE), he made the unusual decision to completely withdraw from public life. He refused to meet with the Mamluk Sultan Qansuh al-Ghawri, who requested his presence at court. He turned away high-ranking officials, judges, and visiting scholars. He retired to his room on the island of Rawda and spent his remaining years in continuous writing and worship.

His explanation, preserved by his student al-Sha'rani, was that he had come to believe that the company of rulers and their associates was spiritually corrupting, that the time spent in court was time stolen from scholarship and worship, and that the scholar's greatest contribution was in his books and in his prayers rather than in political influence. He is reported to have said that he had died to the world in order to live more fully in knowledge.

This withdrawal is reminiscent of other great scholars who deliberately stepped back from worldly engagement in their maturity: al-Ghazali's famous retreat from Baghdad, Ibn al-Jawzi's periods of withdrawal, Shah Waliullah Dehlawi's seclusion. For al-Suyuti, the decision appears to have been principled and consistent, not a response to any particular crisis.

He composed many of his most important works during these final fifteen years of withdrawal, writing by candlelight in his simple room, supported by the income from his scholarly positions (which continued even without his physical attendance) and from the sale of his handwritten copies of his own works.

## The Prophetic Visions

Al-Suyuti claimed to have seen the Prophet ﷺ in his sleep over seventy times. He compiled and narrated these experiences in a small work, and they formed part of his autobiographical self-presentation. In Islamic tradition, seeing the Prophet ﷺ in a dream (ru'ya) is considered a genuine encounter, since the Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever sees me in a dream has truly seen me, for Satan cannot take on my form." (Narrated by al-Bukhari and Muslim.)

Dream encounters with the Prophet ﷺ cannot override the Shariah or add to it, but they are regarded as spiritually significant experiences. Al-Suyuti's claim to numerous such encounters was consistent with his self-image as a scholar of special standing. Al-Sha'rani, his student and the most important early source for his biography, recorded these claims with evident sympathy.

## The Ahl us-Sunnah Assessment

Al-Suyuti's scholarship is enormous and invaluable. His al-Itqan remains the standard reference for Quranic sciences. His tafsir al-Jalalayn has introduced millions of students to classical Quranic commentary. His hadith collections and methodology texts continue to be used in Islamic studies curricula worldwide.

Some scholars of his own time criticized his approach as more compilatory than original — that he was a master assembler of earlier scholarship rather than a genuinely original thinker. This criticism has merit in some fields (his fiqh works are largely derivative of Shafi'i tradition without significant original contribution) but less merit in others (his organizational frameworks for Quranic sciences and his synthesis of hadith methodology show genuine intellectual creativity).

The claim to be the mujaddid and the self-presentation as a scholar of exceptional spiritual standing attracted skepticism from some contemporaries. However, what matters for posterity is not al-Suyuti's assessment of himself but the actual service his works have rendered to the Ummah — a service that is beyond dispute.

## Death and Legacy

Al-Suyuti died on 19 Jumada al-Ula 911 AH (October 17, 1505 CE) on the island of Rawda, as he had lived — in voluntary simplicity, surrounded by books, withdrawn from the affairs of the powerful. He was buried near his home.

Al-Sha'rani, who had studied under him and who is the primary biographical source, described a quiet death: the scholar who had refused audiences with sultans and who had spent his final years between prayer and writing simply stopped breathing one night. His books — hundreds of them — survived him. Many are still read and taught today.

The legacy of al-Suyuti is the legacy of the great tradition of Islamic scholarly compilation and synthesis. He stood at the end of the classical period of Islamic scholarship — after him, the tradition continued but the great age of original systematic scholarship had largely passed. In this sense, his claim to be the "last of the great polymaths" is not self-aggrandizement but a historically perceptive observation about his own position at the closing of an era.
