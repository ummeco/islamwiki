# The Battle of Uhud

## The Mountain That Tested the Community

The Battle of Uhud — fought on the fifteenth of Shawwal, 3 AH (approximately March 625 CE) — is the defining test of the early Muslim community's faith under adversity. At Badr the year before, the Muslims had experienced the intoxication of an improbable victory against a larger force. At Uhud, they experienced the bitterness of a partial defeat caused, the Quran would explain directly, by disobedience to the Prophet's ﷺ command at a critical moment. Uhud was not a Muslim defeat in the final military sense — the Qurayshi army withdrew without achieving its objective of eliminating the Muslim community — but it was a day of severe loss, of the martyrdom of Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib, of the wounding of the Prophet ﷺ himself, and of the most direct Quranic reflection on what it means to endure the consequences of one's own mistakes.

## The Qurayshi Response to Badr

The Quraysh had lost seventy killed at Badr, including their most prominent leaders. Abu Sufyan ibn Harb, who had survived Badr and now led the Quraysh, organized a retaliatory expedition in the following year. The Qurayshi army assembled approximately 3,000 fighters — a far more carefully prepared force than the one that had gone to Badr. Abu Sufyan's wife Hind bint Utbah, who had lost her father Utbah ibn Rabia and her brother Walid at Badr, accompanied the army. She had hired Wahshi ibn Harb, an Abyssinian javelin specialist, specifically to kill Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib — who had killed Utbah at Badr. The Qurayshi army moved north toward Medina.

## The Debate About Strategy

The Prophet ﷺ was inclined to defend Medina from within its walls — a defensive strategy that would have neutralized the Qurayshi cavalry advantage. The senior companions and veterans supported him in this. But the young companions who had not fought at Badr pressed urgently for going out to meet the Quraysh in open battle, fearing that staying inside Medina would look like cowardice. The Prophet ﷺ, after Friday prayer, came out wearing his armor — having accepted the majority opinion — and the army set out. The young companions who had pressed for going out realized, once the Prophet ﷺ had made his decision, that they had forced a course of action against his judgment. They said: "It is not for us to contradict you." The Prophet ﷺ said: "It is not fitting for a prophet, once he has put on his armor, to take it off until Allah has judged between him and his enemies."

## The Archers on the Hill

The Prophet ﷺ stationed fifty archers under Abdullah ibn Jubayr on a small hill on the Muslim left flank — Mount Ainain — with explicit instructions: "Protect our backs. If you see us being slaughtered, do not leave your position. If you see us winning, do not leave your position." The instruction was absolute and unambiguous.

The initial phase of the battle went well for the Muslims. The Qurayshi formation was broken; the Muslims penetrated their lines. The Qurayshi camp was briefly overrun. Seeing what appeared to be a total Muslim victory, the majority of the archers on the hill abandoned their position, descending to collect the spoils. Approximately ten archers, under Abdullah ibn Jubayr's command, remained on the hill, following their orders.

Khalid ibn al-Walid — commanding the Qurayshi cavalry, not yet a Muslim, the military genius who would later become the greatest commander of early Islam — saw the abandoned hill. He led his cavalry around the flank, up the hill, killed the remaining archers, and attacked the Muslim force from the rear. The tide turned instantly. What had been a Muslim advance became a rout. The Muslim formation collapsed. The shout went through the Qurayshi army: "Muhammad has been killed." It was not true, but it produced panic and disorientation in the Muslim ranks that compounded the disaster.

## The Prophet ﷺ Is Wounded

The Prophet ﷺ was not killed, but he was wounded. A stone struck his face, breaking or cracking his front teeth and splitting his lower lip. Another blow wounded his cheek and drove links of his helmet chain into his face. The companions around him — including Abu Bakr, Ali, Talha ibn Ubaydullah (who shielded the Prophet ﷺ with his hand until the tendons of his fingers were severed), Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, and others — fought to protect him. The Prophet ﷺ climbed to higher ground. The Qurayshi army, believing him dead, did not pursue.

When the Prophet ﷺ wiped the blood from his face, he said: "How can a people prosper who have bloodied the face of their prophet while he calls them to their Lord?" Allah revealed in response: "You have no decision in the matter — whether He relents toward them or punishes them, for they are indeed wrongdoers." (Surah Ali Imran, 3:128)

## The Aftermath and the Quranic Response

The Qurayshi army withdrew without destroying Medina or the Muslim community. They had inflicted significant losses — seventy Muslims were martyred at Uhud, including Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib — but they had not achieved a decisive result. Abu Sufyan called out toward the Muslim position: "We shall meet you again at Badr next year." The Prophet ﷺ replied: "That is our appointment."

Hind bint Utbah, after the battle, mutilated Hamza's body — cutting off his nose and ears, splitting open his chest, and cutting out his liver, which she attempted to chew (but could not swallow). When the Prophet ﷺ saw what had been done to his uncle's body, he wept more openly than at almost any other point in the Medinan battles. He said he would show no mercy in retaliation to what had been done — but the revelation commanded restraint.

Surah Ali Imran (3:121-200) is the comprehensive Quranic commentary on Uhud. It is direct about the cause of the partial defeat: "What struck you on the day the two armies met — it was by permission of Allah, so that He might test the believers. And that He might make evident those who are hypocrites." The disobedience of the archers is addressed: "You had certainly desired this worldly life before." But the Quran also provides the framework of endurance: "Do not weaken and do not grieve, and you will be superior if you are true believers." (3:139)

Uhud taught the Muslim community the permanent lesson that military success is conditional — conditional on obedience to the Prophet ﷺ, on discipline in battle, and on freedom from the distractions of worldly gain. It also taught that defeat, when it comes, is not the end: the community that endures, repents, and recalibrates is the community that ultimately prevails.
