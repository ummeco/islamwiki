# Military Expeditions After the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) established a ten-year truce between the Muslim community and the Quraysh of Mecca — but it also freed the Muslim military from the immediate threat of Meccan attack, allowing the Prophet to direct military operations in other directions. The period from 6 to 8 AH saw a significant expansion of Muslim military activity beyond the Meccan theater.

## The Strategic Effect of Hudaybiyyah

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah was understood by the Prophet as a strategic victory, even though its terms appeared unfavorable to many companions. By establishing peace with Mecca, he freed Muslim military capacity for operations elsewhere. The Quran called it "a manifest victory" (Quran 48:1).

In the two-year period between the treaty (6 AH) and its violation (8 AH, when the Quraysh-allied Banu Bakr attacked Banu Khuza'ah), the Muslim forces conducted a series of significant operations.

## The Khaybar Campaign (7 AH)

The largest military operation of the post-Hudaybiyyah period was the conquest of Khaybar — the major oasis settlement approximately 150 km north of Medina that was the center of the Banu Nadir and Banu Qaynuqa communities after their expulsion from Medina. Huyayy ibn Akhtab and other leaders of the anti-Muslim political network had settled there.

Khaybar was a fortified agricultural complex with multiple strongholds. The campaign took approximately twenty days of siege operations and field battles. The key breakthrough came when Ali ibn Abi Talib, who had been ill, was given the command and led the force that breached the main fortress. The Prophet's famous statement before handing the banner to Ali — "I will give it to one who loves Allah and His Messenger, and whom Allah and His Messenger love, and through whom Allah will grant victory" — led to one of the most studied hadith about Ali's status.

After the conquest, the Khaybar farmers — who were skilled agriculturalists — were allowed to remain on their lands as tenants under a sharecropping arrangement (muzara'ah), keeping half the produce and paying the other half to the Muslim state. This arrangement created an important legal precedent for Islamic agricultural contracts.

## The Expedition to Mu'tah (8 AH)

The Battle of Mu'tah (Jumada al-Awwal 8 AH) was the first direct military encounter between Muslim forces and Byzantine imperial power. The Prophet sent a force of 3,000 fighters to the Balqa' region of (present-day Jordan) under the command of Zayd ibn Haritha. Two co-commanders were designated: Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (the Prophet's cousin) and Abdullah ibn Rawaha.

They encountered a Byzantine-Ghassanid force estimated in the sources at 100,000 to 200,000 men — figures that, even allowing for significant exaggeration, indicate a massive numerical advantage. All three of the designated commanders were killed: Zayd, Ja'far, and Abdullah ibn Rawaha died in succession as each one took command. Khalid ibn al-Walid then took command by acclamation of the remaining troops and organized a strategic withdrawal, breaking contact with the enemy.

The Prophet, receiving news of the deaths through revelation before physical news could arrive, mourned: "Zayd took the banner and was killed, then Ja'far took it and was killed, then Abdullah ibn Rawaha took it and was killed." He wept for them. He said of Ja'far: "Ja'far flew in Paradise with two wings of ruby, bloodstained."

Khalid's management of the withdrawal — transforming an apparent defeat into a controlled retreat that preserved the force — earned him the title Sayf Allah al-Maslul (the Drawn Sword of Allah) from the Prophet.

## The Dhat al-Salasil Expedition

In 8 AH, the Prophet sent Amr ibn al-As toward the Syrian-Arabian frontier to visit tribes there and explore alliance possibilities or prevent hostile tribal coalitions from forming. The operation was delicate diplomatically — Amr's mother was from the relevant tribes and he had existing relationships there.

## Smaller Operations

Dozens of smaller expeditions (saraya — small raiding parties sent without the Prophet) and ghazwat (expeditions led by the Prophet himself) occurred during this period. They served to:
- Extend Muslim influence across the Arabian Peninsula
- Intercept supply and communication routes of hostile tribes
- Secure agreements with individual tribal leaders
- Gather intelligence

## The Political Effect

The combined effect of these operations was the steady consolidation of Muslim power across Arabia. By the time the Hudaybiyyah truce was violated in 8 AH and the Prophet prepared to march on Mecca, the Muslim community had become the dominant military power in the region — capable of fielding an army of 10,000 for the Mecca campaign.

## Sources

The individual expeditions are documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi* (which covers the saraya and ghazwat in the most detail).
