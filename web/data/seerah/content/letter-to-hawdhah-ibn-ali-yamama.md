# The Prophet's Letter to Hawdhah ibn Ali of Yamama

Among the letters sent by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) to regional rulers in 7 AH was a letter to Hawdhah ibn Ali — the ruler of the Yamama region in central Arabia, who had been a client king operating under Sassanid Persian patronage. The outcome of this letter was negative, and the Prophet's response to Hawdhah's counteroffer became a statement that defined the non-negotiability of the prophetic mission.

## Hawdhah ibn Ali

Hawdhah ibn Ali was a chief (or king) of the Banu Hanifah tribe in the Yamama region — the same tribal confederation from which the false prophet Musaylimah would later emerge. Hawdhah was a Christian Arab who had cultivated connections with the Sassanid Persian Empire, receiving material support and political recognition from Ctesiphon. He was one of the most powerful local rulers in the Arabian Peninsula who was not aligned with either Mecca or Medina.

## The Letter

The Prophet sent Sulait ibn Amr al-Amiri as his messenger to Hawdhah carrying the standard letter of invitation to Islam. The letter followed the format of the other royal letters.

## Hawdhah's Response

Hawdhah's response was essentially a conditional acceptance with an unacceptable condition. He replied that he was willing to consider the Prophet's message, but on one condition: that he be granted a share of authority after the Prophet — that he be given governance or co-rulership over part of the Muslim territories.

When the messenger returned and delivered this condition to the Prophet, the Prophet's response was clear and definitive:

*"Had he asked me for even a palm grove, I would not have given it to him. His matter (mulk, kingdom) shall perish."*

The rejection was absolute: the prophetic mission was not a political negotiation. Prophethood could not be parceled out in exchange for political loyalty. Authority in the Muslim community derived from faith and merit, not from bargaining.

## The Prophetic Statement's Fulfillment

The Prophet's statement that Hawdhah's kingdom would perish was fulfilled quickly. Hawdhah died — according to the accounts — very shortly after this exchange, around the time of the treaty of Hudaybiyyah or shortly thereafter. His death removed him from the political scene before the Riddah Wars, and the Yamama region, while it later became a center of the Musaylimah false-prophet movement, was brought under Muslim control after the Battle of Yamama in 11 AH.

## Significance

**The non-negotiability of prophethood.** Hawdhah's request for political power-sharing and the Prophet's categorical refusal established a principle: the prophetic mission was not a political enterprise to be negotiated but a divine commission. The Prophet could make treaties, agreements, and political accommodations — but the terms of the prophetic call itself were not negotiable.

**Contrast with other responses.** Hawdhah's conditional response contrasts with al-Mundhir ibn Sawa (Bahrain), who accepted Islam genuinely, and with Heraclius (Byzantium), who was respectful but noncommittal. Hawdhah's attempt to extract political concessions in exchange for religious acknowledgment represented a third, hybrid response — and it was firmly rejected.

**The Yamama region's later history.** Yamama would become historically significant as the base of Musaylimah's false-prophet movement, and the Battle of Yamama — the decisive battle of the Riddah Wars — would take place in the same region. The refusal of Hawdhah and the subsequent events in Yamama are sometimes read as a connected narrative: the region's tribal leadership's reluctance to accept Islam without political concessions foreshadowed the larger crisis that followed the Prophet's death.

## Sources

The account of the letter to Hawdhah ibn Ali is preserved in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*.
