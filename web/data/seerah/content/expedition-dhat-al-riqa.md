# Expedition of Dhat al-Riqa'

The Expedition of Dhat al-Riqa' (the Expedition of the Patched Garments) occurred in Rabi al-Awwal or Jumada al-Awwal, 4 AH — a campaign by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) against the tribes of Ghatafan and their allies in the Najd region who had been gathering forces against the Muslim community. The expedition is particularly remembered in Islamic jurisprudence for the first-time performance of the prayer of fear (salat al-khawf).

## Name and Route

The expedition takes its name from a location called Dhat al-Riqa' ("the place of the patches") — possibly named for the patched appearance of the terrain (variegated earth and rocks), or alternatively for the patched garments of the soldiers, who had worn through their sandals and wrapped their feet in cloth and patches during the difficult mountain march.

The Prophet led approximately 400 (or 700, by other accounts) men into the Najd — the inland plateau of the Arabian Peninsula — toward the territories of the Banu Muharib and Banu Tha'labah of the Ghatafan confederation.

## The Campaign

The tribal forces did not stand and fight but withdrew into the mountains as the Muslim army advanced. There were no major battles. The expedition was more a show of force and deterrence than a fought engagement — demonstrating Muslim military reach into tribal Arabia and discouraging the formation of hostile coalitions.

## Salat al-Khawf (The Prayer of Fear)

The expedition's lasting legal significance lies in the performance of salat al-khawf — the shortened and split prayer performed when under threat of enemy attack. The Muslim force was in proximity to a potentially hostile tribal presence, and conducting the full congregational prayer would have left the force vulnerable.

The Prophet organized the prayer with the force divided into two groups:
- One group prayed one rak'ah with the Prophet while the other faced the enemy
- The first group then moved to face the enemy while the second group joined the Prophet for a rak'ah
- Each group then completed the remaining rak'ah independently

The Quran provides the basis for this prayer arrangement in Surah al-Nisa (4:101-103): "And when you travel throughout the land, there is no blame upon you for shortening the prayer, if you fear that those who disbelieve may disrupt [or attack] you."

The prayer of fear became one of the subjects of most extensive jurisprudential discussion in Islamic law — the four major Sunni schools have somewhat different procedures based on which hadith account they prioritize, and the discussion of salat al-khawf occupies significant space in every major fiqh manual.

## The Encounter of Jabir ibn Abdullah

Among the most famous personal anecdotes from this expedition involves Jabir ibn Abdullah al-Ansari. He had come to the expedition on an old, weak camel that was struggling to keep up with the march. The Prophet noticed and asked about it. Jabir said it was his camel. The Prophet prodded it, supplicated, and the camel became so energetic it pulled ahead of the whole army.

The Prophet later purchased the camel from Jabir for one dinar (or a small number of dinars), and then — upon reaching Medina — gave Jabir the camel back, telling him the money and the camel were both his. The encounter illustrates the Prophet's personal attention to his companions' circumstances and his characteristic generosity.

## Sources

The Dhat al-Riqa' expedition is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi and Kitab al-Khawf) and Sahih Muslim (Kitab Salat al-Musafirin), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*. The salat al-khawf hadith from this expedition are central texts in Islamic jurisprudence.
