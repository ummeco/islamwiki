# Expedition of Hamzah to the Coastal Route

The expedition of Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib to the coastal route (the Eis coastal road, near the Red Sea coast south of al-Juhayna) in Ramadan 1 AH (approximately February or March 623 CE) is considered the first armed expedition in Islamic history — predating the Battle of Badr by approximately a year and a half. Though it produced no fighting, it established the pattern for subsequent Muslim military operations and demonstrated that the new Muslim community in Medina was prepared to defend its interests.

## Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib

Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib was the Prophet's paternal uncle and one of the physically most formidable companions — a man known as the "Lion of Allah and the Lion of His Messenger" (Asad Allah wa asad Rasulihi). He had accepted Islam with characteristic directness: when he heard that Abu Jahl had insulted the Prophet, he went directly to the mosque, struck Abu Jahl with his bow, and declared his Islam then and there.

He was one of the Prophet's most trusted and capable companions. His leadership of the first expedition reflected his status.

## The Expedition

The Prophet sent Hamzah with thirty Muhajirun to intercept a Qurayshi trading caravan. The caravan, carrying goods from Syria, was traveling along the Red Sea coastal road under the leadership of Abu Jahl ibn Hisham with approximately 300 Qurayshi fighters as escort.

The Muslim party was significantly outnumbered — thirty men against 300. They reached the coastal area and prepared to confront the caravan.

## The Non-Encounter

Before any engagement, Majdi ibn Amr al-Juhani — the chief of the Juhayna tribe, who had treaties with both sides — intervened and mediated between the two groups. The Qurayshi caravan continued on its way and the Muslim force returned to Medina without fighting.

Whether Hamzah's force would have attacked despite the numerical disadvantage, or whether the confrontation was primarily intended as a demonstration of presence and capability, is debated in the sources. What is clear is that the expedition was authorized by the Prophet and organized as a military operation — regardless of its peaceful outcome.

## Significance

**The first authorized military expedition.** The expedition of Hamzah established that the Muslim community in Medina was prepared to project military force in defense of its interests — the ability to interrupt Qurayshi trade was one of the community's few points of leverage against an enemy that controlled the Meccan sanctuary and the pilgrimage economy.

**The legal foundation of early Islamic military operations.** In Islamic jurisprudence, the early Medinan expeditions established the legal and ethical framework for Muslim military activity: authorization by the Prophet, defined objectives, rules of engagement, and accountability. Hamzah's expedition was authorized, targeted (a specific trading caravan of the enemy community), and proportionate.

**The economic dimension of the conflict with Mecca.** The Qurayshi trading caravans to Syria were the economic lifeblood of Mecca. The Muslim community's ability to threaten these routes gave it economic leverage in a conflict where Mecca's military superiority was otherwise overwhelming. This strategic logic would eventually lead to the Battle of Badr — where the interception of a major Qurayshi trading caravan led to the confrontation that defined the early Muslim community's relationship with its enemies.

## The Subsequent Expeditions

The expedition of Hamzah was followed quickly by other early expeditions: the expedition of Ubaydah ibn al-Harith to Rabiigh, the expedition of Saad ibn Abi Waqqas to al-Kharrar, and others. Each extended the Muslim community's reach along the Qurayshi trade routes and built the military experience and organizational capacity that would prove decisive at Badr.

## Sources

The expedition of Hamzah to the coastal route is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya* (drawing on Ibn Ishaq), al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
