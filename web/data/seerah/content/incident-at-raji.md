# The Incident at al-Raji'

The massacre at al-Raji' in Safar 4 AH stands alongside the tragedy at Bi'r Ma'unah as one of the most devastating acts of treachery against the Muslim missionary community in the Medinan period. A small group of Muslim teachers — sent to instruct the tribes of the Hijaz in Islam — were ambushed and either killed or sold into captivity in Mecca, where two of them suffered ritual execution.

## The Invitation and the Betrayal

A delegation from the tribes of Adal and al-Qarah came to the Prophet and requested that he send teachers with them to instruct their people in Islam. The request appeared sincere — the Prophet had recently sent such missions to other tribes and was actively engaged in da'wah (invitation to Islam) across Arabia.

He sent a group of six (some accounts say ten) men, led by Marsad ibn Abi Marsad al-Ghanawi (or in some accounts, by Asim ibn Thabit al-Ansari). The group was small — appropriate for a teaching mission, not a military one.

When the group reached the well of al-Raji' (in the territory of Hudhail, near the Hijaz mountains), they were surrounded by approximately 100 armed men from the Banu Lihyan sub-tribe of Hudhail. The treachery was revealed: the Adal and al-Qarah men had brought them into an ambush.

## The Stand and the Martyrdoms

The Muslims realized what was happening. Some of the group retreated to a rocky hilltop to make their stand. The Hudhail men called up: "We promise you by Allah and covenant that if you surrender we will not kill any of you." Most refused to trust the offer.

Asim ibn Thabit al-Ansari responded: "By Allah, I will not accept the protection of a disbeliever today." He and those with him fought until they were killed — Asim and three others died on the hilltop.

Hudhail had placed a bounty on Asim — Sulaym ibn Khalid's mother had vowed to drink wine from Asim's skull because he had killed her two sons at Badr. The Hudhail men cut off his head to claim the bounty. The accounts record a miracle: a cloud of bees (or hornets) descended and protected his body, preventing them from mutilating it. A flood then carried the body away.

## The Captives: Khubaib and Zayd

Three men surrendered under the promise of safety: Khubaib ibn Adi al-Ansari, Zayd ibn al-Dathina al-Ansari, and Abdullah ibn Tariq. The men were taken captive and marched toward Mecca. On the journey, Abdullah ibn Tariq seized a chance and attempted to escape — he was pursued and killed.

Khubaib and Zayd were sold to Meccan families who sought vengeance for Badr losses. Zayd ibn al-Dathina was purchased by Safwan ibn Umayyah to avenge his father Umayyah ibn Khalaf, who had died at Badr. He was executed at Tan'im outside Mecca. Before his execution, Abu Sufyan asked him: "Would you not prefer that Muhammad were in your place and you were with your family?" Zayd replied: "By Allah, I would not even wish a thorn to prick the Prophet where he sits while I am with my family." Abu Sufyan reportedly said: "I have never seen anyone loved as Muhammad is loved by his companions."

Khubaib was purchased by the sons of al-Harith ibn Amir — one of whom he had killed at Badr. He was kept prisoner in Mecca for several months. The accounts describe him asking a woman of the household for a razor to clean himself before his execution; she gave it to him, then discovered that her infant had wandered onto his lap while he held the razor. Khubaib, despite his captive situation, had not harmed the child.

Before his execution at Tan'im, Khubaib performed two rak'ahs of prayer — establishing a Sunnah that Muslims pray before execution if they can. He then composed poetry:

*"I do not care, as long as I die as a Muslim,
On which side I fall, for the sake of Allah."*

He was killed by the Quraysh at Tan'im.

## The Prophet's Response and Legacy

The Prophet performed extended qunut al-nazilah prayers against the tribes responsible. The treachery at al-Raji' is cited in Islamic jurisprudence alongside Bi'r Ma'unah as evidence for the principle that breaking a promise of safe conduct (aman) is among the most serious violations of Islamic ethics in warfare.

Khubaib's prayer before execution became the normative practice: a Muslim condemned to death prays two rak'ahs before the execution is carried out. This practice is documented in Sahih Bukhari and has been preserved in Islamic legal tradition.

The incident also sharpened the Muslim community's wariness about missionary missions without adequate protection, contributing to a more cautious approach to unarmed da'wah expeditions in hostile territory.

## Sources

The incident at al-Raji' is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi, Kitab al-Jihad), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
