# The Story of Suhayl ibn Amr and His Journey to Islam

Suhayl ibn Amr al-Amiri was a senior leader of Quraysh — an eloquent speaker, a skilled negotiator, and for most of his adult life one of the Muslim community's most determined opponents. His journey from prominent Qurayshi leader to sincere Muslim is one of the less-celebrated but historically significant conversion narratives of the seerah.

## Suhayl ibn Amr as a Qurayshi Leader

Suhayl was known throughout Mecca for his eloquence — a prized quality in Arabian society, where the ability to speak persuasively was a mark of leadership. He represented Quraysh in the most significant negotiations with the Muslim community: it was he whom Quraysh sent to negotiate the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH), and the treaty's text records his name as the Qurayshi signatory.

During the Hudaybiyyah negotiations, Suhayl objected to the text opening with "In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate" and insisted on the pre-Islamic "In Your name, O Allah." He also refused to use the title "Muhammad, the Messenger of Allah," insisting on "Muhammad ibn Abdullah" — saying that if he recognized Muhammad as the Messenger of Allah, there would have been no need for a treaty. The Prophet accepted these terms over the objections of some companions.

This episode encapsulates Suhayl's character: principled, politically shrewd, and not moved by social pressure.

## The Conquest of Mecca and the Initial Exclusion

Suhayl was initially excluded from the general amnesty when Mecca was conquered in 8 AH. He was among those whose specific actions had put him in the list of those who could be killed even if found in the Masjid al-Haram. He went into hiding.

Umar ibn al-Khattab, with his characteristic directness, went to the Prophet and said: "O Messenger of Allah, Suhayl ibn Amr is here. Allow me to pull out his front teeth so that his tongue will hang out and he will never be able to give a speech against you again."

The Prophet refused: "I will not mutilate him — if I did, Allah would mutilate me even if I am a prophet. But perhaps he will stand in a position one day that will not cause you displeasure."

This prophetic statement proved prescient.

## The Acceptance of Islam

Suhayl ibn Amr accepted Islam after the conquest of Mecca — the exact timing is not entirely clear in the sources, with some accounts placing it at the conquest itself and others suggesting he came around more gradually. He was given the status of a newly accepted Muslim.

## The Day the Prophet Died

The prophetic statement about Suhayl standing in a position that would "not cause displeasure" was fulfilled at the moment of the Prophet's death. When the Prophet died and many Meccans began to waver — some discussing apostasy and the return of pre-Islamic customs — Suhayl ibn Amr gave a powerful speech in Mecca affirming Islam and condemning those who were considering abandoning it.

He said: "Whoever among you was worshipping Muhammad — Muhammad has died. But whoever was worshipping Allah — Allah is alive and does not die." He then urged the Meccans to remain firm in their Islam and to maintain their military readiness for the ongoing campaigns.

This speech — coming from a man who had been one of Islam's most capable opponents, whose words carried weight in Mecca precisely because of his pre-Islamic credibility — was a major factor in stabilizing Mecca at the moment of potential crisis.

## Subsequent Jihad

Suhayl ibn Amr went on to fight in the early Islamic conquests, including in Syria. The accounts suggest he fought with particular intensity — some Islamic historians read this as a sincere commitment to the religion he had accepted, others note that his fighting against Byzantine Syria was also fighting against those who had been Quraysh's old patrons.

He died during the plague of Amwas (18 AH / 639 CE) in Syria — the same plague that killed Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah and many senior companions. The plague deaths were considered martyrdom.

## Significance

**The Prophet's restraint with enemies.** The Prophet's refusal to allow Umar to mutilate Suhayl — even while acknowledging the harm Suhayl had caused — reflects the Prophet's consistent policy of not taking punitive action against converted enemies beyond what Islamic law required. The same principle governed the general amnesty at Mecca.

**The fulfillment of the prophetic statement.** Suhayl's stabilizing speech at the Prophet's death was the exact "position that will not cause you displeasure" that the Prophet had mentioned — a self-fulfilling prophecy grounded in the Prophet's perceptive reading of character.

**Conversion and credibility.** Suhayl's conversion mattered precisely because of who he had been. His eloquence and leadership standing, which had previously served Quraysh's opposition to Islam, became assets for the Muslim community after his conversion. The Prophet understood that reconciling capable opponents — rather than destroying or humiliating them — built a stronger community.

## Sources

The account of Suhayl ibn Amr is in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and related narrations in the major hadith collections.
