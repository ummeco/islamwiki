# The Afghan Jihad Against the Soviet Union: Faith, Geopolitics, and Consequences

## The Soviet Invasion

On December 25, 1979 CE (15 Muharram 1400 AH), Soviet troops crossed into Afghanistan in a large-scale military operation that would mark the beginning of one of the defining conflicts of the late 20th century. The Soviet Union, which had been backing the People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan since its coup in April 1978 (the Saur Revolution), intervened after the situation had become unstable — competing factions within the Communist government had purged one another, and a grassroots Islamic resistance movement was growing. Soviet planners expected a rapid stabilization operation. Instead, they entered a ten-year war they could not win.

Afghanistan at the time had a population of approximately 15 million people, predominantly Sunni Muslim with a Hazara Shia minority. The country was tribal, rural, and deeply religious — a society that had repelled British imperial expeditions in the 19th century and maintained fierce resistance to outside interference throughout its modern history. The Communist government's radical programs — land redistribution, forced literacy campaigns (particularly for women), abolition of bride prices — had alienated the tribal and religious establishment and provoked rural uprisings across the country even before the Soviet invasion.

The Soviet-installed government of Babrak Karmal was no more legitimate in Afghan eyes than its predecessors. For the Afghan Muslim population, the conflict had a clear character: an atheist Communist superpower had invaded a Muslim land. The response was framed from the beginning in the language of jihad.

## The Afghan Mujahideen

The resistance fighters who opposed the Soviet occupation called themselves mujahideen — those who engage in jihad. The term was apt from an Islamic legal perspective: defending Muslim land from non-Muslim invasion is one of the clearest cases of fard 'ayn (individual obligation) jihad in classical Islamic jurisprudence. Scholars across the Muslim world, including Saudi Grand Mufti Abd al-Aziz ibn Baz, issued fatwas affirming the obligatory character of supporting the Afghan jihad.

The mujahideen were not a unified force. They were organized into multiple parties and commanders with different tribal bases, regional loyalties, and ideological orientations. Among the major parties based in Peshawar, Pakistan:

The **Hizb-i-Islami** under Gulbuddin Hekmatyar was the most hardline Islamist group, with organizational ties to Islamist movements across the Muslim world. The **Jamiat-i-Islami** under Burhanuddin Rabbani, with its military commander Ahmad Shah Massoud (the "Lion of the Panjshir"), was a more moderate Islamist force with a strong base in northern Afghanistan. The **Ittihad-i-Islami** under Abd al-Rabb al-Rasul Sayyaf had close ties to Saudi Arabia and embodied Salafi-influenced theology. The Haqqani network, led by Jalaluddin Haqqani — a formidable commander in the eastern provinces — was effective and widely supported.

Ahmad Shah Massoud deserves particular mention. His defense of the Panjshir Valley against seven major Soviet offensives became legendary. Massoud was a capable military commander and a man of genuine religious conviction who, unlike some of his contemporaries, attempted to maintain ethical standards in warfare and had a vision for a post-Soviet Afghanistan grounded in Islamic principles with democratic consultation. His assassination on September 9, 2001 — two days before the attacks on the United States — eliminated one of the most important potential counterweights to Taliban rule.

## The International Dimension: CIA, Saudi Arabia, and Pakistan

The Afghan jihad was not only an Afghan struggle — it became one of the major Cold War proxy conflicts, and its internationalization had consequences that extended far beyond Afghanistan.

The Carter administration in the United States authorized covert support for the mujahideen beginning before the Soviet invasion (National Security Advisor Zbigniew Brzezinski later acknowledged that covert CIA support began in July 1979, with the deliberate aim of provoking Soviet intervention). The Reagan administration significantly escalated this support under a program managed by the CIA and the Pakistani intelligence service (ISI). At its peak, the CIA was channeling approximately $3 billion per year in weapons, funding, and training to the mujahideen — matched dollar for dollar by Saudi Arabia.

Pakistan, under President Zia ul-Haq, was the essential intermediary. Zia's own religious commitments aligned him with the jihad ideologically, and Pakistan's strategic interest in preventing Soviet expansion to its borders made him a natural partner. The ISI managed the distribution of weapons and funds to the Afghan factions, which allowed it to favor factions it considered most aligned with Pakistani interests.

Saudi Arabia's financial support was driven by both Cold War anti-Communism and genuine Islamic solidarity. The Afghan jihad was framed as a religious duty, and Saudi financing — both official government funds and private donations from wealthy Gulf citizens — was substantial.

The weapons provided to the mujahideen escalated over time. The most consequential was the introduction in 1986 of the shoulder-fired Stinger anti-aircraft missile, which neutralized Soviet air superiority and proved decisive in denying the Red Army the ability to use helicopter gunships effectively.

## The Arab Volunteers and the Seeds of Future Conflict

One of the most consequential developments in the Afghan jihad was the arrival of Arab volunteers — Muslims from across the Arab world who came to Afghanistan to participate in the struggle against the Soviet occupation. The estimated number ranges from 10,000 to 35,000 individuals over the course of the war, drawn from Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Jordan, Algeria, and other countries.

The infrastructure for recruiting and supporting Arab volunteers was organized primarily through the Maktab al-Khidmat (Services Bureau) established in Peshawar in 1984 by the Palestinian scholar Abdullah Azzam and his young Saudi colleague Osama bin Laden. Azzam — a graduate of al-Azhar and the most influential ideological voice calling Muslims to Afghanistan — argued that participating in the Afghan jihad was a personal religious obligation for every capable Muslim, regardless of nationality.

Abdullah Azzam's contribution to the jihad was genuine in its motivations: he was a scholar who believed sincerely in the obligation to defend Muslim lands. But the organizational networks he helped create, the theology of offensive global jihad he began to develop in his later writings (particularly the concept of jihad as the restoration of every formerly Muslim land), and the human networks that formed among the Arab volunteers would outlast the Afghan conflict and reconfigure themselves into what became al-Qaeda.

Osama bin Laden's trajectory illustrates the transformation: he came to Afghanistan as a financier and organizer, not primarily a fighter, motivated by what he understood as genuine religious duty. The experience of the jihad — and particularly the belief that Muslim volunteers had played a decisive role in defeating a superpower — inflated his sense of what small committed Muslim groups could achieve against any enemy, including the United States, which he came to see as a new imperial power occupying Muslim lands (particularly Saudi Arabia, where US troops were stationed beginning in 1990).

## The Soviet Withdrawal and Afghan Collapse

The Soviet Union withdrew its forces from Afghanistan between May 1988 and February 1989, under the Geneva Accords mediated by the United Nations. The withdrawal was the result of a combination of military exhaustion, economic strain, and the political reforms of Mikhail Gorbachev's perestroika era. The Red Army had suffered approximately 15,000 soldiers killed and over 35,000 wounded. Afghan civilian and military casualties were far higher — estimates range from 1 to 2 million dead, with 5-6 million refugees who had fled to Pakistan and Iran.

The mujahideen's victory over the Soviet Union was experienced in the Muslim world as a remarkable vindication — a relatively small Muslim community, armed by outside powers but motivated by faith, had driven out one of the world's two superpowers. The victory fed a significant revival of Islamic confidence and political Islam across the Muslim world.

What followed the Soviet withdrawal was, however, catastrophic for Afghanistan itself. The Communist government in Kabul survived until 1992, then collapsed. The mujahideen factions, deprived of a common enemy, turned on one another. The civil war that followed from 1992 to 1996 destroyed much of Kabul and killed tens of thousands. The Pashtun Taliban movement, which emerged from the madrasas of Pakistan in 1994, swept to power in 1996 on a platform of restoring order and Shariah governance.

The United States, having achieved its Cold War objective of exhausting the Soviet Union, withdrew most of its engagement with Afghanistan after 1989. The CIA's covert support programs were ended; reconstruction assistance was minimal. This abandonment was later recognized as a catastrophic policy failure — one that left a shattered country to slide into civil war and eventually become a base for al-Qaeda.

## The Islamic Scholarly Perspective

The Afghan jihad of 1979-1989 was almost universally recognized by mainstream Islamic scholars as a legitimate defensive jihad. The fatwa of Sheikh Ibn Baz and other scholars affirming the obligation to support the mujahideen reflected the classical Islamic legal position: when a Muslim land is invaded by a non-Muslim power, its defense becomes obligatory on the Muslim community.

The complications arose in the aftermath:

The descent into civil war among factions who had fought together raised difficult questions about the boundaries of legitimate warfare. The Taliban's severe interpretation of Shariah — particularly their treatment of women, their destruction of pre-Islamic heritage (the Bamiyan Buddhas), and their methods of governance — was criticized by many mainstream scholars even among those who had supported the original Afghan jihad. The transformation of some Afghan jihad veterans into advocates of global terror against civilians was condemned unreservedly by the great majority of Islamic scholars worldwide.

The Afghan jihad illustrates a recurring challenge in Islamic political and military history: the difference between defensive jihad against invasion (which has clear legitimacy in Islamic law) and the subsequent political and organizational ramifications of successful resistance movements (which can take directions that diverge sharply from Islamic ethics).
