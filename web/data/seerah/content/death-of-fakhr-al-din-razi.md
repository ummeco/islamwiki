# Death of Fakhr al-Din al-Razi

Fakhr ad-Din Muhammad ibn Umar al-Razi (544-606 AH / 1150-1210 CE) was one of the most powerful theological minds of the Islamic medieval period. Theologian, philosopher, physician, jurist, and Quranic exegete, he synthesized Ash'ari theology with Avicennian (Ibn Sinaic) philosophy and produced a monumental Quranic commentary that remains one of the most important works in the tafsir tradition. His death in Herat in 606 AH (1210 CE) brought to a close one of the most prolific scholarly careers in Islamic history.

## Life

Al-Razi was born in Ray (near present-day Tehran) in 544 AH (1150 CE). He studied under his father and then under major scholars of theology and philosophy, becoming proficient in the Shafi'i school of law, Ash'ari theology, and the Avicennian philosophical tradition.

He traveled extensively across the eastern Islamic world, teaching and debating. His reputation as a debater was formidable: he reportedly engaged philosophers, theologians, Mu'tazilites, Ismailis, and other opponents in public disputations, and he was known for the ability to present an opponent's position more forcefully than the opponent himself before then refuting it.

He spent significant time in Transoxiana (Central Asia) before eventually settling in Herat (present-day Afghanistan), where he died in 606 AH (1210 CE). Some sources report that he was poisoned by Karramite opponents, though this cannot be verified.

## The Mafatih al-Ghayb

Al-Razi's greatest work, the *Mafatih al-Ghayb* (Keys of the Unseen), also known as *al-Tafsir al-Kabir* (the Great Commentary), is one of the most encyclopedic works of Quranic exegesis ever produced. Running to approximately 32 volumes in modern editions, it subjects every passage of the Quran to exhaustive analysis from linguistic, legal, theological, and philosophical perspectives.

Al-Razi approached the Quran with a rational theologian's mind: he was alert to apparent ambiguities, raised objections, considered multiple interpretations, and defended his preferred interpretation through systematic argument. His commentary frequently spends more time on philosophical and theological issues raised by a verse than on the verse itself, prompting the famous quip that his tafsir contains everything except tafsir.

Despite this criticism, the work is invaluable for understanding how an intellectually sophisticated Sunni theologian engaged with the Quran at the height of the Ash'ari-philosophical synthesis.

## The Theological-Philosophical Synthesis

Al-Razi's most significant intellectual contribution was his sustained engagement with Ibn Sina's philosophy from within the Ash'ari theological framework. Where al-Ghazali had attacked Avicennian philosophy in the *Tahafut al-Falasifa*, al-Razi took a more dialectical approach: he engaged with Ibn Sina's arguments on their own terms, incorporated what he found valid, and refuted what he rejected.

His *Muhassal Afkar al-Mutaqaddimin wal-Mutakhkhirin* (Summary of the Thoughts of the Ancients and Moderns) was a comprehensive survey of philosophical and theological positions. His *Sharh al-Isharat wal-Tanbihat* (Commentary on the Hints and Pointers) was a close reading of one of Ibn Sina's major philosophical works.

This engagement produced a sophisticated synthesis in which Ash'ari theology absorbed and transformed philosophical concepts: the divine attributes, the nature of causation, the problem of evil, the structure of the cosmos.

## The Problem of Skepticism

One of the most intriguing features of al-Razi's thought is his systematic use of skeptical arguments. In his theological works, he frequently raised powerful objections to rational theological proofs — objections that he then attempted to answer. In some cases, later readers felt he had raised problems he could not adequately solve.

Al-Suyuti later noted that al-Razi had accumulated doubt upon doubt in his theological writings, leaving readers uncertain rather than certain. This characteristic made him both a great expositor of difficulties in theological reasoning and a troubling figure for those seeking unshakeable certainty.

## Works

Al-Razi was extraordinarily prolific, writing over one hundred works across theology, philosophy, Quranic exegesis, medicine, and occult sciences. Major works include:

- *Mafatih al-Ghayb* (al-Tafsir al-Kabir) — Quranic commentary
- *Muhassal Afkar al-Mutaqaddimin wal-Mutakhkhirin* — survey of philosophy and theology
- *al-Mabahith al-Mashriqiyya* (Eastern Investigations) — philosophical theology
- *al-Arbaun fi Usul al-Din* (The Forty Points on the Principles of Religion)
- *al-Tibb al-Kabir* — medical work
- *Sharh al-Isharat wal-Tanbihat* — commentary on Ibn Sina

## Legacy

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's influence on subsequent Islamic theology was enormous, particularly in the Ash'ari tradition. His comprehensive style of engagement — presenting objections fully before answering them — became standard in later kalam (theological dialectic). His tafsir remained a primary reference for scholars who wanted to understand the philosophical dimensions of Quranic interpretation.

He died in Herat in Muharram 606 AH (1210 CE), just a few years before the Mongol invasions that would devastate the region.
