# Battle of Siffin

The Battle of Siffin (37 AH / 657 CE) was fought between the army of the Caliph Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) and the forces of Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan, the governor of Syria. It was the second major armed conflict of the First Fitnah (civil war) and had lasting consequences for the political and theological development of the Muslim community.

## Background

Following the assassination of the Caliph Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) in 35 AH, Ali ibn Abi Talib was pledged allegiance as the fourth Caliph. However, Muawiyah, as Uthman's kinsman and the powerful governor of Syria, refused to recognize Ali's authority until Uthman's killers were brought to justice.

After Ali's victory at the Battle of the Camel against Talhah, Zubayr, and the forces gathered in Basra, he turned his attention to the Syrian challenge. Negotiations between Ali and Muawiyah failed, and both sides prepared for war.

## The Campaign

Ali marched from Kufa with an army estimated between 80,000 and 120,000 men. Muawiyah assembled a comparable force in Syria. The two armies met at Siffin, on the western bank of the Euphrates River in present-day Syria, in Dhul Hijjah 36 AH (May 657 CE).

For weeks, the two sides engaged in skirmishes, negotiations, and attempts at mediation. Neither Ali nor Muawiyah wished to shed Muslim blood lightly. Delegations were exchanged and terms discussed, but no resolution was reached.

## The Battle

When negotiations broke down, full-scale fighting erupted in Safar 37 AH (July 657 CE). The battle lasted several days, with the fiercest fighting occurring over three consecutive days and nights. The engagement known as Laylat al-Harir (Night of Clangor) saw intense hand-to-hand combat that continued through the darkness.

Notable Companions fought and died on both sides. Ammar ibn Yasir, one of the earliest converts to Islam, was killed fighting for Ali. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) had told Ammar: "You will be killed by the transgressing group." His death on Ali's side was seen by many as confirmation that Ali's cause was the just one.

## The Raising of the Qurans

As Ali's forces gained the upper hand and appeared close to victory, Amr ibn al-As, Muawiyah's chief advisor, proposed a stratagem. The Syrian soldiers raised copies of the Quran on their spears, calling out: "Let the Book of Allah judge between us!"

This placed Ali in a difficult position. Many in his army, particularly the Quran readers, demanded that he accept the call to arbitration. Though Ali recognized it as a political maneuver, the pressure from within his own ranks was too great to resist. He agreed to halt the fighting and submit the dispute to arbitration.

## The Arbitration

Two arbitrators were appointed: Abu Musa al-Ash'ari representing Ali, and Amr ibn al-As representing Muawiyah. They met at Adhruh (or Dumat al-Jandal) in Sha'ban 37 AH (February 658 CE).

The details of what transpired at the arbitration are debated by historians. According to the most widely reported account, Abu Musa and Amr agreed to depose both Ali and Muawiyah and allow the Muslims to choose a new caliph. Abu Musa announced this publicly, but Amr then declared only the deposition of Ali while confirming Muawiyah. Whether this was genuine treachery or a misunderstanding is disputed.

## Consequences

The arbitration satisfied neither side and had three major consequences:

1. **The emergence of the Khawarij**: A faction of Ali's supporters rejected the arbitration entirely, declaring that "judgment belongs to Allah alone." They withdrew from Ali's army and became the Khawarij, the first distinct sect in Islam.

2. **The weakening of Ali's authority**: The inconclusive arbitration undermined Ali's political position without resolving the conflict with Muawiyah.

3. **The foundation of Umayyad rule**: Muawiyah gradually consolidated his position, and after Ali's assassination by a Khawarij in 40 AH, he established the Umayyad dynasty.

## Legacy

The Battle of Siffin represents one of the most painful episodes in early Islamic history. Companions who had stood together in defense of Islam were now divided. The arbitration and its aftermath introduced lasting political and theological questions about legitimate authority, the nature of faith, and the criteria for leadership in the Muslim community. These questions continue to echo in Islamic political thought to this day.
