# The War of Fijar (Harb al-Fijar)

## A War in Sacred Months

The word "fijar" means sacrilege or violation of the sacred. The Harb al-Fijar — the Sacrilegious Wars — took their name from the Arab moral code that had governed the peninsula for generations: the four sacred months of Dhul-Qa'dah, Dhul-Hijjah, Muharram, and Rajab were inviolable times of peace. Tribes that were at blood-feud the rest of the year laid down arms during the sacred months. Trade fairs, pilgrimages, and markets operated under this protection. To fight during the sacred months was fijar — a desecration of an ancient compact.

The War of Fijar was a series of conflicts that erupted in violation of these protections, involving the Hawazin tribe on one side and the Quraysh along with their allies the Kinanah on the other. These wars unfolded in approximately 590 CE, when the Prophet ﷺ was in his late teens — roughly sixteen to nineteen years old — and they left an impression on him that would be referenced throughout his prophetic career.

## Origins of the Conflict

The war's origins trace to a dispute involving a Qurayshi merchant named Urwa ibn Utbah al-Rahhal, who had contracted safe passage (jiwar) for himself and his goods under the protection of a man from the Hawazin. When this protection was violated and Urwa was killed, the Quraysh demanded blood money or retaliation. The Hawazin refused, and the situation escalated.

A key figure in igniting the war was al-Barrad ibn Qays al-Kinani, who killed the Hawazin leader Urwa during the sacred month of Dhul-Qa'dah — a clear violation of the sacred truce. This act of fijar opened the door for further violations, and what followed was a series of battles spread over multiple years.

## Multiple Battles

The War of Fijar was not a single engagement but a series of conflicts across several encounters, the most significant of which took place in the plains near Ukaz — a major trade fair and market location near Mecca where poets competed and merchants traded under the protection of the sacred truce. The fights ranged across different years, with periods of negotiation and resumed hostilities.

The Quraysh, despite their initial reluctance to fight (they understood the value of the sacred truce for their trading interests), found themselves drawn in to support their allies the Kinanah. The Hawazin were formidable fighters — a bedouin tribe known for their martial skill and large numbers.

Eventually, the Quraysh and Kinanah gained the upper hand. A treaty was negotiated in which blood money was assessed for the casualties on both sides. The war ended with the Quraysh and Kinanah acknowledging more deaths on the Hawazin side — requiring them to pay blood money to the Hawazin — and the parties agreeing to close the conflict.

## The Prophet's Participation

The Prophet ﷺ later described his own role in the War of Fijar. He said: "I attended the Fijar war with my uncles. I was shooting arrows for them." (Ibn Hisham) His participation was that of a young man serving his family and tribe in a tribal conflict — not as a leader but as a fighter, retrieving and handing arrows to his uncles on the Quraysh side.

He also described the war as one of the events of his youth that he looked back on without pride but without excessive regret — the context of the age of ignorance (jahiliyyah) before revelation came. He did not glorify the war. He would later teach that fighting in the sacred months was forbidden in Islam (with specific exceptions for defense), and the Fijar Wars served as an example he cited when explaining why the prohibition existed.

## Hilf al-Fudul: The Consequence of Fijar

The most significant consequence of the War of Fijar for the Prophet's ﷺ biography was not the war itself but what followed: the establishment of the Hilf al-Fudul — the Alliance of the Virtuous. The wars had demonstrated the fragility of the existing Meccan system of honor and protection. The sacred month was violated; men were killed who should have been protected; merchants were robbed who had paid for safe passage.

A group of Mecca's leading men — from multiple clans, including the Hashim, Muttalib, Asad, Zuhrah, and Taym — gathered in the house of Abdullah ibn Jud'an and made a solemn compact: they would stand together to restore injustice, to protect the weak and the stranger from oppression, and to return what was wrongly taken regardless of which Meccan clan was responsible.

The Prophet ﷺ attended this gathering as a young man — some accounts say at about fifteen or twenty years of age. He spoke of the Hilf al-Fudul in the strongest possible terms throughout his prophetic career: "I witnessed in the house of Abdullah ibn Jud'an an alliance that I would not exchange even for red camels, and if I were invited to it now in Islam, I would accept." (Ibn Hisham)

## The War That Produced a Covenant

This is the deeper significance of the War of Fijar in the seerah: it was a catalyst. The breakdown of the old tribal honor system — demonstrated by the willingness of Arabs to violate the sacred truce when their interests demanded — produced a response in the best men of Mecca: the Hilf al-Fudul, a covenant of justice that transcended tribal lines.

The Prophet ﷺ participated in both. He fought in the war as a young man doing his tribal duty. He attended the covenant that arose from the war's aftermath as a young man choosing to align himself with justice. In both cases, he was not yet a prophet; he was acting on the natural human instincts of loyalty, honor, and moral seriousness that characterized him even before revelation.

When revelation came twenty years later, it would channel and refine these instincts into a comprehensive legal and moral framework. But the raw material — the deep discomfort with injustice, the commitment to protecting the weak, the understanding that tribal loyalty cannot override basic moral obligations — was already present in the man who had grown up through the War of Fijar and stood in Abdullah ibn Jud'an's house to make the Hilf al-Fudul.
