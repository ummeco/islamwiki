# The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia: The Third Saudi State and Custodian of the Haramayn

## The House of Saud and the Arabian Peninsula

The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, formally proclaimed on September 23, 1932 CE (27 Jumada al-Akhira 1351 AH), was the culmination of three decades of military, diplomatic, and tribal consolidation by Abd al-Aziz ibn Abd al-Rahman Al Saud — universally known as Ibn Saud — that transformed the fractured tribal landscape of the Arabian Peninsula into a unified state. The story of this unification cannot be understood without its historical context: the two previous Saudi states, their alliance with Wahhabi religious reform, and the geopolitical vacuum created by the collapse of the Ottoman Empire.

The First Saudi State (1157-1233 AH / 1744-1818 CE) had been established through an alliance between Muhammad ibn Saud, ruler of Dir'iyya, and Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab, the reformist scholar of Najd whose teachings emphasized the strict monotheism of the Quran and Sunnah and rejected what he saw as innovations (bid'ah) and polytheism (shirk) that had crept into popular Muslim practice. This alliance between political authority and religious legitimacy proved formidable — the First Saudi State controlled most of the Arabian Peninsula before it was destroyed by an Egyptian army sent by the Ottoman Sultan. The Second Saudi State (1238-1309 AH / 1824-1891 CE) revived the same formula before collapsing due to internal dynastic conflict.

Ibn Saud began the Third Saudi State in 1319 AH / January 1902 CE with a daring nighttime raid to recapture Riyadh — the traditional Al Saud capital — from the rival Al Rashid dynasty that had expelled his family to Kuwait. With approximately 40 men, he scaled the walls of Riyadh, killed the Rashidi governor, and planted the Al Saud flag over the Masmak fortress. He was 26 years old.

## Ibn Saud: The Unifier

Abd al-Aziz ibn Abd al-Rahman Al Saud (1293-1373 AH / 1876-1953 CE) was a figure who dominated the Arabian Peninsula for fifty years through a remarkable combination of military prowess, diplomatic skill, religious legitimacy, and personal charisma. Contemporary European and American visitors — including Harry St. John Philby (who became his adviser and later converted to Islam) and the American oil executives who would later negotiate the Arabian-American Oil Company — were struck by his physical presence: approximately 6 feet 3 inches tall, commanding, with a direct manner and a formidable intelligence.

His method of unification was systematic and flexible. Military conquest was essential — he fought hundreds of battles and raids over three decades — but he also used marriage alliances with the leading tribal families (he had many wives over the course of his life, following the Quranic limit of four at any one time), diplomatic negotiations, and the religious legitimacy granted by the Wahhabi ulama who had been the partners of the Al Saud since the 18th century.

Key conquests: al-Hasa (the oil-rich eastern region) in 1331 AH / 1913 CE, taken from the Ottomans; Ha'il (the Rashidi stronghold in northern Najd) in 1340 AH / 1921 CE; the Hejaz (including Mecca and Medina) in 1343-1344 AH / 1924-1925 CE after Sharif Hussein's expulsion. By 1926, Ibn Saud had proclaimed himself King of the Hejaz and Sultan of Najd — a dual title that acknowledged the distinct identities of the regions he had united.

## The Capture of Mecca and Medina

The capture of the Hejaz — the region containing Islam's two holiest cities — was the most consequential of Ibn Saud's conquests and the most sensitive. Mecca and Medina had been under Hashimite Sharif rule for approximately four centuries. Sharif Hussein's Arab Revolt (1916) had made him a hero to some Arab Muslims and a traitor to others; his expulsion from the Hejaz in 1924 came as he was attempting to claim the Caliphate following Atatürk's abolition of the Ottoman Caliphate.

Ibn Saud's forces entered Mecca in October 1924 after minimal fighting. The Madinah garrison surrendered in December 1925. The takeover raised immediate concerns in the wider Muslim world: would the new rulers allow Muslims of all backgrounds — Shia pilgrims, Hanafi, Maliki, and Shafi'i Muslims who differed from Wahhabi theological positions — to perform the Hajj? Would the historic sites associated with the Prophet ﷺ and the Companions be preserved or demolished?

In the event, Hajj was kept open to all Muslims regardless of madhab. However, the Wahhabi religious establishment that came to power with Ibn Saud did oversee the demolition of numerous historic structures — tombs of Companions and family of the Prophet ﷺ, Ottoman-era buildings — on the grounds that they had become sites of potentially polytheistic veneration. This demolition, continuing to this day with modern expansion projects, has been a source of ongoing grief and controversy among many Muslims worldwide.

## The Ikhwan: Religious Warriors and Their Rebellion

The Ikhwan (the Brethren) were the instrument of Ibn Saud's conquests — religiously motivated tribal warriors who settled in agricultural communities (hijar) and dedicated themselves to expanding the Wahhabi-Saudi state. Inspired by the Wahhabi scholars, they were ferocious in battle and uncompromising in their religious positions.

By the late 1920s, the Ikhwan had become a problem. They wanted to continue the expansion into British-protected territories (Kuwait, Iraq's borders, Transjordan) and were angered by Ibn Saud's practical accommodations with British power and by what they saw as his toleration of innovations — automobiles, telegraphs, and dealings with Christian foreigners. Their strict interpretation of Wahhabi teaching left no room for Ibn Saud's political pragmatism.

The confrontation came at the Battle of Sabilla (29 Rajab 1347 AH / March 29, 1929 CE). Ibn Saud, with forces loyal to him and with Wahhabi scholarly sanction for fighting the rebellious Ikhwan, defeated them decisively. Their leaders were captured; the movement was broken. This episode revealed the fundamental tension within the Saudi-Wahhabi alliance: between religious absolutism and political pragmatism — a tension that has not been fully resolved in the century since.

## The Formal Proclamation of the Kingdom

On 27 Jumada al-Akhira 1351 AH (September 23, 1932 CE), the name of the state was formally unified as "al-Mamlaka al-Arabiyya al-Sa'udiyya" — the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia. The date is now Saudi Arabia's National Day. The dual-title arrangement was replaced by a single kingdom under Ibn Saud as king.

The formal structure: an absolute monarchy governed by the Al Saud family, legitimized by the Wahhabi ulama (represented institutionally through the Board of Senior Scholars), and drawing revenue from pilgrimage to the Haramayn. The constitution, when eventually formalized in later decades, declared the Quran and Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ to be the constitution of the kingdom.

## The Oil Discovery

One year after the kingdom's founding, Standard Oil of California (SoCal) signed a concession agreement (May 1933 / 1352 AH) giving it the right to explore for oil in al-Hasa. Oil was discovered in March 1938 at the Dammam No. 7 well. The first commercial quantities of oil were exported in May 1939 — Ibn Saud personally opened the valve at a ceremony attended by American executives.

The consequences of this discovery for Saudi Arabia, the Muslim world, and global politics cannot be overstated. The Arabian-American Oil Company (later Aramco) became the world's largest oil company; Saudi Arabia became the world's largest oil producer; and the revenues from oil exports gave the Saudi government resources that transformed not only the Arabian Peninsula but Islamic institutions globally.

Oil revenue funded: the massive expansion of the Haramayn (the two holy mosques); the establishment of the Islamic University of Medina (1961) and other Islamic educational institutions; the Muslim World League (Rabitat al-Alam al-Islami, 1962); the funding of mosques and madrasas across Africa, Asia, and eventually the Western world; and scholarships for hundreds of thousands of students to study in Saudi Arabia.

## The Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques

The title that came to define Saudi Arabia's self-presentation to the Muslim world — "Khadim al-Haramayn al-Sharifayn" (Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques) — was formally adopted by King Fahd in 1986, replacing the previous title "His Majesty the King." The change was significant: it presented the Saudi ruler not primarily as a political sovereign but as a servant of the global Muslim community, whose primary obligation was to facilitate Muslim worship at Mecca and Medina.

The claim has both profound substance and real limitations. The Saudi government has indeed spent billions facilitating Hajj and Umrah, accommodating the dramatic growth from hundreds of thousands of pilgrims in the 1930s to over two million annually. The expansion projects — whatever their controversies regarding the demolition of historic sites — have created the physical infrastructure necessary for this scale of pilgrimage.

The limitation: the custodian title has also been used to claim religious authority over global Muslim affairs that not all Muslims have been willing to grant.
