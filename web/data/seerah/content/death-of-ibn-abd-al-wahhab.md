# Death of Ibn Abd al-Wahhab

Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab al-Tamimi (1115-1206 AH / 1703-1792 CE) was the Islamic scholar whose teachings formed the theological basis of the Saudi state and gave rise to the Wahhabi movement — one of the most influential and controversial religious reform movements in modern Islamic history. His death in Dir'iyya in 1206 AH (1792 CE) came after decades of teaching, alliance with the Al Saud family, and a campaign of religious reform that had transformed the Arabian Peninsula.

## Life and Early Career

Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab was born in Uyayna, a town in the Najd region of central Arabia, in 1115 AH (1703 CE) to a scholarly family. His father and grandfather were Islamic scholars of the Hanbali tradition, and he received a traditional religious education.

He traveled to pursue his education, studying in Madinah under scholars including the Hanbali scholar Abdullah ibn Ibrahim ibn Sayf and the Moroccan scholar Muhammad Hayat al-Sindi, whose emphasis on returning to the Quran and Sunnah directly influenced his thinking. He also spent time in Basra.

On his return to Najd, he developed his distinctive reformist program, beginning to preach in Huraymala and then in Uyayna, where the ruler Uthman ibn Muammar initially supported him.

## Theological Positions

Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's central concern was the purification of Islamic practice from what he identified as innovations (bid'a) and from practices that he considered shirk (associating partners with Allah). His campaign targeted:

**Veneration of tombs and shrines.** He considered the veneration of graves and the seeking of intercession (tawassul) from deceased saints to be major sins, some rising to the level of shirk. He ordered the destruction of shrines and domed tombs, including those over the graves of Companions.

**Popular religious practices.** Various superstitions, charms, and folk practices common in Arabian society were condemned.

**Return to Quran and Sunnah.** He advocated returning to the texts of the Quran, the authentic Sunnah, and the understanding of the Salaf (the early generations of Islam), rejecting what he saw as the accumulated innovations of later centuries.

His approach was rooted in the Hanbali tradition and strongly influenced by the writings of Ibn Taymiyya (d. 728 AH), whose opposition to tomb veneration and mystical practices he extended and popularized.

## The Pact with Al Saud

In 1157 AH (1744 CE), Ibn Abd al-Wahhab formed a historic alliance with Muhammad ibn Saud, the ruler of Dir'iyya. The two men agreed that Ibn Abd al-Wahhab would provide religious legitimacy and the Al Saud would provide military power. Ibn Saud agreed to support Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's teachings and implement his religious program in exchange for the scholar's support for his political rule.

This alliance, between religious authority and political power, became the foundation of Saudi governance and has continued in various forms to the present day.

Under this alliance, Dir'iyya expanded its control across the Najd, imposing Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's religious standards across the territory. Eventually the movement extended to Mecca and Madinah.

## Controversies

Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's movement generated fierce controversy from his own time. His opponents — including his own brother Sulayman ibn Abd al-Wahhab — wrote refutations arguing that:
- His condemnation of common Islamic practices as shirk was excessive and unjust
- His declaration of takfir (declaring Muslims to be disbelievers) against practitioners of these customs was contrary to established scholarly consensus
- His followers' destruction of shrines and use of force against other Muslims was indefensible

These controversies have continued into the modern period, with ongoing scholarly debate about whether Ibn Abd al-Wahhab's positions represent a correct application of the Hanbali tradition or a dangerous deviation from the scholarly consensus of Sunni Islam.

## Death and Legacy

Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab died in Dir'iyya in 1206 AH (1792 CE) at approximately 89 years of age. He had outlived his first ally Muhammad ibn Saud and was still active into old age.

His legacy is vast and contested. His teachings influenced numerous subsequent reform movements across the Muslim world — some of which bear little resemblance to his own positions. The institutionalization of his teachings in Saudi Arabia, backed by oil wealth from the mid-twentieth century, gave his approach global reach through the funding of mosques, schools, and publications worldwide.
