# The Tragedy at Bi'r Ma'unah

The tragedy at Bi'r Ma'unah in Safar 4 AH was one of the most devastating losses of individual Muslim lives in the early Islamic period — not a battle in the conventional sense, but a massacre of an unarmed missionary party by treachery. Seventy (or seventy-one) companions were killed. The Prophet mourned this loss deeply, and the event had lasting significance in Islamic history and law.

## Background

A tribal chief named Abu Bara' Amir ibn Malik al-Kilabi came to Medina and met the Prophet. He did not accept Islam but expressed interest in Islamic teaching and suggested that the Prophet send a group of Muslim teachers to the people of Najd. He offered his personal protection.

The Prophet hesitated, fearing treachery in that region. Abu Bara' assured him that under his personal aman (protection), the missionaries would be safe.

The Prophet sent a group of seventy men — specifically selected for their Quranic knowledge, piety, and teaching ability. They were among the most accomplished of the Companions in terms of religious learning. They earned their living by cutting wood and bringing it to Medina, spending their nights in prayer and study. They are remembered in Islamic tradition as the "Qurra'" — the Quranic reciters.

## The Treachery

The group traveled toward Najd and camped at the well of Bi'r Ma'unah — between the lands of Asim and Khubayb. They sent one man, Haram ibn Milhan, with a letter from the Prophet to the chief of the Amir ibn Sa'sa'ah tribe.

The chief killed Haram ibn Milhan immediately upon reading the letter. Haram reportedly said as he was struck: "Allah is great! By the Lord of the Ka'bah, I have succeeded!" — expressing his acceptance of martyrdom even in the moment of betrayal.

The tribesmen then surrounded the camp and attacked the main group. The seventy companions were killed. Only two men had been away from the camp; when they returned and found the massacre, they were attacked and one was killed, the other (Ka'b ibn Zayd) survived wounded and returned to Medina.

## The Prophet's Response

When news reached the Prophet, he was devastated. For an entire month, he performed the qunut al-nazilah — a special supplication recited during the Fajr prayer, in which he invoked Allah against the tribes of Ri'l, Dhakwan, and Usayyah who had participated in the massacre. This was one of the longest sustained periods of qunut al-nazilah recorded in the seerah.

Anas ibn Malik said: "We recited the qunut for one month, and I have never seen the Prophet so grieved over anything as he was over his companions at Bi'r Ma'unah."

## The Quranic Response

The companions of Bi'r Ma'unah are associated with the revelation of a Quranic verse that was recited for a period and then its recitation was abrogated while its meaning was preserved: "Convey to our people that we have met our Lord and He is pleased with us and we are pleased with Him." This is reported as a message that Jibril (Gabriel) brought to the Prophet from the slain companions — a divine communication of their spiritual state.

## Abu Bara's Reaction

Abu Bara' was furious at the betrayal — his personal guarantee had been violated by tribesmen under his nominal authority. He retaliated against the chief who had organized the massacre and reportedly sought to redress the violation. But the damage was done.

## The Simultaneous Tragedy at al-Raji'

In the same period, a separate group of Muslim missionaries was betrayed at al-Raji' — a well in the Hijaz. This group was smaller (six or ten men), but the betrayal was similar: they were invited by a tribal leader who claimed to want Islamic teaching and then handed over to their enemies. Khubaib ibn Adi and Zayd ibn al-Dathina were sold to Mecca and executed there.

The dual tragedies of Bi'r Ma'unah and al-Raji' occurring simultaneously cast the Prophet's period into acute grief.

## Legal Significance

The Bi'r Ma'unah incident shaped several principles in Islamic law:

**The status of missionaries killed in peacetime.** The seventy companions were unarmed teachers killed under a promise of protection. Their status as martyrs was uncontroversial, and their manner of death highlighted the Islamic prohibition on betrayal of aman (guarantees of safety).

**Qunut al-Nazilah.** The Prophet's sustained use of qunut prayers against the perpetrators established this practice as legitimate in Islamic law for disasters and attacks on the Muslim community. Imam al-Shafi'i and others preserved the qunut al-Nazilah as a Sunnah; Imam Malik holds it permissible only in the Fajr prayer; all major schools recognize some form of it.

## Sources

The account of Bi'r Ma'unah is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi, Kitab al-Jihad), Sahih Muslim, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
