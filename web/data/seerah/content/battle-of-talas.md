# Battle of Talas

The Battle of Talas (Rajab 133 AH / July 751 CE) was fought between the Abbasid Caliphate and the Tang Dynasty of China near the Talas River in present-day Kazakhstan. It was the only major military confrontation between the Arab-Muslim world and imperial China, and it had lasting consequences for the cultural and religious landscape of Central Asia.

## Background

### Central Asian Power Dynamics

By the mid-eighth century CE, Central Asia was contested by three major powers: the Abbasid Caliphate (which had just replaced the Umayyads in 132 AH / 750 CE), the Tang Dynasty of China, and various Turkic kingdoms and confederations caught between them. The region along the Silk Road was commercially vital and culturally diverse.

The Tang Dynasty had extended its influence deep into Central Asia through a network of military garrisons, tributary relationships, and alliances with local rulers. Similarly, the Muslim armies had been advancing eastward since the conquest of Transoxiana (Ma wara al-Nahr) under Qutaybah ibn Muslim in the early second century AH.

### The Immediate Cause

The immediate trigger was a succession dispute in the kingdom of Ferghana (in present-day Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan). The Abbasid governor of Transoxiana, Abu Muslim al-Khurasani, supported one claimant to the throne, while the deposed ruler fled to the Tang court and requested Chinese military intervention. The Tang general Gao Xianzhi (known in Islamic sources as a formidable commander) marched westward with a mixed force of Chinese troops and local allies.

## The Armies

The Abbasid forces were commanded by Ziyad ibn Salih, operating under the authority of Abu Muslim al-Khurasani, the powerful governor who had orchestrated the Abbasid revolution. The Muslim army consisted of Arab soldiers, Khurasani troops, and allied Turkic contingents, particularly the Qarluq Turks.

The Tang force under Gao Xianzhi comprised Chinese regular troops and auxiliaries drawn from various Central Asian vassal states, including soldiers from Ferghana and other local kingdoms.

Estimates of the size of both armies vary widely in the sources, ranging from 30,000 to over 100,000 on each side, though modern historians generally consider the lower figures more plausible.

## The Battle

The two armies met in July 751 CE near the Talas River (in present-day Kazakhstan, near the border with Kyrgyzstan). The battle lasted approximately five days, an unusually prolonged engagement for the period.

The fighting was fierce and initially inconclusive, with neither side able to gain a decisive advantage. The turning point came when the Qarluq Turks, who had been allied with the Tang forces or were positioned as a third force, switched sides and attacked the Chinese rear. This sudden betrayal threw the Tang formation into chaos.

The combined assault from the Muslim front and the Qarluq rear shattered the Chinese army. Gao Xianzhi managed to escape the battlefield with a portion of his forces but suffered enormous losses. The Muslim victory was complete.

## Consequences

### End of Chinese Influence in Central Asia

The Battle of Talas effectively ended Tang Dynasty ambitions in Central Asia. Although the Tang court initially planned a retaliatory campaign, the devastating An Lushan Rebellion that erupted in 755 CE forced China to recall its western garrisons. China would not project significant military power into Central Asia again for nearly a thousand years.

### Islamization of Central Asia

With Chinese influence removed, Islam spread rapidly among the Turkic peoples of Central Asia. While Muslim missionaries and merchants had already been making inroads, the political vacuum left by China's withdrawal accelerated the process. Within a few generations, the Turkic peoples of the region became predominantly Muslim, a transformation that shaped the demographics and culture of Central Asia to this day.

### Transfer of Technology

One of the most celebrated consequences of the battle involves the transmission of papermaking technology. According to tradition, among the Chinese prisoners taken at Talas were skilled papermakers. They were brought to Samarkand, where they established paper mills. From Samarkand, the technology of papermaking spread westward through the Muslim world, reaching Baghdad, Damascus, Egypt, and eventually Europe.

The introduction of paper had a transformative effect on Islamic civilization. It was cheaper and more abundant than parchment, enabling the explosion of scholarly writing, book production, and bureaucratic administration that characterized the Abbasid golden age. The great libraries and translation movements of Baghdad were made possible in part by the availability of paper.

### Abbasid Consolidation

The victory at Talas strengthened Abbasid prestige in the eastern provinces and reinforced the authority of Abu Muslim al-Khurasani, though the caliph al-Mansur would later have Abu Muslim executed in 137 AH (755 CE) out of fear of his growing power.

## Legacy

The Battle of Talas stands as a pivotal moment in world history. It determined that Central Asia would become part of the Islamic world rather than the Chinese cultural sphere. The Turkic peoples who embraced Islam after Talas would go on to establish some of the most powerful Muslim dynasties in history, including the Seljuks, the Khwarezmians, and the Ottomans.
