# Al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam: The Disciple of the Messenger

## A Youth of Noble Blood

Al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (may Allah be pleased with him) came into the world bearing one of the most distinguished lineages in all of Arabia. His father, al-Awwam ibn Khuwaylid, was the brother of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with her), the Prophet's beloved first wife. His mother, Safiyyah bint Abd al-Muttalib, was the paternal aunt of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself. Al-Zubayr was thus doubly connected to the Prophet ﷺ through bonds of blood — a cousin on his father's side and a nephew by marriage through the family of Khadijah. These ties meant that he grew up knowing the Prophet ﷺ not as a distant religious figure but as a member of his own extended family.

He was approximately fifteen years old when Islam came to him. Ibn Sa'd records his age at conversion as either fifteen or sixteen, placing his birth roughly at the time when the Prophet ﷺ was in his mid-twenties and had not yet received revelation. Al-Zubayr was among the earliest handful of converts to Islam — fifth or sixth by most counts in the sirah literature — brought to the faith by Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) in those first days when the Muslim community could be counted on the fingers of one hand.

## The Sword Drawn in Islam's Defense

One of the most dramatic episodes of the early Meccan period involves al-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him) and a false rumor. The account, recorded by Ibn Hisham, relates that while the early Muslims were still meeting secretly and the Prophet ﷺ had not yet begun his public call, a report spread through Mecca that the Prophet ﷺ had been captured or killed by enemies. The rumor was entirely false, but it reached al-Zubayr before it could be corrected.

Without hesitation — without waiting for confirmation, without calculating personal risk — al-Zubayr drew his sword and began moving through the streets of Mecca toward wherever the Prophet ﷺ might be found, prepared to fight whoever had harmed him. He came upon the Prophet ﷺ in the upper part of Mecca, unharmed and composed. The Prophet ﷺ asked him what had happened, and al-Zubayr explained. The Prophet ﷺ prayed for him and prayed for the sword he carried.

This is recorded as the first time a sword was ever drawn in the defense of Islam. Al-Zubayr was a youth of fifteen. The instinct he displayed — the readiness to sacrifice himself for the Prophet ﷺ — was not calculation or religious duty performed reluctantly. It was love, immediate and unconditional. This single episode captures the essence of al-Zubayr's relationship to Islam: total, fierce, and unequivocal.

## Persecution by His Uncle

The family of al-Zubayr did not uniformly welcome his conversion. His uncle (some accounts specify a maternal uncle) subjected him to a severe form of physical torture intended to force him to renounce Islam. He would wrap the young al-Zubayr in palm-fiber matting and burn it, filling the room with choking smoke, demanding that he say the words of disbelief. Al-Zubayr refused consistently. Whatever pain he endured, whatever fear might reasonably have gripped a fifteen-year-old boy, he would not renounce. He replied, according to Ibn Hisham: "I will never return to disbelief."

This pattern of torture and refusal was common among the early converts in Mecca. What is notable in al-Zubayr's case is his age. The adults who faced persecution — Abu Bakr, Uthman, Bilal, Ammar — had the accumulated experience of years to draw upon. Al-Zubayr faced this ordeal as a teenager, which makes his steadfastness all the more striking.

## Marriage into the Family of Abu Bakr

Among the bonds that connected the earliest converts was the marriage of al-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him) to Asma bint Abi Bakr (may Allah be pleased with her), the daughter of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq. Asma was herself a figure of extraordinary courage — it was she who brought food to the Prophet ﷺ and her father in the Cave of Thawr during the Hijrah, tearing strips from her waist-belt to carry it, earning her the title Dhat al-Nitaqayn (the Woman of the Two Belts). The union of al-Zubayr and Asma produced Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him), who would go on to become one of the great figures of early Islamic history in his own right — eventually declaring himself the Caliph of the Muslims in Mecca and holding out for years against the forces of Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan.

This marriage placed al-Zubayr at the very center of the inner circle of the early Muslim community: cousin of the Prophet ﷺ, nephew of Khadijah, son-in-law of Abu Bakr. Few men occupied so many crucial nodes in the network of the early Muslims.

## The Title Hawari al-Rasul

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself gave al-Zubayr the title by which history remembers him: Hawari al-Rasul, the Disciple of the Messenger of Allah. The word hawari carries deep resonance in the Islamic tradition. It is used in the Quran (3:52, 5:111, 61:14) to describe the disciples of Isa ibn Maryam (peace be upon him) — those who said "We are the helpers of Allah" and stood with the prophet of their time when others hesitated or fled.

A hadith recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari establishes the context for this title. During one of the battles, the Prophet ﷺ called for a volunteer to perform a reconnaissance mission into enemy territory — a dangerous assignment requiring both courage and skill. Al-Zubayr came forward. The Prophet ﷺ called again; al-Zubayr came forward again. When this happened a third time, the Prophet ﷺ said: "Truly, every prophet has a hawari (disciple), and my hawari is al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam."

This statement — recorded in Bukhari, the most authoritative hadith collection in Sunni Islam — is a definitive testament to al-Zubayr's place in the Prophet's estimation. To be called the equivalent of the most devoted disciples of Isa (peace be upon him) is an honor that transcends ordinary praise.

## Military Prowess Throughout Islamic History

Al-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him) was among the most feared warriors of the early Islamic army. He participated in virtually every major military engagement during the Prophet's lifetime: Badr, Uhud, the Trench, Khaybar, and the Conquest of Mecca. At the Battle of Uhud, he distinguished himself under conditions of extreme difficulty when the Muslim army was briefly broken. At the Conquest of Mecca, he carried one of the three standards that entered the city. At the Battle of Yarmouk (15 AH), which secured Muslim control of Syria against the Byzantine army, al-Zubayr charged the Byzantine lines alone on horseback while the Muslim army watched — a moment of extraordinary individual valor that al-Tabari records in detail.

He participated in the early conquests of Egypt under Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him) and distinguished himself there as well. His physical courage was the instrument through which his faith expressed itself most visibly, and the Muslim conquests of the early period are inseparable from the contribution of men like al-Zubayr.

## Among the Ten Promised Paradise

Al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam was among the ten companions (Ashra Mubashshara) to whom the Prophet ﷺ explicitly promised Paradise by name. The hadith recording this is found in collections including those of al-Tirmidhi and Abu Dawud, and reports the Prophet ﷺ listing ten companions whom he guaranteed would enter Paradise. Among them, along with Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, Said ibn Zayd, Talhah, and Abu Ubayda, is al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam.

This collective promise is one of the most significant pieces of testimony in the entire hadith corpus. It places al-Zubayr among the elite of the elite — not merely companions of the Prophet ﷺ, but those whom the Prophet ﷺ guaranteed by explicit divine knowledge would enter Paradise.

## The Battle of the Camel and His Death

The final chapter of al-Zubayr's life was shaped by the political turmoil that followed the murder of Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) in 35 AH. Al-Zubayr, along with Talhah and Aisha (may Allah be pleased with them all), moved to Basra in what is described in the sirah literature as an attempt to pressure Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) to bring Uthman's killers to justice. What followed became the Battle of the Camel (36 AH) — a conflict that no party desired but that circumstances made unavoidable.

The night before the battle, a remarkable encounter took place. According to accounts in al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir, Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) came to the opposing camp and reminded al-Zubayr of a day when the Prophet ﷺ had asked al-Zubayr: "Do you love Ali?" Al-Zubayr had said yes. The Prophet ﷺ then said: "Know that you will fight him, and you will be the oppressor in that fight." When Ali reminded him of this, al-Zubayr reportedly wept, said he had forgotten it, and withdrew from the battle without fighting.

While withdrawing, al-Zubayr was killed treacherously by a man named Amr ibn Jurmuz who followed him and struck him down while he was at prayer. When the news reached Ali, he wept. He said of the man who brought him the sword of al-Zubayr: "Give the murderer of Ibn Safiyyah the news of hellfire." Al-Zubayr's son Abdullah retrieved his body and buried him. His tomb is in al-Basra.

Al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (may Allah be pleased with him) lived his life exactly as it had begun — with a sword drawn for the right cause, and a heart devoted to Allah and His Messenger.
