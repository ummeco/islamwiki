# Jamal al-Din al-Afghani: The Father of Pan-Islamic Modernism

## A Man of Many Masks

Few figures in modern Islamic history have generated as much scholarly controversy as Jamal al-Din al-Afghani (approximately 1254-1314 AH / 1838-1897 CE). He was a political activist who dressed as a scholar, a philosopher who spoke the language of religion, a pan-Islamist who may have been of Shia background, and an anti-imperialist who accepted money from multiple imperial powers. His contradictions were not incidental — they were strategic, and understanding them is essential to understanding both his influence and his legacy.

What is beyond dispute is his impact. Across four decades of restless travel — through Afghanistan, India, Egypt, Turkey, Iran, France, and Russia — al-Afghani catalyzed a generation of Muslim intellectuals and political activists. His student Muhammad Abduh transformed al-Azhar. His writings inspired the Iranian Constitutional Revolution. His rhetoric against British imperialism helped shape the consciousness of an entire generation of colonized Muslims.

## Origins and Background

The question of al-Afghani's origins is itself historically significant. He consistently presented himself as an Afghan Sunni Muslim from Asadabad in Afghanistan, emphasizing the Sunni dimension of his identity for strategic reasons — as an Afghan, he could present himself as acceptable to both the Sunni Ottoman court and to Sunni audiences across the Muslim world. However, substantial historical evidence — examined carefully by scholar Nikki Keddie in her authoritative biography *Sayyid Jamal ad-Din "al-Afghani": A Political Biography* (1972) — suggests he was actually born in Asadabad in Iran and was of Iranian Shia background.

This matters theologically but should not overshadow his political significance. Whether Sunni or Shia by background, al-Afghani's public message was pan-Islamic — he called for unity between Sunni and Shia Muslims against European domination, arguing that internal sectarian divisions served only the interests of colonial powers. In this, he was theologically pragmatic to a degree that made traditional scholars of both sects uncomfortable.

He received a traditional Islamic education and demonstrated mastery of Islamic philosophy, kalam, and classical literature from an early age. He also absorbed the works of Islamic philosophers — al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, and the traditions of Islamic Neo-Platonism — giving his thought a philosophical sophistication that distinguished him from purely political activists.

## The Indian Years and British Colonialism

Al-Afghani arrived in British India in the 1850s and experienced directly the humiliating apparatus of colonial rule — the English-medium schools producing a class of educated Indians who had lost confidence in their own civilization, the systematic exclusion of Muslims from positions of power following the 1857 rebellion, and the pervasive ideology that Asian societies were inherently inferior and in need of Western guidance.

His response was a counter-ideology: Muslims had produced the greatest civilization in history; the current weakness was temporary and reversible; the keys to recovery were internal reform and resistance to foreign domination. In India, he encountered the emerging debate about how Muslims should respond to colonialism — whether through accommodation with the British (as Sir Sayyid Ahmad Khan argued) or through rejection and resistance.

Al-Afghani sided emphatically against accommodation. His lectures and discussions in India — he was later expelled by the British colonial authorities — argued that Sir Sayyid's approach would produce collaborators rather than leaders, servants of empire rather than architects of Islamic revival.

## Egypt and the School of Reform

Al-Afghani's most productive years were spent in Egypt (approximately 1871-1879 CE). Cairo was then under the rule of Khedive Ismail and later Khedive Tewfik — nominally autonomous governors under Ottoman suzerainty but increasingly under British influence. Egypt was accumulating massive debts to European creditors, and European control over Egyptian finances was tightening.

In Cairo, al-Afghani gathered around him a group of young Egyptian intellectuals who became some of the most important Muslim thinkers of the 20th century. The most significant was Muhammad Abduh (1849-1905 CE), a young scholar from a peasant background who was studying at al-Azhar when he encountered al-Afghani. Under al-Afghani's influence, Abduh began integrating rationalist philosophy with Islamic theology — a synthesis he would later develop into Islamic modernism.

Al-Afghani's lectures in Egypt addressed philosophy, politics, and social reform. He argued that reason and revelation were compatible, that the apparent conflict between Islam and modern science was based on a misunderstanding of both, and that Muslims needed to open ijtihad (independent reasoning) rather than blindly following traditional legal opinions that had been formulated for medieval circumstances.

He also engaged directly in Egyptian politics, supporting the Urabi movement — the constitutional and nationalist movement led by Colonel Ahmad Urabi that briefly challenged the Khedive and British influence. When the British intervened militarily in 1882 to suppress the Urabi movement and establish a "temporary" occupation that lasted until 1956, al-Afghani was already in exile.

## Paris, al-Urwa al-Wuthqa, and Global Pan-Islamism

After his expulsion from Egypt and a difficult period in India, al-Afghani went to Paris — the center of French intellectual life and a gathering point for political exiles from across the globe. There, from approximately 1882-1884 CE, he reunited with Muhammad Abduh, and together they produced one of the most influential periodicals in modern Islamic history: al-Urwa al-Wuthqa (The Firmest Bond — a Quranic phrase from 2:256).

Al-Urwa al-Wuthqa was not a scholarly journal but a political newspaper with a clear agenda: awakening Muslims to the dangers of European imperialism, calling for Muslim unity across national and sectarian lines, and arguing that Islamic civilization contained within itself the resources for revival. The newspaper was banned by British authorities in India and Egypt — which only increased its prestige and circulation. Complete sets were smuggled across the Muslim world and read by everyone from students to statesmen.

The newspaper articulated what became the core theses of pan-Islamism: (1) the Muslim ummah constitutes a single political community that should act in solidarity; (2) European colonialism is the primary threat to this community; (3) the response must be internal reform and external resistance; (4) the Caliphate (then represented by the Ottoman Sultan) should serve as the focal point of Muslim unity.

## The Famous Debate with Ernest Renan

In 1883 CE, al-Afghani engaged in one of the most significant intellectual exchanges of the 19th century with the French orientalist Ernest Renan. Renan had delivered a lecture at the Sorbonne titled "Islam and Science" (L'Islamisme et la science), in which he argued that Islam was constitutionally hostile to scientific reason, that Islamic civilization's contributions to science had been despite Islam rather than because of it, and that the Muslim mind was fundamentally incapable of philosophical thought.

Al-Afghani's response, published in the Journal des Débats, was nuanced and has been differently interpreted by different scholars. He did not flatly deny Renan's argument; instead, he made a more sophisticated case: all organized religion, including Christianity, had historically been in tension with free philosophical inquiry; Islam was not uniquely hostile to reason; and the key variable was not theology but politics and social conditions. He also suggested that Islamic civilization had produced philosophy and science in periods of freedom and had suppressed them in periods of political oppression.

What is notable is what al-Afghani did not say: he did not simply defend traditional Islamic theology as adequate without reform. His response acknowledged the critique while contextualizing it historically. This approach — engaging seriously with rationalist criticism rather than dismissing it — was characteristic of al-Afghani's intellectual style.

## Iran and the Tobacco Boycott

Al-Afghani's most dramatic political success came in Qajar Iran, where he spent significant time in the 1880s. The Shah Nasir al-Din had granted an extraordinary concession to a British subject, Major G.F. Talbot: a monopoly over the production, sale, and export of all Iranian tobacco for fifty years in exchange for £15,000 per year and a quarter of the profits. The Tobacco Concession of 1890 was among the most blatant examples of colonial economic exploitation in the Islamic world.

Al-Afghani wrote the Shah directly, warning that such concessions would eventually reduce Iran to vassalage. When ignored, he shifted his approach — working through the religious establishment to mobilize public opposition. He was also in correspondence with the leading Shia marja (religious authority) Mirza Hasan Shirazi in Samarra. In December 1891, Shirazi issued a fatwa declaring the use of tobacco haram as long as it remained under foreign control. The fatwa was obeyed with remarkable discipline across Iran — even in the Shah's own household. Facing economic paralysis, the Shah canceled the concession in 1892.

The Tobacco Boycott was a watershed event: the first successful mass movement in the modern Muslim world, organized through religious authority rather than political parties. Al-Afghani's role in the background was significant, though the credit belongs primarily to the Iranian ulama who mobilized their communities.

## Istanbul and Death in Exile

Al-Afghani spent his final years in Istanbul under the ambiguous patronage of Sultan Abdulhamid II. The Sultan was pursuing his own pan-Islamic policy — using the symbolism of the Caliphate to counter European imperialism and to maintain loyalty among Muslim subjects. He initially found al-Afghani useful and then found him dangerous. Al-Afghani spent his final years under a comfortable but real house arrest in Istanbul, his movements restricted, his correspondence monitored.

He died in Istanbul in 1314 AH / 1897 CE, reportedly of cancer of the jaw. His remains were later transferred to Afghanistan in 1944, where he received a state funeral and was interred in the grounds of Kabul University.

## The Ahl us-Sunnah Assessment

Assessing al-Afghani from an Ahl us-Sunnah perspective requires distinguishing between his political insights and his theological positions.

His political analysis was prescient. The dangers he warned against — colonial economic domination, the fragmentation of the Muslim world into competing nationalist units, the corruption of local elites by foreign powers — proved accurate. His insistence that Muslims must be the authors of their own revival rather than passive recipients of Western education remains a valid and important principle.

His theological positions are more complex. Some scholars have noted that his philosophical background — with its affinity for Islamic Neo-Platonism and possibly Ismaili thought — placed him at some distance from orthodox Sunni kalam. His pragmatic approach to theology (using Islamic vocabulary to mobilize people politically regardless of their sectarian background) could be read as either sophistication or as subordinating faith to politics.

His student Muhammad Abduh ultimately developed a more systematic theology; his other student Rashid Rida moved in a more traditionally Sunni direction. The fact that both claimed al-Afghani's legacy says something about the deliberate ambiguity of his positions.

What is clear is that al-Afghani opened a conversation about the relationship between Islam and modernity, between Islamic identity and political organization, that the Muslim world is still conducting today.
