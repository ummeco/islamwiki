# Ottoman Empire at Its Peak under Suleiman

## The Empire Suleiman Inherited

When Suleiman I ascended the Ottoman throne in 926 AH / 1520 CE following the death of his father Selim I, he inherited an empire already transformed by conquest. Selim had doubled the empire's territory in eight years — destroying the Safavid army at Chaldiran (920 AH / 1514 CE), annexing eastern Anatolia and Kurdistan, sweeping through Syria and Egypt (922 AH / 1516-1517 CE), and bringing the Hejaz with the holy cities of Mecca and Medina under Ottoman suzerainty. The last Abbasid shadow caliph in Cairo, al-Mutawakkil III, was brought to Istanbul, and according to later Ottoman tradition, transferred the title of caliph to the Ottoman sultan — giving Selim and his successors the claim of custodians of the entire Muslim world. Suleiman thus began his reign as sultan of an already formidable power. What he built upon that foundation would transform the Ottoman state into the preeminent civilizational power of the sixteenth century.

Suleiman ruled for forty-six years — 926 to 974 AH / 1520 to 1566 CE — the longest reign of any Ottoman sultan. During those decades, he pushed Ottoman frontiers to their maximum extent on three continents, reformed the legal and administrative structure of the empire, and presided over a cultural and artistic flowering that produced some of the most magnificent monuments of Islamic civilization. European contemporaries called him "the Magnificent." His own subjects, and subsequent Islamic tradition, knew him as "Kanuni" — the Lawgiver.

## Western Campaigns: Into the Heart of Europe

The conquest of Belgrade in 927 AH / 1521 CE announced that Suleiman intended to complete what his predecessors had begun in Europe. Belgrade, the "Gate of Hungary," had resisted Mehmed II's siege in 1456. Suleiman took it within weeks, opening the entire Hungarian plain. The Knights of St. John at Rhodes, whose island fortress controlled the eastern Mediterranean sea lanes, fell the following year (928 AH / 1522 CE) after a six-month siege — Suleiman allowed the surviving knights to depart with honor, a gesture that enhanced his reputation across Europe.

The decisive moment came at the Battle of Mohacs on 29 Muharram 932 AH / August 29, 1526 CE. The Hungarian army under King Louis II met the Ottomans on a flat field north of the Danube. The battle lasted less than two hours. Ottoman artillery and janissary fire obliterated the Hungarian cavalry charges; Louis II fled and drowned in a marsh. Hungary ceased to exist as an independent kingdom. Suleiman occupied Buda and installed a vassal king, John Zápolya, while withdrawing his main forces.

The siege of Vienna in 935 AH / 1529 CE marked the westernmost Ottoman military thrust into Europe. Suleiman marched with an enormous army, but a combination of factors — late start to the campaign due to rain and mud, the difficulty of moving heavy siege artillery over the Alpine approaches, and a determined Austrian defense — forced him to withdraw after three weeks without breaching the city walls. The failure at Vienna was the only significant military setback of his reign, and Ottoman armies would not attempt it again until 1683. Nevertheless, the threat was real enough that the Habsburgs would spend the next 150 years negotiating rather than fighting with Istanbul.

A second Hungarian campaign in 944 AH / 1538 CE consolidated Ottoman control, and Transylvania became an Ottoman protectorate. By the mid-sixteenth century, the Ottoman Empire's European frontier ran through the middle of Hungary, with Ottoman forces raiding into modern Austria and Slovakia. The psychological impact on European Christendom — which saw itself as locked in existential struggle with the Ottomans — was profound.

## Eastern Campaigns: The Safavid Conflict

The Ottoman-Safavid conflict was not merely political but theological. The Safavids of Persia were Twelver Shia who had forcibly converted Iran from a predominantly Sunni country to a Shia one in the early sixteenth century and were actively fomenting rebellion among Shia populations in Anatolia (the "Qizilbash" Turkic tribes). Suleiman launched three major campaigns against them: 940-941 AH / 1534-1535 CE, 948-955 AH / 1548-1555 CE, and 973-974 AH / 1566 CE (the last of which he did not complete due to his death).

The first campaign brought the capture of Tabriz and Baghdad. Baghdad had special significance: it was the seat of the Abbasid Caliphate until its destruction by the Mongols in 656 AH, and its shrine complex — including the tomb of Abu Hanifa النعمان, founder of the Hanafi school of law, and the shrine of Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani — made it a city of profound Sunni Islamic importance. The second campaign advanced deep into Persia but failed to force a decisive battle; the Safavids employed a strategy of withdrawal and scorched earth, denying the Ottomans a decisive victory. The Peace of Amasya (962 AH / 1555 CE), the first formal peace treaty between the two powers, established borders that roughly approximated what would become the modern Turkey-Iran boundary.

## The Lawgiver: Legal and Administrative Reforms

Suleiman's cognomen "Kanuni" reflects what many Ottoman contemporaries considered his greatest achievement: the codification of Ottoman law. Islamic empires faced a perennial challenge — Islamic sharia (divine law) was the supreme authority in principle, but no premodern state could govern millions of people across diverse regions with the classical fiqh corpus alone. Custom, administrative necessity, and local tradition all required legal frameworks. The Ottomans, from Mehmed II onward, had developed "kanun" (from the Greek "canon") — sultanic legislation that complemented but did not replace sharia.

Suleiman commissioned his chief jurist Ebussuud Efendi, one of the greatest scholars of the empire, to harmonize the kanun with sharia. The result was a comprehensive legal code covering criminal law, land tenure, taxation, commercial relations, and administrative procedure. Crucially, Ebussuud framed much of the kanun in formal Islamic legal terms — not as sultanic decree overriding Islamic law but as the sultan's legitimate interpretation and application of it within the boundaries of Islamic jurisprudence. This synthesis of dynastic authority and religious legitimacy was a signal achievement of Ottoman statecraft.

The devshirme system — the periodic levy of Christian boys who were educated in Ottoman schools, converted to Islam, and trained for the military and civil service — reached its full development under Suleiman. The elite janissary corps and the top ranks of the bureaucracy were staffed by these men, who owed their position entirely to the sultan and thus formed a counterweight to the hereditary Turkish aristocracy. The system was administratively effective but would eventually generate its own problems.

## Architecture and Cultural Patronage

Suleiman's reign produced some of the finest monuments of Ottoman and Islamic architecture. The architect Mimar Sinan (896-996 AH / 1490-1588 CE), who served as chief imperial architect for five decades, built over 370 structures during his career, of which the most famous from Suleiman's reign is the Süleymaniye Mosque complex in Istanbul (completed 964 AH / 1557 CE). Built on a hill overlooking the Golden Horn, the Süleymaniye is a vast külliye — not merely a mosque but a complex including schools (one teaching hadith, one teaching medicine), a hospital, a soup kitchen, a caravanserai, a bath, and Suleiman's own mausoleum. Sinan considered it a mature work; he regarded the Selimiye Mosque in Edirne (built for Suleiman's successor Selim II) as his masterpiece.

In Jerusalem, Suleiman undertook the restoration of the Dome of the Rock and the rebuilding of the walls of the Old City — the walls that stand to this day were constructed during his reign, completing the project in 952-954 AH / 1545-1548 CE. This investment in Jerusalem's infrastructure reflected Ottoman consciousness of their role as guardians of Islam's holy sites.

Suleiman himself was a poet of considerable accomplishment, writing under the pen name "Muhibbi" ("the loving one"). His Diwan contains nearly 3,000 couplets in Ottoman Turkish. The Ottoman court under him became a center of literary and artistic production, with patronage flowing to poets, calligraphers, illuminators, and craftsmen.

## Palace Politics: The Harem and the Question of Succession

Ottoman succession politics were inherently violent. The principle that only a son of the reigning sultan could succeed, combined with the absence of primogeniture, meant that a sultan's death triggered a war among his sons, with the winner executing his brothers. Suleiman altered this pattern in ways that had lasting consequences.

His relationship with Hürrem Sultan (known in the West as Roxelana), a Ruthenian concubine who became his legal wife — an unprecedented step — gave her an extraordinary position at court. She bore him six children. Her influence, and that of subsequent powerful women in the harem, gave rise to what later Ottoman historians called the "Sultanate of Women" (Kadınlar Saltanatı) — a period from roughly 1530 to 1650 when the mothers and wives of sultans exercised significant political influence.

The rivalry between Hürrem and Grand Vizier Ibrahim Pasha (Suleiman's childhood friend whom he had raised to the highest office) ended with Ibrahim's execution in 942 AH / 1536 CE — allegedly on Hürrem's influence. Later, Suleiman was convinced — by a combination of court intrigue and Hürrem's advocacy for her own sons — that his eldest son Mustafa, born of another concubine and widely popular with the army, was plotting rebellion. In 960 AH / 1553 CE, Suleiman had Mustafa strangled in his presence, an act that shocked the empire and was later commemorated in Ottoman literature as a tragedy. Hürrem died in 974 AH / 1566 CE, the same year as Suleiman himself. Their son Selim II inherited the empire.

## The Death at Szigetvár and the Empire He Left

In the summer of 974 AH / 1566 CE, Suleiman launched what would be his thirteenth and final campaign — against the fortress of Szigetvár in southern Hungary, defended by the Croatian captain Nikola Šubić Zrinski. The emperor was seventy-one years old and severely ill with gout and other ailments. He was carried in a litter and could no longer ride his horse. He died in his tent on the night before the final assault, on September 6-7, 1566 CE. His death was kept secret by his Grand Vizier Sokollu Mehmed Pasha for forty days until the army returned to Istanbul and the succession was secured.

The empire Suleiman left spanned approximately 15 million square kilometers and included southeastern Europe from Hungary to the Aegean, Anatolia, the Fertile Crescent, Egypt and North Africa to the borders of Morocco, and the Arabian Peninsula including the Hejaz with Mecca and Medina. It encompassed the former heartlands of three of the four major early caliphates — the Umayyads of Damascus, the Abbasids of Baghdad, and the Fatimids of Cairo. The Ottoman sultan held the title of Caliph and Servant of the Two Holy Mosques. The population was approximately 15-30 million, speaking dozens of languages and practicing Islam, Christianity, and Judaism.

## The Ottoman Legacy in Islamic History

Suleiman's reign represents the peak of a political-military achievement that had begun with Osman I in the late thirteenth century and would persist — in diminished form — until 1922. For Muslims across the world, the Ottoman state provided a sense that Islam had a political center, a protector, and an institutional expression. The Caliph in Istanbul was a real figure, not a nominal one. The Hajj routes through Ottoman territory were maintained and secured. The holy cities were administered and embellished by an Islamic state with real power.

The challenges that would eventually undo the empire — European economic and military expansion, internal bureaucratic rigidity, the fiscal strain of constant warfare — were already present in Suleiman's time, but in 1566 they remained manageable. Suleiman died at the height of Ottoman power, and the memory of his reign shaped Ottoman self-understanding for the three centuries of gradual decline that followed.

## Significance

The reign of Suleiman I stands as one of the great chapters of Islamic political history. The achievement of governing a multi-ethnic, multi-religious empire stretching across three continents for nearly half a century, while simultaneously maintaining Muslim religious institutions, patronizing scholarship and architecture, reforming the legal system, and projecting military power from Vienna to Baghdad, has few parallels. The Süleymaniye Mosque still stands in Istanbul. The walls of Jerusalem he built still stand. The legal synthesis of Ebussuud Efendi shaped Ottoman jurisprudence for centuries. For students of Islamic civilization, the Ottoman Empire under Suleiman represents one model of how Islamic governance could be realized at scale in the premodern world.
