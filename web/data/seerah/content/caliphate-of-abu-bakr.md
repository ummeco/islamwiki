# Caliphate of Abu Bakr as-Siddiq

Abu Bakr Abdullah ibn Abi Quhafah as-Siddiq (d. 13 AH / 634 CE) was the closest companion of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) and the first Caliph of Islam. His caliphate lasted approximately two years and three months (11-13 AH / 632-634 CE), but it was among the most consequential periods in Islamic history — a time in which the community faced existential threats and emerged from them with its unity preserved and its mission expanded.

## Immediate Challenges: The Riddah Wars

Abu Bakr's caliphate was immediately tested by a crisis that threatened to dissolve the nascent Muslim state. Upon the Prophet's death, numerous tribes across Arabia renounced their allegiance to Medina, either rejecting Islam altogether or insisting they had pledged loyalty to the Prophet personally and owed nothing to his successor. Several false prophets emerged, including Musaylimah ibn Habib in Yamama and Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid in Najd.

Abu Bakr's response was decisive. Many of his companions, including Umar ibn al-Khattab, counseled lenience, suggesting that if people said the shahada (testimony of faith), they should not be fought. Abu Bakr famously replied: "By Allah, I shall fight those who separate prayer from zakah (mandatory almsgiving)! For zakah is the due upon wealth. By Allah, if they withhold from me even a hobbling rope that they used to pay to the Messenger of Allah, I shall fight them for withholding it."

This legal distinction — that Islam required both the testimony of faith and the practical obligations including zakah — became foundational in Islamic jurisprudence for defining the boundaries of the Muslim community's obligations.

## Military Campaigns

Abu Bakr organized eleven military forces and sent them simultaneously to different regions of Arabia. Khalid ibn al-Walid, the greatest Muslim general of the era, was given the most critical assignment: the campaign against the largest and most organized of the false-prophet movements, that of Musaylimah in Yamama.

The Battle of Yamama (11 AH / 633 CE) was among the costliest in the early Muslim era. Musaylimah had gathered a large force and the fighting was intense. Among those killed in the battle were hundreds of Companions who had memorized the Quran — the Huffaz (memorizers) of the early community. This loss prompted Umar ibn al-Khattab to urge Abu Bakr to commission an official collection of the Quranic text lest it be lost with the deaths of those who had memorized it.

## The Collection of the Quran

The collection of the Quran under Abu Bakr was one of the most consequential acts of his caliphate. He appointed Zayd ibn Thabit — the Prophet's primary scribe — to oversee the project. Zayd collected the Quran from written sources (scattered parchments, bones, stones, and palm leaves) and from the memories of those who had memorized it, verifying each portion against the memories of two witnesses.

The resulting compilation (mushaf) was kept in the custody of Abu Bakr and then Umar, and later entrusted to Umar's daughter Hafsa, one of the Prophet's widows. It formed the basis for the standardized Uthmanic codex produced during the caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan.

## Dispatching Usamah's Army

Among Abu Bakr's first acts was the completion of an obligation left by the Prophet. Shortly before his death, the Prophet had organized a military expedition under Usamah ibn Zayd ibn Haritha to campaign into the Byzantine frontier. The Prophet had died before the army departed. Many companions urged Abu Bakr to recall the army given the internal crises, but Abu Bakr refused: "By Allah, I will not untie a knot that the Messenger of Allah tied."

The army's successful campaign and return helped stabilize Abu Bakr's authority and demonstrated his commitment to the Prophet's intentions.

## Expansion Toward Iraq and Syria

As the Riddah Wars concluded and Arabia was reunified, Abu Bakr authorized campaigns beyond the peninsula. Khalid ibn al-Walid was sent toward Iraq, initiating the conquest of the Sassanid-controlled Fertile Crescent. Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah and other commanders were sent toward Byzantine Syria.

These campaigns, begun under Abu Bakr, would continue to transform the world under Umar ibn al-Khattab.

## Death and Legacy

Abu Bakr died in Jumada al-Thani, 13 AH (August 634 CE), after a period of illness believed to have been triggered by cold water bathing. He was 61 or 63 years of age. He designated Umar ibn al-Khattab as his successor before his death — a practice of designation that became one of the recognized modes of Caliphal succession in Islamic political theory.

He was buried next to the Prophet in Aisha's chamber, which is now enclosed within the Masjid an-Nabawi.

Abu Bakr's caliphate established the principle that the Muslim community's unity and obligations — including prayer, zakah, and obedience to legitimate authority — were non-negotiable. His firmness during the Riddah Wars preserved Islam's integrity as both a faith and a political order at the moment of its greatest vulnerability.
