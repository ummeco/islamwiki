# The Revelation of the Hijab Verses

## Modesty as Legislation

The revelation of the hijab (covering/veil) verses — contained primarily in Surah al-Ahzab (33:53, 33:59) and Surah al-Nur (24:31) — represents one of the most significant legislative developments of the Medinan period, establishing the framework for modesty and gender interaction that Islamic civilization has observed for fourteen centuries. These verses were not revealed in a single moment but across the Medinan period, with each revelation adding a layer to what became a comprehensive framework governing dress, privacy, and interaction between unrelated men and women.

## The Verse of Hijab (33:53)

Surah al-Ahzab 33:53 — known as the ayat al-hijab — was revealed in 5 AH in the context of the Prophet's ﷺ marriage to Zaynab bint Jahsh. After the wedding feast, some guests remained in the Prophet's ﷺ house beyond a socially appropriate time, apparently reluctant to leave. The verse addressed this situation and in doing so established much broader principles: it directed those who wished to ask the Prophet's ﷺ wives anything to do so from behind a curtain (hijab). The verse also established a set of boundaries for the Prophet's ﷺ household: visitors should not enter his rooms without permission, should not linger after meals, and should speak to his wives only through a screen.

While this verse specifically addressed the Prophet's ﷺ wives, its principles of modesty, privacy, and the physical separation of unrelated men and women in certain contexts informed the broader framework for Muslim society. The Prophet's ﷺ wives were declared "Mothers of the Believers" — a status requiring particular modesty — but the principle of a physical boundary for modesty was extended outward through subsequent verses.

## The Jilbab Verse (33:59)

Surah al-Ahzab 33:59 addressed all Muslim women: "O Prophet, tell your wives and your daughters and the women of the believers to bring down over themselves part of their outer garments. That is more suitable that they will be known and not be abused." The verse establishes the jilbab — a loose outer covering worn over the regular clothes — as the standard for Muslim women in public. The purpose stated is both practical (to be recognized as Muslim women and therefore not subjected to harassment) and principled (a dignified, modest bearing in public).

The verse was revealed in the context of Medina's social situation: free Muslim women and enslaved women sometimes could not be distinguished in public, which led to harassment of free women who were expected to be protected. The jilbab allowed free Muslim women to signal their status. The Islamic legal tradition has read this verse broadly as establishing the principle of hijab for all Muslim women, not merely as a contextual solution to Medinan social conditions.

## The Khimar Verse (24:31)

Surah al-Nur 24:31 addressed believing women with more specific instructions: "And tell the believing women to reduce some of their vision and guard their private parts and not expose their adornment except that which necessarily appears thereof, and to wrap their khimar (head covering) over their chests and not expose their adornment except to their husbands, their fathers, their husbands' fathers, their sons, their husbands' sons, their brothers, their brothers' sons, their sisters' sons, their women, those their right hands possess, or those male attendants having no physical desire, or children who are not yet aware of the private aspects of women."

The khimar was a head covering already worn by Arab women, but typically worn back over the shoulders, leaving the chest visible. The verse's instruction to draw it over the chest (jayyub — plural of jayb, the chest opening) established the Islamic standard: the head covering should extend to cover the chest as well. This verse, combined with 33:59, forms the primary Quranic basis for the Islamic covering that scholars agree on: covering the hair and body in public, before unrelated men.

## The Communal and Individual Dimensions

The hijab verses are not solely about individual modesty but about the construction of a social environment of dignity and security. The Quran addresses both men and women: the verses on lowering the gaze (24:30) address men first, before the verses on women's dress. The framework establishes mutual responsibility — men guarding their gaze, women covering their adornment — as two sides of the same social covenant.

For the Muslim women of Medina who received these verses, the covering was not experienced as restriction but as definition — a marker of their honored status as Muslim women, of their belonging to a community with shared standards of dignity, and of the Prophet's ﷺ care for their protection and respect in public life.

## The Scholarly Consensus and Its Limits

The four major schools of Islamic law (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) agree on the obligation of hijab for Muslim women: covering all of the body in public before unrelated men except — in the majority opinion — the face and hands. Some scholars, following the most restrictive reading, include the face as part of the obligatory covering; others hold that only the face and hands may remain uncovered. The point of agreement across the madhabs is the obligation itself, not merely the recommendation.

The hijab verses of the Quran are also understood as establishing a principle about the relationship between Muslim women and the community that surrounds them — that a Muslim woman's modest bearing in public is not a sign of social subordination but of conscious, active participation in a shared community covenant. She covers because she has been told by her Lord to cover, and her compliance is an act of submission (islam) in the literal sense of the word. The verses addressed her directly — 'tell the believing women' — not indirectly through her male relatives. She was the addressee of the command and the agent of its fulfillment. In this sense the hijab verses are among the most direct addresses to Muslim women in the Quran — respecting their agency as the persons whose belief and practice the command required, not merely as subjects of a social regulation imposed from outside.
