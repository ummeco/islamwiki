# The Martyrdom of Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib

## The Lion of Allah

Hamza ibn Abd al-Muttalib was the Prophet's ﷺ paternal uncle and milk brother — older by perhaps two years, raised alongside him in the same household of Abd al-Muttalib. He was among the most powerful physical presences in Mecca: a renowned hunter, a warrior of extraordinary courage, and a man whose acceptance of Islam in the sixth year of prophethood had shifted the balance of power in the early community. When Hamza converted — in a moment of protective rage after Abu Jahl had struck the Prophet ﷺ in the street, and Hamza confronted him at the Kaaba — the Quraysh recognized that the balance had changed. The Prophet ﷺ gave him the title Asad Allah wa-Asad Rasulih: the Lion of Allah and the Lion of His Messenger. At Badr, Hamza had fought in the front rank and killed Utbah ibn Rabia — the father of Hind bint Utbah — in single combat. That killing set in motion the chain of events that ended with his death at Uhud.

## The Contract on Hamza's Life

Hind bint Utbah, wife of Abu Sufyan, had lost her father Utbah and her brother Walid at Badr. She wanted Hamza dead. She identified Wahshi ibn Harb — an Abyssinian slave owned by Jubayr ibn Mut'im — as the instrument. Wahshi was renowned for his accuracy with the javelin. Hind promised him his freedom if he killed Hamza. Jubayr ibn Mut'im, whose uncle Tuh'aymah had also been killed at Badr, added his own incentive: freedom and reward if Hamza was brought down.

Wahshi later described his commitment to the task in hadiths preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari. He was not interested in any other target. He would follow Hamza throughout the battle, waiting for the right moment, and throw his javelin once. He had no desire to fight anyone else.

## The Battle and the Killing

At Uhud, the battle began well for the Muslims. Hamza fought with characteristic ferocity in the initial engagement. The Qurayshi front lines were broken. But when the archers on the hill abandoned their position and Khalid ibn al-Walid's cavalry swept around the flank, the Muslim formation collapsed and the fighting became chaotic and close.

Wahshi had tracked Hamza through the early phases of the battle. He described the moment: he saw Hamza making his way through the crowd, killing everyone he encountered, like a massive camel being turned to face those who opposed him. Wahshi waited, positioned himself behind a rock, and threw his javelin. It struck Hamza in the lower abdomen — in the area of the groin, passing through. Hamza fell. He tried to rise and move toward Wahshi but collapsed from blood loss. Wahshi waited until he was certain Hamza had died, then recovered his javelin and returned to the Qurayshi camp, his contract fulfilled, his freedom secured.

Wahshi later converted to Islam and met the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet ﷺ, in a narration that reveals both his profound grief and his extraordinary self-control, said to Wahshi: "Can you hide your face from me?" — he could not bear to see the face of the man who had killed his uncle. Wahshi moved away from the Prophet's ﷺ presence out of respect. He lived into old age as a Muslim and died, according to some accounts, as a fighter in later campaigns.

## The Mutilation

After the battle, Hind bint Utbah moved through the field of the Qurayshi dead and Muslim dead. When she found Hamza's body, she cut off his ears and nose and fashioned them into anklets. She split open his chest, removed his liver, and attempted to eat it — but could not swallow it. Some scholars note that Allah protected Hamza's body even in this, preventing his liver from being consumed by his killer's kin.

When the Prophet ﷺ came upon Hamza's mutilated body — his face disfigured, his chest split open — he wept with an intensity that those around him had rarely witnessed. He said: "Never before have I stood over a death that has caused me more grief than this." He covered Hamza's body with a cloth and prayed over him. He declared that he would take retaliation — that he would mutilate seventy of the Quraysh in return — but before he could act on this, the revelation came: "And if you punish, punish with the equivalent of that with which you were harmed. But if you are patient — it is better for those who are patient." (Surah al-Nahl 16:126) He chose patience. He forgave. And Hind bint Utbah herself — who had demanded Hamza's death, worn his ears as ornaments, and attempted to eat his liver — accepted Islam years later at the conquest of Mecca and received the Prophet's ﷺ pardon.

## The Burial and the Titles

Hamza was buried at Uhud. The Prophet ﷺ prayed the funeral prayer over him seventy times — once for each of the seventy martyrs, returning to Hamza's body each time. This number reflects the depth of the Prophet's ﷺ love for his uncle. Hamza's grave at the base of Mount Uhud remains one of the most visited sites in Islamic history, visited by millions of pilgrims annually.

The Prophet ﷺ called him Sayyid al-Shuhada: the Master of the Martyrs. This title carries permanent theological weight in Islamic tradition — Hamza is the paradigmatic martyr, the companion who gave his life while the Prophet ﷺ was himself present and wounded, whose death is mourned in the Quran's longest discussion of a single battle.

## His Place in the Seerah

The martyrdom of Hamza at Uhud is one of the pivotal losses of the Medinan period. His death at the hands of a hired javelin-thrower, followed by the mutilation of his body by Hind, represents the darkest moment of the day — more personal to the Prophet ﷺ than even his own wounding. The Prophet's ﷺ response to that darkness — weeping openly, declaring the desire for retaliation, and then accepting the revelation commanding restraint — is a model of how prophetic grief is contained and redirected. He did not suppress his love for Hamza or pretend the loss was not devastating. He expressed grief fully and then obeyed what Allah commanded.

Hamza had been a Muslim for approximately sixteen years when he died. He had migrated to Medina, participated in Badr and Uhud, and died without having seen the community that would carry his legacy — the conquest of Mecca, the spread of Islam across Arabia, the caliphates. He died in what felt like a moment of defeat, his body desecrated in the field. But his title endures: Asad Allah — the Lion of Allah — and Sayyid al-Shuhada — the Master of the Martyrs.
