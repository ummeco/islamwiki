# Huyayy ibn Akhtab Convinces Banu Qurayza to Break Their Treaty

The betrayal of Banu Qurayza during the Battle of the Trench (Khandaq, 5 AH) was precipitated largely by the actions of one man: Huyayy ibn Akhtab al-Nadiri — the leader of Banu Nadir who had been expelled from Medina in 4 AH and who now sought to forge an alliance that he hoped would destroy the Muslim community. His successful persuasion of Banu Qurayza's chief, Ka'b ibn Asad, to break the tribe's treaty with the Prophet was one of the most consequential individual acts of the Medinan period.

## Background

Banu Qurayza was the last remaining Jewish tribe in Medina. Two other major Jewish tribes — Banu Qaynuqa and Banu Nadir — had previously been expelled from Medina for treaty violations. Banu Qurayza had maintained their treaty with the Prophet and had remained in Medina.

Huyayy ibn Akhtab was the former chief of Banu Nadir, who had helped organize the exile of his own tribe through his hostile provocations against the Muslim community. After the expulsion, he went to Khaybar and became one of the primary organizers of the Coalition (Ahzab) that formed against the Muslims — using Meccan, Ghafatan, and other tribal connections to assemble the army that would besiege Medina in 5 AH.

## The Approach to Ka'b ibn Asad

When the Confederate army of approximately 10,000 fighters arrived at Medina and found their way blocked by the trench, the strategic situation was a stalemate. The Confederates could not cross the trench; the Muslims could not safely come out from behind it. The siege became a test of supplies and morale.

Huyayy ibn Akhtab identified Banu Qurayza's position as a potential game-changer. If Banu Qurayza broke their treaty and attacked the Muslim positions from within the city, the Muslims would be caught between two forces.

He went to the fortress of Ka'b ibn Asad, the chief of Banu Qurayza. Ka'b refused to let him in at first — he recognized the danger of what Huyayy was proposing. Ka'b reportedly said: "You are ill-omened. I have a treaty with Muhammad, and I have not seen anything from him but faithfulness and honoring of agreements."

Huyayy persisted, eventually gaining entry. He argued that this was the greatest army ever assembled — 10,000 warriors — and that Muhammad would never recover from it. If Ka'b joined them, the Jewish tribes would regain their position in Medina. If the Confederates failed — which he claimed was impossible — Ka'b could simply return to his previous arrangement with the Prophet.

Ka'b was convinced. He tore the treaty document and declared Banu Qurayza's alliance with the Confederates.

## Huyayy's Personal Guarantee

To seal the arrangement, Huyayy reportedly made Ka'b a personal guarantee: if the Confederate army withdrew without achieving its objective, Huyayy himself would enter Banu Qurayza's fortress with them and share their fate. This guarantee was significant — it demonstrated that Huyayy was fully committed to the alliance and was not simply using Banu Qurayza as a pawn.

He kept his word: when the Confederate siege failed and Banu Qurayza's position became untenable, Huyayy entered their fortress and remained with them through the subsequent siege. He was among those killed after Banu Qurayza surrendered to the Muslim forces.

## The Effect on the Muslim Camp

When news of Banu Qurayza's defection reached the Prophet, the effect on the besieged Muslim community was severe. The trench protected the northern approaches to Medina, but Banu Qurayza's fortresses were in the southern residential areas — if Banu Qurayza attacked, Muslim families and non-combatants would be at risk. The Prophet sent a party of 400 to guard the southern areas and secure the families.

Morale in the Muslim camp fell sharply. The Quran captured this moment: *"[Remember] when they came at you from above you and from below you, and when eyes shifted [in fear], and hearts reached the throats and you assumed about Allah [various] assumptions."* (Quran 33:10)

## Nu'aym ibn Masud's Counter-Deception

The defection of Banu Qurayza was partially neutralized through the strategic deception carried out by Nu'aym ibn Masud, a Ghafatan tribesman who secretly accepted Islam and offered his services to the Prophet. By sowing distrust between the Confederates and Banu Qurayza — telling each party that the other planned to betray them — Nu'aym undermined the coalition's ability to coordinate an attack at the critical moment.

## Huyayy's Trial

After the failure of the Confederate siege and the subsequent Muslim siege of Banu Qurayza's fortress (which lasted approximately 25 days), Banu Qurayza surrendered and agreed to accept the judgment of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, the chief of Banu Aws (a tribe with whom Banu Qurayza had a long alliance). Sa'd's judgment — that the fighting men be killed, the women and children taken as captives, and their wealth distributed — was carried out.

Huyayy ibn Akhtab was among those executed. At his execution, he reportedly said: "I do not blame myself for having opposed Muhammad," attributing the failure to divine decree while maintaining his position. He was executed shortly after his statement.

## Sources

The account of Huyayy ibn Akhtab and the Banu Qurayza affair is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*. The Quranic account is in Surah al-Ahzab (33:9-27).
