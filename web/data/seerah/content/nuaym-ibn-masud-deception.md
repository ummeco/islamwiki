# Nu'aym ibn Mas'ud's Strategic Deception

During the Battle of the Trench (5 AH), with the Confederate army besieging Medina and Banu Qurayza having broken their treaty with the Muslims, Nu'aym ibn Mas'ud al-Ashja'i carried out a covert intelligence operation that is studied in Islamic military history as a textbook example of strategic deception — sowing distrust between the Confederates and Banu Qurayza, effectively paralyzing the most dangerous attack the Muslim community had yet faced.

## Nu'aym's Secret Conversion

Nu'aym ibn Mas'ud was from the Ghafatan tribe — part of the Confederate alliance against the Muslims. He secretly came to the Prophet and declared his Islam, offering his services. He said: "O Messenger of Allah, I have become a Muslim without my people knowing. So command me to do whatever you wish."

The Prophet replied: "You are only one man among us. So go and create confusion among the enemy for us, for war is deception (al-harbu khud'ah)." — citing the principle that strategic deception is permissible in warfare.

## The Operation: Three Conversations

Nu'aym devised a plan to sow mutual distrust between three parties: the Quraysh-led Confederates, the Ghafatan tribal contingent, and Banu Qurayza. He visited each party separately and planted doubts about the others.

**To Banu Qurayza:** He went to the Banu Qurayza fortress — he had been their friend before his conversion — and told them: "You know my affection for you and the trust between us. The Quraysh and Ghafatan are not in the same situation as you. This is your land and your wealth, your women and your children. You cannot just move if things go badly. But the Quraysh and Ghafatan — if they find an opportunity they will fight, but if things are difficult they will go back to their own lands and leave you to face Muhammad alone. So do not fight with them until you take hostages from their nobles — men who you hold as surety — so that if they withdraw you can use the hostages to reach a settlement with Muhammad."

Banu Qurayza agreed this was wise counsel.

**To Quraysh:** Nu'aym went to Abu Sufyan (the Confederate commander) and said: "I have learned something and considered it my duty to inform you, so keep this confidential. Banu Qurayza have regretted what they did (breaking the treaty with Muhammad) and have sent to him asking if they should send him hostages from among you — nobles of Quraysh and Ghafatan — whom he can execute as an atonement for their treaty violation. So if they ask you for hostages, do not send a single man."

**To Ghafatan:** He gave the same warning to the Ghafatan tribal contingent.

## The Effect

Nu'aym's deception was perfectly designed. When the Confederates now coordinated with Banu Qurayza and asked them to attack the Muslim positions, Banu Qurayza (following the advice they thought they had received from a trusted friend) demanded hostages first. Abu Sufyan and the Ghafatan leaders, hearing this demand, immediately thought: "Nu'aym told us the truth."

Each party now viewed the other with suspicion. Banu Qurayza feared they were being used and would be abandoned. The Confederates feared Banu Qurayza was negotiating with the Prophet behind their backs. The coordinated simultaneous attack that might have broken the Muslim defenses never materialized.

## The Role in the Confederate Withdrawal

Nu'aym's deception was one of several factors that contributed to the Confederate retreat. The other major factors were:

- The trench itself, which had blocked the army for weeks
- The failure to find crossing points
- Supply difficulties as the siege extended
- The strong winds and cold that swept the camp (the Quran references this: "We sent against them a wind and armies you did not see" — Quran 33:9)
- Declining morale and tribal tensions within the coalition

When Abu Sufyan finally ordered the withdrawal, he could count multiple reasons for failure — and Nu'aym's operation was among the most consequential of the factors that prevented the decisive blow from being struck.

## Legal and Ethical Dimensions

Nu'aym's operation is cited in Islamic military jurisprudence in discussions of the permissibility of deception in war. The Prophet's authorization of the operation and his statement "al-harbu khud'ah" (war is deception) established that strategic deception of the enemy is permissible in Islamic warfare — a principle affirmed by all major schools of jurisprudence.

The operation involved Nu'aym making statements to different parties that were designed to deceive — technically lying to each about what the others had said. Islamic jurists have analyzed whether this constitutes prohibited lying (kidhb) or the permissible strategic deception of warfare. The mainstream position holds that wartime tactical deception of the enemy (as opposed to lying to Muslims or to protected persons) is permissible, and the Prophet's explicit authorization settles the matter.

## Nu'aym After the Trench

Nu'aym continued as a Muslim and participated in subsequent campaigns. He later became an important figure in the early Muslim community of Medina. His conversion story — a Confederate fighter who secretly embraced Islam and turned the Confederate alliance's greatest strategic opportunity into its decisive failure — made him a celebrated figure in early Islamic military history.

## Sources

The account of Nu'aym ibn Mas'ud is recorded in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*. The phrase "al-harbu khud'ah" is in Sahih Bukhari and Sahih Muslim.
