# Construction of the Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal, built between approximately 1042-1058 AH (1632-1648 CE) by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal, is widely considered the greatest architectural achievement of the Mughal Empire and one of the finest examples of Islamic architecture in the world. A mausoleum complex on the banks of the Yamuna River in Agra, it represents the culmination of centuries of development in Islamic funerary and garden architecture.

## Background

Shah Jahan (r. 1037-1068 AH / 1628-1658 CE) was the fifth Mughal Emperor and a passionate patron of architecture. His reign is often called the golden age of Mughal architecture. Among his many building projects — including the Red Fort in Delhi, the Jama Masjid, and extensive additions to the Agra Fort — the Taj Mahal stands apart.

Mumtaz Mahal (born Arjumand Banu Begum, 998-1040 AH / 1593-1631 CE) was Shah Jahan's third and most beloved wife. She died in 1040 AH (1631 CE) while giving birth to their fourteenth child in Burhanpur. Shah Jahan was reportedly devastated by her death and reportedly went into mourning for two years, emerging with his hair turned white. He resolved to build her the greatest tomb ever constructed.

## Design and Construction

The chief architect is traditionally identified as Ustad Ahmad Lahauri, though the historical evidence for this attribution is debated. The complex was designed through collaboration among architects from across the Mughal Empire and beyond, drawing on Persian, Central Asian, Indian, and Ottoman architectural traditions.

**The Main Mausoleum.** The central structure is a white marble mausoleum on a raised marble platform (plinth). The building is symmetrical on all four sides, featuring four slender minarets at the corners and a central bulbous dome approximately 58 meters high, flanked by four smaller domed chattris (kiosks). The entire surface is covered in white marble from Makrana, Rajasthan, inlaid with colored gemstones in intricate floral patterns using the pietra dura technique.

**The Calligraphy.** Quranic verses are inlaid in black marble calligraphy around the arched portals and throughout the interior. The calligraphic program was designed by Amanat Khan Shirazi, whose name appears inscribed at the base of the main arch. The choice of verses emphasizes themes of paradise, divine mercy, and the resurrection — appropriate to a funerary monument. The calligraphy on the main entrance gate features Surah al-Fajr (89), with the invitation to "enter My Garden" (89:30) prominently displayed.

**The Garden.** The complex includes a formal char bagh (four-quartered garden) divided by water channels — a classic Islamic garden design representing the four rivers of paradise described in the Quran (47:15). The garden is approximately 300 by 300 meters and originally featured more diverse plantings than the formal lawns of today.

**Supporting Structures.** The complex includes a mosque (masjid) on the west side and a mirror-image jawab (guest house) on the east, flanking the main mausoleum on both sides to maintain the strict symmetry of the overall design.

## Construction

The project employed an estimated 20,000 artisans, craftsmen, and laborers from across South Asia and Central Asia. Materials were transported from across the empire and beyond: white marble from Makrana, red sandstone from Fatehpur Sikri, cornelian from Baghdad, turquoise from Tibet, jade from China, jasper from Punjab, diamonds from Golconda.

Construction took approximately 16 years for the main mausoleum and garden complex, from 1042 AH (1632 CE) to approximately 1058 AH (1648 CE). The surrounding outworks were completed later.

## Theological Significance

The Taj Mahal is explicitly an Islamic monument. Its Quranic inscriptions, its garden plan based on Quranic descriptions of paradise, and its function as a mausoleum all situate it within the Islamic funerary tradition that combines commemoration of the dead with the affirmation of resurrection and divine mercy.

The classical scholars had debated the permissibility of elaborate tombs — the hadith literature contains prohibitions on building over graves. The Mughal rulers, following the Hanafi school, permitted and actively promoted monumental funerary architecture, while traditional scholars, particularly those influenced by Wahhabi thought, have criticized this tradition.

## Legacy

The Taj Mahal is the most visited monument in India and one of the most recognized structures in the world. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 CE, it has become a universal symbol of love, architectural beauty, and Mughal civilization. For Muslims worldwide, it stands as evidence of the extraordinary aesthetic achievements of Islamic civilization at its height.
