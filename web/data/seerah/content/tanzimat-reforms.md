# Ottoman Tanzimat Reforms

## The Crisis That Produced Tanzimat

By the first decades of the nineteenth century, the Ottoman Empire faced a structural crisis that its ruling class could not ignore. In a generation, it had lost Greece (recognized independent 1829 after a war of independence backed by European powers), been nearly destroyed by its own viceroy in Egypt (Muhammad Ali Pasha's campaigns), watched Serbia gain autonomy, and seen the humiliating contrast between its military performance and European armies become impossible to rationalize. The empire was behind — militarily, administratively, technologically, and economically — and its educated officials knew it.

The Ottoman response was the Tanzimat — a term meaning "reorganization" or "reordering" — a period of top-down reform initiated by the imperial government that ran from 1255 AH / 1839 CE through approximately 1293 AH / 1876 CE. The reforms were driven primarily by a new class of Western-educated Ottoman bureaucrats who had absorbed European political ideas through diplomatic service, study abroad, and the French-language press that circulated among the Ottoman elite. Their program was not simply imitation of Europe — they were Ottoman patriots trying to save the empire — but it involved the systematic introduction of principles and institutions drawn from the European experience: legal equality for all subjects regardless of religion, codified law, representative institutions, and secular courts for commercial matters.

The Tanzimat period produced transformations in Ottoman governance that permanently altered the empire's character and whose legacy shaped the successor states — Turkey, the Arab countries — for the twentieth century.

## The Hatt-i Sharif of Gülhane: 1255 AH / 1839 CE

The opening document of the Tanzimat era was the Hatt-i Sharif of Gülhane (the Noble Edict of the Rose Chamber), proclaimed by Sultan Abdülmecid I on November 3, 1839 CE. It was drafted primarily by Mustafa Reshid Pasha, the Foreign Minister who had spent years as Ottoman ambassador in London and Paris and who understood that European backing for Ottoman territorial integrity would require demonstrated progress toward "civilization" as Europeans defined it.

The Hatt-i Sharif announced three fundamental principles: the guarantee of life, honor, and property for all subjects regardless of religion; the regularization of taxation to replace arbitrary exactions; and the regularization of military conscription to replace the irregular and brutal press-gang methods then in use. The announcement of these principles before an assembly of ministers, provincial governors, religious leaders, and foreign ambassadors was itself a significant gesture — it implied a constitutional dimension to the reform, though there was no mechanism for enforcement.

The edict's guarantee of equality regardless of religion was the most revolutionary element. The Ottoman millet system had for centuries organized non-Muslims into self-governing communities under their own religious leadership, tolerated as dhimmis paying the jizya tax. The Tanzimat proposed a different principle: that all subjects of the empire, whether Muslim, Christian, or Jewish, were equal before the law and had identical civic rights. This was in tension with classical Islamic governance theory, which distinguished between Muslim and non-Muslim subjects, and the Islamic scholars (ulama) viewed it with concern even where they did not openly oppose it.

## The Hatt-i Humayun of 1272 AH / 1856 CE

A second foundational document, the Hatt-i Humayun (Imperial Edict) of 1856, came in the immediate aftermath of the Crimean War — which the Ottomans had fought alongside Britain and France against Russia — and reflected European pressure to formalize and extend the rights of Christian minorities in the empire as a condition of continued European support. It was more specific than the 1839 edict: it explicitly abolished remaining distinctions between Muslim and non-Muslim subjects in taxation, military service, law courts, and access to government positions. Non-Muslims could now serve in the Ottoman army (or pay a fee instead), attend Ottoman state schools, and hold government offices from which they had previously been excluded.

The reaction among Muslim subjects was mixed. Many ordinary Muslims felt that the reforms degraded their status — that the elevation of non-Muslim subjects to formal equality with Muslims was an unacceptable concession to European pressure rather than a genuine Ottoman initiative. The Balkan Christian communities, conversely, found that formal legal equality did not satisfy their nationalist aspirations for independent statehood, and the reform period coincided with the intensification rather than the resolution of the "Eastern Question" — the problem of what would happen as nationalist movements among Ottoman Christian subjects gained strength.

## Legal Codification: The Mecelle

One of the most significant jurisprudential achievements of the Tanzimat was the Mecelle (Mecelle-i Ahkâm-i Adliyye — the Civil Code of Legal Rules), compiled between 1284-1293 AH / 1867-1876 CE under the direction of Ahmed Cevdet Pasha. The Mecelle was the first systematic codification of Islamic civil law in the Hanafi tradition — an attempt to organize the vast and often internally inconsistent corpus of classical Hanafi fiqh into a Western-style civil code with numbered articles that could be applied by state courts.

The project was significant for several reasons. It demonstrated that Islamic law could be systematized and codified in a form compatible with modern judicial administration — a response to those who argued that only European-style secular law could meet the needs of a modern state. It established a committee of scholars working collaboratively on legal codification, rather than individual fatwas by individual muftis. And its structure — modeled on the Napoleonic Code — showed the influence of European legal forms even when the substantive content remained Islamic.

The Mecelle governed civil transactions in the Ottoman Empire and its successor states for decades; it remained in force in some countries into the twentieth century.

## The New Bureaucratic Elite and the Ulama's Diminishing Role

The Tanzimat period created, or accelerated the creation of, a new type of Ottoman official: educated in European languages (primarily French), familiar with European political thought and administrative methods, trained in secular schools rather than in the medresse system, and oriented toward Western models of governance. Figures like Mustafa Reshid Pasha, Ali Pasha, and Fuad Pasha embodied this new elite — they were Muslim Ottomans who were simultaneously Europeanized in their intellectual formation and committed to the empire's survival.

This new elite did not simply replace the traditional ulama but competed with them for influence over the empire's direction. The ulama retained control of religious law, personal status courts (divorce, inheritance, custody), religious education through the medresse network, and the enormous waqf endowment system. But the Tanzimat progressively transferred jurisdiction over commercial disputes, criminal law, and administrative matters to new courts applying new codes — not Islamic law but codes modeled on French and European precedents.

The reform of the waqf system was particularly significant. Waqf endowments — properties dedicated in perpetuity to religious and charitable purposes — had funded mosques, schools, hospitals, and the scholarly establishment for centuries. The Tanzimat period saw increased state supervision of waqf administration and eventually the concentration of waqf management in a Ministry of Waqfs, effectively giving the state control over revenues that had previously been independent of government. This was part of a broader pattern of subordinating Islamic institutional life to the state that both the Tanzimat reformers and their successors pursued.

## The Young Ottomans: Islam and Constitutionalism

Not all Ottoman intellectuals accepted the Tanzimat's implicit framing of reform as Westernization. A generation of Ottoman writers and thinkers known as the Young Ottomans — including Ibrahim Shinasi, Namik Kemal, and Ziya Pasha — argued in the 1860s and 1870s for a constitutionalist transformation of the empire that would draw on Islamic principles rather than simply imitating European models.

Namik Kemal (1256-1304 AH / 1840-1888 CE) was the most influential of these figures. In his journalism and plays — he was imprisoned and exiled multiple times for his writing — he argued that Islamic political thought contained genuine principles of shura (consultation), adl (justice), and limits on despotism that paralleled Western constitutionalist ideas. The solution to Ottoman decline was not to abandon Islamic principles but to recover their authentic application. His play Vatan yahut Silistre (Fatherland or Silistria) stirred intense patriotic sentiment and was suppressed by the government.

The Young Ottomans' vision — a constitutional Islamic empire — was different from both the secular Tanzimat reformers and the traditional ulama. It represented an attempt to synthesize Islamic governance theory with nineteenth-century constitutional thought that would recur throughout the Arab and Muslim world in the twentieth century.

## The Constitution of 1293 AH / 1876 CE and Its Suspension

The culmination of the Tanzimat era was the Ottoman Constitution of 1876, drafted primarily by Midhat Pasha. It established a parliament with an elected Chamber of Deputies and an appointed Senate, guaranteed civil liberties, and established an independent judiciary. For a brief period in 1877-1878, an Ottoman parliament actually met and legislated — a genuinely historic moment.

Sultan Abdülhamid II, who had accepted the constitution as the price of his accession to the throne in 1876, suspended it in 1878, citing the emergency created by the Ottoman-Russian war of 1877-1878. He ruled without parliament for thirty years, until the Young Turk Revolution of 1908 forced its reinstatement. His reign represented a different response to Ottoman decline — not Westernizing reform but pan-Islamic solidarity and authoritarian modernization.

## The Tanzimat's Contradictions

The Tanzimat era's internal contradictions were profound and ultimately unresolvable within the Ottoman framework. The reformers sought to save the empire by adopting European methods while retaining its multi-ethnic, Muslim-led character. But the European methods they adopted — legal equality regardless of religion, secular courts, codified law replacing sharia — undermined the specifically Islamic basis of Ottoman governance that had justified Muslim subjects' loyalty to the empire. Meanwhile, the Christian minorities whose rights the reforms were partly designed to protect were increasingly drawn toward nationalism and independence rather than Ottoman loyalty, however reformed.

The legal reforms created a dual system — Islamic courts for personal status matters, secular courts for everything else — that satisfied neither Islamic scholars (who saw their jurisdiction shrinking) nor secular modernizers (who found the Islamic courts incompatible with full legal rationalization). This dual system, with its implications for the status of Islamic law, would recur in post-Ottoman Muslim states throughout the twentieth century.

The Tanzimat did not save the empire. What it did was create administrative institutions, a legal infrastructure, and an educated elite that would form the foundation of the successor states — including the Turkish Republic, which took Tanzimat secularism to its logical conclusion, and the Arab states, which inherited both the administrative apparatus and the unresolved questions about the relationship between Islamic law and modern governance.
