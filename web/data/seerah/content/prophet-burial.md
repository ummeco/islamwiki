# Burial of the Prophet

The burial of the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) on 13 Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH (approximately 9-10 June 632 CE) was carried out with careful deliberation by his family and closest companions, in circumstances made uniquely difficult by the grief of the community and the question of where and how a prophet should be interred.

## The Delay in Burial

The Prophet's body lay in Aisha's chamber while the companions debated and the political crisis of succession was resolved at the Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah. By Arabian custom, the dead were buried quickly; but the prophetic burial required both resolution of the leadership question and careful preparation.

There was also a brief debate about where the burial should occur. Various opinions were raised: Mecca (the sacred city of his birth), the Baqee' cemetery in Medina where many companions had been buried, or even Jerusalem. Abu Bakr settled the question by citing a hadith he had heard from the Prophet: "No prophet is buried except in the place where he died." The burial would take place in Aisha's chamber.

## Preparation of the Body

The Prophet's body was washed by members of his family and close male relatives. The primary participants were: Ali ibn Abi Talib (his cousin and son-in-law), al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (his uncle), al-Fadl ibn Abbas, Qutham ibn Abbas, Usamah ibn Zayd, and Shaqran (a servant). They kept the Prophet's garments on during the washing, out of respect.

He was wrapped in three white cloths from Yemen — not the two-piece ihram garments and not a shirt with a collar, but three enveloping sheets. His body was then placed on a bed.

## The Prayer Over the Prophet

The funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) over the Prophet was performed in a manner unlike any other funeral in Islamic history. The companions could not agree on who would lead the prayer — it was not fitting for any single person to stand in the Prophet's place as imam — and so the prayer was performed in groups without a single imam.

The men entered in groups: first the men of the Prophet's family, then the Muhajirun (Meccan emigrants), then the Ansar (Medinan supporters), then women, then children. Each group prayed the funeral prayer individually over the body. The prayer continued through the night and into the following day, with groups rotating to allow all Muslims access.

## The Grave

A grave was dug in Aisha's chamber beneath the bed on which the Prophet had died. There was a brief question about the type of grave: Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah, who had learned the Syrian style (shaq — a straight trench), and Abu Talha al-Ansari, who knew the Medinan style (lahd — a niche dug into the side of the trench at the bottom, in which the body is placed), were both mentioned. The companions sent for both, praying that Allah would determine who arrived first. Abu Talha arrived first, and the lahd-style grave was used — the style that became the Sunnah for burial.

## The Burial

The Prophet was lowered into the grave by Ali ibn Abi Talib, al-Fadl ibn Abbas, and Qutham ibn Abbas. He was placed on his right side, facing the qiblah (Mecca). The grave was covered with seven unfired bricks and raised slightly above ground level — the Sunnah being to mark the grave without elevating it elaborately.

## The Location and Later History

The Prophet was buried in Aisha's chamber, which was adjacent to the Masjid an-Nabawi. The graves of Abu Bakr and Umar ibn al-Khattab were later placed in the same chamber (both requested to be buried near the Prophet). Aisha lived in the chamber until her death in 58 AH, navigating her life around the graves with the screens that separated her living space from the burial site.

Over subsequent generations, the mosque expanded to incorporate the chamber. The current green dome — an Ottoman construction — marks the location of the graves. The site today is within the main prayer hall of the Masjid an-Nabawi in Medina, and the area around the Prophet's grave (the rawdah — the "garden" between the Prophet's grave and his minbar) is considered by the Prophet himself to have said it is "a garden from the gardens of Paradise."

The Prophet had warned against making graves into places of worship. Islamic scholars across the centuries have maintained a careful distinction between the permissibility of visiting the Prophet's grave and giving salutation (salam) upon him — which is encouraged — and any practice of directing prayer to or through the Prophet, which is rejected as shirk (associating partners with Allah).

## Sources

The burial is documented in detail across the major hadith collections: Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, and the biographical works of Ibn Hisham and Ibn Saad (al-Tabaqat al-Kubra).
