# The Prophet's Letter to Heraclius, Emperor of Byzantium

In 7 AH (628-629 CE), the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) dispatched letters to the rulers of the major powers of his age, inviting them to Islam. The letter to Heraclius (Hiraql) — Byzantine Emperor (r. 610-641 CE) — is the most documented of these letters, preserved in detail by Sahih Bukhari through the account of Abu Sufyan, who was present when Heraclius summoned him to his court.

## The Letter

The Prophet sent Dihyah ibn Khalifah al-Kalbi as his messenger to Heraclius. The letter read:

*"In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate. From Muhammad, the servant of Allah and His Messenger, to Heraclius, the great leader of the Romans. Peace be upon whoever follows the guidance. To proceed: I invite you to Islam. Accept Islam and you will be safe; accept Islam and Allah will give you a double reward. If you turn away, then upon you is the sin of your people."*

The letter then cited the Quranic verse (3:64): *"Say, 'O People of the Scripture, come to a word that is equitable between us and you — that we will not worship except Allah and not associate anything with Him and not take one another as lords instead of Allah.'"*

## Heraclius's Investigation

The account preserved in Sahih Bukhari (through Ibn Abbas) reports that when Dihyah arrived at the Byzantine court, Heraclius received the letter and was interested enough to conduct an inquiry. He sent for Arabs who might know Muhammad, and Abu Sufyan ibn Harb — at that time still a non-Muslim and a trading enemy of the Prophet — was brought from a trading party in the region.

Heraclius questioned Abu Sufyan through an interpreter:

- **"What is his lineage?"** — "Noble."
- **"Has anyone among you claimed prophethood before him?"** — "No."
- **"Was he ever accused of lying before his claim?"** — "No."
- **"Do the nobles follow him or the weak?"** — "The weak."
- **"Are his followers increasing or decreasing?"** — "Increasing."
- **"Has anyone apostatized in disappointment?"** — "No."
- **"Does he betray?"** — "No, though we have a truce with him now."
- **"Have you ever fought him? What were the results?"** — "Sometimes we beat him, sometimes he beats us."
- **"What does he command?"** — "He commands us to worship Allah alone, not to associate anything with Him, to pray, to give charity, to be chaste, to keep our oaths."

After the questioning, Heraclius reportedly said to Abu Sufyan: "I asked you about his lineage and you said it was noble — prophets are sent from noble lineages. I asked if anyone had claimed prophethood before him and you said no — if one had, I would have said he was following a precedent. I asked if he had ever been accused of lying and you said no — a man who does not lie to people will not lie to Allah..."

He concluded: "If what you say is true, he will take the ground beneath my feet. I knew a prophet was coming, but I did not think he would come from among you."

## Heraclius's Reaction to His Bishops

According to the Bukhari account, Heraclius gathered his bishops and announced his intention to consider Islam — at which point they protested vehemently. He reportedly backed down, telling them he had only been testing their faith. Some seerah scholars interpret this as Heraclius personally being convinced but politically unable to abandon his position.

## Historical Assessment

The Bukhari account is among the most important non-Muslim source documents for early Islamic history, precisely because it records Abu Sufyan — then a hostile non-Muslim witness — testifying to the consistency and character of the Prophet's claims. Heraclius's interrogation is a model of investigation: he asked methodical questions designed to test whether the signs of genuine prophethood were present.

Whether Heraclius privately believed is unknown. He did not accept Islam. He died in 641 CE, and within a decade of the Prophet's letter the Arab-Muslim armies had taken most of Byzantine Syria — including Jerusalem — fulfilling the territorial transformation Heraclius had apparently anticipated.

## Historical Context

The letter was sent during or just after the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, which had given the Prophet a period of peace and the political confidence to reach out to the major powers. Multiple rulers received similar letters: the Sassanid Emperor Khosrow II (who tore the letter — reportedly an ill omen), the Negus of Abyssinia, Muqawqis of Egypt, and others.

## Sources

The primary account is in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab Bad al-Wahy and Kitab al-Jihad). Ibn Hisham's seerah and al-Tabari's history preserve the full text of the letter. The historical account of Heraclius is corroborated by Byzantine sources that note his troubled reign and the Arab expansion.
