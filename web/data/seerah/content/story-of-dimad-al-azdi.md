# Dimad al-Azdi and the Incomparable Khutba

Among the many encounters that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had with the people of Arabia in the Meccan period, few demonstrate more directly the intrinsic power of the Prophetic speech than the meeting with Dimad al-Azdi. He came to Mecca as a healer, heard the rumors that Quraysh was spreading about the Prophet ﷺ, and offered to cure him — and left as a Muslim, converted not by argument or social pressure but by the force of words he had never heard anything like.

The episode is recorded with particular care in Sahih Muslim, where it appears as a complete narrative in the chapter on the Friday sermon, and it preserves what scholars have called the Khutbat al-Haja — the opening words of the Prophet's ﷺ sermon — in one of their earliest and most complete forms.

## Who Was Dimad al-Azdi?

Dimad (may Allah be pleased with him) was from the tribe of Azd Shanu'a, a Yemeni tribal grouping. He was a practitioner of traditional Arabian healing — a specialist in what the pre-Islamic world called ruqya, the treatment of mental illness and possession through spiritual means combining spoken words, spells, herbs, and ritual acts. His practice was not fringe by the standards of his time and culture; such practitioners were respected figures in Arabian society, sought out for conditions that the ordinary physician could not address.

He traveled widely for his work. The accounts do not specify exactly when he came to Mecca — the scholars place this encounter somewhere in the middle-to-late Meccan period, before the Hijra — but his presence there was professional: he had come to ply his trade.

## The Quraysh's Claim About the Prophet

When Dimad arrived in Mecca, he encountered the Quraysh's ongoing propaganda campaign against the Prophet ﷺ. Among the labels Quraysh used to dismiss the Prophet ﷺ and warn people against following him was majnun — possessed, or crazy, a term with specific cultural resonance in a world where mental illness was attributed to jinn possession. By calling the Prophet ﷺ "majnun," they were simultaneously dismissing his message as incoherent and explaining his unusual behavior (the revelation experiences, the claims of angelic contact) within a framework that their audience understood.

For Dimad, this was a professional opportunity. If there was a man in Mecca suffering from possession, he might be able to help. The accounts suggest he heard the Quraysh's claims at face value — not necessarily believing them himself, but taking them as a presenting symptom to investigate. He sought out the Prophet ﷺ and offered his services.

## The Prophet's Response: Khutbat al-Haja

What the Prophet ﷺ said to Dimad in response to the offer of healing is the heart of this episode. The Prophet ﷺ gave what is now known as Khutbat al-Haja — the "sermon of need" or "invocation of need" — a formulation of opening praise and testimony that he used at the beginning of important addresses. Sahih Muslim records it in the narration of Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him and his father):

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Inna al-hamda lillah, nahmaduhu wa-nasta'inuhu wa-nastaghfiruhu. Wa-na'udhu billahi min shururi anfusina wa-min sayyi'ati a'malina. Man yahdihillahu fala mudilla lahu, wa-man yudlilhu fala hadiya lahu. Wa-ashhadu an la ilaha illallahu wahdahu la sharika lahu, wa-ashhadu anna Muhammadan abduhu wa-rasuluh."

In translation: "Verily all praise belongs to Allah — we praise Him, seek His help, and ask His forgiveness. We seek refuge in Allah from the evils of our own souls and from the misdeeds of our actions. Whoever Allah guides, none can misguide him; and whoever He leads astray, none can guide him. I bear witness that there is no god but Allah alone, with no partner; and I bear witness that Muhammad is His servant and Messenger."

The Prophet ﷺ then added: "Amma ba'd" (as for what follows) — and proceeded with whatever was the substance of his address.

## Dimad's Reaction

Dimad heard these words and stopped. His reaction is recorded with precision in the Sahih Muslim narration: he said, "I have heard the words of soothsayers (kuhhan), the incantations of sorcerers, and the speech of poets — but I have never heard anything like these words. They have reached the depth of the ocean."

This is a remarkable statement from a man whose profession required him to know the full range of extraordinary speech that existed in Arabia. Dimad was not an ordinary listener — he was a specialist in unusual language, a man who dealt daily with incantations, formulas, and ritual speech. His categories were well-developed and his comparisons were not idle. He had a professional basis for saying that what he heard exceeded the range of everything he knew.

The power he recognized in the words was not rhetorical cleverness or poetic elegance — though the Arabic of the Khutbat al-Haja is beautiful. What struck him was something he described as reaching "the depth of the ocean" — an Arabic expression for encompassing totality, saying everything that could be said about something. The words contained a completeness he had not encountered before.

Dimad extended his hand to the Prophet ﷺ and said: "Extend your hand to me that I may pledge allegiance to you on Islam." He took the shahada and became a Muslim on the spot.

## The Prophet's Response to His People

What followed demonstrates another dimension of the Prophet's ﷺ character and method. When Dimad pledged allegiance, the Prophet ﷺ said: "And for your people." This was not merely a blessing or a general wish — it was a specific statement that Dimad's Islam extended in its benefit to his tribe. The Prophet ﷺ recognized immediately that a practitioner with Dimad's reputation and social standing could be an effective transmitter of the message among his own people.

Dimad said: "And for my people." He accepted this responsibility without hesitation.

The exchange reflects the Prophet's ﷺ consistent approach to individual conversions: he saw each person not only as an individual soul to be guided but as a node in a social network through which the message could travel further. Every conversion was also a potential gateway.

## The Theological Significance of the Khutbat al-Haja

The opening formula that converted Dimad has been used as the opening of sermons, lectures, and formal addresses throughout Islamic civilization from the Prophet's ﷺ lifetime to the present day. Its continued use is not mere tradition or sentiment — it contains a complete theological statement in compressed form.

The praise of Allah (hamd) establishes the fundamental orientation: everything begins with Allah. The seeking of help and forgiveness (isti'ana and istighfar) acknowledges human dependency and human fallibility simultaneously. The refuge from the evils of the self (shurur al-nafs) acknowledges that the greatest danger a person faces is internal. The declaration that guidance belongs entirely to Allah establishes tawhid in its applied, practical form — there is no guidance from any source other than Him.

The scholars of Islamic rhetoric (bayan) have analyzed this formula extensively. Al-Nawawi in his commentary on Sahih Muslim and others before and after him note that it achieves in approximately fifty Arabic words what elaborate theological treatises spend chapters trying to establish. The economy of the language is itself part of what Dimad recognized as unlike anything he had encountered.

The later tradition of beginning formal addresses with this formula is supported by multiple narrations. The Prophet ﷺ reportedly used it at the beginning of important sermons, and the Companions preserved it and transmitted it to their students. Its use became a mark of following the Prophetic method in formal speech — and a constant reminder of the Dimad episode in which these words alone were sufficient to convert a healer who had come to offer a cure.

## What His Conversion Represents

The episode of Dimad al-Azdi is often cited in the theological literature as evidence for what the scholars call i'jaz al-Quran and i'jaz al-nubuwwa — the inimitability of the Quran and the miraculous character of the Prophet's ﷺ speech. The argument runs as follows: here was a man professionally equipped to evaluate extraordinary speech, with no social motive to convert (he was not from Mecca, had no existing relationships with the Muslims, was not under social pressure), who heard the Khutbat al-Haja and declared it exceeded everything he knew.

Dimad's conversion was purely based on the content and quality of what he heard. There was no gradual process of socialization, no family influence, no economic incentive. He heard words, recognized in them something beyond ordinary human speech, and responded accordingly.

This is why the scholars consistently place this episode in the category of proof rather than merely in the category of history. The testimony of an expert — someone with genuine competence to judge what he was evaluating — carries particular evidential weight. Dimad was that expert, and his verdict was unambiguous.

## The Legacy of the Khutbat al-Haja

The opening formula that the Prophet ﷺ gave Dimad (and used consistently in his public addresses) has been transmitted through the generations in an unbroken chain. Every Friday khutba that opens with these words, every academic lecture by a Muslim scholar, every formal address at a Muslim gathering that begins with the hamdalah and shahada — all of these carry the trace of that moment in Mecca when a Yemeni healer heard something that reached the bottom of the ocean and could not look away.

The formula itself is a form of dhikr (remembrance of Allah) compressed into the opening of speech. By beginning an address with praise of Allah, acknowledgment of human dependence on divine guidance, and the testimony of faith, the speaker orients both himself and his audience before a single word of substantive content is uttered. This is the pedagogical wisdom embedded in what might appear to be a formulaic opening.

Muslim scholars who study rhetoric (ilm al-bayan) and those who study the sciences of hadith both draw from this episode. The hadith scholars cite it for the chain of transmission of the Khutbat al-Haja and its status as established Sunnah. The rhetoric scholars cite it for the effect of the words on Dimad as evidence of the Prophet's unique eloquence — which the Quran (18:110) emphasizes was not his own but was divine communication through a human vessel.
