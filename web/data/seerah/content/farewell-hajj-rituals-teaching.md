# Teaching of Hajj Rituals During the Farewell Pilgrimage

During the Farewell Hajj of 10 AH, the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) performed every rite of Hajj with the explicit awareness that tens of thousands of companions were watching and learning — and that these rites, performed by him, would constitute the normative model for all future generations of Muslims. He said: "Take your Hajj rites from me." This statement transformed the Farewell Hajj into a living instructional event.

## The Entry into Mecca

The Prophet entered Mecca on the 4th of Dhul Hijjah (some sources say 3rd). He entered through the higher pass (Thaniyyat Kada'), as was his practice when entering the city. On arrival, he proceeded directly to the Masjid al-Haram.

Upon reaching the Ka'bah, he began the tawaf — the circumambulation of the Ka'bah seven times, beginning and ending at the Black Stone (al-Hajar al-Aswad). He walked briskly (raml) in the first three circuits and at a normal pace in the remaining four. He touched or gestured toward the Black Stone at each circuit's beginning.

## The Sa'i Between Safa and Marwa

After the tawaf, the Prophet performed the sa'i — seven transits between the hills of Safa and Marwa, commemorating Hajar's search for water for her son Ismail. He climbed Safa first, faced the Ka'bah, recited the takbir (Allahu Akbar) three times, and supplicated. He walked to Marwa, running between the two green markers at the valley floor (a practice maintained today within the enclosed sa'i corridor).

## The Standing at Arafat

On the 9th of Dhul Hijjah — the central day of Hajj — the Prophet proceeded to the plain of Arafat after the morning prayer. He stood near Jabal al-Rahmah (the Mount of Mercy), delivering the Farewell Sermon, which covered fundamental Islamic principles of law, ethics, and social justice.

The standing at Arafat (wuquf) is the essential act of Hajj without which the pilgrimage is invalid. The Prophet stated: "Hajj is Arafat." He prayed Dhuhr and Asr combined at midday and then stood in supplication until sunset.

## Muzdalifah and the Night

After sunset, the Prophet departed Arafat calmly — not rushing — and moved to Muzdalifah. He prayed Maghrib and Isha combined upon arrival and spent the night there. In the morning, he prayed Fajr early and then performed another period of dhikr (remembrance) and supplication while facing the qiblah, remaining until the sky brightened.

He then departed Muzdalifah before sunrise (an exception was made for the weak, elderly, and women to depart earlier). At Muzdalifah, pebbles were collected for the subsequent throwing.

## Mina and the Jamarat

On the 10th of Dhul Hijjah (Eid al-Adha), the Prophet arrived at Mina and performed the following rites in sequence: throwing seven pebbles at the large pillar (Jamrat al-Aqabah), sacrificing his sacrificial animal, having his head shaved, and returning to Mecca to perform the tawaf al-ifadah (the circumambulation that completes the Hajj). He then returned to Mina.

On each of the following two or three days (the Days of Tashreeq), he threw seven pebbles at each of the three pillars (the small, medium, and large Jamarat), supplicating at the first two and moving on after the third.

## Key Juridical Teachings from the Farewell Hajj

The Farewell Hajj settled numerous questions in Islamic jurisprudence:

**The permissibility of the three forms of Hajj.** The Prophet's performance of Hajj al-Qiran (combined Hajj and Umrah in one ihram) while some companions performed Hajj al-Tamattu (Umrah then exiting ihram before Hajj) and others Hajj al-Ifrad (Hajj alone) established all three forms as valid.

**The sequence of rites.** When a companion asked about performing rites in the wrong order, the Prophet consistently replied: "There is no harm, there is no harm." This established that minor order-of-rites mistakes do not invalidate the Hajj.

**The obligatory nature of the wuquf.** The standing at Arafat is the heart of Hajj. Everything else can be missed with a compensatory sacrifice (fidyah); missing Arafat means the entire Hajj must be repeated.

## Jabir's Account

The most comprehensive account of the Farewell Hajj is the long hadith of Jabir ibn Abdullah al-Ansari, preserved in Sahih Muslim. Jabir accompanied the Prophet throughout the pilgrimage and recorded every detail: the Prophet's movements, his prayers, his sermons, and his instructions. This hadith — sometimes called the "Hajj hadith of Jabir" — is among the longest single narrations in the major hadith collections and is the primary reference for Islamic Hajj jurisprudence.

## Legacy

The Farewell Hajj defined Islamic pilgrimage practice for all time. Every Hajj guide in history — from Imam al-Nawawi's manuals to contemporary government Hajj guidelines — derives its structure from the Prophet's actions during these days. The "take your Hajj rites from me" instruction has been understood as the Prophet's own commission to the community to preserve and transmit the pilgrimage exactly as he performed it.
