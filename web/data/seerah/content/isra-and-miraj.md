# The Isra' wal-Mi'raj — The Night Journey and the Ascent

## The Most Singular Night in Human History

The Isra' wal-Mi'raj — the nocturnal journey from Mecca to Jerusalem and the subsequent ascent through the seven heavens to the presence of Allah — is the most extraordinary event in the Prophet's ﷺ earthly biography. It stands apart from every other event in the seerah not merely in its miraculous character but in what it reveals about the Prophet's ﷺ station before Allah: a direct encounter with the Divine, unmediated by the usual channels of revelation, in which the final architecture of Islamic worship was established.

The precise date of the Isra' wal-Mi'raj is among the most debated questions in seerah chronology. The majority of classical scholars place it in the eleventh or twelfth year of prophethood — within the Meccan period, after the Year of Sorrow and before the first pledge of Aqabah. Some scholars place it earlier. The Quranic reference to it opens Surah al-Isra' (17:1): "Glorified is He who took His servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed, to show him of Our signs. Indeed, He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing."

## The Preparation

The Prophet ﷺ was at the Hijr — the semicircular area adjacent to the Kaaba considered part of the original structure — when Jibril came to him. In some accounts the Prophet ﷺ was between sleeping and waking; in the most authoritative readings, this was a physical, bodily journey — a position held by the overwhelming majority of classical scholars based on the Quranic use of "His servant" (abdihi), which denotes the body and soul together, and the word "night journey" (asra bihi), which implies physical transport. The challenge to this interpretation is what gave the event its dramatic Meccan aftermath: the Quraysh who heard the Prophet's ﷺ account the next morning and found it unbelievable were reacting to a claim of physical travel, not a dream.

Jibril split open the Prophet's ﷺ chest, washed his heart with water from Zamzam, filled it with faith and wisdom, and returned it. The Prophet ﷺ was then mounted on al-Buraq — a white creature, larger than a donkey and smaller than a mule, whose stride reached the limit of its sight — and traveled with Jibril to Bayt al-Maqdis (Jerusalem) in a journey that covered over a thousand miles in a moment.

## Al-Masjid al-Aqsa

At Jerusalem, the Prophet ﷺ dismounted, tethered al-Buraq to a ring at the gate of the masjid, and entered. He found within it the assembled company of the prophets: Ibrahim, Musa, Isa, and others. Jibril called the adhan and the iqamah. The Prophet ﷺ led them all in prayer — the final prophet standing at the front of every prophet before him, a profound statement of the seal and culmination of the prophetic chain.

Before departing, the Prophet ﷺ was offered two vessels — one of wine, one of milk. He chose the milk. Jibril said: "You have been guided to the fitra — the natural disposition. If you had taken the wine, your ummah would have gone astray."

## The Ascent Through the Heavens

The Prophet ﷺ ascended with Jibril through the seven heavens, meeting a different prophet at each:

- **First heaven:** Adam — the father of humanity, who greeted the Prophet ﷺ and welcomed him as his righteous son and righteous prophet. He saw souls on his right and left: the souls of the blessed moved him to joy, the souls of the damned to grief.
- **Second heaven:** Isa ibn Maryam (Jesus) and Yahya ibn Zakariyya (John the Baptist) — two cousins in prophethood and in lineage.
- **Third heaven:** Yusuf (Joseph) — described in the narration as having been given half of all beauty.
- **Fourth heaven:** Idris (Enoch).
- **Fifth heaven:** Harun (Aaron).
- **Sixth heaven:** Musa (Moses) — who wept when the Prophet ﷺ passed, saying: "This young man's followers will enter Paradise in greater numbers than my followers."
- **Seventh heaven:** Ibrahim (Abraham) — reclining against the Bayt al-Ma'mur (the celestial Ka'bah), around which seventy thousand angels circumambulate every day and never return.

Beyond the seventh heaven, the Prophet ﷺ was taken to Sidrat al-Muntaha — the Lote Tree of the Utmost Boundary, the cosmic limit beyond which no created being has passed. The tree was described as indescribably beautiful: its fruit like water jars, its leaves like elephant ears, covered by colors and forms from Allah's command that no human language can capture. It was there that the revelation of Allah came closest, "until there was a distance of two bow-lengths or nearer."

## The Encounter with Allah and the Command of Prayer

At Sidrat al-Muntaha, the Prophet ﷺ received the direct command from Allah: the five daily prayers, established as the foundation of the Muslim's relationship with his Lord. The original command was fifty prayers a day. On the descent, Musa stopped the Prophet ﷺ and said: "Your ummah will not be able to bear this. I have had experience with people — I tested the Children of Israel and they were not able to bear what was lesser. Return to your Lord and ask for a reduction."

The Prophet ﷺ returned to Allah and asked; the number was reduced by ten. He descended past Musa again; Musa sent him back. This continued until the prayers were reduced to five. Musa urged him to return once more. The Prophet ﷺ said: "I am embarrassed before my Lord. I accept and submit." The decree was fixed: five prayers in action, equal to fifty in reward — a statement of divine generosity embedded in the establishment of the most central practice of Islam.

## The Morning and the Qurayshi Reaction

The Prophet ﷺ returned to Mecca before morning. He told the Quraysh what had happened. Their reaction was immediate mockery: a journey to Jerusalem and back in a single night was physically impossible. Even some who had accepted Islam were shaken. The Prophet ﷺ described Bayt al-Maqdis in detail — its gates, its columns, the surrounding area — and the Qurayshis who had been to Jerusalem confirmed that his description was accurate. This did not change the minds of those who had decided to disbelieve, but it confirmed for the believers the truth of what had occurred.

Abu Bakr heard the account and said: "If he says it, it is true. I believe him on revelation from the sky — should I not believe him on a journey to Jerusalem?" It was from this moment that Abu Bakr earned the epithet al-Siddiq — the one who affirms the truth completely.

## The Significance of This Night

The Isra' wal-Mi'raj is not merely a miraculous event — it is theologically structured. Several of its elements are foundational to Islamic practice and understanding:

**The five prayers** emerge from this night as the central pillar of Islamic worship. Every Muslim who prays stands in a relationship with Allah established in the direct divine encounter at Sidrat al-Muntaha.

**Jerusalem's sanctity** in Islam is anchored here. Al-Masjid al-Aqsa is identified in the Quran as a masjid of divine blessing, and the Prophet's ﷺ leadership of the prophets in prayer there established its place as the third holiest site in Islam.

**The universality of the prophetic chain** is expressed in the meeting with every prophet. The Prophet ﷺ is not the negator of what came before but the completion of it — the one who stood at the front because he was last, not because the others were wrong.

**The encounter with Moses on the descent**, in which Musa's concern for the Prophet's ﷺ ummah produced the reduction of prayers, is a remarkable expression of inter-prophetic brotherhood and mutual care for the communities that would follow them.

This night took place, according to the most plausible chronologies, at a moment when the Prophet ﷺ had just endured the deaths of his greatest human supports and the rejection of Taif. That the most intimate divine encounter in human history came at precisely this moment is understood by scholars as a divine response to the Prophet's ﷺ patience and perseverance — a mercy given not to the comfortable but to the one who had endured the most.
