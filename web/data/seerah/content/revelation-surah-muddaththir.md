# The Revelation of Surah al-Muddaththir: The Commission

## From Experience to Mission

There is a distinction, in the early seerah literature, between two moments: the first revelation, and the first command. The first revelation — the verses of Surah al-Alaq in Cave Hira — was an experience, an appointment, an announcement. The Prophet ﷺ received it, was overwhelmed by it, and came home trembling. But it was not, in itself, a command to go and deliver a message. That command — the explicit commissioning as a public messenger to humanity — came with the second major revelation: the opening verses of Surah al-Muddaththir (Chapter 74).

Some classical scholars, including those cited by al-Jabiri in his analysis of prophetic chronology, argued that al-Muddaththir was technically the first revelation — precisely because it contains the first explicit command to action. The correct resolution, established by the weight of hadith evidence and endorsed by the majority of scholars, is that al-Alaq was the first in absolute terms and al-Muddaththir was the first command to action. Together, they form the two-stage beginning of the prophetic mission: appointment, then commissioning.

## The Revelation

After the initial experience in Cave Hira and the subsequent pause (al-fatra) in which no revelation came, the Prophet ﷺ was walking when he heard a voice and looked up to see Jibril seated on a throne between heaven and earth — a sight so overwhelming that he immediately went home and asked Khadijah to cover him. While covered, the verses of Surah al-Muddaththir were revealed:

*"O you who wraps himself in his cloak — arise and warn! And your Lord, glorify. And your garments, purify. And uncleanliness, shun. And do not show favor to acquire more. And for the sake of your Lord, be patient."*
(Surah al-Muddaththir, 74:1-7)

From this moment, Jabir ibn Abdullah — who narrated this account in Sahih al-Bukhari — said: "Then revelation came powerfully and continuously." The pause was over. The mission had begun in earnest.

## The Content of the Commission

Each verse of this opening commission carries a specific instruction that shaped the prophetic mission for the next twenty-three years.

**"Arise and warn"** — This is the fundamental mandate. The Prophet ﷺ was not to remain in the cave contemplating his experience. He was to go to people with a message of warning — about the reality of Allah, the Day of Judgment, and the consequences of denying the truth. The verb "arise" (qum) implies urgency; the verb "warn" (andhir) specifies the method. Not merely comfort, not merely promise, but warning: addressing the reality of what people face if they continue in ignorance.

**"Your Lord, glorify"** — The mission is grounded in the glorification and magnification of Allah. The word used is "kabbir" — from the same root as "Allahu Akbar." The practice of takbir that opens every prayer finds its Quranic origin in this verse.

**"Your garments, purify"** — Understood both literally and metaphorically. Physical purity — taharah — was established as a prerequisite for worship and for the one who carries the divine message. The metaphorical meaning extended to the purification of deeds, character, and conduct. The one delivering the message of Allah must embody its values.

**"Uncleanliness, shun"** — The Arabic "ruz" or "rujz" in this verse refers to idols and to moral filth more broadly. The command to shun it is absolute — not to negotiate with it, not to accommodate it, but to decisively separate from it.

**"Do not show favor to acquire more"** — An instruction against the kind of transactional generosity that seeks worldly benefit in return. The Prophet's mission was not to be a means of accumulating social capital or material reward. His service to people was to be pure — done for Allah, not for what he might receive in return.

**"Be patient"** — The seventh and final instruction of this commissioning surah is the one that would be tested most severely in the years to come. Patience (sabr) for the sake of Allah — not passive resignation but active endurance in the face of persecution, mockery, and opposition — is the quality the Prophet ﷺ would need most in Mecca, and it is the quality that the Quran most frequently commanded and praised.

## The Beginning of Continuous Revelation

With Surah al-Muddaththir's commission, the Quranic revelation became a sustained, ongoing process that would continue until the Prophet's ﷺ final illness twenty-three years later. Verses and surahs would descend in response to events, in answer to questions, as commands and prohibitions, as comfort and encouragement, as warnings to enemies and reminders to believers. The Quran — 114 surahs, 6,236 verses — was assembled verse by verse and surah by surah across those twenty-three years, each piece perfectly placed within a divinely ordered whole.

The commission of al-Muddaththir was the beginning of that extraordinary process. The Prophet ﷺ who had entered the cave in private retreat emerged from this experience as the public Messenger of Allah — the man who would not rest, who would not stop, who would be patient through everything, and who would deliver every word of the revelation to all of humanity.

## The Full Surah al-Muddaththir

The remainder of Surah al-Muddaththir (verses 8-56) continues in the register of warning that the opening commission established. Verses 8-10 describe the Day of Judgment — "When the trumpet is blown — that Day will be a difficult day, not easy for the disbelievers." Verses 11-31 contain one of the most devastating Quranic condemnations of an individual: the account of "al-Walid," widely identified by the classical commentators as al-Walid ibn al-Mughira, one of the most powerful Qurayshi nobles, who heard the Quran, privately acknowledged to himself that it was beautiful and could not possibly be human speech ("By Allah, it has sweetness and is adorned with grace"), but then publicly attributed it to magic (sihr) to maintain his social standing. The Quran records this internal deliberation and external betrayal with surgical precision: "He thought and planned — so perish he! How he planned! Again, perish he! How he planned! Then he looked — then he frowned and scowled — then he turned back and was arrogant — and said: This is nothing but magic repeated — this is nothing but the words of a human being." (74:18-25)

This passage is among the most illuminating in the Quran for understanding the psychology of rejection: the acknowledgment of truth followed by its deliberate suppression for social and material reasons. The Quran does not condemn those who sincerely did not believe; it condemns those who recognized the truth and chose to deny it anyway. Al-Walid ibn al-Mughira represents this archetype, and the revelation of Surah al-Muddaththir ensured that his private betrayal of his own recognition was recorded for all time.

The surah concludes with verses about the intercession question and the gates of Paradise — framing the entire mission of warning and guidance within the ultimate eschatological reality that the Prophet ﷺ was sent to address.
