# The Prophet's Marriage to Hafsa bint Umar

Hafsa bint Umar ibn al-Khattab was among the earliest converts to Islam and one of the Prophet's wives who played a significant role in Islamic history both during the Prophet's lifetime and in the crucial period after his death. Her marriage to the Prophet in 3 AH came after the death of her first husband at the Battle of Badr.

## Hafsa's Background

Hafsa was the daughter of Umar ibn al-Khattab — the man who would become the second Caliph and one of the most transformative figures in early Islamic history. She had accepted Islam in Mecca along with her family and had made the hijra to Medina with her first husband Khunayz ibn Hudhafa al-Sahmi.

Khunayz fought at Badr in 2 AH and was wounded in the battle. He died in Medina of his wounds shortly after the battle's conclusion, leaving Hafsa a widow. She was young — approximately eighteen or nineteen years old.

## The Widowhood and the Proposal

Umar ibn al-Khattab was troubled by his daughter's widowhood. He offered Hafsa in marriage to both Abu Bakr and then Uthman ibn Affan — both declined at the time for their own reasons (Uthman was reportedly considering a different marriage). Umar was hurt by these refusals and went to the Prophet to tell him what had happened.

The Prophet said: "Hafsa will marry someone better than Uthman, and Uthman will marry someone better than Hafsa." By this he meant himself and his daughter Umm Kulthum — the Prophet proposed to Hafsa himself and gave Uthman his daughter in marriage.

## The Marriage

The marriage was contracted in Sha'ban 3 AH (approximately February 625 CE). Hafsa was approximately 18 years old at the time.

Hafsa was described as a woman of strong character and independent mind — qualities that she had inherited from her father. The Prophet reportedly considered divorcing her at one point due to a domestic conflict, but Jibril (Gabriel) is reported to have advised him not to do so, as she was described as "a great faster and one who stands in prayer, and she is your wife in Paradise."

## The Quran's Custody

Hafsa's most enduring contribution to Islamic history came after the Prophet's death. After the Battle of Yamama (11 AH) in which many Quranic memorizers were killed, Abu Bakr commissioned Zayd ibn Thabit to compile the Quran into a single written document. The resulting compiled mushaf (codex) was given into the custody of Abu Bakr and, after his death, passed to Umar. After Umar's death, it went to Hafsa.

When Uthman ibn Affan became Caliph and undertook the standardization of the Quranic text to produce copies for distribution across the expanding Islamic world, he sent to Hafsa to obtain her mushaf as the authoritative text from which copies would be made. The copies made under Uthman's direction were produced from Hafsa's copy.

When the copies were complete and sent to the major cities, Hafsa's mushaf was returned to her. It remained in her custody until her death. Upon her death, Marwan ibn al-Hakam (governor of Medina) reportedly had the mushaf destroyed — preventing any text different from the Uthmanic codex from being used as an alternative authority.

Hafsa's role as custodian of the Quranic manuscript connects her directly to the textual history of the Quran.

## Death and Legacy

Hafsa died in Sha'ban 45 AH (approximately 665 CE) during the caliphate of Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan. She was approximately 60 years old.

She narrated 60 hadith from the Prophet, preserved in the major hadith collections. Her narrations cover subjects including the Prophet's worship, his domestic practices, and legal matters.

She is one of the "Mothers of the Believers" (Ummahat al-Mu'minin) — a title accorded to all the Prophet's wives by the Quran (33:6) — and her role as guardian of the first Quranic manuscript makes her place in Islamic history unique.

## Sources

Hafsa's biography is in Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, and her narrations are in Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, and the Sunan collections. The account of the Quranic compilation is in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab Fada'il al-Quran).
