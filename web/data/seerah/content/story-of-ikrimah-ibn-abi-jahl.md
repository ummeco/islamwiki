# The Conversion of Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl

Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl al-Makhzumi — the son of Abu Jahl, one of the Prophet's most bitter enemies — became one of the most striking examples of transformation in the seerah: a man who had organized military expeditions against the Muslims, who had fought against the Prophet at multiple battles, who had initially fled Mecca when it was conquered, yet who accepted Islam and went on to die as a martyr in the early Islamic conquests.

## Background: Ikrimah and Abu Jahl

Abu Jahl ibn Hisham was among the most relentless persecutors of the early Muslim community — so much so that the Prophet nicknamed him "Abu Jahl" ("Father of Ignorance") in contrast to his real name Abu al-Hakam ("Father of Wisdom"). He was killed at the Battle of Badr (2 AH), discovered among the dead by Ibn Masud.

Ikrimah inherited his father's hostility toward Islam. He participated in the military campaigns against the Muslims, including at Uhud and at the Battle of the Trench, and had a personal history of violent opposition to the Prophet's mission.

## The Conquest and Flight

When the Prophet marched on Mecca in Ramadan 8 AH, Ikrimah was among those excluded from the general amnesty — his specific crimes having put him in a small category of individuals whom the Prophet initially said could be killed even if found clinging to the cloth of the Ka'bah.

When Mecca fell, Ikrimah fled by sea, reportedly attempting to reach Yemen or perhaps East Africa to escape the Muslim state's reach.

## Umm Hakim's Intercession

Ikrimah's wife was Umm Hakim bint al-Harith — a Meccan woman who accepted Islam on the day of the conquest. Having accepted Islam herself, she went to the Prophet and asked for Ikrimah's pardon. The Prophet granted it. Umm Hakim then went after her husband, reportedly finding him on a ship that was in danger of sinking. She called out to him that she had obtained his pardon from the Prophet.

Ikrimah returned with her to Mecca.

## The Conversion

When Ikrimah came before the Prophet, he accepted Islam and pronounced the shahada. The Prophet received him warmly and stood up to greet him — an extraordinary honor. He instructed his companions: "Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl will come to you as a believing emigrant; do not abuse his father, for abuse of the dead harms the living and does not reach the dead."

This instruction established a principle in Islamic practice: insulting the deceased is not permissible, even if the deceased was a disbeliever and an enemy.

Ikrimah reportedly told the Prophet: "O Messenger of Allah, by Allah, I will never spend any wealth to turn people from the way of Allah — which I used to do — except I will spend twice as much on the path of Allah. And I will never prevent anyone from attending the struggle in the way of Allah — which I used to do — except I will go in it twice as many times myself."

## Character After Conversion

Ikrimah proved to be among the most committed and courageous Muslims after his conversion. He participated in the Battle of Ta'if shortly after the conquest of Mecca and in subsequent campaigns. His transformation was so complete that those who had known him before found him almost unrecognizable in character.

## Death at Yarmouk

Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl died at the Battle of Yarmouk in 15 AH (636 CE) — the decisive battle in which the Muslim army defeated the Byzantine forces and secured Muslim control over Greater Syria. He died as a martyr (shahid) on the battlefield, having fought with exceptional courage.

His death at Yarmouk — fighting for the Islam he had spent years opposing — was understood by early Muslim historians as one of the most powerful examples of divine guidance's transformative power.

## Legacy

Ikrimah's story has been cited by Islamic scholars across the centuries as evidence for several principles:

**The acceptance of sincere conversion.** The Prophet did not hold Ikrimah's history against him once he had genuinely accepted Islam. His past opposition was forgiven completely, and he was integrated into the Muslim community as a full member.

**The prohibition on insulting the dead.** The Prophet's instruction not to insult Abu Jahl in Ikrimah's presence established a broader principle about respecting the dead, regardless of their status.

**The transformative potential of faith.** From the son of the Prophet's most bitter enemy to a martyr for Islam — Ikrimah's trajectory was used in Islamic moral literature as an example of how radically faith can transform character.

## Sources

The account of Ikrimah's conversion is in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and related narrations in the hadith collections.
