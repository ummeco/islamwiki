# The Aligarh Movement

The Aligarh Movement was an educational and social reform movement among Indian Muslims in the latter half of the nineteenth century CE, led by Sir Syed Ahmad Khan (1232-1316 AH / 1817-1898 CE). Its primary vehicle was the Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College founded at Aligarh in 1875 CE (later to become Aligarh Muslim University), which sought to combine modern Western education with Islamic values and to prepare Muslim youth for participation in British colonial administration. The movement had a lasting impact on Muslim political thought in South Asia.

## Background

The aftermath of the Indian Rebellion of 1857 CE (the "Sepoy Mutiny" or the First War of Independence) was catastrophic for the Muslim community of the Indian subcontinent. The British suppression of the rebellion was particularly harsh toward Muslims, who were seen as the primary instigators. The Mughal Emperor Bahadur Shah Zafar, the last symbolic ruler of the Mughal dynasty, was exiled, ending 300 years of Mughal presence in Delhi. Many Muslim nobles lost their estates. Muslim participation in government administration dropped sharply.

At the same time, Hindu communities, particularly in Bengal, had engaged more readily with British colonial education and were rapidly filling administrative and professional positions. Muslims, many of whom viewed English education as culturally threatening and religiously suspect, fell further behind.

## Sir Syed Ahmad Khan

Sir Syed Ahmad Khan was born in Delhi in 1817 CE to a noble family with connections to the Mughal court. After the 1857 Rebellion, in which he served the British administration loyally (he protected British civilians in Bijnor during the uprising), he became convinced that the key to Muslim survival and prosperity under British rule lay in modern education and political cooperation with the British.

He was knighted by Queen Victoria and served on the Legislative Council of the Governor-General of India.

## The Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College

Sir Syed founded the Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College at Aligarh in 1875 CE. It was modeled on the residential colleges of Oxford and Cambridge, combining modern Western sciences, English literature, and European history with Islamic studies. The college aimed to produce Muslim graduates who could compete for positions in the colonial administration while maintaining their Islamic identity.

The curriculum was deliberately integrative: English and Urdu, modern science and Islamic theology, Western political philosophy and Quranic studies. Islamic instruction was compulsory, and the Islamic character of the institution was maintained while it engaged seriously with modern knowledge.

## Theological and Political Dimensions

Sir Syed was also a religious thinker, and his theological positions were controversial. He interpreted Quranic verses dealing with natural phenomena in ways compatible with modern science, rejecting what he saw as superstitious literalism. His approach influenced some later Muslim modernist thinkers but was also criticized by traditional scholars who felt he accommodated Western scientific assumptions too uncritically.

Politically, he advocated Muslim cooperation with British rule rather than opposition to it. He argued that given the Muslim community's weakened position, confrontation with the British was self-destructive, and that education and self-improvement were the priorities. This position put him in conflict with both Hindu nationalists and with Muslim leaders who favored a more assertive political stance.

## Legacy

The Aligarh Movement's most immediate legacy was the institution it created. Aligarh Muslim University (as the college became in 1920 CE) produced generations of Muslim scholars, lawyers, administrators, and political figures. Many of the leaders of the Pakistan Movement, including Muhammad Ali Jinnah (who received an honorary degree from Aligarh), had connections to the institution.

The broader intellectual legacy of the Aligarh Movement — the project of combining Islamic identity with modern education — shaped Muslim educational institutions across South Asia. The debate it raised about how Muslims should engage with modernity, Western education, and colonial power remains relevant to Muslim communities in many parts of the world.

Sir Syed died in 1898 CE. He did not live to see either British departure from India or the partition that created Pakistan — but the institution he founded played a significant role in the events that led to both.
