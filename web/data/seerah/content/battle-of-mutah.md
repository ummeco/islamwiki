# The Battle of Mutah

## The First Confrontation with Byzantium

The Battle of Mutah — fought in Jumada al-Ula 8 AH in the region of the Syrian frontier (in present-day Jordan) — was the first direct military encounter between the Muslim community and Byzantine-aligned forces. It began as a diplomatic mission: the Prophet ﷺ sent fifteen men as emissaries to the Ghassanid ruler of Busra. On the way, the group passed through the territory of the governor of Mutah, Shurahbil ibn Amr al-Ghassani, who executed the Muslim emissary Harith ibn Umayr — a violation of the diplomatic immunity that was universally recognized in Arabia. This was a direct provocation, and the Prophet ﷺ responded by sending a force of 3,000 to the Syrian frontier.

The expedition was led by Zayd ibn Haritha — the Prophet's ﷺ former slave and adopted son, the first person after Khadijah to accept Islam as an adult. The Prophet ﷺ appointed a chain of command before departure: if Zayd was killed, Jafar ibn Abi Talib would take command; if Jafar was killed, Abdullah ibn Rawaha would command; and if he too was killed, the army should choose a leader from among themselves. This three-level succession plan was unprecedented in the Prophet's ﷺ appointments and signaled that he knew the battle would be severe.

## The Scale of the Opposition

The Muslim force arrived at Mutah to find an opposing army that the sources estimate at anywhere from 10,000 to 200,000 — a Byzantine and Ghassanid coalition of a scale the 3,000-strong Muslim force had not anticipated and could not match in open battle. The Muslim commanders held a council of war. Abdullah ibn Rawaha argued that they had not come for numbers but for one of two goods: victory or martyrdom. The army pressed forward.

Zayd ibn Haritha fell fighting with the battle standard in his hand. Jafar ibn Abi Talib took the standard and continued fighting until his right arm was cut off. He passed the standard to his left arm. His left arm was cut off. He held the standard to his chest until he fell. He was found with ninety wounds on the front of his body — all of them from facing the enemy, none from fleeing. The Prophet ﷺ, who received the news of the battle through what the narrations describe as divine vision in Medina, told the companions what had happened before the messengers arrived: Zayd took the standard and was killed; Jafar took it and was killed; Abdullah ibn Rawaha took it and was killed. He was weeping. Then he said: "And then one of the swords of Allah took the standard" — referring to Khalid.

## Khalid's Command

Khalid ibn al-Walid had been in Islam for months. He had converted in Safar 8 AH and Mutah was in Jumada al-Ula — perhaps three or four months after his shahada. Yet when Abdullah ibn Rawaha fell and the command passed informally to the army, Khalid was chosen. His tactical skills were known even to the Muslims: this was the man who had nearly destroyed them at Uhud. In the crisis at Mutah, they gave him the standard.

Khalid reorganized the remnants of the Muslim force. He changed unit positions to simulate the arrival of reinforcements. He mounted a fierce counterattack that inflicted casualties on the Byzantine force and created confusion about what the Muslim army's real strength was. Under this cover, he managed an orderly withdrawal — the Muslim army returned to Medina having lost its three appointed commanders and numerous fighters, but not annihilated, not surrendered, and not captured.

The Prophet ﷺ received the returning army and called Khalid "Sayf Allah" — the Sword of Allah — saying he was a sword that Allah had drawn against the unbelievers. It is the title Khalid carried for the rest of his life.

## The Companions' Reception

When the returning army reached Medina, some companions received them coldly, throwing dust on them and saying: "You fled from the path of Allah." The Prophet ﷺ corrected this: "They are not fleers — they are returners, if Allah wills." A strategic withdrawal conducted under overwhelming force to preserve the army for future use was not cowardice; it was the right decision. The Prophet ﷺ defended the judgment of Khalid and the surviving commanders explicitly.

## Jafar and the Two Wings

The Prophet ﷺ reported seeing Jafar ibn Abi Talib in paradise with two wings of emerald, flying wherever he wished — his arms replaced with wings as compensation for the two arms he had lost carrying the battle standard. Jafar ibn Abi Talib is known in the Islamic tradition as Jafar al-Tayyar (Jafar the Flyer) on account of this report. He was among the most beloved of the Prophet's ﷺ relatives — the Prophet ﷺ visited his family for three days after the news of his death, himself preparing food for the household because they were in grief.

The Battle of Mutah established several principles that would govern the expansion of the Muslim community into the Byzantine world: that confronting Byzantine power was possible, that disciplined withdrawal was not defeat, and that the martyrdom of commanders did not mean the end of the mission. Three commanders fell and the army returned. Six years later, Muslim armies would be in Syria permanently.

## The Strategic Marker

Mutah is the northernmost military operation of the Prophet's ﷺ lifetime. It signaled that the Muslim community was willing and able to project force to the Syrian frontier — a distance that most Arabian powers of the era did not attempt. The Ghassanids and their Byzantine allies had executed a Muslim emissary and been met with 3,000 fighters. The message was received: the diplomatic immunity of Muslim messengers was not a courtesy that could be violated without consequence. The Expedition of Tabuk the following year — when the Prophet ﷺ marched 30,000 to the frontier — was the follow-up declaration that Mutah had opened.
