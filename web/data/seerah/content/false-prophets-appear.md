# Appearance of False Prophets in the Prophet's Lifetime

During the final years of the Prophet's life — primarily in 9-10 AH — several individuals claimed prophethood across Arabia, attracting significant followings among Arabian tribes. These false-prophet movements, which the Prophet addressed directly and which Abu Bakr suppressed militarily in the Riddah Wars, were among the most dangerous internal threats to the early Muslim community.

## The Prophetic Prediction

The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) had foretold the emergence of false prophets. In a hadith recorded in Sahih Bukhari and Muslim, he said: "The Hour will not come until approximately thirty liars (kadhdhabin) appear, each of them claiming to be a messenger of Allah." In another narration: "There will be Dajjals (deceivers) and liars — thirty or more — among them women."

This prophecy was fulfilled in the Prophet's lifetime and continued to be fulfilled in subsequent generations, which Islamic scholars have cited as evidence of the prophetic knowledge of unseen matters (ilm al-ghayb).

## Musaylimah al-Kadhdhab (The Liar)

The most prominent and dangerous of the false prophets was Musaylimah ibn Habib al-Hanafi, from the Banu Hanifah tribe of Yamama (central Arabia). Musaylimah had come to Medina with a delegation from his tribe and — according to the sources — had accepted Islam. He then returned to Yamama and claimed prophethood.

Musaylimah sent a letter to the Prophet claiming shared prophethood: "From Musaylimah the Messenger of Allah to Muhammad the Messenger of Allah. Half the earth is ours and half is Quraysh's — but Quraysh are an extravagant people." The Prophet replied: "In the name of Allah the Most Merciful. From Muhammad the Messenger of Allah to Musaylimah the Liar. Peace be upon whoever follows guidance. The earth belongs to Allah, who bequeaths it to whom He wills among His servants."

Musaylimah's movement grew significantly after the Prophet's death and required a major military campaign — the Battle of Yamama in 11 AH — to suppress. He was killed in that battle.

## Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid al-Asadi

Tulayha was a chief of the Banu Asad tribe who claimed prophethood during the Prophet's lifetime, then apostasized. His movement collapsed at the Battle of Buzakha (11 AH) when Khalid ibn al-Walid's forces defeated him. Unlike Musaylimah, Tulayha survived, eventually accepted Islam sincerely, and later fought with distinction in the early Muslim conquests, reportedly dying as a martyr.

His case became an important reference point in Islamic discussions of the permissibility of accepting repentance from apostates who had previously claimed prophethood.

## Al-Aswad al-Ansi in Yemen

Al-Aswad ibn Ka'b al-Ansi, known as Dhul Khamar ("the one with the veil/garment"), was a Yemeni soothsayer who claimed prophethood and conquest. He was a powerful speaker and magician who attracted a significant following, and he managed to seize control of large parts of Yemen — including the city of San'a — and drove out the Prophet's appointed governors.

The Prophet dispatched letters to the Muslims of Yemen urging them to resist al-Aswad. Al-Aswad was killed by a group of Muslims and his own wife the night before the Prophet's death — the news of his killing reportedly reached Medina at approximately the same time as the Prophet's death.

## Sajah bint al-Harith

Sajah was a prophetess from the Banu Tamim tribe — one of the rare women to lead such a movement. She initially allied with Musaylimah, reportedly entering a temporary marriage with him (which was annulled after the Ridda Wars). Her movement collapsed after Musaylimah's death, and she eventually accepted Islam during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

## Common Features of the Movements

Islamic historians have noted several common features:

**Tribal basis.** Each false prophet drew primarily on tribal identity and loyalty, claiming prophethood in the name of their tribe's status and interests.

**Economic motivation.** Several movements were connected to resistance to paying zakah — the mandatory almsgiving that the Muslim state collected. The false prophets often promised freedom from this obligation.

**Imitation of prophetic forms.** Several of the false prophets produced what they claimed were revelations, imitated the Quran's style (poorly), and adopted the external forms of prophetic authority: followers, prayers, and claims of receiving divine messages.

**Rapid collapse after defeat.** Most of the movements dissolved quickly once their leaders were killed, suggesting that personal charisma and tribal politics rather than genuine religious conviction were the primary binding forces.

## Islamic Jurisprudence on False Prophets

The Riddah Wars and the treatment of false-prophet movements established several principles in Islamic jurisprudence:

**The finality of prophethood.** Any claim to prophethood after Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) is, by definition, false. This is grounded in Quran 33:40 ("Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but he is the Messenger of Allah and the Seal of the Prophets") and the hadith literature on the finality of prophethood.

**The treatment of followers.** Those who followed false prophets but accepted Islam were accepted back into the Muslim community. The leaders who persisted in their claims and refused were treated as enemies of the state.

## Sources

The accounts of the false prophets are documented in al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Waqidi's *Kitab al-Ridda*, and the relevant sections of Sahih Bukhari and Sahih Muslim.
