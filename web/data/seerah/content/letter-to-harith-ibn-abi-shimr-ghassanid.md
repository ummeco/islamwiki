# The Prophet's Letter to al-Harith ibn Abi Shimr al-Ghassani

Among the letters sent by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) to the rulers of the major powers in 7 AH was a letter to al-Harith ibn Abi Shimr al-Ghassani — the Arab king of the Ghassanid state, a Christian Arab kingdom that served as a buffer state and client kingdom of the Byzantine Empire on the eastern edge of Byzantine Syria.

## The Ghassanids

The Ghassanids were Arab tribes who had migrated from Yemen to the Syrian steppe and had gradually built a significant kingdom as Byzantine allies. They had converted to Miaphysite Christianity and served the Byzantines as frontier defense against the Persians and as a buffer against the nomadic Arab tribes of the interior. Al-Harith ibn Abi Shimr (also known as Jabalah ibn al-Ayham in later accounts) was among the last significant Ghassanid rulers.

The Ghassanids are relevant to Islamic history for another reason: the Battle of Mu'tah (8 AH), in which the first Muslim military engagement with Byzantine-allied forces occurred, involved Ghassanid forces in the fighting that killed Zayd ibn Haritha, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib, and Abdullah ibn Rawaha.

## The Letter

The Prophet sent Shuja' ibn Wahb al-Asadi as his messenger to the Ghassanid king. The letter followed the format of the other royal letters: beginning with the divine names, identifying the sender as the Messenger of Allah, and inviting the recipient to Islam with the promise of security and double reward for accepting, and warning of the sin of his people if he refused.

The letter cited the Quranic verse (Quran 3:64) inviting People of the Scripture to common ground with the Muslims.

## Al-Harith's Reaction

According to the seerah accounts, al-Harith ibn Abi Shimr received the letter and his reaction was considerably more hostile than that of Heraclius or al-Muqawqis. He reportedly said: "Who would take my kingdom from me?" and threatened to march against the Muslims. He reportedly wrote to Emperor Heraclius reporting the situation and asking permission to move against the Muslims.

Heraclius reportedly responded with words cautioning restraint and suggesting that the prophet who had appeared in Arabia was the expected prophet — counsel that al-Harith disregarded.

The messenger Shuja' ibn Wahb returned to the Prophet without a positive response but safely, and reported what had occurred. The Prophet reportedly said, indicating al-Harith's impending downfall: "His kingdom will perish."

## The Ghassanids' Historical Trajectory

Within a few decades, the prediction was fulfilled. The Byzantine-Sassanid Wars (602-628 CE) severely weakened both empires. When the Arab-Muslim armies under Khalid ibn al-Walid and Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah moved into Byzantine Syria (12-15 AH / 634-636 CE), the Ghassanid kingdom quickly lost its independent power. After the decisive Battle of Yarmouk (15 AH / 636 CE), Byzantine control of Syria effectively ended and the Ghassanid kingdom ceased to exist as a political entity. The Arab tribes of Syria were absorbed into the Islamic world.

## Significance

**Arab tribal rulers.** The Ghassanid letter illustrates that the Prophet's outreach extended to Arab Christian rulers as well as to the major non-Arab powers. The Ghassanids' Arab identity made their eventual incorporation into the Islamic world a matter of tribal realignment rather than merely military conquest.

**The prophetic statement.** Al-Harith's hostile response and the subsequent decline of the Ghassanid kingdom are cited in Islamic tradition as another fulfilled prophetic statement — parallel to the prediction about Khosrow's empire being torn apart.

**Pre-Mu'tah context.** The hostile reception of the Prophet's messenger to the Ghassanids contributed to the tense political context in which the expedition to Mu'tah was later organized, when the Prophet's messenger to a Syrian ruler was reportedly killed by a Ghassanid-affiliated chieftain.

## Sources

The letter to al-Harith ibn Abi Shimr is recorded in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*.
