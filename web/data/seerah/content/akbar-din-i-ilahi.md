# Akbar and Din-i-Ilahi

The reign of the Mughal Emperor Akbar (r. 963-1014 AH / 1556-1605 CE) represented both the height of Mughal political power and a significant challenge to Islamic orthodoxy in the Indian subcontinent. His religious policies, culminating in the proclamation of the Din-i-Ilahi (Divine Religion) around 990 AH (1582 CE), generated fierce controversy among Muslim scholars and set the context for the subsequent Islamic reform movements led by figures like Ahmad Sirhindi.

## Akbar's Reign

Akbar came to the throne at age thirteen after his father Humayun's sudden death and, under the regency of Bairam Khan, gradually assumed full control of the empire. By the 1570s, he had consolidated Mughal rule over most of northern India and established an administrative system of considerable sophistication.

He was an illiterate man of intense intellectual curiosity — he had texts read aloud to him and engaged in oral debates with scholars of all traditions. His court was one of the most religiously diverse in the world, including Muslim theologians, Hindu pandits, Jain monks, Zoroastrian priests, and Portuguese Jesuit missionaries.

## The Ibadat Khana

In 979 AH (1575 CE), Akbar established the Ibadat Khana (House of Worship) at Fatehpur Sikri, initially as a place for Muslim scholars and Sufi masters to discuss theology and spirituality. Over time, he expanded the discussions to include non-Muslim participants: Hindu pandits, Jain acharyas, Zoroastrian priests, and Jesuit missionaries.

What Akbar witnessed — learned representatives of different religions frequently arguing about which tradition was superior while agreeing on little — may have contributed to his evolving view that no single tradition had an exclusive claim to religious truth.

## The Din-i-Ilahi

Around 990 AH (1582 CE), Akbar announced what court historian Abu al-Fazl called the Din-i-Ilahi (the Divine Religion) or Tawhid-i-Ilahi (Divine Unity). It was not a full religion with a scripture and ritual system but rather an order of personal loyalty to Akbar, combined with an eclectic set of principles.

Members of the Din-i-Ilahi took Akbar as their spiritual guide (murshid), exchanged the formal Muslim greeting with a unique greeting, adopted certain practices, and participated in the emperor's court on terms that symbolically elevated Akbar's spiritual authority.

The principles Akbar associated with it included: respect for all religions, rationalism, vegetarianism (adopted from Jain influence), prohibition of cow slaughter, veneration of the sun and fire (possibly Zoroastrian influence), and certain departures from standard Islamic practice in the court context.

The Din-i-Ilahi attracted only a small number of followers — estimates suggest under twenty individuals formally entered the order — and disappeared with Akbar's death in 1014 AH (1605 CE).

## Scholarly Response

Akbar's religious experiments alarmed orthodox Muslim scholars. His suspension of the traditional permission for certain food customs, his experiments with the Friday prayer, and his apparent elevation of his own religious authority above the Quran and Sunnah were condemned as violations of Islamic principles.

Shaykh Ahmad Sirhindi (d. 1034 AH / 1624 CE) emerged as the leading scholarly voice against the Akbarian religious innovations, arguing in his voluminous correspondence that Akbar's policies had deviated from the Quran and Sunnah and calling for a return to strict Islamic observance. Sirhindi's influence was felt in the reign of Akbar's successors.

## Historical Assessment

Akbar's religious policies have been assessed very differently by different observers. Some modern historians view him as a proto-secular ruler seeking to create a pluralistic basis for his diverse empire. Some Indian nationalist historians praised him as a model of religious tolerance. Traditional Muslim scholars viewed his policies as dangerous innovations that threatened Islamic identity in the subcontinent.

What is clear is that his policies generated a significant backlash that shaped the subsequent trajectory of Islam in the Indian subcontinent, strengthening reformist and revivalist movements.
