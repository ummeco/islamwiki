# The Prophet Appoints Abu Bakr to Lead the Prayer

During the final days of the Prophet's illness in 11 AH, as his condition prevented him from leading the communal prayers himself, he designated Abu Bakr as-Siddiq to stand in his place as imam for the congregation at the mosque in Medina. This appointment — small in its immediate practical scope — carried enormous significance for the question of succession and the institutional history of the Muslim community.

## The Circumstances

The Prophet led the communal prayers for as long as his strength allowed. When he could no longer do so, the companions needed to know who should lead in his place. Multiple narrations from Sahih Bukhari and Sahih Muslim record the exchange.

The Prophet said: "Command Abu Bakr to lead the people in prayer." (In some narrations: "Tell Abu Bakr to lead the people in prayer.")

## The Objection and the Insistence

Aisha bint Abi Bakr — whose chamber the Prophet was staying in during his illness — intervened. In one account she said to the Prophet: "O Messenger of Allah, Abu Bakr is a very tender-hearted man; when he stands in your place he will weep and the people will not be able to hear him. Would you command Umar instead?"

In another narration, she and Hafsa bint Umar together suggested alternatives. The Prophet's response was consistent: "Command Abu Bakr to lead the people in prayer. You are like the women around Yusuf (Joseph)."

This final phrase — comparing their suggestions to the machinations of the Egyptian women in the Quranic story of Yusuf — was a gentle rebuke, indicating that the Prophet saw through whatever motives lay behind their suggestions (Aisha may have been concerned that Abu Bakr would be too visibly grief-stricken in a role so associated with the Prophet). He repeated the instruction a second and third time when they continued to press.

## Abu Bakr's Response

When informed, Abu Bakr initially hesitated, reportedly saying to Umar: "O Umar, you lead the people in prayer." Umar demurred, saying he would not do so unless the Prophet commanded it. Abu Bakr then led the prayers.

According to the narrations, Abu Bakr did indeed weep when leading the prayers — so much so that at times the congregation could not follow his recitation. But he continued.

## The Prophet's Final Appearance at Prayer

On what would be among his last appearances, the Prophet was brought to the mosque during a congregational prayer led by Abu Bakr. When Abu Bakr sensed the Prophet's presence, he stepped back to allow him to take the imam's position. The Prophet gestured for him to stay in his place and then sat beside him, leading from a seated position while Abu Bakr and the congregation stood.

This last shared prayer — the Prophet seated, Abu Bakr standing beside him — was witnessed by the entire congregation and carried immense symbolic weight. The Prophet was, visually and actually, handing the imam's role to Abu Bakr while still present to validate the transfer.

## Why This Appointment Mattered

Islamic scholars across the centuries have seen the Prophet's insistence on Abu Bakr leading the prayers as one of the clearest indicators of his preference for Abu Bakr's succession to communal leadership. The imam of the communal prayer was not merely a ceremonial role — it was the symbolic and practical center of the Muslim community's religious life.

Al-Nawawi, Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani, and other classical commentators on Sahih Bukhari discuss this point at length: if the Prophet wished to designate Abu Bakr as a spiritual and communal leader, placing him as imam of the prayers was the most natural and recognized way to do so.

Sunni Muslims see this as part of the broader evidence for Abu Bakr's legitimacy as the first Caliph, alongside his long companionship with the Prophet, his role as the Prophet's companion in the Cave of Thawr during the hijra, his role in the collection of the Quran, and the community's general recognition of his qualities.

## Recorded in the Hadith

This incident is among the most richly attested events of the final days of the Prophet's life. It appears in:

- **Sahih Bukhari**: Kitab al-Adhan (chapter on the call to prayer), Kitab al-Salah, and Kitab al-Maghazi
- **Sahih Muslim**: Kitab al-Salah
- **Sunan Abi Dawud** and other collections

The multiple, independent chains of transmission and the agreement of Aisha (the primary narrator of these events) across all major collections make this one of the best-documented incidents of the seerah's final chapter.
