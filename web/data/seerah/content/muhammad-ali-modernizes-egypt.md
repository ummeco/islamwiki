# Muhammad Ali Pasha and Modernization of Egypt

## The Albanian Commander Who Became Egypt's Ruler

Muhammad Ali Pasha (1184-1265 AH / 1769-1849 CE) is one of the most consequential figures in modern Arab and Islamic history — a man who, starting from obscure origins as an Albanian soldier in the Ottoman army, transformed Egypt from a provincial backwater into a modernizing regional power and established a dynasty that would rule the country until 1952. His methods were ruthless, his ambition was boundless, and his results were transformative in ways that neither he nor his contemporaries could fully anticipate.

He was born in Kavala, in what is now northern Greece, then an Ottoman province with a substantial Albanian population. His early life was not scholarly — he traded in tobacco and then entered military service. He arrived in Egypt in 1215 AH / 1801 CE as part of the Ottoman expeditionary force sent to expel the French. In the political chaos that followed the French departure, he maneuvered skillfully between competing Ottoman factions and Mamluk households, building a personal power base among the Albanian soldiers who formed one element of the occupying force. By 1220 AH / 1805 CE, he had secured the position of Ottoman viceroy of Egypt — confirmed by the Ottoman Sultan under pressure from the Egyptian population and ulama who preferred him to the alternatives.

## The Mamluk Massacre of 1226 AH / 1811 CE

The most notorious act of Muhammad Ali's consolidation of power was the massacre of the Mamluk leadership. The Mamluks — the military caste that had effectively ruled Egypt for centuries before the French and Ottoman interventions — remained a potential threat to his authority and a rival power center that could not be tolerated. Muhammad Ali invited the major Mamluk beys to a ceremony at the Cairo Citadel in March 1811 CE, ostensibly to celebrate his son's appointment to lead an Arabian campaign. As they passed through the narrow passage of the Bab al-Azab, the gates were closed and his Albanian and Ottoman soldiers opened fire. The Mamluks in the citadel were killed; simultaneous actions across Cairo and Egypt targeted Mamluk households and retainers. Within days, the Mamluk ruling class that had dominated Egypt for two centuries was effectively eliminated as a political force.

The massacre was brutal, calculated, and successful. It removed the most significant internal obstacle to Muhammad Ali's complete control of Egypt and freed him to reorganize the country's administration without reference to the power-sharing arrangements that had constrained previous governors.

## Military and Administrative Modernization

Muhammad Ali's fundamental insight was that the European military superiority demonstrated by Napoleon's expedition could be replicated by Egypt if it adopted European training, weapons, and organizational methods. He began building a modern army in the 1820s, initially using Sudanese slaves taken during his Sudanese campaigns (1235-1236 AH / 1820-1821 CE), and then transitioning to conscripted Egyptian peasants (fallaheen) — a revolutionary change, since Egyptian peasants had never before served in the military and were not considered military material. He hired European officers — French in particular — as instructors and established military schools.

The military schools required literacy and technical training, which required a broader educational infrastructure. Muhammad Ali established a network of secular schools (alongside but independent of the traditional religious system centered on al-Azhar) teaching European languages, engineering, mathematics, and military sciences. Most significantly, he dispatched student missions to Europe — primarily France — sending cohorts of Egyptian students to study in Paris, Marseille, and other cities. The first major mission, in 1241 AH / 1826 CE, included forty-four students. These missions produced Egypt's first generation of European-educated technocrats and would have long-term consequences for Egyptian intellectual life.

One of the members of the first Paris mission, Rifa'a al-Tahtawi (1216-1290 AH / 1801-1873 CE), wrote Takhlis al-Ibriz fi Talkhis Bariz (An Imam in Paris, or The Extraction of Gold — his account of his years in France), one of the foundational texts of the Arab Nahda. Al-Tahtawi engaged seriously with French political thought — democracy, constitutionalism, natural rights — and asked how they could be reconciled with or adapted for Islamic governance. His work established a tradition of Arab intellectual engagement with Western modernity that would preoccupy Arab thinkers for the next two centuries.

## Economic Transformation and the Egyptian Cotton Economy

Muhammad Ali reorganized Egypt's economy through state monopoly. He nationalized much of the agricultural land, forcing peasants to grow the crops he designated (initially grain for the military, then cotton, indigo, and sugarcane for export) and sell them to state agents at fixed prices. He established state factories producing textiles, weapons, and other goods — Egypt briefly had one of the largest textile industries in the world in the 1830s, though it was dismantled by European pressure.

The introduction of long-staple Egyptian cotton — which produced fibers of exceptional quality suitable for fine textile manufacture — transformed Egypt's place in the global economy. The cotton economy, however, also tied Egypt more tightly to European financial markets and made it vulnerable to the fluctuations of global commodity prices. This integration into the European-dominated world economy, which Muhammad Ali set in motion, would eventually lead to the debt crisis that gave Britain its pretext for occupying Egypt in 1882.

## The Arabian Campaign and Restoration of the Hejaz

One of Muhammad Ali's earliest major military campaigns, launched at Ottoman request, was the suppression of the first Saudi-Wahhabi state in Arabia, which had seized the Hejaz and disrupted Ottoman control of Mecca and Medina. His son Tusun led the initial campaign from 1226 AH / 1811 CE, and his son Ibrahim Pasha completed it with the destruction of Dir'iyyah in 1233 AH / 1818 CE. The restoration of Ottoman (and by extension Egyptian) control over the Two Holy Mosques gave Muhammad Ali enormous prestige and consolidated his relationship with the Ottoman sultan.

The Sudan campaigns (1235-1236 AH / 1820-1821 CE) extended Egyptian control into the upper Nile valley, providing access to gold, ivory, and enslaved people. Khartoum was founded as an Egyptian garrison town. Egyptian rule of Sudan, which Muhammad Ali established, would persist — with interruptions — until Sudanese independence in 1956.

## The Near-Destruction of the Ottoman Empire: 1246-1256 AH / 1831-1840 CE

The most extraordinary episode of Muhammad Ali's career was his near-conquest of the Ottoman Empire itself. Motivated by grievances over the distribution of rewards after his Arabian and Greek campaigns, and frustrated by the Ottoman sultan's failure to grant him the Syrian governorship he sought, Muhammad Ali sent his son Ibrahim Pasha with the Egyptian army into Ottoman Syria in 1246 AH / 1831 CE. Ibrahim defeated Ottoman forces at the Battle of Homs (1247 AH / 1832 CE) and at the Battle of Konya in Anatolia (1248 AH / 1832 CE), advancing to within 300 kilometers of Istanbul. Had Ibrahim continued, the Ottoman capital would likely have fallen.

He was stopped not by Ottoman military resistance but by European diplomatic intervention. The European powers — particularly Britain, Austria, and Russia — recognized that the collapse of the Ottoman Empire would create a vacuum that would destabilize the eastern Mediterranean and trigger conflict among themselves. Under intense European pressure, and with Russian forces actually landing on the Bosphorus to defend Istanbul, Muhammad Ali was forced to withdraw in exchange for the hereditary governorship of Egypt.

He returned to Syria in 1255 AH / 1839 CE when Ottoman forces attacked him, again defeating the Ottomans, this time at the Battle of Nezib. Once again the European powers intervened, this time more decisively: the Convention of London (1256 AH / 1840 CE) demanded his withdrawal from Syria and his renunciation of the Arabian, Cretan, and other territories, in exchange for confirmed hereditary rule of Egypt and nominal authority over Sudan. He accepted, and the dynasty that would rule Egypt until 1952 was secured.

## The Impact on Islamic Institutions: Al-Azhar

Muhammad Ali's relationship with al-Azhar — the leading institution of Sunni Islamic learning in the Arab world — was fraught with tension. The Azhari ulama had been political actors under the Ottoman system, mediating between the Mamluk ruling class and the Egyptian population, and had occasionally been able to influence governance. Muhammad Ali systematically reduced their political and economic power.

He confiscated the waqf (endowment) properties that had funded al-Azhar's operations for centuries, replacing the traditional system of endowment-based scholarship with state funding — which gave the state control over the institution. He excluded al-Azhar from the new secular educational system he was building, creating a two-track educational structure that persisted well into the twentieth century: a traditional religious track through al-Azhar and a modern secular track through state schools. This bifurcation had profound consequences for Egyptian intellectual life, creating two educated classes with different languages, different reference points, and different relationships to Islamic tradition.

The ulama retained their role in personal status law and popular religious life, but their political influence was sharply curtailed. The shift from an Islamic legal establishment with genuine political power to an Islamic establishment that legitimized state decisions from a subordinate position was one of the defining features of Muhammad Ali's modernization — and one that would shape Egyptian and Arab political life for generations.

## The Printing Press and Public Sphere

Muhammad Ali established the Bulaq printing press in 1236 AH / 1821 CE, the first Arabic printing press in Egypt operated at significant scale. Its initial purpose was practical: printing military manuals, technical texts, and translations of European works needed for his modernization program. But it also produced Arabic-language newspapers, including the government gazette Al-Waqa'i' al-Misriyya (Egyptian Events), and began publishing classical Arabic texts — making the literary and scholarly heritage of Islamic civilization accessible in printed form in Egypt for the first time.

The printing press and the associated expansion of Egyptian literacy created the conditions for the Nahda — the Arab cultural and intellectual awakening of the nineteenth century. The generation of writers, translators, and journalists produced by Muhammad Ali's educational system would argue, debate, and publish their way toward an Arab modernity that was simultaneously engaged with European thought and rooted in Islamic and Arabic tradition.

## Significance and Contested Legacy

Muhammad Ali is called the "Father of Modern Egypt" — a title that acknowledges his role in creating the institutional infrastructure of the Egyptian state that exists today, while sidestepping the question of what was destroyed in its creation. His modernization program required the dismantling of traditional Islamic institutional power, the forcible transformation of the peasantry from subsistence farmers into conscript soldiers and export-crop producers, and the massacre of the Mamluk ruling class. Its long-term effects — the integration of Egypt into the European-dominated global economy, the creation of a secular educated class, the subordination of al-Azhar to the state — shaped Egyptian society in ways that continue to generate debate.

From an Islamic perspective, the evaluation of Muhammad Ali is complex. He was a Muslim ruler who expanded the geographical reach of Muslim power, restored Ottoman control of the Hejaz, and patronized Islamic institutions even as he curtailed their independence. He was also a ruler who prioritized state power over Islamic law, whose relationship with the ulama was instrumental rather than reverential, and whose legacy includes a pattern of secularizing state modernization that subsequent Egyptian governments replicated.
