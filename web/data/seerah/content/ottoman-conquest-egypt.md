# The Ottoman Conquest of Egypt: The Shift of Islamic Power to Istanbul

## After Marj Dabiq

The destruction of the Mamluk army at Marj Dabiq in 922 AH (August 1516 CE) had stripped away the Sultanate's military capacity, but Egypt itself remained. The Mamluk state had always been centered on Egypt — the Nile Valley, with its incomparable agricultural wealth, was the foundation on which the entire system rested. The last Mamluk Sultan, Tuman Bay (Tumanbay), was not present at Marj Dabiq. He had been governing Egypt while Qansuh al-Ghawri commanded the Syrian campaign. When news of the catastrophe reached Cairo, Tuman Bay was proclaimed Sultan and began urgent preparations to resist the Ottoman advance.

Sultan Selim I moved methodically. Having consolidated control of Syria and received the submission of the Hejaz, he turned south toward Egypt in the late months of 922 AH (early 1517 CE). His army crossed the Sinai — the same desert that armies from both directions had crossed since the dawn of Egyptian history — and approached Cairo from the northeast.

## The Battle of Ridaniyya

The decisive battle was fought at Ridaniyya (al-Raydaniyya), northeast of Cairo, on 28 Dhu al-Hijja 922 AH (January 22, 1517 CE). Tuman Bay had prepared carefully. He had positioned his forces on advantageous ground, placed his artillery facing the direction from which he expected the Ottoman army to approach, and created defensive earthworks. He understood that the fundamental Ottoman advantage was firepower, and he attempted to offset it with terrain and fortification.

But Selim outmaneuvered him. The Ottoman army swung around Tuman Bay's position in a wide flanking movement, approaching from a direction that negated the orientation of the Mamluk artillery. The battle became a more even infantry engagement, but the Ottoman Janissaries' discipline and training proved decisive. Tuman Bay's forces were defeated, though not destroyed — a significant portion of the Mamluk army escaped into Cairo.

What followed was several days of bitter urban fighting. Cairo's narrow streets and densely built districts provided cover for Mamluk soldiers who refused to accept defeat. They fought block by block in the older parts of the city, inflicting casualties on the Ottomans and making Selim's triumph costly. Ibn Iyas, the Mamluk chronicler who witnessed these events, recorded the street fighting with anguished precision — watching the city he loved become a battlefield and the regime he had served collapse around him.

## The Capture of Tuman Bay

Tuman Bay escaped the fall of Cairo and organized resistance in the Delta and in Upper Egypt. He appealed to the Bedouin tribes for support and maintained a guerrilla resistance for several months. He came close to inflicting a significant defeat on Ottoman forces at Imbaba in Rabi' al-Awwal 923 AH (March 1517 CE), but the Ottomans ultimately prevailed again.

Tuman Bay was eventually betrayed by Bedouin tribesmen who informed on his hiding place for a promised reward. He was captured in 923 AH and brought to Selim I. His fate was determined: he was publicly hanged at the great Bab Zuwayla gate of Cairo — the same gate from which Mamluk sultans had traditionally launched their military campaigns, decorated with the heads of their enemies, and at which criminals were publicly executed. That the last Mamluk Sultan died at this gate was both historically ironic and deliberately symbolic.

Ibn Iyas recorded that Tuman Bay died with dignity, calling on Allah and the Prophet ﷺ before his death. He was reportedly not yet forty years old. His brief sultanate — from his proclamation after Marj Dabiq to his execution — had lasted only seven months.

## The Preservation of the Mamluk System

One of the most striking aspects of the Ottoman conquest of Egypt is what was not destroyed. Selim I and his advisors made a pragmatic decision: the elaborate Mamluk administrative system was too complex, too locally embedded, and too efficient at revenue collection to simply tear down and replace with Ottoman institutions. Instead, the Ottomans appointed a governor (wali, later called the Beylerbeyi) with overall authority while leaving the Mamluk beys in charge of local administration.

This decision had lasting consequences. The Mamluk beys — the surviving military commanders of the old system — retained their estates, their household troops, and their local authority. They competed among themselves for influence, as they had always done. The Ottoman governor managed the competition and collected the revenues due to Istanbul. In practice, Egypt functioned as a semi-autonomous province in which the fiction of Ottoman sovereignty overlaid a reality of Mamluk administrative power.

This arrangement persisted, with various modifications, until the early 19th century CE. When Napoleon's French army invaded Egypt in 1213 AH (1798 CE), they found a country governed by Mamluk beys under nominal Ottoman sovereignty — a system that had been in place for nearly three centuries.

## The Caliphate Question

One of the most historically significant and disputed aspects of the Ottoman conquest of Egypt is the fate of the shadow Abbasid Caliphate. Since 659 AH (1261 CE), the Mamluk Sultans had maintained a member of the Abbasid family in Cairo with the title of Caliph. The shadow caliphs had no real power — they legitimized Mamluk authority, performed ceremonial functions, and maintained the symbolic continuity of the caliphal institution that had been destroyed by the Mongols in Baghdad in 656 AH.

The last shadow Caliph in Cairo was al-Mutawakkil III (he had held the position twice, deposed and restored). After the Ottoman conquest, Ottoman sources record that al-Mutawakkil III formally surrendered the caliphal title and insignia to Selim I — that he handed over the mantle (burda) of the Prophet ﷺ, the sword of the Prophet ﷺ, and the flag, along with a formal document of transfer.

Modern historians are divided on this account. Many note that the claim appears prominently in later Ottoman sources but is not well-attested in contemporary chronicles, including Ibn Iyas's eyewitness account which does not mention a formal caliphate transfer. The Ottoman interest in claiming caliphal legitimacy grew stronger over subsequent decades — particularly as they sought religious authority to match their political power — and the caliphate transfer story may have been elaborated or constructed to serve this need.

What is clear is that the Ottoman sultans did increasingly assume caliphal titles and authority from the 16th century onward. Sultan Suleiman I in particular was addressed as "Caliph of the Muslims" in religious correspondence and made extensive use of caliphal symbolism. Whether this represented a formal legal transfer from the last Abbasid to the first Ottoman claimant, or a gradual assumption of caliphal authority backed by real power, the practical reality was the same: the Ottoman Sultan became the de facto holder of the most important symbolic office in Sunni Islam.

## Al-Azhar Under Ottoman Rule

The conquest of Egypt brought the most important Sunni institution in the Arab world under Ottoman governance. Al-Azhar — founded in 361 AH (972 CE) by the Fatimid dynasty (initially as a Shia institution, later transformed under the Ayyubids to Sunni teaching) — had by the 16th century CE been for several centuries the foremost center of Sunni Islamic scholarship in Egypt and among the most important in the world.

The Ottomans were generally supportive of al-Azhar. They did not interfere with its curriculum or personnel significantly. They endowed it with additional waqf (religious endowment) resources and supported its scholars. The relationship between the Ottoman governors and al-Azhar's scholarly class (the ulama) was one of mutual dependence: the Ottomans needed al-Azhar's religious legitimacy and scholarly authority for their governance; al-Azhar needed Ottoman political protection and financial support.

Al-Azhar during the Ottoman period (1517-1798 CE) maintained its Sunni Shafi'i character, trained scholars from across the Islamic world, and served as a model of traditional Islamic learning. It produced important scholars and commentaries, though this period is sometimes characterized as one of consolidation and commentary rather than original scholarly breakthrough — a generalization that requires qualification when one examines specific scholars and their contributions.

## The Servant of the Two Holy Mosques

The conquest of Egypt and the prior submission of the Hejaz gave the Ottoman Sultan the most prestigious religious title in the Islamic world: Khadim al-Haramayn al-Sharifayn — Servant of the Two Noble Sanctuaries (Mecca and Medina). This title carried enormous symbolic weight and real responsibilities.

The Ottomans took their custodianship of the holy cities seriously. They funded regular maintenance and expansion of the Masjid al-Haram in Mecca and the Masjid al-Nabawi in Medina, organized and protected the annual Hajj caravans from across the empire, provided water (the Zamzam well) and provisions for pilgrims, and appointed responsible governors for the Hejaz. The Hajj caravan from Damascus and the one from Cairo — the two great annual pilgrim caravans — were state-sponsored and heavily guarded.

This custodianship became central to Ottoman legitimacy. When the Ottoman state weakened in the 18th and early 19th centuries CE, the ability to protect the pilgrimage routes came under challenge from the first Saudi-Wahhabi state in the 19th century AH / 18th century CE. The Wahhabi capture of Mecca and Medina in 1218 AH (1803 CE) and their disruption of traditional pilgrimage practices was seen as a direct challenge not just to local customs but to Ottoman imperial authority.

## The New Order

With the Ottoman conquest of Egypt in 923 AH (1517 CE), the political map of the Islamic world reached a configuration it would maintain for roughly four centuries. The Ottoman Empire now controlled the Arabian Peninsula (including Mecca and Medina), Egypt, Syria, Lebanon, Palestine, Anatolia, and the Balkans. Iraq was conquered subsequently under Suleiman I from the Safavids.

This concentration of Islamic political authority in a single empire was historically unusual. It brought certain advantages: a single legal system applying Hanafi fiqh across vast territories, a unified military protecting the Hejaz, and a single administration capable of mobilizing large-scale resources. It also brought the disadvantages of empire: bureaucratic centralization, the distance of decision-making from local conditions, and the dependence of the entire Islamic world's political order on the health of a single state.

When the Ottoman state weakened in the 17th and 18th centuries CE, the consequences were felt across the entire Islamic world. The fall of the Ottomans in 1337 AH (1918 CE) left not merely Turkey but the entire former Ottoman Arab world in political disarray — a disarray whose effects continue to shape the region in the present day.
