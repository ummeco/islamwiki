# General Amnesty on the Day of the Conquest of Mecca

When the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) entered Mecca on 20 Ramadan, 8 AH (11 January 630 CE), he did so at the head of an army of 10,000 — and he immediately announced a general amnesty for its inhabitants. This proclamation, which forgave the city's leaders for twenty years of persecution, torture, exile, and war against the Muslim community, is remembered as one of the most remarkable acts of mercy in the historical record.

## The Entry

The Muslim army entered Mecca in four columns from different directions. The Prophet's column entered from the north (Thaniyyat Kada'). He was riding his camel, lowering his head in humility before Allah, reciting Surah al-Fath. His head was so low, according to the accounts, that his beard nearly touched the camel's back — an expression of gratitude and humility rather than triumph at one of history's most significant moments.

There was minimal armed resistance. The Meccan leadership, led by Abu Sufyan who had accepted Islam the previous day, had been advised to stay in their homes or in the mosque or in Abu Sufyan's house and they would be safe. The only serious fighting was at Khandama, where Khalid ibn al-Walid's column faced resistance from a small group, killing twelve or thirteen Meccans before it was suppressed.

## The Announcement at the Ka'bah

The Prophet went directly to the Masjid al-Haram and the Ka'bah. He circumambulated it on his camel, touching the Black Stone with his staff. The Ka'bah and its surroundings were filled with 360 idols that had been placed there over the pre-Islamic centuries. The Prophet pointed to each idol with his staff, reciting the Quranic verse: *"Truth has come and falsehood has perished; falsehood is ever bound to perish."* (Quran 17:81) — and each idol toppled as he pointed to it.

Bilal ibn Rabah — the freed slave who had been tortured in Mecca for his faith years earlier — then climbed atop the Ka'bah to give the call to prayer (adhan). For Bilal, it was the most powerful possible expression of reversal: from being dragged through Mecca's streets under a boulder to standing above its holiest site calling the faithful to worship.

## The General Amnesty

After the Ka'bah's purification, the Prophet gathered the Meccans and addressed them. They expected punishment — they had made war on the Prophet for twenty years, tortured his followers, killed his companions, persecuted his family, and exiled the Muslim community twice. The Prophet said:

*"O people of Quraysh, what do you think I will do to you?"*

They replied: "Good — you are a noble brother and the son of a noble brother."

The Prophet replied: *"Go — you are free."*

This declaration — *"Go — you are free"* (ithaqu al-tulaqā) — was the general amnesty for the people of Mecca. In one stroke, twenty years of hostility were forgiven. No executions were ordered. No property was confiscated. No families were enslaved.

A small number of individuals were excluded from the general amnesty — those who had committed specific crimes of murder or particularly egregious persecution of Muslims. Even from this list, the Prophet accepted the repentance of virtually all who came to him.

## Individuals Pardoned

Among those specifically pardoned after initially being excluded:

**Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl** — the son of Abu Jahl, one of the Prophet's fiercest enemies, who had personally led military expeditions against the Muslims. He fled to Yemen; his wife Umm Hakim brought him back. When he presented himself to the Prophet and accepted Islam, the Prophet received him warmly. Ikrimah later became one of the most courageous Muslim warriors, dying as a martyr at the Battle of Yarmouk in 15 AH.

**Wahshi ibn Harb** — the enslaved man who had killed Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib at Uhud as a spearman. He had been freed for the act. He converted to Islam and the Prophet accepted his repentance, but said: "Do not show me your face often" — reflecting genuine human grief over Hamzah's death, even while accepting the conversion.

**Hind bint Utba** — Abu Sufyan's wife, who had ordered the mutilation of Hamzah's body and reportedly chewed on his liver. She came to the Prophet veiled, accepted Islam, and was accepted. Only after her acceptance did she reveal her identity.

## The Theological Dimension

The general amnesty was not merely political strategy. It was a direct enactment of the Quranic principle: *"Repel evil by that which is better, and thereupon, the one who is hostile to you will become as if he were a devoted friend."* (Quran 41:34)

The Prophet's closest companions — people who had been tortured, whose family members had been killed, who had been driven from their homes — accepted this amnesty without public protest. It represented a collective spiritual achievement of extraordinary proportions.

## Significance

The conquest of Mecca and the general amnesty that followed became the model for Islamic conquest in subsequent generations. The early Islamic conquests of Syria, Egypt, Persia, and beyond were typically characterized — at least in the normative Islamic understanding — by similar principles: protection of non-combatants, preservation of places of worship, freedom for inhabitants to maintain their religion, no compulsion to convert, and amnesty for those who submitted peacefully.

## Sources

The account of the conquest and the amnesty is preserved in Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
