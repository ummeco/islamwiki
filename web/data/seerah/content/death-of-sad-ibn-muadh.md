# The Death of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh

## The Chief of the Aws

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh was the chief of the Aws tribe — one of the two main Ansar tribes of Medina, the other being the Khazraj. He had accepted Islam at the hands of Musab ibn Umayr, the companion the Prophet ﷺ had sent to Medina before the Hijra, and his conversion had brought the entire Banu Abd al-Ashhal clan into Islam in a single day. He was among the most honored of the Ansar — a man whose physical presence the narrations describe as commanding, whose character was marked by both fierce loyalty and measured judgment.

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh participated in the Battle of Badr and became one of the most respected voices in the early Muslim community. It was Sa'd — at the shura (council) before Badr — who gave the speech that most moved the Prophet ﷺ: "We have believed in you and we have testified that what you brought is the truth, and we have given you our covenants and our commitments to hear and obey. So go ahead, O Messenger of Allah, and we will be with you wherever you wish... We are experienced in war, and we are trustworthy in combat. Perhaps Allah will show you from us what will cool your eyes."

## The Arrow at the Trench

During the Battle of the Trench (5 AH), Sa'd ibn Mu'adh was struck by an arrow in his arm — specifically in the brachial artery, the major blood vessel of the upper arm. The Prophet ﷺ had a tent set up for him in the mosque so that he could visit him easily. Sa'd, lying wounded and aware that his injury was likely fatal, made a specific supplication: "O Allah, if You have decreed that there remain any conflict between me and Quraysh, then preserve me for it. But if You have decreed that the war between us and them is over, then let this wound be the cause of my martyrdom, and do not let me die until I have been cooled regarding Banu Qurayza."

Allah answered both requests. Sa'd survived the battle and was carried on a donkey to pronounce the judgment on Banu Qurayza after their surrender — the judgment that the adult male fighters would be executed, the women and children taken captive, the property distributed. When the Prophet ﷺ said "You have judged by the judgment of Allah from above seven heavens," the words were directed to a dying man whose wound was reopened by the exertion of the journey. Sa'd returned to the mosque and his wound — which had begun to heal — broke open again and bled until his death.

## The Death and the Divine Response

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh died in 5 AH — some narrations place his death shortly after the judgment of Banu Qurayza, others immediately after. When he died, the Prophet ﷺ described the divine response: "The Throne of the Most Merciful (Arsh al-Rahman) shook at the death of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh." This hadith, preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, is one of the most striking in the hadith literature: a statement that the death of a single human being caused the Throne of Allah to move — not literally in a physical sense that Islamic theology would assign to the divine attributes, but as an expression of the magnitude of Sa'd's standing before Allah.

The companions were present at his burial. Sa'd had died as a martyr — killed by his wound in the path of Allah — and the Prophet ﷺ carried his bier. A man from among the hypocrites remarked: "I have never seen a bier lighter than this one." The Prophet ﷺ replied: "And how should it not be light? The angels are carrying it." Another narration records that Sa'd's mother was present and weeping; the Prophet ﷺ said to her: "Your son's actions were pure, and now his reward with Allah is pure."

## His Legacy

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh's death left a significant gap in the Medinan leadership. He had been the chief of a major tribe, a battle veteran, a man whose judgment the Prophet ﷺ trusted so completely that he submitted the fate of Banu Qurayza to his ruling. His death — as a martyr in the wake of the Trench and Banu Qurayza siege — marked the end of the first major phase of Medinan politics. The city he had helped defend from the confederate coalition would, within two years of his death, see the Prophet ﷺ depart for the Hudaybiyyah and then return as the conqueror of Mecca. Sa'd did not live to see the conquest his tribe had helped make possible.

The hadith about the Throne shaking has been discussed extensively in Islamic theology. The mainstream scholarly understanding is that it expresses, in language beyond ordinary human frames of reference, that Sa'd's death was an event of cosmic significance — that the death of a righteous person is not merely a biological event but a moment recognized and responded to at the level of divine reality. The Prophet ﷺ did not say this of everyone who died. He said it of Sa'd.

## Sa'd and the Ansar

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh's life encapsulates the story of the Ansar in the most concentrated form. He heard Musab ibn Umayr present Islam before the Hijra, recognized its truth, and brought his entire clan into the religion within a single day. He lived through Badr, Uhud, and al-Khandaq — the three defining battles of the Medinan period. He pronounced the judgment that ended the last internal security crisis of Medina's Medinan period. And then he died of his wound, the instrument of Allah's will in that judgment, recognized by the Prophet ﷺ as having risen to a cosmic significance that most human beings cannot reach.

The Ansar's story is one of the great stories of the seerah — a city that opened its arms to a prophet and his community when they had nowhere to go, that gave their wealth, their land, and finally their lives in defense of a message they had embraced freely. Sa'd ibn Mu'adh was their greatest chief in that generation and his death, honored by the Throne's shaking, was the seerah's acknowledgment of what the Ansar had given.
