# The First Compilation of the Quran: Abu Bakr's Preservation of Revelation

## The Crisis at Yamama

In the twelfth year after the Hijra, the Muslim armies fought one of the most consequential battles in the history of Islam. The Battle of Yamama, waged against the false prophet Musaylima al-Kadhdhab and his followers among the Banu Hanifa, resulted in a decisive Muslim victory — but at a devastating cost. Among the fallen were an estimated seventy reciters of the Quran, men who had committed its entirety to memory and transmitted it with the precision that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself had instilled in them.

The loss shook the Muslim community to its core. Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), the second most senior Companion and future Caliph, recognized immediately what this meant for the preservation of revelation. If battles continued to claim the lives of the huffaz — those who carried the Quran in their hearts — then a portion of the divine text could one day be lost, or disputed, with no definitive written record to settle the matter.

It was this fear that drove Umar to the caliph, Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him), with an urgent proposal: that the scattered written records of the Quran be gathered and compiled into a single authoritative document.

## Abu Bakr's Initial Hesitation

The exchange between Umar and Abu Bakr is preserved in detail in Sahih al-Bukhari, in the Book of the Virtues of the Quran (Kitab Fada'il al-Quran). Umar approached Abu Bakr and said: "The killing was heavy among the reciters of the Quran on the day of Yamama, and I fear that there will be more casualties among the reciters in other battles, and that a great portion of the Quran may be lost. I think you should order the Quran to be compiled."

Abu Bakr's response was immediate and principled: "How can I do something that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ did not do?" He was not being obstinate. He was expressing a genuine theological concern. To compile the Quran in a bound volume was an act that the Prophet ﷺ had not explicitly commanded, and Abu Bakr was deeply cautious about innovating in religious matters. He feared that to act without prophetic precedent in something as sacred as the preservation of revelation might itself constitute a form of overstepping.

Umar continued to press his case, however, and — as the hadith records — "Allah expanded Abu Bakr's chest toward that which He had expanded Umar's chest." The Caliph came to recognize that Umar's reasoning was sound: the principle underlying the action (preserving the Quran) was itself rooted in the Quran and the Sunnah. As Umar himself argued, it was a good deed (khayr), and good deeds serve the religion even if not explicitly mandated in a specific instance.

Abu Bakr then recalled something important — that the Prophet ﷺ himself had employed scribes to record revelation as it was received. The raw material already existed in written form. The task was to gather, verify, and preserve it systematically.

## The Appointment of Zayd ibn Thabit

Having made his decision, Abu Bakr summoned Zayd ibn Thabit al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him), a young man from Medina who had served as one of the Prophet's ﷺ primary scribes. Zayd was uniquely qualified: he had written down revelation directly under the Prophet's supervision, he was a hafiz who had memorized the entire Quran, and he had been present at the final review of the Quran with the Prophet ﷺ in the year of his death. In that final Ramadan, Jibril (peace be upon him) had reviewed the entire Quran with the Prophet twice — and Zayd had been present.

Zayd's own reaction, as recorded in Bukhari, mirrors Abu Bakr's initial hesitation: "By Allah, if he had asked me to move a mountain, it would not have been heavier upon me than what he had ordered me to do regarding the Quran." The weight of the task was not lost on him. He was being asked to take responsibility for something of cosmic significance.

Abu Bakr's words to him were direct: "You are a wise young man, and we have no doubts about you. You used to write down the revelation for the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. So search for the Quran and compile it." Zayd accepted and set to work.

## The Methodology of Verification

What distinguished Zayd's compilation from a mere personal project was his extraordinarily rigorous methodology of verification. He did not simply gather every piece of parchment or palm rib on which Quranic verses had been written and stitch them together. He imposed a dual standard that protected against error, forgery, or memory lapses.

For a verse to be accepted into the compilation, two conditions had to be met simultaneously: first, it had to be found in written form, having been written in the presence of the Prophet ﷺ himself; and second, it had to be attested by at least two witnesses who could testify from memory that they had heard it from the Prophet ﷺ directly.

This dual requirement — written record confirmed by living memory — was a methodological masterstroke. Written records could theoretically be forged or misdated. Memory alone could err or be confused. But the convergence of both forms of testimony provided a degree of certainty that no single source could offer.

Zayd himself said: "I searched for the Quran and gathered it from date leaf stalks, thin white stones, and the breasts of men." The physical media on which the Quran had been written during the Prophet's lifetime were varied: pieces of leather (adim), shoulder bones (aktaf), palm leaf ribs (asib), flat white stones (likhaf), and the wooden boards or tablets (alwah) that scribes used. These were gathered from across Medina, from the houses of Companions and the mosque.

## The Famous Incident of Surah al-Tawbah

One of the most cited episodes in the account of this compilation involves the final two verses of Surah al-Tawbah — verses 128 and 129, which begin: "There has certainly come to you a Messenger from among yourselves..." These two verses presented Zayd with a difficult moment in his work.

As Zayd gathered and verified verses, he found that these two particular verses of al-Tawbah were not found with any written source except with one man: Abu Khuzayma al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him). This was a problem, because the methodology required written attestation, and a single witness would ordinarily not suffice.

However, Zayd recalled something significant. The Prophet ﷺ had on a specific occasion declared that Abu Khuzayma's single testimony equaled the testimony of two men. This ruling had been pronounced in a non-scriptural context — relating to a transaction between the Prophet ﷺ and a man named Sawad ibn Qays — but it established Abu Khuzayma's exceptional trustworthiness as a witness in the Prophet's own estimation.

Thus, the two verses were accepted based on Abu Khuzayma's sole written testimony, which by prophetic ruling counted as two witnesses. This careful, principled reasoning illustrates the level of scholarly precision Zayd brought to his task.

## The Physical Compilation and Its Custodians

The completed compilation — the suhuf (sheets) — was bound and delivered to Abu Bakr, who kept it with him until his death. The sheets were written on the same materials used during the revelation period, collected and arranged by surah in the order established by the Prophet ﷺ himself.

When Abu Bakr died (13 AH), the suhuf passed to Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), who retained them throughout his caliphate. Upon Umar's martyrdom (23 AH), the sheets passed to his daughter Hafsah bint Umar (may Allah be pleased with her), who was also one of the Prophet's ﷺ wives and a hafizah of the Quran. Hafsah kept the suhuf in her possession and they would later serve as the primary reference when Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) undertook the standardization of the Quran into a single codex approximately two decades later.

## The Distinction Between Abu Bakr's Compilation and Uthman's Codex

It is important to understand the difference between what Abu Bakr accomplished and what Uthman later did. Abu Bakr's compilation was an emergency preservation measure — the gathering of all existing written Quranic material into a single secure collection. It was not a standardized, single-script codex designed for distribution. It remained in one place, with successive custodians, as a master reference.

Uthman's later work (approximately 25 AH) addressed a different problem: the emergence of variant recitations in different regions of the expanding empire, leading to disputes among Muslims. Uthman used Hafsah's suhuf as the primary reference source and produced multiple identical copies in a standardized Qurayshi script, which were then distributed to major cities. The personal copies held by individual Companions were subsequently burned to prevent confusion.

The two projects were therefore complementary and sequential: Abu Bakr preserved the raw material; Uthman standardized the presentation.

## The Theological Significance: Divine Preservation

Allah the Exalted declared in the Quran: "Verily, it is We who have sent down the Reminder, and it is We who are its Guardians" (15:9). This promise of preservation was not merely passive — it operated through human agents whom Allah guided and equipped.

The classical scholar Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani (may Allah have mercy on him), in his Fath al-Bari — his monumental commentary on Sahih al-Bukhari — explains that Abu Bakr's compilation was among the most virtuous acts any Caliph performed, for it protected the religion at its source. Had the written Quran been lost and only memorization remained, future generations would have faced unresolvable disputes. The suhuf became the anchor of textual authority.

This event also demonstrates a broader principle in Islamic jurisprudence: maslaha mursalah (unrestricted public interest) as a valid basis for legal decisions not explicitly mandated by text. The Companions understood that protecting the Quran was itself a Quranic and prophetic imperative, even if the specific mechanism of compilation was not textually prescribed.

The fact that the Quran read by 1.8 billion Muslims today — in every language, in every corner of the earth — traces its textual lineage directly to Zayd ibn Thabit's work under Abu Bakr, later confirmed by Hafsah's custody and Uthman's distribution, is one of the most remarkable facts in the history of world religions. No other scripture can trace such a meticulous, documented chain of preservation.

## Conclusion

The compilation of the Quran under Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) stands as one of the most consequential decisions in the history of Islam. Driven by the crisis of Yamama, initiated by Umar's urgency, entrusted to Zayd ibn Thabit's extraordinary diligence, and authorized by Abu Bakr's piety and wisdom, this project secured the foundation upon which all subsequent Islamic scholarship rests.

The methodology employed — dual verification through written records and oral testimony — established principles of textual criticism that would later be refined and applied to the science of hadith. The custodianship chain — Abu Bakr, Umar, Hafsah — ensured continuity until the definitive Uthmani codex unified the Muslim world around a single, agreed-upon text.

In it, we see the fulfillment of Allah's promise: the Quran was preserved not by miracle alone, but through the careful, dedicated, God-fearing work of those whom Allah chose to be instruments of its protection.
