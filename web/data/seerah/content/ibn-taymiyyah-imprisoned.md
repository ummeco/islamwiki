# Ibn Taymiyyah's Imprisonments: Faith Tested by Power

## The Pattern of a Scholar's Persecution

The multiple imprisonments of Shaykh al-Islam Ahmad ibn Taymiyyah (may Allah have mercy on him) form one of the most instructive narratives in Islamic scholarly history — instructive not because imprisonment is praiseworthy in itself, but because the reasons for his imprisonment reveal the genuine tensions between scholarly commitment to the Quran and Sunnah on one hand and political authority and majoritarian scholarly opinion on the other.

Ibn Taymiyyah was imprisoned between four and six times (sources differ on how to count certain periods of restriction and exile) over approximately three decades of his scholarly career. Each imprisonment arose from a specific controversy in which he had taken a position that authorities — whether political rulers or a coalition of opposing scholars — found intolerable enough to warrant imprisonment.

Reading these imprisonments carefully reveals something important: in virtually every case, subsequent generations of Sunni scholars have concluded that Ibn Taymiyyah's positions were either correct or defensible, and that the imprisonments were politically motivated attacks on a scholar whose independence of mind and popular influence made him threatening to various interests. His biographers Ibn Kathir and al-Dhahabi, who knew him personally, treated his imprisonments as parallels to the trial of Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal — the great Hanbali Imam imprisoned for refusing to accept the Mu'tazili doctrine of the created Quran under Abbasid pressure.

## First Imprisonment: Egypt, 699 AH / 1299 CE

The immediate cause of Ibn Taymiyyah's first imprisonment was his *al-Aqeedah al-Hamawiyya* — a theological treatise on the divine attributes, written in response to a question from the people of Hama. In this text, he defended the Athari (Hanbali) position on the divine attributes against what he considered the *ta'wil* (allegorical reinterpretation) of the Ash'ari school.

This brought him into direct conflict with Ash'ari scholars in Egypt, who charged him with *tajsim* (anthropomorphism — likening Allah to created things). The charge was, from his perspective, a misrepresentation: he explicitly stated in all his theological works that Allah's attributes do not resemble created attributes in any way, and that the divine attributes are affirmed *bi-la kayfiyya* (without asking how). The Ash'ari position held that certain attributes could not be taken in their apparent sense and required allegorical interpretation; Ibn Taymiyyah held this itself amounted to a denial of the attribute's real meaning.

He was summoned to Cairo in 699 AH (1299 CE) to face a tribunal. His trial before Egyptian scholars and officials resulted in his imprisonment in Alexandria. He was subsequently released after several months. The scholarly community was divided: many scholars, including those who disagreed with his specific positions, recognized that the charges against him were exaggerated and politically motivated.

## Second and Third Imprisonments: Egypt, 705-712 AH

His second major imprisonment (705-707 AH) and third (709-712 AH) arose from related controversies, particularly around the question of travel to visit shrines and graves for the purpose of prayer and intercession.

Ibn Taymiyyah's position on this issue was one of the clearest and most consistent throughout his career. He distinguished sharply between two categories:

**Permitted**: Visiting the graves of the righteous to recite Quran for them, to make du'a for them, and to reflect on death and the hereafter. This is established by the Prophet's ﷺ own practice and his saying: "I used to forbid you from visiting graves, but now visit them, for they remind you of the hereafter" (Muslim).

**Not permitted**: Traveling specifically to a grave or shrine for the purpose of making du'a *to* the deceased or seeking their intercession with Allah (*tawassul bil-amwat*). He argued this was not established in the Sunnah and represented a form of religious innovation that, if unchecked, slides toward the polytheistic practice of seeking help from the dead.

His opponents — including scholars of the Shafi'i and Maliki schools — held that certain forms of *tawassul* through the deceased were permitted based on the broader principle of seeking intercession through those of elevated status with Allah. This was, and remains, a genuine scholarly disagreement within Sunni Islam.

The political dimension was significant: the tomb of the Prophet ﷺ in Medina was a major site of popular veneration, and any suggestion that certain practices at graves were impermissible was immediately interpreted as an attack on the Prophet's status. Ibn Taymiyyah's careful distinction between the Prophet ﷺ himself (whose intercession on the Day of Judgment is affirmed in the Quran and hadith — *al-Shafa'a*) and certain practices at his grave was not always reflected in how his opponents characterized his views.

He was imprisoned in Egypt, in the Citadel of Cairo, for periods that collectively amounted to several years during this phase. Throughout these imprisonments, he continued writing and teaching, as his biographers consistently note. Imprisonment, for him, was inconvenient but not incapacitating.

## Fourth Imprisonment: Damascus, 720 AH

The fourth major imprisonment (720 AH) arose from a different controversy: his fatwa on divorce. The established position in all four madhabs was that a man who pronounced the divorce formula three times in a single session had issued three divorces, making the divorce irrevocable (until a subsequent marriage with another husband, known as *tahlil* — also controversial for other reasons). This position, while not the only early opinion on the matter, had become the operative consensus of the schools.

Ibn Taymiyyah issued a fatwa that such a triple utterance constituted only one revocable divorce — citing reports from some Companions and early scholars who held this view, and arguing that this was the stronger position from the evidential perspective. His argument had genuine merit: the Quranic model for divorce spreads three utterances across three separate periods, and some Companion reports (including one from Ibn Abbas) suggest that triple utterances were counted as one divorce during the Prophet's ﷺ time and Abu Bakr's time.

The practical consequence of his fatwa was potentially enormous: women who had been told they were irrevocably divorced might be told by Ibn Taymiyyah that they were not. This disrupted established legal practice in a way that many scholars and authorities considered dangerously destabilizing.

The Mamluk authorities imprisoned him in Damascus for approximately nine months (720-721 AH) over this fatwa. He was eventually released, though the legal controversy did not end.

## The Final Imprisonment: Damascus Citadel, 726-728 AH

Ibn Taymiyyah's fifth and final imprisonment — the one from which he would not emerge alive — began in 726 AH (1326 CE). The proximate cause was again the shrine visitation controversy: a new fatwa he had issued on the impermissibility of traveling specifically to visit graves for the purpose of seeking the intercession of the deceased.

Scholars who opposed him petitioned the Mamluk authorities in Egypt and in Damascus. The political moment was unfavorable to him: certain of his former supporters had withdrawn their protection, and his popular following — which had made him difficult to suppress in previous years — was not sufficient to prevent this final measure.

He was confined in the Citadel of Damascus. What distinguished this imprisonment from previous ones was its severity: at some point in 728 AH, the authorities denied him writing materials — paper, ink, and books. Ibn Kathir reports that Ibn Taymiyyah then wrote with charcoal on whatever surfaces were available.

His student Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya, who was imprisoned alongside him for a period, later described the scholarly work that continued even under these conditions — discussions of theology, jurisprudence, Quran commentary, and spiritual matters continued among the imprisoned scholars.

## His Famous Statement: The Prison of the Free Man

Among the most quoted sayings attributed to Ibn Taymiyyah in the accounts of his biographers is his reported response to the threat of imprisonment and worse: "What can my enemies do to me? My paradise is in my breast — wherever I go, it goes with me. If they imprison me, it is seclusion with Allah (*khalwa*). If they exile me, it is travel in the path of Allah. If they kill me, it is martyrdom."

Ibn Kathir, his devoted student, preserves this saying and the broader picture of Ibn Taymiyyah's psychological and spiritual composure under pressure. He was not a bitter or resentful man — he distinguished sharply between his scholarly opponents (whom he debated with the full rigor of his intellect) and personal enemies (toward whom he practiced forgiveness). He reportedly stated that he bore no personal grudge against those who had him imprisoned and would not prevent them from entering his home if they came.

## The Legacy of the Imprisonments

The historical significance of Ibn Taymiyyah's imprisonments operates on several levels:

**For Islamic jurisprudence**: His cases established important precedents for the question of when scholarly disagreement justifies political suppression. The broader Sunni scholarly tradition eventually concluded that his imprisonments were unjust — examples of political authority overstepping into scholarly matters. Al-Dhahabi's private letter to Ibn Taymiyyah, sometimes called *al-Nasiha al-Dhahabiyya*, acknowledged his scholarly greatness while gently criticizing aspects of his approach; but even al-Dhahabi did not endorse the imprisonments as just.

**For Islamic thought**: The imprisonments produced conditions in which some of his most significant writing was done. The Citadel imprisonment of 726-728 AH, despite its final severity, allowed time for reflection and composition that produced some of his final theological formulations.

**For his students**: Particularly for Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya, who shared his imprisonment and absorbed the full depth of his teacher's scholarship under the intense conditions of confinement. The works Ibn al-Qayyim subsequently produced bear the direct imprint of this intimate scholarly relationship.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "The most severely tested people are the prophets, then those most similar to them in righteousness, then those most similar to them" (al-Tirmidhi, classed as hasan sahih). Ibn Taymiyyah, in his repeated imprisonments for holding positions he had derived from the Quran and Sunnah, stands as one of the most prominent exemplifications of this principle in Islamic scholarly history.
