# The Beginning of the Prophet's Final Illness

The Prophet Muhammad's (peace and blessings be upon him) final illness began in the month of Safar, 11 AH, after his return from the Farewell Pilgrimage. It was characterized by a severe and persistent fever that gradually worsened over approximately two weeks until his death on 12 Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH.

## Origins of the Illness

Classical Islamic sources report several accounts of the illness's origin. The Prophet himself is reported to have connected it to the poisoning incident at Khaybar (7 AH), in which a Jewish woman named Zaynab bint al-Harith had placed poison in a roasted sheep presented to the Prophet. The Prophet tasted the meat and then stopped, saying that the shoulder had told him it was poisoned. He reportedly said: "I still feel the effect of the food I ate at Khaybar."

Aisha reported that the illness was associated with pain in the Prophet's head and high fever — so intense that visitors could feel the heat of his fever without directly touching him. He reportedly said: "No prophet dies until he has been shown his place in Paradise, then given the choice."

## The Progression

The illness began while the Prophet was in the home of Maymunah bint al-Harith. As his condition worsened, he asked permission from his other wives to spend his illness in Aisha's chamber, as it was his turn to be with her and he wished to remain in one place. All his wives agreed. He was helped to Aisha's room, supported by Ali ibn Abi Talib and al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib.

Aisha recorded that at times his fever was so extreme that she would pour water over him to bring his temperature down — using water from seven different wells or seven skins of water. Despite his condition, he continued to attend the communal prayers for as long as he was able. He would sometimes go to the mosque supported by companions, or was carried there.

## Directing Abu Bakr to Lead Prayer

As the illness progressed and the Prophet could no longer lead the communal prayers, he designated Abu Bakr as-Siddiq to lead in his place. When Aisha suggested Umar ibn al-Khattab, or (in some accounts) suggested that Abu Bakr was too tender-hearted and wept easily, the Prophet was firm: "Command Abu Bakr to lead the people in prayer."

This designation carried significance beyond the immediate practical matter of who would lead the salah. Many companions understood it as an indication of the Prophet's preference for who should lead the community after his death.

## Final Appearances

On the Sunday before his death, during a brief improvement in his condition, the Prophet appeared at the door of Aisha's chamber while Abu Bakr was leading the Fajr prayer. The companions, seeing him, were overjoyed and assumed he was recovering. He smiled at the sight of them praying in orderly rows — a sight that, Anas ibn Malik later said, he had never seen more beautiful. The Prophet motioned for the prayer to continue and withdrew.

He also gave a final khutbah (sermon) in the mosque during this period, addressing the community from his sickbed or from the minbar. He spoke of the importance of prayer, of care for those in one's charge, and offered words of farewell that the companions recognized, in retrospect, as such.

## Theological Reflection

The Prophet's illness and the manner of his death reinforced several theological principles:

**The humanity of the Prophet.** The Prophet suffered as humans suffer — with fever, pain, and the agonies of dying. This was not a weakness but a sign of his humanity, which is one of Islam's insistences: the Prophet was a human being chosen by Allah, not a divine or semi-divine figure.

**The perfection of the religion.** The verse "This day I have perfected for you your religion" (Quran 5:3) had been revealed at Arafat during the Farewell Pilgrimage — a divine signal that the mission was complete. The Prophet's death was therefore not an interruption of an incomplete work, but the natural conclusion of a completed one.

**The test for the community.** Umar's initial denial, and the community's grief, revealed how deeply the companions had loved the Prophet. Abu Bakr's measured response demonstrated the necessity of grounding faith in the Quran's testimony rather than emotional attachment to the Prophet's person.

## Sources

The account of the Prophet's final illness is preserved in extensive detail across Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghaazi and Kitab al-Marda), Sahih Muslim, and the biographical works of Ibn Hisham and Ibn Saad. Aisha is the primary transmitter of many of these accounts, given that she was the one who cared for the Prophet through much of his final days.
