# Expedition Against Banu Lihyan

The expedition against Banu Lihyan in Jumada al-Awwal 6 AH (approximately October 627 CE) was organized by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) in part as a punitive response to the massacre of Muslim missionaries at al-Raji' in 4 AH, in which Banu Lihyan had been implicated, and as a show of force in the Tihamah region. It is also notable for demonstrating the Prophet's personal humor and lightness of manner even in military operations.

## Background: Al-Raji'

In Safar 4 AH, a group of six to ten Muslim missionaries had been betrayed at the well of al-Raji' — a location in the territory of the Hudhail tribe. Banu Lihyan was a sub-tribe of Hudhail, and some sources indicate their involvement in the massacre in which Khubaib ibn Adi and other companions were killed. The Prophet had mourned these losses and sought to respond.

## The Expedition

The Prophet organized approximately 200 men and marched toward the territory of Banu Lihyan in the Hijaz mountains — the region between Mecca and Ta'if. He took a route through the Najd to conceal his direction of march, reaching the Banu Lihyan territory after a difficult mountain journey.

However, Banu Lihyan had received warning and fled into the mountain heights before the Muslim force arrived. The Prophet could not bring them to battle. He moved to the Red Sea coast and then toward the territory of Quraysh's allies, demonstrating Muslim military mobility through the area.

## The Prophet's Humor During the Expedition

Despite the expedition's failure to find its primary target, it is remembered in the hadith literature for a moment that reveals the Prophet's lighter side. During the march, he divided the army into two groups and organized a race or competitive march between them. The accounts suggest he was maintaining troop morale and unit cohesion during a difficult operation that had not achieved its military objective.

When the force reached a gathering point after the competitive march, he asked the winning group to seek forgiveness (istighfar) for the losing group — turning a friendly military competition into an act of spiritual generosity.

## The Du'a' Against Banu Lihyan

The Prophet's sustained qunoot al-nazilah (supplication against those who harmed the Muslim community) during the month following the al-Raji' massacre had included du'a' against Banu Lihyan and the other tribes involved. The subsequent expedition, even without a battle, was understood as a fulfillment of the obligation to seek redress for the martyrs.

## Context in the Seerah

The expedition to Banu Lihyan falls in the period between the Battle of the Trench (Dhul Qa'dah 5 AH) and the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (Dhul Qa'dah 6 AH) — a period of multiple smaller military operations as the Prophet sought to maintain Muslim military initiative in the face of the Quraysh coalition's continued threat.

Several expeditions occurred in this period: the Banu Lihyan expedition, the Expedition of Dhul Qarad (in response to a raid on the Prophet's camels), and the Expedition of Banu al-Mustaliq. Together they reflect the Prophet's strategy of maintaining military pressure across multiple fronts while consolidating the community's internal strength after the severe test of the Trench.

## Minor Character Revealing Episodes

The expedition to Banu Lihyan, though a minor military operation that achieved no battle, is valued in Islamic biographical literature for the glimpses it provides of the Prophet's humanity: his playfulness in organizing the march competition, his concern for morale during a frustrating campaign, and his integration of spiritual practice (the forgiveness supplication) into the campaign's activities.

These details — preserved by companions who participated — give texture to the seerah that purely political and military accounts lack.

## Sources

The expedition to Banu Lihyan is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*, and Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*.
