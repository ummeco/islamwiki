# The Prophet's Last Sermon in the Mosque

## The Final Address from the Pulpit

During the final illness, the Prophet ﷺ came to the mosque for the last time on a Saturday, four days before his death — though the narrations vary on the exact day. He came out with Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib and Ali ibn Abi Talib supporting him on each side, his feet dragging, his head wrapped in a cloth against the pain of the fever. He mounted the pulpit — the same pulpit from which he had led the Friday prayers and addressed the community across the Medinan years — and spoke to the assembled congregation for what would be the last time.

The address from the pulpit during the final illness is distinct from the addresses at the Farewell Pilgrimage at Arafat, at Mina, and during the Days of Tashreeq. The Arafat addresses are the Farewell Sermon in the classical sense; this address in the mosque was a final message to the Medinan community specifically, delivered while he was gravely ill and which the companions received with the understanding that they were hearing the Prophet ﷺ for the last time from his pulpit.

## The Content of the Address

The narrations preserve several elements of the final mosque address. Among the most widely cited are:

The Prophet ﷺ asked forgiveness for any companion he had wronged and offered restitution: "If I have flogged someone's back, here is my back — let him take retribution. If I have taken someone's property, here is my property — let him take it." The companions wept. A man stood and asked the Prophet ﷺ to repay a debt of three dirhams; the Prophet ﷺ paid it without questioning.

The Prophet ﷺ expressed his farewell to the community: "Perhaps one of you hopes to meet me at the Hawd (the Pool), and I hope to meet you there too." He said that the pool was real and that he would be there for those who came to him.

He warned the community about the graves of the prophets — forbidding the kind of veneration that had been practiced over the graves of the prophets before him. He said: "Do not make my grave a place of worship. Allah's curse is upon those who take the graves of their prophets as mosques."

He spoke about prayer and the rights of those whom the right hands possessed (enslaved people). Some narrations record these as among his final repeated commands — "Prayer, prayer, and what your right hands possess" — aligning with the same reported emphasis during his final illness at home.

## The Companions' Grief

The companions who were present described the address with an awareness of finality that the narrations preserve explicitly. They knew from the state of the Prophet ﷺ — carried to the pulpit, his feet dragging, his face flushed with fever — that they were likely hearing him for the last time. Some wept openly. Others fixed the words in their memories with the attention people give to things they know they will not hear again.

The pulpit itself — the minbar — had been constructed for the Prophet ﷺ in the early Medinan period when the size of the congregation had grown too large for him to be heard and seen while addressing from the floor. He had mounted it for the first time and then came back down to touch the date-palm trunk against which he had previously leaned — the trunk, according to the narrations, was heard to weep at being abandoned. Now, in his final illness, he mounted the minbar for the last time, supported by two companions, to say farewell to the community he had built.

## The Farewell in Three Stages

The Islamic tradition recognizes three distinct farewell addresses by the Prophet ﷺ in the final period of his life: the Farewell Sermon at Arafat (the primary and most comprehensive), the addresses at Mina during the Days of Tashreeq (which repeated and extended Arafat's themes), and this final mosque address during the illness (which contained the personal farewell, the offer of restitution, and the warnings about the grave). Together they form a triad of final messages — the theological summary at Arafat, the extended Hajj instruction at Mina, and the personal farewell in Medina.

The offer of restitution from the pulpit — "here is my back, here is my property, let him take what is owed" — was a form of the Prophet's ﷺ prophetic scrupulousness in his final days: ensuring that he left nothing unresolved, no debt unpaid, no wrong unacknowledged. He had lived his entire public life under the scrutiny of the community, and he left it the same way — publicly, on the pulpit, inviting accountability. The man who stood and asked for three dirhams received them. The Prophet ﷺ died without financial debts.

## The Legacy of the Pulpit

The minbar in Medina's mosque has been a site of continuous Islamic preaching since the Prophet ﷺ built it and mounted it for the first time. The institution of the Friday khutba — the sermon from the pulpit — derives directly from the Prophet's ﷺ own practice in Medina, and the minbar has served as the model for every pulpit in every mosque in fourteen centuries of Islamic practice. The Prophet ﷺ who mounted it for the last time, pale and feverish, supported on both sides, to offer restitution and say farewell, left the pulpit as one of the most symbolically charged objects in Islamic civilization — the place where the community heard its Prophet for the last time.

## The Man on the Pulpit

There is an image in the seerah that appears nowhere else: the Prophet ﷺ being supported on each side by Abbas and Ali, feet dragging, approaching the minbar of his own mosque. He had walked that same route hundreds of times under normal circumstances — to lead the Friday prayer, to address the community at moments of decision, to read the Quran aloud to those assembled. Now he was being carried. The congregation watched him mount the pulpit for the last time and heard him say: if I have wronged you, I offer restitution. If I have taken your property, here it is. If I have flogged anyone, here is my back. Then he told them he hoped to meet them at the Pool. Then he warned them about making his grave a place of worship. They wept, and he descended, and that was the last time the Prophet ﷺ addressed them from the minbar — carrying the same scrupulousness in his last address that he had carried in his first.
