# UN Partition of Palestine and the Nakba

## Background: Mandatory Palestine and the Question of Sovereignty

By the early twentieth century, the land of Palestine — known in Islamic tradition as Bayt al-Maqdis and its environs — had been part of the Ottoman Empire for four centuries. When the Ottoman Empire collapsed following World War I, Britain assumed control under a League of Nations Mandate. The Balfour Declaration of 1917 had already promised British support for "a national home for the Jewish people" in Palestine, a commitment that placed the British Mandate authority in direct tension with the Arab majority population. By 1947, the demographics of Mandatory Palestine stood at approximately 1.3 million Arab inhabitants (Muslim and Christian) and roughly 600,000 Jewish residents, the majority of whom had immigrated during the preceding decades of Zionist settlement activity.

The United Nations, newly formed after World War II, inherited the Palestine question from the British, who announced in February 1947 their intention to surrender the Mandate and refer the matter to the UN. A UN Special Committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) was formed, and after investigating conditions, it produced a majority plan recommending partition.

## UN General Assembly Resolution 181

On November 29, 1947 / 1367 AH, the United Nations General Assembly passed Resolution 181, the Partition Plan for Palestine. The vote was 33 in favor, 13 against, and 10 abstentions. Every Muslim-majority state that was a UN member voted against the resolution. The plan proposed dividing Mandatory Palestine into a Jewish state (comprising roughly 56% of the land) and an Arab state (approximately 43%), with Jerusalem and Bethlehem under international trusteeship.

The Arab states and the Arab Higher Committee of Palestine rejected the plan on several grounds. The fundamental argument was that the consent of the indigenous majority population was required for any legitimate political arrangement — a principle already enshrined in the UN Charter's provisions on self-determination. Arabs constituted approximately two-thirds of the population yet were allocated less than half the land, and the Jewish state as proposed would include a substantial Arab minority with no mechanism to ensure their rights. The Arab world also contested the moral logic of solving the problem of Jewish persecution in Europe by displacing the Palestinian Arab population. Al-Azhar al-Sharif in Cairo issued statements affirming Palestinian rights and calling on Muslims to support their brothers in Palestine.

## The 1948 War and the Nakba

When the British Mandate expired on May 14, 1948, David Ben-Gurion declared the establishment of the State of Israel. The following day, the armies of Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, and Lebanon entered Palestine. The resulting conflict — known to Israelis as the War of Independence and to Palestinians as the Nakba (catastrophe) — lasted until armistice agreements were signed in 1949.

The military outcome left Israel controlling approximately 78% of Mandatory Palestine, significantly more than the UN partition plan had allocated. The human catastrophe was immense: more than 700,000 Palestinian Arabs — approximately half the Arab population of Mandatory Palestine — were displaced from their homes, towns, and villages. Over 400 villages were depopulated, many destroyed. Palestinians fled or were expelled during military operations; the lines between voluntary flight out of fear and organized expulsion remain contested among historians, though documented expulsions occurred in multiple localities.

The Deir Yassin massacre of April 9, 1948, in which around 107 Palestinian villagers were killed by Jewish paramilitary forces, became a symbol of the violence accompanying the displacement. The killings spread fear and accelerated flight in the region. The expulsions from Lydda and Ramle in July 1948 resulted in 50,000–70,000 people being marched out at gunpoint in summer heat, an event corroborated by Israeli military records.

## The Status of Jerusalem and Al-Aqsa

The fate of Jerusalem was among the most consequential outcomes of the 1948 war. Jordan's Arab Legion captured the Old City, including the Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock. East Jerusalem and the West Bank came under Jordanian administration. West Jerusalem was held by Israel. The internationalization envisioned by the UN partition plan was never implemented.

Jerusalem holds singular significance in Islam. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was transported from Masjid al-Haram to Masjid al-Aqsa during the Isra (Night Journey) before ascending to the heavens in the Mi'raj — an event referenced directly in the Quran (17:1). Al-Aqsa was the first qibla (direction of prayer) for Muslims before it was changed to the Ka'bah in Mecca. It is universally regarded as the third holiest site in Islam. The continued Muslim connection to and custodianship of Jerusalem's holy sites is therefore not merely political but embedded in Islamic theology and history.

Jordan maintained custodianship over the Waqf (Islamic Endowment) properties in East Jerusalem, including the Al-Aqsa compound. The Hashemite custodianship of the holy sites in Jerusalem continues to the present day as a formal arrangement.

## The Refugee Crisis and Its Persistence

The United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees in the Near East (UNRWA) was established in 1949 specifically to manage the Palestinian refugee crisis, which was expected to be temporary. Seventy-five years later, UNRWA still operates, serving over five million registered Palestinian refugees and their descendants in Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, the West Bank, and Gaza.

Palestinian refugees and their descendants have been denied the right to return to their original homes and lands. Israel's Law of Return (1950) granted any Jewish person the right to immigrate to Israel and receive citizenship, while simultaneously Palestinian refugees who had lived in those same lands for generations were barred from return. UN General Assembly Resolution 194 (1948) affirmed the right of return for Palestinian refugees wishing to return and live in peace — a resolution cited annually but never implemented.

The refugee camps, established as temporary shelters in 1948-1949, became permanent communities. Generations of Palestinians were born, raised, and died in camps in Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, Gaza, and the West Bank, holding onto land deeds and keys to houses that no longer exist.

## Formation of the OIC and Islamic Political Response

The fall of East Jerusalem to Israeli forces in the Six-Day War of 1967, and the arson attack on Al-Aqsa Mosque on August 21, 1969, galvanized the Muslim world institutionally. The Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) was formally established in 1969, with the liberation of Jerusalem and Al-Aqsa among its founding purposes. The OIC became the collective institutional voice of Muslim-majority states on Palestinian rights.

The question of Palestine became inseparable from Islamic identity and politics across the twentieth century. The Palestinian struggle was framed not merely as a nationalist cause but as an Islamic obligation — the defense of the first qibla, the protection of Islamic holy sites, and the return of a displaced Muslim and Christian Arab population.

## The Ongoing Occupation

Following the Six-Day War of 1967, Israel occupied the West Bank and Gaza Strip. The West Bank remains under Israeli military occupation with an extensive settlement enterprise. Gaza was subjected to an Israeli-Egyptian blockade following Hamas's takeover in 2007. The two-state solution envisioned in the Oslo Accords (1993) and subsequent international frameworks has not been realized. The Palestinian population under occupation has grown to over five million in the West Bank and Gaza, in addition to the millions in diaspora and refugee camps.

The UN, Arab League, OIC, and the majority of the world's governments affirm that a just resolution requires an end to the occupation, the establishment of a Palestinian state with East Jerusalem as its capital, and a just resolution to the refugee question. The status of Jerusalem — claimed as the capital of Israel by Israeli law but unrecognized by most of the world — remains among the most contentious issues in contemporary international relations.

## Islamic Significance and Scholarly Positions

Classical and contemporary Muslim scholars are unanimous that Al-Aqsa Mosque and the broader holy precincts of Jerusalem are among the most sacred sites in Islam and that their defense and liberation is a religious obligation. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Do not undertake a journey (for religious purposes) except to three mosques: this mosque of mine (in Medina), the Sacred Mosque (in Mecca), and the Mosque of Al-Aqsa (in Jerusalem)" (Bukhari and Muslim). The continued occupation of Jerusalem, the restrictions on Muslim worship at Al-Aqsa, and the displacement of the Palestinian people are therefore not merely political grievances but matters touching the Islamic faith directly.

The suffering of Palestinian civilians — Muslims and Christians alike — over more than seventy-five years of displacement, refugee status, occupation, and periodic military conflict constitutes one of the most prolonged humanitarian crises of the modern era. Muslim communities worldwide observe Yawm al-Quds (Jerusalem Day) and engage in advocacy, prayer, and support for Palestinian relief as expressions of solidarity.
