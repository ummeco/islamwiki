# Masjid al-Dirar (The Mosque of Harm)

## The Mosque Built as Opposition

During the period of preparation for the Expedition of Tabuk, a group of hypocrites in Medina constructed a mosque near the Quba mosque area and requested the Prophet ﷺ to pray in it and give it his blessing. They claimed it was built to serve worshippers who lived far from the main mosque, particularly the sick and elderly, and to provide shelter for those unable to reach the Prophet's ﷺ mosque in the rain. The request seemed reasonable. The Prophet ﷺ, occupied with the preparations for the Tabuk expedition, told them he would pray in it when he returned.

The mosque had been built by twelve hypocrites, and the narrations preserve the names of those identified. The Quran later revealed their actual purposes: the mosque had been built "in harm, disbelief, and division among the believers, and as a station for those who had warred against Allah and His Messenger before" (Surah al-Tawbah 9:107). The reference to "those who had warred against Allah and His Messenger before" is understood by the scholars of tafsir as referring to Abu Amir al-Rahib — a Christian monk from the Khazraj who had initially been respected, opposed the Prophet ﷺ from early in the Medinan period, and eventually departed to the Byzantines seeking an alliance against the Muslim community. The mosque had been intended as a communication point for his planned return — a base of operations for anti-Muslim activity that could be disguised as religious practice.

## The Command to Demolish

When the Prophet ﷺ returned from Tabuk and was approaching Medina, the revelation came with the full account of the mosque's true purpose. He sent two companions — Malik ibn al-Dukhshum and Ma'n ibn Adi, according to some narrations — to burn and demolish the structure before he reached Medina. They carried out the order. The mosque was torn down and burned, and the location was turned into a garbage dump, the narrations report.

The companions who had built Masjid al-Dirar came to the Prophet ﷺ insisting on their innocence and their genuine devotion. The Quran had already rendered the verdict: "Never stand in it. A mosque founded from the first day on piety is more worthy for you to stand in. In it are men who love to purify themselves, and Allah loves those who purify themselves." (Surah al-Tawbah 9:108). The reference in the following verse to the mosque "founded from the first day on piety" is widely understood as referring to Masjid Quba — the first mosque established in the Islamic community, built by the Prophet ﷺ himself immediately upon his arrival at Medina after the Hijra.

## The Legal Principle

The demolition of Masjid al-Dirar established a legal principle in Islamic jurisprudence that remains foundational: a mosque built for purposes contrary to its stated function — whether for factional division, as a center for coordinating anti-Muslim activity, or as a tool of harm disguised as religious practice — is not entitled to the sanctity that protects mosques built genuinely for worship. The outward form of a masjid does not automatically confer the rights and protections attached to mosques if the building was constructed with corrupt intent and serves corrupt purposes.

Classical scholars of fiqh have used this case to address questions about mosques built by sects whose theology deviates from Ahl us-Sunnah, about mosques used to spread subversive doctrines, and about buildings constructed under the name of a masjid but serving as gathering points for harmful activity. The consensus among classical scholars is that the Masjid al-Dirar ruling applies when the founding intent is corrupt and documented — not as a general license to demolish any mosque whose attendees are disliked, but as a specific ruling for structures whose establishment was itself an act of opposition to the community.

## The Context Within the Tabuk Narrative

Masjid al-Dirar belongs to the broader Tabuk narrative of hypocrisy exposed. The same expedition that revealed the three sincere companions who stayed without excuse also revealed the hypocrites who stayed with excuses, those who made a false mosque, and the internal divisions that the crisis of full mobilization brought to the surface. Surah al-Tawbah — which covers this entire period — addresses Masjid al-Dirar in a single sustained passage (9:107-110) that situates it within the broader condemnation of those who build their commitment "on the edge of a crumbling cliff" and who will fall with their structure into the fires of hell.

The mosque's location being turned into a garbage dump after its demolition was not incidental: it was a deliberate public declaration that the building that had been presented as a place of prayer was now revealed as something else entirely — and treated accordingly.

## Abu Amir al-Rahib's End

Abu Amir al-Rahib, the figure who had planned to use Masjid al-Dirar as his base upon return, never came back. He reached the Byzantine court seeking support for a campaign against the Muslim community, received no meaningful assistance, and died in the Byzantine lands in obscurity. The network he had tried to build through the mosque died with his plan. The Quranic revelation that exposed Masjid al-Dirar's purpose had come before any of it could be activated, and the demolition closed the door that had been built for his return.

## The Contrast with Masjid Quba

The Quranic verse that commanded the destruction of Masjid al-Dirar established the contrast immediately: "A mosque founded from the first day on piety is more worthy for you to stand in." Masjid Quba, where the Prophet ﷺ had prayed upon first arriving at Medina after the Hijra and which he had helped build with his own hands, is the referent. The contrast between a mosque whose founding act was the Prophet's ﷺ own prayer and physical labor, and a mosque whose founding act was a plan of opposition disguised as devotion, could not be sharper. The Quran locates the sanctity of a mosque not in its physical structure but in the intention from which it arose and the worship that truly fills it. Masjid Quba's founding intention was piety; Masjid al-Dirar's was harm. The structure was the same — four walls, a roof, a space for prayer — but the buildings were, in the deepest sense, opposite things.
