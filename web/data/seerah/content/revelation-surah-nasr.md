# Revelation of Surah al-Nasr

Surah al-Nasr (Chapter 110 of the Quran, "The Help" or "The Victory") is a brief three-verse chapter revealed near the end of the Prophet's life that carries special significance in Islamic tradition. It was understood by the Prophet's senior companions, and particularly by Abdullah ibn Abbas and Umar ibn al-Khattab, as an announcement of the Prophet's imminent death — and therefore as a surah of farewell as much as of celebration.

## The Text

*Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim*

*"When the victory of Allah has come and the conquest [of Mecca],"* (1)
*"And you see the people entering into the religion of Allah in multitudes,"* (2)
*"Then exalt [Him] with praise of your Lord and ask forgiveness of Him. Indeed, He is ever Accepting of Repentance."* (3)

## When It Was Revealed

Scholars of Quranic revelation (asbab al-nuzul) differ on the precise occasion, but the dominant view is that Surah al-Nasr was revealed during or shortly after the Farewell Hajj of 10 AH — making it among the last complete surahs to be revealed to the Prophet. Some accounts place its revelation on the Day of Tashreeq at Mina during the Farewell Hajj.

It is classified as a Medinan surah.

## Umar and Ibn Abbas's Understanding

The most famous account surrounding this surah involves a gathering of senior companions called by Umar ibn al-Khattab after the Prophet's death. Umar asked those present — including many who were present at the conquest of Mecca and at the Farewell Hajj — what they understood Surah al-Nasr to mean.

Several companions offered the apparently natural interpretation: it announces the conquest and commands gratitude and seeking forgiveness.

When he came to Abdullah ibn Abbas (who was quite young at the time — perhaps 13-14 years old), Ibn Abbas said: "I think it contains the announcement of the death of the Messenger of Allah." Allah had told His Prophet: when you see the final victories, the multitudes entering Islam — then know that your mission is complete. Return to praising your Lord and seeking His forgiveness. Your time has come.

Umar said: "I know nothing beyond what you have said."

## The Prophet's Response

The Prophet himself, according to various narrations, indicated that the surah announced his death. Aisha reported that after the revelation of Surah al-Nasr, the Prophet frequently said in his bowing (ruku) and prostration (sujud): "Subhanak Allahumma wa bi hamdika, Allahumma ighfir li" ("Glory be to You, O Allah, and praise be to You; O Allah, forgive me") — incorporating the surah's command into his ongoing worship. He continued this pattern until his death.

This is understood as the Prophet's own acknowledgment of the surah's meaning: he recognized it as a directive to intensify worship and seek forgiveness in his final period.

## Theological and Spiritual Significance

**The surah as a complete lifecycle.** Islamic scholars see in Surah al-Nasr the full arc of the prophetic mission compressed into three verses: the opening mission (in need of divine help), the triumph (conquest and multitudes embracing Islam), and the conclusion (return to Allah, seek forgiveness, praise). It is, in this reading, the Quran's own narrative summary of the Prophet's life.

**Gratitude and humility at success.** The surah commands not celebration of personal achievement at the moment of triumph, but glorification of Allah and seeking His forgiveness. This is understood as corrective to any human tendency toward self-congratulation: even the most magnificent worldly success is an occasion for deeper submission, not pride.

**The command to seek forgiveness.** The Prophet was protected from sin (isma), yet he sought forgiveness constantly — a practice that Islamic scholars explain as the highest form of worship for one of the Prophet's station. The surah commanded this, and the Prophet obeyed. For ordinary Muslims, the lesson is that seeking forgiveness (istighfar) should intensify in moments of achievement and apparent strength, not only in failure.

## Position in the Quran

Surah al-Nasr is the 110th surah (chapter) in the Quran — near the very end — and one of the three shortest surahs (along with al-Kawthar and al-Ikhlas). Its position near the end of the Quran is sometimes noted as corresponding to its revelatory position near the end of the prophetic mission.

## Sources

The revelation context is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Tafsir), Sahih Muslim, and the major tafsir works including Ibn Kathir's *Tafsir al-Quran al-Azim* and al-Tabari's *Jami al-Bayan*.
