# The Printing Press and the Arab World

The introduction of moveable-type printing in the Arab world was significantly delayed compared to Europe, with enduring consequences for the spread of literacy and the development of Islamic scholarship in the modern period. While Gutenberg's press in Europe dates to approximately 1440-1450 CE (844-854 AH), Arabic-script printing using moveable type did not gain widespread acceptance in the Arab-Muslim world until the nineteenth century CE — a gap of nearly four centuries.

## The Resistance to Moveable Type

Several reasons are cited by historians for the slower adoption of printing in the Arab world:

**Calligraphic tradition.** Arabic script, unlike Latin letters, involves connected cursive letterforms that vary according to their position in a word (initial, medial, final, isolated). The beauty of hand-written Arabic — including the Quran — was deeply valued, and early mechanical type could not replicate the aesthetic quality of skilled calligraphy. This was not merely aesthetic snobbery: the Quran, the central text of Islamic civilization, was associated with the physical beauty of handwriting as an act of devotion.

**The copyist profession.** A vast profession of copyists (warraqs) depended on hand-copying manuscripts for their livelihood. Their guilds and interests contributed to resistance to printing technology.

**Theological concerns.** Some scholars expressed concern about the use of moveable type for the Quran, arguing that the physical act of writing was part of the text's sacred character. Printing the Quran using moveable type seemed to some to demean it.

**Ottoman ban.** The Ottoman Empire, which controlled much of the Arab world, banned Arabic-script printing presses for Muslims from the fifteenth to the early eighteenth century. Sultan Selim III lifted the ban for Arabic-script printing in 1727 CE (1139 AH) under Ibrahim Muteferrika, who established the first Arabic-script press in Istanbul, primarily for military and administrative texts.

## Early Arabic Printing

The first books printed in Arabic script were actually produced in Europe for Christian communities in the Middle East:
- The Psalms were printed in Arabic at Fano, Italy, in 1514 CE
- A complete Arabic Bible was printed in Rome in 1591 CE

The Maronite Christian printing press at Quzhayyah in Lebanon (1610 CE / 1019 AH) is often cited as the first printing press in the Arab world, though it used Syriac script and served Christian communities.

## Muhammad Ali's Egypt

The decisive moment for Arabic printing came in Egypt under Muhammad Ali Pasha (r. 1805-1848 CE / 1220-1264 AH). Muhammad Ali recognized printing as essential for the modernization program he was implementing. He established the Bulaq Press (al-Matba'a al-Amiriyya al-Kubra) in 1820 CE (1235 AH), which became the most important printing establishment in the Arab world.

The Bulaq Press printed administrative documents, military manuals, scientific texts, and eventually classical Arabic literature and Islamic scholarship. It catalyzed the Nahda (Arab cultural renaissance) of the nineteenth century by making Arabic texts widely available in printed form.

## Printing the Quran

The printing of the Quran using moveable type was the most sensitive issue. The first Quran printed using moveable type appeared in Hamburg in 1694 CE for scholarly purposes. An edition was printed in St. Petersburg in 1787 CE for distribution in the Russian Empire's Muslim communities.

The first Quran printed in the Muslim world was in Kazan in 1803 CE, followed by Tehran in 1828 CE. Egypt's Bulaq Press printed its first Quran in 1833 CE (1249 AH) — this edition became highly influential and its text was used as the basis for many subsequent printed Qurans.

## Legacy

The delayed adoption of printing in the Arab-Muslim world had complex effects. The handwritten manuscript tradition preserved a vast heritage of texts in libraries across the Muslim world. However, the lag in printing technology contributed to slower rates of literacy expansion and a slower dissemination of scholarship compared to post-Gutenberg Europe.

The nineteenth and twentieth centuries saw an explosion of print culture in the Arab world, with newspapers, journals, novels, and scholarly works transforming Muslim intellectual life. Journals like *al-Manar* (founded 1898 CE) and newspapers across the Arab world played a role similar to the pamphlet culture of the European Reformation in spreading new religious and political ideas.
