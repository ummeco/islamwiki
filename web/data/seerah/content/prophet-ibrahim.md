# Prophet Ibrahim (Peace Be Upon Him)

## Khalilullah — The Friend of Allah

Ibrahim (Abraham, peace be upon him) occupies a position in Islamic theology that no other prophet except Muhammad ﷺ rivals in scope and legacy. He is Khalilullah — the intimate Friend of Allah, a title conferred by Allah Himself. He is Abu al-Anbiya' — the Father of the Prophets — because both Ismail and Ishaq descended from him, and from their lines came the final and greatest chain of prophethood, culminating in Muhammad ﷺ. The Quran mentions Ibrahim in twenty-five surahs, devotes more sustained theological attention to him than to nearly any other prophet, and makes his millah — his way — the standard against which the way of Islam is defined: "Then follow the religion of Ibrahim, inclining toward truth, and he was not among those who associate partners with Allah." (Quran 3:95) The prayers of every Muslim incorporate Ibrahim — the Abrahamic salawat recited in every prayer is a daily acknowledgment of his station.

## Born Among Idol-Makers

Ibrahim was born in Babylon, in what is today Iraq, into a family that was not merely surrounded by polytheism but actively embedded in it. His father — called Azar in the Quran (6:74) — was an idol-maker, a craftsman who fashioned the very objects his people worshipped. From the beginning, Ibrahim's relationship with the religion of his family was one of principled estrangement.

The Quran describes Ibrahim's search for truth as a process of rational inquiry applied to the world around him. "And thus We showed Ibrahim the realm of the heavens and the earth that he would be among the certain in faith." (Quran 6:75) He observed the stars, the moon, and the sun — the greatest luminaries in the sky, the objects that more primitive religious sensibilities might most naturally exalt as divine. When a great star appeared, he said: "This is my Lord." But when it set, he said: "I do not love things that set." (Quran 6:76) He repeated this reasoning with the moon and the sun, each time watching the object of potential veneration disappear beneath the horizon, each time reaffirming the conclusion that anything which rises and sets — anything subject to time, to change, to dependence on something beyond itself — cannot be the Lord of the worlds.

His conclusion was unambiguous: "Indeed, I have turned my face toward He who created the heavens and the earth, inclining toward truth, and I am not of those who associate others with Allah." (Quran 6:79) This is tawhid arrived at by reason illuminated by divine guidance — not blind faith, but rational conviction. The Quran presents Ibrahim's process as a model for how the thinking believer approaches the question of God.

## The Confrontation with His People and the Idols

Ibrahim did not keep his conclusions to himself. He confronted his father directly, with gentleness but without compromise: "O my father, why do you worship that which does not hear and does not see and will not benefit you at all?" (Quran 19:42) His father's response was rage: "If you do not desist, I will surely stone you, so avoid me a prolonged time." (Quran 19:46) Ibrahim bore the rejection with patience and prayer: "He said: Peace will be upon you. I will ask forgiveness for you of my Lord. Indeed, He is ever gracious to me." (Quran 19:47) He continued to pray for his father's guidance until it became clear that his father had permanently chosen shirk — at which point Ibrahim disavowed the relationship, as the Quran records. (Quran 9:114)

The confrontation with his people was more dramatic. Taking advantage of a religious festival when the city was empty, Ibrahim approached the idols and destroyed them — all except the largest, around whose neck he hung the axe. When the people returned and found their gods in rubble, they summoned Ibrahim. He deployed a devastating logical trap: "Rather, this — the largest of them — did it, so ask them, if they should be able to speak." (Quran 21:63) The people fell silent, then recovered enough to say: "You know that these do not speak." Ibrahim replied: "Then do you worship instead of Allah that which does not benefit you at all or harm you? Uff to you and to what you worship instead of Allah. Then will you not use reason?" (Quran 21:66-67) The idols could not defend themselves. The idol-makers knew it. Ibrahim had used their own moment of silence against them.

## Thrown into the Fire of Nimrod

The people condemned Ibrahim to be thrown into a great fire. The king — named Nimrud (Nimrod) in the traditions of the classical scholars — had constructed a massive pyre. The Quran records what happened with the economy of divine certainty: "We said: O fire, be coolness and safety upon Ibrahim." (Quran 21:69) Ibrahim walked out unburned. The miracle was absolute and public, witnessed by the people who had intended to destroy him. Yet the Quran records that even after this, most did not believe. The heart that has chosen arrogance is not moved by miracles — a truth repeated across the entire prophetic narrative.

Ibrahim, having survived, departed from his homeland. He declared: "Indeed, I will go to where I have been commanded by my Lord." (Quran 37:99) He migrated to the Levant and then to Egypt, and the story of his journeys becomes the foundation of the sacred geography of Islam.

## Sarah, Hajar, Ismail, and the Blessed Zamzam

Ibrahim's wife Sarah was barren for the span of their youth together. As an act of generosity, she gave him her Egyptian handmaiden Hajar as a wife. Hajar bore Ibrahim a son: Ismail — the first child of the man who would become the Father of the Prophets.

Then came one of the most extraordinary acts of trust in the history of prophethood. Acting on divine command, Ibrahim brought Hajar and the infant Ismail to a barren valley in the Hijaz — to the site that would become Mecca. He left them there with a small provision of dates and water, then turned to leave. Hajar called after him: "O Ibrahim, where are you going? Are you leaving us in this valley where there is no person and nothing?" She repeated the question several times, and he did not look back. Then she said: "Has Allah commanded you to do this?" He said yes. She said: "Then He will not cause us to be lost." (Narrated from Ibn Abbas, recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari)

Hajar's water ran out. The infant cried from thirst. She ran between the hills of Safa and Marwa seven times — the same ritual that millions of pilgrims perform to this day in the Hajj — searching desperately for any sign of water. Then water burst forth at the feet of Ismail: the spring of Zamzam, which has flowed without interruption from that moment to this. The Prophet ﷺ said: "May Allah have mercy on the mother of Ismail. If she had left Zamzam as it was — or he said, if she had not scooped from it — it would have been a flowing river." (Sahih al-Bukhari, *Kitab al-Anbiya'*)

## Building the Ka'bah and the Call to Hajj

Ibrahim returned to his son and his wife in Mecca, and together father and son built the Ka'bah — the first house of worship ever established on earth for the worship of Allah alone. The Quran records their prayer as they worked: "And when Ibrahim was raising the foundations of the House and Ismail with him: Our Lord, accept this from us. Indeed You are the Hearing, the Knowing. Our Lord, and make us Muslims submitting to You and from our descendants a Muslim nation submitting to You. And show us our rites and accept our repentance. Indeed, You are the Accepting of Repentance, the Merciful." (Quran 2:127-128)

When the Ka'bah was complete, Allah commanded Ibrahim to call all of humanity to Hajj. He replied: "O my Lord, how shall my voice reach them?" Allah told him: "You call, and upon Us is the delivery." Ibrahim climbed to a high point and called out — and the scholars of tafsir hold, drawing on narrations reported by Ibn Abbas and others, that his call reached every human soul who would ever live, even those not yet born. Every person who performs Hajj until the Day of Judgment does so in response to Ibrahim's call. The rites of Hajj — the circumambulation of the Ka'bah, the sa'y between Safa and Marwa, the standing at Arafat — are all rooted in the acts of Ibrahim, Hajar, and Ismail.

## The Great Trial: The Sacrifice

The greatest test of Ibrahim's life was the command to sacrifice his son. The Quran recounts it with profound precision: Ibrahim saw in a dream that he was slaughtering his son. He informed his son, and his son's response is among the most moving passages in the Quran: "O my father, do whatever you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, among the patient." (Quran 37:102)

The Quran does not name the son who was to be sacrificed. The majority of classical scholars — including Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, and others — held that it was Ismail, not Ishaq, for several reasons: the command came before Ishaq was born, the ram that was substituted is associated with the rites of Mecca and Mina where Ismail lived, and the subsequent announcement of Ishaq's birth in the same passage (Quran 37:112) reads most naturally as a reward given after the trial with Ismail was complete. A minority opinion among classical scholars held that it was Ishaq. Both views exist in the scholarly tradition and were debated by the early generations. The stronger and more widely held view in Islamic scholarship is that it was Ismail.

Ibrahim laid his son down and prepared to fulfill the command. Allah called out: "O Ibrahim, you have fulfilled the vision. Indeed, We thus reward the doers of good. Indeed, this was the clear trial." (Quran 37:104-106) A great ram was provided as the ransom. This sacrifice — the udhiyah — is commemorated by Muslims around the world every year on Eid al-Adha, the day of sacrifice, which falls at the culmination of the Hajj.

## Father of the Prophets

From Ismail descended the Arab line of prophets, culminating in Muhammad ﷺ. From Ishaq — born to Sarah by Allah's gift in her old age, long after human biology would have made it impossible — descended the line of Israelite prophets: Ya'qub, Yusuf, Musa, Dawud, Sulayman, 'Isa, and all the prophets between them. Ibrahim's prayer for righteous descendants was answered across millennia: "My Lord, make me an establisher of prayer, and from my descendants. Our Lord, and accept my supplication." (Quran 14:40)

The title Khalilullah — Friend of Allah — is among the highest titles any created being can hold. The Quran confirms it: "And Allah took Ibrahim as Khalil." (Quran 4:125) The Prophet ﷺ explained that if he were to take a khalil from this ummah, he would have taken Abu Bakr — "but your companion is the Khalil of Allah," meaning Ibrahim. (Sahih Muslim, *Kitab Fada'il al-Sahabah*) Every Muslim, in every prayer, asks Allah to send blessings on Ibrahim and his family alongside the blessing on Muhammad ﷺ and his family — an eternal acknowledgment of the bond between the first and last of the Abrahamic prophets.
