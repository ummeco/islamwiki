# The Battle of Hunayn

The Battle of Hunayn (Shawwal 8 AH / January-February 630 CE) was fought in the valley of Hunayn, between Mecca and Ta'if, approximately two weeks after the conquest of Mecca. It was one of the most dramatic battles of the Islamic era — beginning with a near-catastrophic reversal when the Muslim army was ambushed, then turning into a decisive Muslim victory through the steadfastness of the Prophet and his core companions.

## Background

Following the conquest of Mecca, the tribes of Hawazin and Thaqif — powerful confederations southeast of Mecca — assembled a coalition army to confront the rapidly expanding Muslim state before it consolidated its control of the Hijaz. Their leader, Malik ibn Awf al-Nasri, organized a force that the sources estimate at 20,000 fighters. In an unusual and ultimately disastrous tactical decision, Malik ordered that the army march with their families, livestock, and wealth — believing that the presence of what they were fighting for would prevent anyone from retreating.

The Prophet learned of the approaching army and marched from Mecca with approximately 12,000 fighters — 10,000 from the existing Muslim army plus 2,000 new Muslims from Mecca who joined after the conquest.

## The Ambush

The valley of Hunayn was narrow, flanked by hills and rocky terrain — ideal ambush country. Malik ibn Awf had positioned archers and fighters in the hills on both sides of the valley before the Muslim army entered. When the Muslim advance troops rode into the valley in the dark of early morning, the Hawazin archers unleashed a coordinated volley that sent the advance units into a rout.

The panic spread rapidly. The newly converted Meccans, many of whom had no deep commitment to Islam, fled. A large portion of the Muslim army broke and ran. The Quran described the moment: *"Allah has already given you victory in many regions and on the day of Hunayn, when your great number pleased you but it did not avail you at all, and the earth was confining to you with its vastness; then you turned back, fleeing."* (Quran 9:25)

## The Steadfastness of the Prophet

In the chaos of the retreat, the Prophet remained firm. He did not flee. According to the accounts in Sahih Bukhari and Muslim, he called out to the fleeing soldiers:

*"O men, where are you going? I am the Prophet, this is no lie. I am the son of Abd al-Muttalib!"*

He tried to advance toward the enemy even as those around him were running. His uncle Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, who had a loud voice, called the Ansar (Medinan Muslims) and those who had pledged under the Tree at Hudaybiyyah. Upon hearing Abbas's call, they returned from the rout — "like cows returning to their calves," as the accounts describe — and rallied around the Prophet.

## The Turning Point

As the companions rallied, the battle turned. The Hawazin forces, who had initially broken the Muslim line, now found themselves facing a reorganized enemy who had overcome the initial shock. The Muslim counterattack was devastating. The Hawazin and Thaqif forces were eventually routed, their families and livestock captured, and their coalition broken.

The Prophet then besieged Ta'if, the stronghold of the Thaqif tribe, though the siege was ultimately unsuccessful and the Prophet withdrew.

## Distribution of Spoils

The Battle of Hunayn yielded enormous spoils: six thousand captives, 24,000 camels, over 40,000 sheep, and 4,000 silver coins. The distribution of these spoils caused significant controversy. The Prophet gave generous shares to the newly converted Meccan leaders (the Tulaqa — "freed ones") and to the leaders of allied tribes, in an effort to consolidate their commitment to Islam through generosity. The Ansar (Medinan Muslims) received nothing extra.

Some Ansar expressed hurt at this — they had borne the greatest risks and sacrifices and now watched the recently converted Meccans receive large gifts. When this reached the Prophet, he called the Ansar together and addressed them:

"O Ansar, I have heard what you said. Did I not come to you when you were astray and Allah guided you through me? Were you not poor and Allah enriched you through me? Were you not enemies and Allah united your hearts through me?"

They replied: "Yes, to Allah and His Messenger belongs the favor and grace."

The Prophet then said: "O Ansar, are you distressed about worldly things that I have used to win over a people who have just entered Islam, while I have entrusted you to your Islam? Are you not satisfied that other people take away sheep and camels while you take the Messenger of Allah back with you to your homes? By Allah, what you take home with you is better than what they take home."

The Ansar wept and said: "We are satisfied with the Messenger of Allah as our portion and lot." This exchange — recorded in Sahih Bukhari — is among the most moving accounts of the Prophet's relationship with the Ansar.

## Quranic Verses

Surah al-Tawbah (9:25-27) references the battle directly, making Hunayn one of the very few named battles mentioned in the Quran.

## Sources

The Battle of Hunayn is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Sahih Muslim, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*.
