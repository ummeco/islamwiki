# Ahmad Sirhindi and the Revival of Islamic Practice

Shaykh Ahmad al-Faruqi al-Sirhindi (971-1034 AH / 1564-1624 CE), known as Mujaddid Alf Thani (Renewer of the Second Millennium), was one of the most influential Islamic scholars and Sufi masters of the Mughal era. Through his voluminous correspondence, his students, and his reform of the Naqshbandi Sufi order, he played a crucial role in reviving strict Sunni orthodoxy in the Indian subcontinent at a time when it faced significant challenges from both the eclectic religious policies of the Mughal Emperor Akbar and from heterodox Sufi practices.

## Context: The Akbari Challenge

The Mughal Emperor Akbar (r. 963-1014 AH / 1556-1605 CE) ruled over one of the largest and most diverse Muslim empires in history. His religious policies evolved toward an increasingly syncretic approach: he patronized scholars from multiple religious traditions, promoted a new eclectic "divine religion" (Din-i-Ilahi) that drew on Islam, Hinduism, Zoroastrianism, and other traditions, and in some respects relaxed Islamic legal standards at court.

These policies alarmed orthodox scholars, who feared the erosion of Islamic identity in the subcontinent's pluralistic environment. Ahmad Sirhindi emerged as the leading voice of Islamic traditionalism against this trend.

## Life and the Naqshbandi Order

Ahmad Sirhindi was born in Sirhind (in Punjab, present-day India) in 971 AH (1564 CE) to a family claiming descent from Umar ibn al-Khattab (hence the epithet "al-Faruqi"). He received a thorough education in the Islamic sciences — tafsir, hadith, fiqh, and kalam — before coming under the influence of Khwaja Baqi Billah, the head of the Naqshbandi Sufi order in India.

He became Baqi Billah's most distinguished disciple and, after Baqi Billah's death in 1012 AH (1603 CE), the leading figure of the Naqshbandi order in the subcontinent. He reformed and revitalized the order, giving it a more rigorous doctrinal orientation and a greater emphasis on adherence to the Sharia alongside spiritual development.

## Maktubat

Sirhindi's primary legacy is his vast correspondence, collected in *Maktubat-i Imam Rabbani* (Letters of the Lord of the Rabbis — a title indicating his spiritual authority). The three volumes of letters, addressed to Mughal nobles, officials, scholars, and students, constitute a comprehensive statement of his theological, legal, and spiritual positions.

Key themes in the Maktubat:

**Defense of Sharia.** Sirhindi consistently argued that spiritual development cannot replace or supersede adherence to Islamic law. The Sufi path is the inner dimension of the Sharia, not an alternative to it.

**Critique of wahdat al-wujud.** Ibn Arabi's philosophical doctrine of the "unity of being" (wahdat al-wujud), which some Sufis interpreted as implying that all things are ultimately one with God, was widespread in Mughal Sufi circles. Sirhindi criticized this interpretation as liable to lead to pantheism and proposed the alternative concept of wahdat al-shuhud (unity of witnessing) — the mystic experience of divine unity is a subjective state of the mystic, not an ontological statement about reality.

**Encouragement of Sharia at court.** Many of his letters were addressed to Mughal nobles and officials, urging them to enforce Islamic legal standards, support scholars, and resist the influence of Akbar's more syncretic policies.

## Political Influence

Sirhindi's efforts bore fruit under Akbar's successor, Emperor Jahangir (r. 1014-1037 AH / 1605-1627 CE). After initially having Sirhindi imprisoned (1028 AH / 1619 CE) due to political concerns, Jahangir released him. His student and follower Shah Jahan (r. 1037-1068 AH / 1628-1658 CE) was more favorably disposed to orthodox Islam, and the shift in Mughal religious policy from Akbar's syncretism toward greater Islamic orthodoxy — culminating in Aurangzeb's reign — was significantly influenced by the Sirhindi tradition.

## Legacy

Ahmad Sirhindi's designation as "Mujaddid of the Second Millennium" (the renewer sent at the beginning of the Islamic millennium in 1000 AH) reflects the tremendous esteem in which he was held. His reform of the Naqshbandi order, under the name Naqshbandi-Mujaddidi, became one of the most influential Sufi orders across South Asia, Central Asia, and the Middle East.

His theological contributions, particularly his critique of wahdat al-wujud and his insistence on the unity of Sharia and Tariqa (the path), shaped Sunni Sufi discourse for centuries.

He died in Sirhind in 1034 AH (1624 CE) and was buried there. His tomb became a major pilgrimage site.
