# The Prophet's Marriage to Umm Salamah

Umm Salamah (Hind bint Abi Umayyah al-Makhzumiyya, also known as Ramla) was among the earliest converts to Islam and one of the Prophet's wives whose marriage came after the death of her first husband. Her story — from the first hijra to Abyssinia, through widowhood, to becoming one of the Prophet's wives — represents one of the most complete portraits of a believing woman in the early Islamic community.

## Early Life and First Marriage

Umm Salamah was from Banu Makhzum — the same clan as Khalid ibn al-Walid and Abu Jahl, and a prestigious Qurayshi family. Her first husband was Abu Salamah ibn Abd al-Asad, also a Makhzumi and one of the earliest converts to Islam.

She and Abu Salamah were among the first Muslims to make the hijra to Abyssinia — fleeing Meccan persecution. They returned to Mecca and then, when the final hijra to Medina was organized, she attempted to travel with her husband. The Banu Makhzum clan physically prevented her from joining Abu Salamah, holding her back while allowing him to go. Her own family (Banu Umayya) then took her infant son Salamah from her.

She remained for approximately a year — separated from both husband and child — until the clans relented and restored both her son and her freedom to travel. She then made the hijra to Medina alone with her son, meeting her husband there.

## Abu Salamah's Death

Abu Salamah participated in the major early battles, including Badr and Uhud. He was wounded at Uhud — an arrow wound that did not fully heal. He died in 4 AH from the recurring effects of his wound, having also participated in the expedition to Qatan in his weakened state.

Before his death, Abu Salamah made a du'a' (supplication) that if he died before Umm Salamah, Allah would give her a husband better than him. Umm Salamah narrated later that when the Prophet later proposed to her, she realized the answer to Abu Salamah's prayer: who could be better than the Prophet of Allah?

## The Proposal and Marriage

After Abu Salamah's death, Umm Salamah was widowed with four young children. Abu Bakr proposed to her and she declined. Umar proposed and she declined. When the Prophet proposed, she expressed hesitation — not rejection — citing three concerns: she was of advanced age (older than some of his other wives), she was a woman of jealous temperament, and she had many dependent children.

The Prophet addressed each concern. Her age was not an obstacle. Her jealous temperament — he would pray to Allah to remove it. Her children — he would care for them.

The marriage took place in Shawwal 4 AH, approximately four months after Abu Salamah's death. She was — among his wives — his only wife who had previously been married and who had children from a previous marriage. Her children from Abu Salamah were raised in the Prophet's household.

## Her Scholarly Role

Umm Salamah was a woman of significant learning and independent judgment. She is most famous in the seerah for her advice at Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) — when the companions refused to obey the Prophet's command to sacrifice their animals and end the pilgrim state after the treaty was signed. She advised the Prophet not to speak to them but to perform the sacrifice himself first; when the companions saw him act, they immediately followed. Her psychological insight resolved a genuine crisis in community leadership.

She transmitted numerous hadith from the Prophet — approximately 378 narrations are attributed to her in the hadith collections. She was also known to raise questions about Quranic interpretation, particularly regarding whether verses addressed to believing men applied equally to believing women. Her question reportedly contributed to the revelation of Quran 33:35, which explicitly mentions both men and women in its address.

## Death and Legacy

Umm Salamah lived until approximately 62 AH (680 CE) — she was the last of the Prophet's major wives to die, and she lived to see the tragedy of Karbala (61 AH) in which her step-grandson Husayn ibn Ali was killed. She died in grief for him.

She is buried in the Baqee' cemetery in Medina.

## Sources

Umm Salamah's biography is documented in Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, and her narrations are spread across all major hadith collections. The Hudaybiyyah incident is in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Shurut).
