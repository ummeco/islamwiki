# Sa'id ibn Zayd: Among the Earliest to Embrace the Truth

## A Family Already Searching for God

The story of Sa'id ibn Zayd (may Allah be pleased with him) cannot be understood without first understanding his father, Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl — one of the most remarkable figures in the history of pre-Islamic Arabia. Zayd was a man who, long before the Prophet ﷺ received his first revelation, had rejected the idolatry of his tribe and set out on a solitary search for the true religion of Ibrahim (Abraham), peace be upon him.

Ibn Hisham records that Zayd ibn Amr stood leaning against the Ka'bah and declared openly: "O Quraysh, by Allah, none of you is upon the religion of Ibrahim except me." He refused to eat meat slaughtered for the idols. He refused to bow to statues. He traveled widely — to Syria, to the regions of the Byzantines and the Jewish communities — seeking scholars and men of religious learning who could guide him to the pure monotheism he felt in his heart. He was told by Jewish scholars: "You are seeking a religion that does not yet exist in our world, but a prophet is about to emerge from your land — follow him." Byzantine Christian scholars told him similar things.

Zayd ibn Amr did not live to see the Prophet's mission. He died before the first revelation, somewhere on one of his journeys. But the Prophet ﷺ honored his memory explicitly. In a hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari, the Prophet ﷺ said: "He will be resurrected as an ummah (community) by himself." This remarkable statement — that a single man would be raised on the Day of Judgment as if he were an entire nation, on account of his sincere search for truth — establishes the spiritual stature of Sa'id's father in Islamic understanding.

There is another famous hadith, recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, in which the young Muhammad ﷺ (before his prophethood) encountered Zayd ibn Amr and offered him food from a sacrificial feast. Zayd refused, saying: "I do not eat what has been slaughtered for anything other than Allah." The Prophet ﷺ later said: "I have never eaten from the food slaughtered for idols after that day." This encounter between the Prophet ﷺ and Sa'id's father is one of the quietly significant moments in the pre-prophetic record.

## Sa'id's Own Conversion

Sa'id ibn Zayd (may Allah be pleased with him) grew up in this household of spiritual searching. He was from the Banu Adi clan of the Quraysh — the same clan as Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), which made him and Umar kinsmen. When Abu Bakr began calling people to Islam in the earliest days of the prophetic mission, Sa'id was among those who responded. He had married Fatimah bint al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with her), Umar's sister, and the two of them — husband and wife — accepted Islam together in the very early period, well before Umar's own famous conversion.

This chronology is significant. Sa'id and his wife Fatimah were Muslims before Umar was. They were worshipping and learning from the Prophet ﷺ in secret while Umar still counted himself among the opponents of Islam. The household of Fatimah and Sa'id was, in fact, where Umar encountered the Quran in the famous account of his conversion — he came to his sister's house in anger, found them reciting, and the words of Surah Ta-Ha penetrated his heart and turned him around.

In this way, Sa'id ibn Zayd was instrumental, through his marriage and household, in one of the most consequential conversions in Islamic history: the conversion of Umar ibn al-Khattab, whose entry into Islam the Prophet ﷺ had prayed for and which transformed the strength and visibility of the early Muslim community.

## The Story of Umar's Conversion and Sa'id's Household

The account is recorded by Ibn Hisham in al-Sirah al-Nabawiyyah and corroborated in multiple hadith collections. Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) had set out to kill the Prophet ﷺ. On the way, he was informed that his own sister Fatimah and her husband Sa'id ibn Zayd had accepted Islam. He redirected his steps to their house.

When he arrived, he could hear recitation from within. He entered in anger, demanding to know what they had been reciting and striking Sa'id. When his sister Fatimah came between them and he struck her such that she bled, the sight of her blood shocked him. She held up the page of Surah Ta-Ha and told him to purify himself and read it. What Umar read broke him open. He went from there directly to the Prophet ﷺ and accepted Islam.

Sa'id ibn Zayd's home was thus the site of this transformation. He was personally present at the moment that brought Umar into the fold of Islam. The Quran had been in his household, his wife had been willing to bleed for it, and the encounter that changed the course of early Muslim history had taken place under his roof.

## Among the Ten Promised Paradise

Sa'id ibn Zayd (may Allah be pleased with him) is one of the Ashra Mubashshara — the ten companions explicitly promised Paradise. The hadith, recorded by al-Tirmidhi and Abu Dawud with chains that the scholars have assessed as authentic, names him alongside Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali, al-Zubayr, Talhah, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf, and Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah.

This promise places Sa'id in the most elite company among the companions of the Prophet ﷺ. The ten promised Paradise are, in the Ahl us-Sunnah tradition, the highest-ranked companions apart from the Prophet ﷺ himself, and their collective guarantee of Paradise is one of the most frequently cited traditions in discussions of companion biography.

## Military Service and the Early Campaigns

Sa'id ibn Zayd participated in the major military campaigns of the early Islamic period. He was at Badr — though some accounts note complications about his presence at specific engagements due to earlier missions. He fought at Uhud. He was present at the major engagements that shaped the early Muslim state.

He is also associated with the early conquests of Syria (al-Sham). Al-Tabari records his participation in the campaigns under Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah and Khalid ibn al-Walid, and there are accounts of his presence at significant battles during the caliphates of Abu Bakr and Umar.

The early Islamic conquests of Syria were campaigns that transformed the political and religious map of the ancient world. The Byzantine Empire lost its southern provinces — territories that had been Christian Roman for centuries — in a series of engagements during 13-15 AH. Sa'id ibn Zayd was among the men who fought in these campaigns, carrying with him the faith of his father who had searched for the true religion in those same Syrian lands and died before finding it.

## His Father's Spiritual Legacy and Islamic Memory

The Prophet ﷺ spoke of Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl in terms that established him as a unique figure in Islamic tradition. In addition to the hadith about him being resurrected as an ummah by himself, the Prophet ﷺ said — according to accounts in Ibn Hisham and al-Tabari — that he would enter Paradise despite dying before Islam. This is understood by scholars as a special case: a man who attained the state of pure monotheism (hanifiyyah) and rejected idolatry before the message could reach him. He was, in the terminology of later theology, among the ahl al-fatra — those who lived in a period between prophets — and who will be judged by their sincere response to whatever truth was available to them.

For Sa'id ibn Zayd, carrying this heritage was both an honor and a responsibility. He had grown up in a household that had already broken with the idolatry of the Quraysh before the Quran arrived to confirm and complete that break. When Islam came, it felt to him not like a foreign arrival but like the fulfillment of a journey his father had begun. The hanifiyyah that Zayd sought through years of travel, his son found in Mecca through the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

## The Story of Urwah's Land and Sa'id's Supplication

One of the most remarkable accounts involving Sa'id ibn Zayd in his later life concerns a land dispute. A woman named Arwa bint Aws brought a complaint against Sa'id, claiming that he had wrongfully taken a portion of her land. Sa'id denied it vigorously, and the matter was brought to Marwan ibn al-Hakam, who was then the governor of Medina.

When the dispute was not resolved in a manner satisfying to him, Sa'id made a supplication. He raised his hands and said: "O Allah, if she has lied, blind her and cause her to die in her land." The narrations in al-Bukhari and other collections record that Arwa was subsequently blinded and that she died after falling into a ditch on the very land in question.

This account is not presented in the Islamic sources as a mere curiosity. It is presented as evidence of the acceptance of Sa'id's supplications — a quality noted of several companions who were known for the answering of their prayers. More broadly, it serves as a warning against false accusations against the companions of the Prophet ﷺ. The sanctity of the companions — their honor and their good name — is a matter the hadith literature addresses repeatedly, and Sa'id's story is among the most striking illustrations of divine protection of that honor.

Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani cites this account in al-Isabah and notes that the narration about Urwah's land is well-established in the sources. He contextualizes it as evidence of Sa'id's spiritual standing and his accepted supplication — qualities that the Prophet ﷺ was known to pray for in specific companions.

## His Later Life and Death

Sa'id ibn Zayd (may Allah be pleased with him) lived through the entire early period of Islamic history — the caliphates of Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and into the caliphate of Ali. He was a witness to the tremendous expansion of the Islamic world from a persecuted minority in Mecca to the rulers of Arabia, Persia, Syria, Egypt, and beyond.

He died in approximately 50-51 AH, during the caliphate of Muawiya ibn Abi Sufyan. He was among the last of the ten promised Paradise to die, along with Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas. He was buried in Medina. Ibn Kathir records in al-Bidayah wal-Nihayah that he spent his final years in relative withdrawal from political life, preferring prayer and remembrance to involvement in the conflicts that divided the community.

His life traced a perfect arc: from the household of a father who searched the world for God, to the earliest circle of those who found God's final messenger, to the battlefields where Islam was established, to the private devotion of old age. Son of a man who was promised to be resurrected as an ummah, he was himself promised Paradise — and his household had been the site where another ummah's most powerful leader had found his way to the truth.
