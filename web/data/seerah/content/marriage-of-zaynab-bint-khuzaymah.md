# The Prophet's Marriage to Zaynab bint Khuzaymah

Zaynab bint Khuzaymah al-Hilaliyya — known as Umm al-Masakin ("Mother of the Poor") for her exceptional generosity to the destitute — was married to the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) in 3 AH after the death of her previous husband Ubaydah ibn al-Harith at the Battle of Badr. Her marriage to the Prophet was among his shortest, as she died approximately two to three months after the marriage.

## Who Was Zaynab bint Khuzaymah

Zaynab was from the Banu Hilal clan of the Hawazin tribal confederation. She was known across Mecca and Medina for her charity toward the poor and needy — the title "Mother of the Poor" reflects a consistent practice of feeding, clothing, and sheltering those in need from her own resources. This title, recorded by multiple narrators, suggests that her generosity was distinctive enough to have become her defining characteristic.

She had been previously married, possibly to more than one husband before the Prophet. The most clearly documented of her previous marriages was to Ubaydah ibn al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib — the Prophet's cousin who died at the Battle of Badr (2 AH) from wounds received there. Some accounts mention an earlier marriage to Abdullah ibn Jahsh.

## The Marriage

After the death of Ubaydah, Zaynab became a widow. The Prophet proposed to her and the marriage was contracted in Ramadan 3 AH. The mahr (dowry) set was 400 dirhams.

The marriage united the Prophet with a woman who embodied one of the core values he had spent his entire prophethood trying to establish: generosity to the poor and the marginalized. Her reputation as "Mother of the Poor" aligned precisely with the Prophet's own priorities and with the Quranic emphasis on caring for the needy.

## Her Brief Marriage and Death

Zaynab bint Khuzaymah died in Rabi al-Akhir 4 AH — approximately two to eight months after her marriage to the Prophet, depending on the source. The cause of her death is not clearly documented. She was buried in the Baqee' cemetery in Medina.

She was one of only two of the Prophet's wives to die during his own lifetime — the other being Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, who died in Mecca before the hijra.

## The Prophet's Wives Who Died Before Him

The deaths of Khadijah and Zaynab during the Prophet's lifetime made them unique among his wives. The other wives (Aisha, Hafsa, Umm Salamah, Umm Habibah, Safiyyah, Maymunah, Zaynab bint Jahsh, Juwayriyah, and Sawdah) survived the Prophet and are counted among the "Mothers of the Believers" (Ummahat al-Mu'minin) with the full status that attaches to that title.

## The Title "Umm al-Masakin"

The title "Mother of the Poor" is cited in Islamic moral literature as evidence that individual acts of generosity, sustained over time, create a recognizable character that others name and remember. Zaynab bint Khuzaymah did not merely give charity; she did so with such consistent dedication that she was identified with it.

Islamic scholars cite her as an example of the prophetic encouragement of sadaqah (voluntary charity) beyond the obligatory zakah — the Prophet had said "Charity does not diminish wealth" and consistently encouraged his community toward generosity. Zaynab exemplified this teaching in practice.

## Historical Significance

Zaynab bint Khuzaymah holds a unique place in the seerah as one of the very few of the Prophet's wives about whom so little is recorded (owing to the brevity of her marriage) yet who is remembered with the distinctive honor of her charitable title. She was buried in Baqee' and the Prophet personally participated in her funeral — one of the brief but poignant episodes of his Medinan life.

## Sources

The account of Zaynab bint Khuzaymah's marriage and death is documented in Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra* (which has the most detail), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani's *al-Isaba fi Tamyiz al-Sahaba*.
