# Al-Isra wal-Mi'raj: The Night Journey and Ascension in Detail

Among all the events of the Meccan period, al-Isra wal-Mi'raj — the Night Journey from Mecca to Jerusalem and the subsequent Ascension through the heavens — stands apart in its theological weight and in its place at the center of Islamic devotional life. The five daily prayers that structure every Muslim's day were established through this event; the Quran devoted a full surah and substantial passages elsewhere to commemorating it; and the early Muslim community's response to the Prophet's ﷺ account of it became one of the earliest tests distinguishing sincere faith from doubt.

## The Date: Scholarly Debate

The exact date of al-Isra wal-Mi'raj has been among the more contested questions in seerah chronology. Scholars have proposed dates ranging from the early Meccan period to just before the Hijra. The majority opinion among the classical scholars places it approximately one to one-and-a-half years before the Hijra — in approximately the eleventh or twelfth year of the prophethood. Some place it in the year of the deaths of Khadijah and Abu Talib, which would be roughly the tenth year.

What most scholars agree on is the broad context: al-Isra wal-Mi'raj occurred during the Meccan period, after the deaths of Khadijah and Abu Talib, and before the Hijra. It came during a period of intense difficulty for the Prophet ﷺ — after the Year of Grief and the failed Ta'if mission — and its timing suggests a divine gift of consolation and affirmation at the moment of greatest earthly hardship.

The 27th of Rajab has become the most widely observed date in Islamic tradition for commemorating this event, though the classical hadith scholars noted that the specific date within Rajab (or even the year) is not definitively established from the primary sources.

## Al-Isra: The Night Journey to Jerusalem

The Quran opens Surah al-Isra (17:1) with the declaration: "Exalted is He who took His Servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed, to show him of Our signs. Indeed, He is the Hearing, the Seeing."

This single verse establishes the essential elements: a divine act ("Exalted is He who took"), a specific human subject ("His Servant" — a term of honor for the Prophet ﷺ), a time (by night — "bi abdihi laylan"), a starting point (al-Masjid al-Haram, the Sacred Mosque in Mecca), a destination (al-Masjid al-Aqsa, the Farthest Mosque in Jerusalem), the sacred status of Jerusalem's surroundings (whose area We have blessed), and the purpose (to show him Our signs).

The hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim fill in the details that the Quranic verse does not provide. The Prophet ﷺ was at the Ka'bah — some narrations say he was sleeping in the area known as the Hijr Ismail, others say at the home of Umm Hani bint Abi Talib. Jibril (peace be upon him) came to him and opened his chest — as had been done at the beginning of his mission — washing his heart with Zamzam water and filling it with wisdom and faith in some narrations.

## Al-Buraq: The Mount of the Journey

The transport for the journey was a creature called al-Buraq (from "barq," lightning). The hadith in Sahih Muslim, narrated by Anas ibn Malik (may Allah be pleased with him), describes it: "A white animal, smaller than a mule and larger than a donkey. It placed its hoof at the farthest point it could see." This last detail — that each stride of al-Buraq reached to the horizon of its sight — encodes the speed of the journey in physical metaphor.

The Prophet ﷺ was brought to al-Buraq and mounted it. Some narrations describe al-Buraq initially resisting until Jibril told it: "Are you ashamed before Muhammad? By Allah, no one more honored than him has ever mounted you." The creature calmed and the journey began.

The journey from Mecca to Jerusalem — approximately 1,500 kilometers — was covered in a single night, before the dawn prayers. This physical impossibility by ordinary means is itself part of the sign: al-Isra was a miraculous event, not a natural journey.

## Jerusalem: Prayer with the Prophets

At al-Masjid al-Aqsa in Jerusalem, the Prophet ﷺ dismounted and entered the mosque. The hadith collections record that he found assembled there all the prophets who had come before him — Ibrahim (Abraham), Musa (Moses), Isa (Jesus), and others (peace be upon them all). The Prophet ﷺ prayed with them as their imam (prayer leader). This act of leading the prayer of all the previous prophets was itself a statement about his position: the final prophet, the seal of the prophethood, now stood before all those who had preceded him and led them in the worship of Allah.

After the prayer, two cups were brought to the Prophet ﷺ — one containing milk and one containing wine. Jibril presented them and the Prophet ﷺ chose the cup of milk. Jibril said: "You have chosen the fitra (natural human disposition). Had you chosen the wine, your ummah would have gone astray." The choice was both practical — wine was prohibited, though the formal prohibition had not yet been revealed as a direct command — and symbolic: the fitra, the natural human disposition toward pure monotheism and moral clarity, was the path the Prophet ﷺ represented.

## Al-Mi'raj: The Ascension Through the Heavens

From Jerusalem, the ascent began. The Quran references this in Surah al-Najm (53:1-18): "By the star when it descends — your companion has not strayed, nor has he erred, nor does he speak from desire. It is not but a revelation revealed, taught to him by one intense in strength — one of soundness. And he rose to his true form while he was in the higher horizon. Then he approached and descended — and was at a distance of two bow lengths or nearer. And he revealed to His servant what he revealed. The heart did not lie about what it saw. So will you dispute with him over what he saw? And he certainly saw him in another descent — at the Lote Tree of the Utmost Boundary. Near it is the Garden of Refuge. When there covered the Lote Tree that which covered it, the sight did not turn aside, nor did it transgress its limit. He certainly saw of the greatest signs of his Lord."

The detailed account of the seven heavens comes from the long hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, which preserve the fullest narratives of the ascension. Jibril accompanied the Prophet ﷺ and at each heaven knocked and was asked who was there, and when he identified himself and the Prophet ﷺ, the gates were opened.

## The Seven Heavens and Their Inhabitants

In the **first heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ encountered Adam (peace be upon him), the father of humanity. Adam greeted him and acknowledged him, and the narration describes him looking to his right (where souls of the righteous were gathered) and smiling, then looking to his left (where souls of the wrongdoers were gathered) and becoming sad — a detail that the scholars have connected to Adam's paternal relationship to all of humanity.

In the **second heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ met Yahya (John) ibn Zakariya and Isa (Jesus) ibn Maryam (peace be upon them), described as cousins. They are mentioned together because of their familial connection and their temporal proximity in history.

In the **third heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ met Yusuf (Joseph) ibn Ya'qub (peace be upon him). The narration in Sahih al-Bukhari records the Prophet ﷺ saying about Yusuf: "He was given half of all beauty." This statement from the Prophet ﷺ echoes the famous account in Surah Yusuf where the women of Egypt cut their hands upon seeing his face.

In the **fourth heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ met Idris (peace be upon him). The Quran (19:57) says that Allah "raised him to a high station" — and his presence in the fourth heaven is understood by the scholars as a reference to this elevated position.

In the **fifth heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ met Harun (Aaron) ibn Imran (peace be upon him), the brother of Musa, whom the narrations describe as being beloved by his people — confirmed by the large crowds around him in the fifth heaven.

In the **sixth heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ met Musa (Moses) ibn Imran (peace be upon him). This encounter would become crucial in one of the night's most significant exchanges. When the Prophet ﷺ passed him on his way up, Musa wept. When asked why, he said: "I weep because a young man was sent after me and more of his followers will enter Paradise than mine." This statement of Musa, preserved in the hadith, is simultaneously a recognition of the Prophet's ﷺ greatness and a reflection of Musa's own love for his community.

In the **seventh heaven**, the Prophet ﷺ met Ibrahim (Abraham) peace be upon him, who was leaning against al-Bayt al-Ma'mur — the Frequented House in the seventh heaven, the celestial counterpart of the Ka'bah on earth. Seventy thousand angels enter it every day for worship, and none return again — the number of angels is so vast that each one gets a single entry in eternity.

## Sidrat al-Muntaha: The Lote Tree of the Utmost Boundary

Beyond the seventh heaven, the Prophet ﷺ reached Sidrat al-Muntaha — the Lote Tree of the Utmost Boundary. This is the point mentioned in Surah al-Najm (53:14), and it represents the furthest limit that any created being — including Jibril — had previously reached. Jibril told the Prophet ﷺ here that he could go no further: "This is my limit. If I were to go further, I would burn."

The description of the Lote Tree in the hadith is one of the most visually remarkable passages in the prophetic literature: it was covered with golden moths (or golden butterflies), its leaves were like the ears of elephants, and its colors were indescribably varied. What covered it was something that only Allah knows in full.

At or near Sidrat al-Muntaha, the Prophet ﷺ saw divine signs — described in Surah al-Najm as "the greatest signs of his Lord." The scholars debate the exact nature of what he saw; the hadith in Muslim preserve that he saw divine light (nur), and the sound discourse maintains a careful agnosticism about the precise nature of this vision, consistent with the Islamic principle that Allah is beyond full human comprehension.

## The Command of Fifty Prayers

At this point in the ascension, the command was given: the Muslims were to pray fifty times each day. The number fifty is remarkable — it suggests the depth of the devotional life that this community was being called to.

On his return through the heavens, the Prophet ﷺ passed by Musa again. Musa asked him what obligation had been placed on his community. When told fifty prayers, Musa told him: "Your community will not be able to bear fifty prayers. Go back and ask your Lord to reduce it." The Prophet ﷺ went back and the obligation was reduced by ten. He passed by Musa again; Musa told him to go back again. This exchange continued in a remarkable series — ten prayers removed each time, a return to ask, another reduction — until the obligation reached five.

Musa told him: "Even five prayers — your community will not be able to bear it. Go back and ask for further reduction." At this point, the narration records that the Prophet ﷺ said: "I would be ashamed to ask my Lord again. I accept and submit." A voice was heard: "I have established My obligation and made it easier for My servants. Whoever prays five prayers will receive the reward of fifty."

The exchange with Musa about prayer is one of the most discussed passages in the hadith literature. Musa's advice was grounded in his own experience with the Children of Israel — a people who had repeatedly struggled to maintain the obligations given to them. His pastoral concern for the new Muslim community reflects a genuine fraternity among prophets. The final five-prayer obligation with the reward of fifty carries the mathematical and spiritual elegance characteristic of the divine mercy: the reward of the original obligation, the sustainable structure of the reduced one.

## The Report to the Quraysh: Abu Bakr Becomes al-Siddiq

The morning after the night journey, the Prophet ﷺ reported what had happened to the Quraysh in the masjid. Their reaction was immediate and largely one of rejection and mockery. Many of those who had been vacillating or hesitant used this report as grounds for abandoning any consideration of Islam — if this is what he claims, he is clearly deluded or lying.

The Quraysh went to Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) expecting that this claim would shake even the Prophet's closest companion. Abu Bakr's response has been preserved as one of the defining moments of his life: "If he has said it, then it is true." He asked them: "Do you find it remarkable that he receives revelation in the night? I accept that." He immediately, without hesitation, confirmed his belief in the entirety of the report.

It was for this response that the Prophet ﷺ gave Abu Bakr the title "al-Siddiq" — the one who confirms, the one of absolute truthfulness and affirmation. The title recognized that Abu Bakr's faith was not conditional on the ease of what he was asked to believe. He accepted what the Prophet ﷺ said because the Prophet ﷺ said it, full stop.

## Was It Physical or Spiritual?

The scholars across all four Sunni madhabs have addressed the question of whether al-Isra was a physical journey of the body or a spiritual vision of the soul. The majority position — held by Ibn Abbas, Muawiya (may Allah be pleased with them), and the vast majority of scholars after them — is that it was a physical journey of both body and soul together.

The evidence cited for the physical journey includes: the Quran says "His Servant" — and the Arabic word "abd" (servant) refers to the combination of body and soul, not the soul alone; the word "asra" (took by night) in Quran 17:1 is used in Arabic for physical journeys; the Quraysh's reaction of shocked disbelief would make no sense if it had been announced merely as a spiritual vision (spiritual visions were commonly reported and accepted); and the specific detail of al-Buraq as a physical mount would be unnecessary for a purely spiritual journey.

The minority position — that it was a spiritual vision (ru'ya) — finds its basis in the verse of Surah al-Isra (17:60) that mentions "the vision (ru'ya) We showed you" in context. But the majority tafsir of that verse holds it refers to a different experience, and that the overwhelming weight of evidence points to a physical journey.

## The Spiritual Legacy

Al-Isra wal-Mi'raj established the five daily prayers as the central pillar of the Muslim's devotional life — "the pillar of the religion," as the hadith describes them. Every Muslim who stands in salah is connected, through this prayer, to the night the Prophet ﷺ ascended through the heavens and received directly from the divine command the obligation that would structure Muslim civilization across all subsequent generations.

The event also established the spiritual geography of the Islamic world: al-Masjid al-Haram in Mecca, al-Masjid al-Aqsa in Jerusalem, and al-Masjid al-Nabawi in Medina are the three mosques that the Prophet ﷺ identified as the only destinations for special intention-based travel. Jerusalem's place in that list derives directly from the Isra.
