# The Prophet's Letter to the Negus of Abyssinia

The letter the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) sent to the Negus (Najashi) of Abyssinia in 7 AH holds a special character among the royal letters of that year, because the Prophet and the early Muslim community had a long and warm relationship with the Abyssinian kingdom and its rulers.

## Background: The Special Relationship with Abyssinia

The Negus of Abyssinia — historically identified as Ashama ibn Abjar — had sheltered the first Muslim emigrants who fled Mecca's persecution in the fifth year of the prophethood (approx. 615 CE). When Quraysh sent envoys demanding the return of the Muslims, the Negus had refused, stating that he could see from their description of Jesus that the Muslim position was close to his own. He had protected the Muslims for years, and the Prophet felt a lasting gratitude and respect toward him.

When news of Ashama's death reached the Prophet (reportedly before the Prophet's letter could receive a definitive response), the Prophet performed the funeral prayer in absentia (salat al-ghayb) for him — an act unprecedented for any other non-Muslim of his time, and one that indicated the Prophet's genuine belief that the Negus had died in a state of spiritual orientation toward monotheism.

## The Letter

The letter to the Negus of 7 AH was carried by Amr ibn Umayyah al-Damri. Its text, as preserved in the seerah literature:

*"In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate. From Muhammad, the Messenger of Allah, to Ashama al-Najashi, the ruler of Abyssinia. Peace be upon you. I praise Allah for you — the King who has no partner, the Peaceful, the Believer, the Protector. I bear witness that Jesus son of Mary was the spirit of Allah and His word, which He cast to Mary the chaste, the good, the virgin, so she conceived Jesus. Allah created him from His spirit and His breath, as He created Adam by His hand and His breath. I invite you to Allah alone with no partner, and to His obedience, and to follow me and believe in what has come to me — for I am the Messenger of Allah. I have sent to you my cousin Ja'far ibn Abi Talib and a group of Muslims. When they reach you, extend your hospitality to them..."*

## The Content and Theological Sensitivity

The letter's treatment of Jesus is notable for its theological care. The Prophet acknowledged Jesus as "the spirit of Allah and His word" — a Quranic description of Jesus (Quran 4:171) — while clearly situating him as a created being rather than as divine. This language was carefully calibrated for an Abyssinian Christian audience: it affirmed Jesus's special status while maintaining Islamic monotheism.

The formula "the spirit of Allah" (ruhun minhu) does not mean that Jesus is Allah or a part of Allah, but rather that his creation involved a special divine breath — as was Adam's, as noted in the same verse. Islamic tafsir is unanimous on this point, and the letter reflects this precision.

## The Negus's Response

Classical sources report that when the letter was read to the Negus, he placed it on his eye or pressed it to his chest. He reportedly affirmed: "By Allah, he did not go further than this — the span of a nail" — meaning that Jesus was exactly as Muhammad described him, no more and no less.

There are narrations indicating that the Negus sent his son with a delegation to the Prophet, but that the delegation was lost at sea. He reportedly embraced Islam privately (some sources say explicitly, others suggest it was a private spiritual conviction), but did not publicly convert given his political circumstances.

## The Funeral Prayer in Absentia

When the Negus died, the Prophet received news of his death on the same day. He gathered the companions and performed the funeral prayer in absentia (salat al-ghayb) — praying over him as one prays over an absent deceased Muslim. This prayer is recorded in Sahih Bukhari and Muslim. Scholars have discussed whether this indicates that the Negus was a Muslim or that the Prophet was making a special exception; the majority view is that the Prophet had genuine knowledge that the Negus had died in a state of faith.

## Significance

**Earliest diplomatic correspondence.** The letters to the rulers of the major powers in 7 AH represent the first systematic Islamic diplomatic correspondence — a formal assertion of the Muslim community's status as a political entity with the right and obligation to invite world leaders to Islam.

**The Abyssinian relationship.** The warmth of the Prophet's letter to the Negus reflects a relationship built on genuine gratitude. The Abyssinian shelter of the early Muslims was never forgotten in Islamic historical memory, and the Negus is remembered as one of the most righteous of rulers encountered by the early Muslim community.

**The funeral prayer precedent.** The salat al-ghayb performed by the Prophet for the Negus is the basis for a practice that some Muslim scholars allow in cases where a deceased Muslim cannot be reached for a normal funeral, while others restrict it to the Prophet's specific act.

## Sources

The letter and account are preserved in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, and the funeral prayer in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Jana'iz) and Sahih Muslim.
