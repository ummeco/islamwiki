# Detailed Provisions of the Constitution of Medina

The Constitution of Medina (Sahifat al-Madinah or Wathiqat al-Madinah) was a document established by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) shortly after the hijra in 1 AH (approximately 622-623 CE). It is one of the earliest written political constitutions in history — a document establishing the rights, obligations, and governance arrangements of the diverse community of Medina, comprising Muslim immigrants from Mecca (Muhajirun), Muslim residents of Medina (Ansar), and the Jewish tribes of Medina.

## Discovery and Preservation

The text of the Constitution is preserved in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya* (drawing on Ibn Ishaq's earlier work) and in Abu Ubayd's *Kitab al-Amwal*. Western scholars, particularly Leone Caetani in the early 20th century and then W. Montgomery Watt, brought detailed academic attention to the document. Moshe Gil's analysis in the 1970s and subsequent scholarship have examined its structure and authenticity.

The text as preserved is approximately 47-52 clauses, depending on how it is divided. Some scholars argue it represents two originally separate documents; others hold it is a single compact. The majority view in modern Islamic and Western scholarship accepts it as an authentic early document.

## The Major Provisions

**Communal Identity.** The document establishes that the Muhajirun and Ansar from each clan constituted a single ummah (community) together with the Jews who associated with them: "They are one community (ummah) to the exclusion of other people." This was a revolutionary statement in a world organized by kinship — a political community defined by shared loyalty and covenant rather than blood.

**Mutual Defense.** The document obligated all parties to mutual defense: "The Jews shall contribute to the costs of war as long as they are fighting alongside the believers." Each group would maintain its own internal affairs but shared the obligation of defending Medina against external attack.

**Prohibition of Internal Treachery.** "No one of them shall go out to war except with the permission of Muhammad, though none shall be prevented from taking vengeance for a wound." Internal disputes were to be referred to Allah and the Prophet: "And if any dispute or controversy likely to cause trouble should arise, it must be referred to Allah and Muhammad."

**Non-Aggression Among Parties.** "A believer shall not kill a believer on behalf of an unbeliever, nor shall he aid an unbeliever against a believer." The document established a framework for inter-communal non-aggression while maintaining each group's right to its own legal traditions.

**Status of the Jewish Tribes.** The Jewish tribes (specifically named: Banu Awf, Banu al-Najjar, Banu al-Harith, Banu Sa'idah, Banu Jusham, Banu al-Aws, and Banu Tha'labah, among others) were recognized as having their own religion and their own internal legal systems. "The Jews of Banu Awf are one community together with the believers. The Jews have their religion (din) and the Muslims have their religion." Each tribe would meet its own blood money obligations under their own traditions.

**The Position of the Prophet.** "Whatever disagreement between the parties arises must be referred to Allah and to Muhammad." This established the Prophet as the supreme arbiter of the political community — not merely the leader of the Muslims, but the political authority for the entire covenant community of Medina.

**Mecca's Quraysh.** The Quraysh of Mecca — who were at war with the Muslims — received no protection: "Quraysh shall not be protected nor shall they who help them." The document drew a clear line between the Medina covenant community and its external enemies.

**Inviolability of Medina.** "Yathrib (Medina) shall be a sanctuary for the people of this document." This made Medina itself a protected space, parallel in concept to the sanctity of Mecca.

## Historical Significance

**The first written constitution.** The document's claim to being among history's first written political constitutions is based on its establishment of a governance structure, rights and obligations, and dispute-resolution mechanisms for a multi-religious, multi-tribal community through voluntary agreement rather than conquest.

**The concept of ummah.** The document's use of "ummah" to describe the covenant community — including both Muslims and Jews — shows that the term's meaning in this early period was broader than its later Islamic theological use. It referred to a political community organized by shared covenant.

**The template for Islamic governance.** The constitution established patterns that would characterize the early Islamic state: protection of non-Muslim communities under agreed terms, the Prophet's authority as supreme arbiter, shared defense obligations, and internal legal autonomy for each religious community. These patterns recurred in the dhimma arrangements of the subsequent Islamic caliphates.

## Later Violations and Consequences

The document's framework was violated multiple times: Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza — three of the major Jewish tribes of Medina — each, in turn, took actions that the Muslim authorities considered violations of the treaty terms, resulting in their expulsion or (in the case of Banu Qurayza) a harsher outcome. The sequence of events from 2-5 AH saw the Medinan covenant community restructured as the treaty arrangements collapsed.

## Sources

The primary text is in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*. Modern analyses include W. Montgomery Watt, *Muhammad at Medina* (1956); R.B. Serjeant's studies of the document's authenticity; and Uri Rubin's research on its textual transmission.
