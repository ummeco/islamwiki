# The Death of the Prophet

On the twelfth of Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH (8 June 632 CE), the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) died in the city of Medina, in the chamber of his wife Aisha bint Abi Bakr, with his head resting in her lap. His death marked the end of the prophetic mission and the beginning of a new chapter in Islamic history.

## The Final Illness

The Prophet's final illness began after his return from the Farewell Pilgrimage. He had led the pilgrimage in Dhul Hijjah 10 AH and returned to Medina in the sacred month of Muharram 11 AH. Aisha later reported that the illness had begun with a headache and fever — and the Prophet himself reportedly said to her: "O Aisha, I still feel the pain from the food I ate at Khaybar." This is understood by scholars as a reference to the poisoning attempt made years earlier by a Jewish woman named Zaynab bint al-Harith, whose poisoned mutton the Prophet had tasted before spitting it out. The martyrdom of Bishr ibn al-Bara', who ate more of the same meat, was attributed to this incident.

The Prophet's fever was exceptionally high. Aisha reported pouring seven skins of water over him to bring it down. During the illness he moved between the chambers of his wives, until he asked permission to spend his remaining days in Aisha's chamber, where he died.

## Leading Prayer from His Sickbed

As the illness progressed, the Prophet could no longer lead the communal prayers at the mosque adjacent to his chamber. He directed that Abu Bakr as-Siddiq lead the prayers in his place — a decision that would carry enormous significance for the question of succession. Aisha initially objected, suggesting that Abu Bakr was a man who wept easily and might not project authority from the Prophet's position; the Prophet insisted.

On one occasion, during a brief improvement, the Prophet went to the mosque supported by two companions and prayed in congregation. He found Abu Bakr leading the prayer; Abu Bakr stepped back to allow the Prophet to take his place, but the Prophet gestured for him to continue. The Prophet prayed seated at Abu Bakr's side.

## The Final Days

The Prophet continued to receive visitors even in his final days and spoke about the community's spiritual state. He warned against making graves into places of worship, saying: "May Allah curse the Jews and Christians; they took the graves of their prophets as places of worship." He urged: "Prayer! Prayer! And what your right hands possess (i.e., care of those in your charge)."

On the morning of his death, the companions were gathered for the Fajr prayer when the Prophet briefly appeared at the door of Aisha's chamber. The companions, seeing him, were filled with joy and believed he was recovering. He smiled at the sight of them praying in orderly rows and motioned them to continue. He then withdrew and closed the curtain.

## The Death

Shortly after Fajr, the Prophet breathed his last. He had been placing his hand in a vessel of water and wiping his face, saying: "La ilaha illallah. Indeed death has its agonies." Among his last words were: "O Allah, with the Highest Companion" — and he repeated this three times until his hand fell and his breathing ceased.

Aisha reported that he died while his head was between her chest and her neck, and that Allah merged his saliva with hers at the last moment: she had chewed a miswak (toothstick) to soften it and given it to him to clean his teeth, which she counted as a blessing.

## The Announcement

Umar ibn al-Khattab initially refused to accept that the Prophet had died, declaring that he would kill anyone who said so. It was Abu Bakr who restored calm. He arrived at Aisha's chamber, uncovered the Prophet's face, kissed him, and said: "O my Prophet! O my best friend!" He then went out and addressed the people:

"Whoever among you worshipped Muhammad — Muhammad has indeed died. And whoever worshipped Allah — Allah is Ever-Living and never dies."

He then recited: "Muhammad is not but a messenger. Messengers before him have passed on. So if he dies or is killed, will you turn back on your heels?" (Quran 3:144).

This brought the companions back to their senses. Umar later said that when he heard Abu Bakr recite this verse, his legs gave out and he fell to the ground, understanding for the first time that the Prophet was truly gone.

## The Burial

The companions did not immediately agree on where to bury the Prophet. After deliberation, Abu Bakr recalled a hadith stating that prophets are buried where they die. The Prophet was buried beneath the floor of Aisha's chamber, in the spot where he died. Generations later, this chamber was incorporated into the Masjid an-Nabawi, which now surrounds the Prophet's tomb — the green dome visible above it was added in the Mamluk era.

## The Weight of the Loss

Anas ibn Malik said: "I have never seen a day brighter than the day the Prophet came to Medina, and I have never seen a day darker or more frightening than the day he died." The sense of catastrophe that fell over the community was profound. The Prophet had been the center around which the community's spiritual life, legal guidance, and moral authority turned. His death left a community suddenly facing questions of leadership, interpretation, and continuity without the living prophetic voice that had guided them for over two decades.
