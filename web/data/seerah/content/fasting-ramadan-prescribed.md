# Fasting in Ramadan Prescribed

## The Obligation That Defines a Month

The obligation of fasting in the month of Ramadan was revealed in the second year after the Hijra, in Sha'ban 2 AH — approximately eighteen months after the Prophet's ﷺ arrival in Medina. The revelation came through Surah al-Baqarah (2:183-187), a passage of five verses that established the fast, defined its scope, described its exceptions, and identified the Night of Decree (Laylat al-Qadr) as a night within it more blessed than a thousand months. From these five verses, the entire structure of Islamic fasting practice — observed by over a billion people every year — was derived.

The month of Ramadan takes its name from the root r-m-d, associated with the scorching heat of summer in Arabic. Whether the fast was named for the month it happened to fall in, or whether the month had always carried a sacred character that predated Islam, is discussed in classical scholarship. What is certain is that after the revelation of these verses, Ramadan became the most sacred month in the Islamic year — the month in which the Quran was first revealed, the month of the Night of Decree, the month of fasting, of increased worship, and of the community's annual return to its most basic relationship with Allah.

## The Quranic Foundation

The revelation of the fast begins with the most direct possible framing: "O you who believe, fasting has been prescribed upon you as it was prescribed upon those before you, that you may attain taqwa" (2:183). Several features of this verse deserve attention.

**The universal precedent:** The fast is presented as a continuation of a practice given to earlier communities — "as it was prescribed upon those before you." This anchors Islamic fasting in the long tradition of Abrahamic religious practice while distinguishing it in its specific form.

**The purpose — taqwa:** The stated purpose of the fast is not spiritual discipline for its own sake but the development of taqwa — consciousness of Allah, piety, God-fearing alertness. The fast is designed to produce a quality of inner orientation toward Allah, not merely to produce the external act of abstaining from food. The Prophet ﷺ made this explicit in a hadith: "Whoever does not leave false speech and action by it, Allah has no need for him to leave his food and drink."

**The scope:** The following verses defined the fast as abstaining from food, drink, and marital relations from the appearance of the white thread of dawn until the black thread of night (i.e., from Fajr until Maghrib). They acknowledged and defined the exceptions: those who are ill or on a journey may break the fast and make up the missed days later. The elderly and those for whom fasting is permanently difficult may pay fidyah (compensation) by feeding a poor person for each day missed.

## Laylat al-Qadr

Embedded within the Quranic passage on fasting is the identification of Laylat al-Qadr: "The month of Ramadan in which was revealed the Quran, a guidance for people and clear proofs of guidance and criterion. So whoever among you witnesses the month, let him fast it." (2:185) The Quran also reveals in Surah al-Qadr: "The Night of Decree is better than a thousand months. The angels and the Spirit descend therein by permission of their Lord for every matter. Peace it is until the emergence of dawn." (97:3-5)

This night — occurring in the last ten nights of Ramadan, most probably on an odd night (21st, 23rd, 25th, 27th, or 29th) — is the night on which the Quran began its descent. It is the most sacred night in the Islamic calendar. The Prophet ﷺ would seclude himself (i'tikaf) in the mosque for the last ten nights of Ramadan every year until his death, specifically seeking this night in worship. He said: "Whoever stays up in prayer on Laylat al-Qadr out of faith and hoping for reward, his previous sins are forgiven."

## The Structure of Ramadan Practice

From the Quranic verses and the prophetic practice that elaborated them, the full structure of Ramadan emerged:

**Suhur** — the pre-dawn meal, taken before Fajr, which the Prophet ﷺ encouraged as a blessing: "Take suhur — there is blessing in it."

**Iftar** — the breaking of the fast at Maghrib, which the Prophet ﷺ encouraged to do promptly: "My ummah will remain upon goodness as long as they hasten to break the fast."

**Tarawih** — the night prayers of Ramadan, performed in congregation after Isha, in which significant portions of the Quran are recited. The Prophet ﷺ established the practice and the companions continued and formalized it.

**I'tikaf** — the seclusion in the mosque for the last ten nights, withdrawing from ordinary activity to devote full attention to worship and the seeking of Laylat al-Qadr.

**Zakat al-Fitr** — the obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan before the Eid prayer, to ensure that the poor can celebrate Eid with food.

The Prophet ﷺ described Ramadan's spiritual structure in a widely narrated hadith: "When Ramadan enters, the gates of Paradise are opened, the gates of Hell are closed, and the shayatin are chained." This description — of a month in which the ordinary conditions of spiritual struggle are altered in favor of the believers — is understood as both literal (the divine arrangement of the month) and as a description of what becomes possible when the believer applies himself fully to worship without the distractions that ordinarily attend it.

## The Night the Obligation Was Revealed

The companions who heard the revelation of the fast for the first time were already experienced Muslims — they had fasted on Ashura (the tenth of Muharram), which the Prophet ﷺ had instituted as a voluntary fast based on the practice of Musa. When the Ramadan fast was revealed as obligatory, the Ashura fast became voluntary rather than obligatory. But the community's prior experience of voluntary fasting meant that the obligation of a month's fast, while significant, was not entirely unprecedented in their practice.

The first Ramadan of Islam — observed in 2 AH — was also the Ramadan of the Battle of Badr. The Muslim force that fought at Badr on the seventeenth of Ramadan, the most significant battle of the early Islamic period, was fasting. The combination of the first obligatory Ramadan and the first major battle is one of the most compact demonstrations of the Islamic principle that the spiritual and the practical are not separate realms: the fasting community and the fighting community were the same people, on the same day.
