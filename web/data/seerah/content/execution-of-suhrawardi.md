# Execution of Suhrawardi the Illuminationist

Shihab ad-Din Yahya ibn Habash ibn Amirak al-Suhrawardi (549-587 AH / 1154-1191 CE) was the founder of the Illuminationist (Ishraqi) school of Islamic philosophy, one of the most original and influential philosophical movements in the history of Islamic thought. His execution at the age of approximately 36 in Aleppo, ordered by Salah ad-Din al-Ayyubi's son al-Malik al-Zahir Ghazi, cut short one of the most brilliant philosophical careers of the medieval period.

## Life and Education

Al-Suhrawardi was born in the village of Suhraward in northwestern Iran (present-day Zanjan province). He pursued his education across the major centers of learning in the Islamic world, studying in Maragheh and Isfahan before traveling extensively through Anatolia and Syria.

He was a prodigiously gifted student and became deeply learned in the Peripatetic (Aristotelian) philosophy that dominated Islamic intellectual life, as well as in theology, Sufi spirituality, ancient Persian wisdom traditions, and Hermetic philosophy. His travels brought him to Aleppo in northern Syria, then under the rule of the Ayyubid dynasty, where he joined the court of al-Malik al-Zahir Ghazi, the young son of Salah ad-Din.

## Illuminationist Philosophy

Al-Suhrawardi's philosophical system, which he called Hikmat al-Ishraq (the Philosophy of Illumination), was a radical departure from the Peripatetic tradition. While Ibn Sina (Avicenna) had worked within the Aristotelian framework of essence and existence, al-Suhrawardi proposed that light (nur) was the fundamental reality of all existence.

In his magnum opus *Hikmat al-Ishraq* (Wisdom of Illumination), written when he was approximately 30, he argued:

- Existence is constituted by degrees of light and darkness
- God is the Light of Lights (Nur al-Anwar), the source from which all other lights emanate
- The intelligible world consists of self-subsisting lights (the Platonic Forms, reinterpreted through the metaphysics of light)
- Human knowledge is ultimately achieved through illumination — a direct experience of light — not through Aristotelian abstraction

He drew on Plato, the ancient Persian concept of *xvarnah* (divine effulgence), Hermetic wisdom, and Islamic mysticism to construct a synthesis that he claimed recovered a primordial wisdom (al-hikma al-atiqah) shared by all the ancient sages.

## The Controversy

Al-Suhrawardi's ideas attracted both devoted students and bitter opponents. The religious scholars of Aleppo accused him of holding heretical positions, including:

- Claims to be a prophet or a figure with special divine illumination
- Denying bodily resurrection
- Reviving "infidel" Persian and Greek philosophical traditions
- Teaching doctrines incompatible with Islamic theology

Some of the accusations may have been misunderstandings or deliberate misrepresentations by enemies. Al-Suhrawardi's use of ancient Persian and Greek material was scholarly and philosophical, not a rejection of Islam. He was himself a practicing Muslim and argued that his philosophy was consistent with Quranic revelation interpreted esoterically.

However, the accusations were serious enough to warrant a formal complaint to Salah ad-Din, who was then occupied with his campaigns against the Crusaders. Salah ad-Din reportedly expressed reluctance to execute him and wrote to his son accordingly, but ultimately deferred to the religious scholars and his son's judgment.

## The Execution

Al-Suhrawardi was executed in Aleppo in 587 AH (1191 CE), the same year that saw Salah ad-Din's triumphant return to Jerusalem after the Third Crusade (Saladin had recaptured Jerusalem in 583 AH / 1187 CE). The method of execution is disputed in the sources: some say he was strangled, others that he was starved to death in prison, and still others that he was killed by the sword.

He was approximately 36 years old at his death. He is known in Arabic as *al-Maqtul* (the Murdered One) or *al-Shahid* (the Martyr, though this designation is not universally accepted in the religious sense).

## Legacy

Al-Suhrawardi's philosophy, far from being extinguished by his execution, became one of the most enduring currents in Islamic intellectual history, particularly in the Persian-speaking world. His works were preserved and studied in Iran and the Indian subcontinent:

In Iran, the Safavid period (tenth to twelfth century AH / sixteenth to eighteenth century CE) saw a major revival of Illuminationist philosophy. Scholars like Mulla Sadra (d. 1050 AH / 1640 CE) engaged deeply with al-Suhrawardi's work, though Mulla Sadra ultimately developed his own synthesis (the Transcendent Wisdom).

Al-Suhrawardi's influence reached the Mughal court in India, where his works were studied and translated. His philosophy of light influenced Persian and Urdu poetry across many centuries.

His school represents one of the most creative syntheses in Islamic intellectual history — a fusion of Islamic spirituality, Greek and Persian philosophy, and visionary mysticism that produced a metaphysical system of great originality.
