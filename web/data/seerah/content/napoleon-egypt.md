# Napoleon's Invasion of Egypt

## The Strategic Pretext and the Expedition's Design

On July 1, 1798 CE / 14 Muharram 1213 AH, a French expeditionary force of approximately 38,000 soldiers under the command of General Napoleon Bonaparte landed on the Egyptian coast near Alexandria. The strategic justification given to the French Directory was the disruption of British trade routes to India — Egypt being a potential overland shortcut between the Mediterranean and the Red Sea. Britain had recently consolidated its position in India through the defeat of Tipu Sultan, and France was looking for ways to threaten British interests without engaging the Royal Navy directly.

The expedition was unusual from the beginning in combining military conquest with an enormous scientific and scholarly program. Napoleon brought with him 167 "savants" — scientists, engineers, mathematicians, artists, linguists, and antiquarians — organized into the Commission des Sciences et Arts. Their task was to study Egypt systematically: its ancient monuments, natural history, geography, existing industries, and social organization. The result, the Description de l'Égypte published between 1809 and 1828 in 24 volumes, was the founding document of modern Egyptology and a monument of Enlightenment encyclopedism applied to the non-European world.

Egypt at this time was nominally a province of the Ottoman Empire, but effective power lay with the Mamluk military caste — descendants of slave soldiers who had ruled Egypt intermittently since the thirteenth century, now organized into competing households under their beys (lords). The Ottoman governor was a figurehead. Egypt's population of perhaps three million, mostly Muslim Egyptians, was governed by a Mamluk oligarchy that was itself ethnically distinct (largely Caucasian in origin) from the Arabic-speaking peasants and townspeople beneath it.

## The March to Cairo and the Battle of the Pyramids

Napoleon's forces moved quickly from Alexandria inland toward Cairo. The Egyptian city's population and the Mamluk defenders initially underestimated the threat. The decisive engagement came on July 21, 1798 CE / 6 Safar 1213 AH, near the village of Embabeh, in what Napoleon theatrically called the Battle of the Pyramids (the pyramids of Giza were visible on the horizon, though some distance away).

The Mamluk cavalry — superb horsemen who had dominated Egyptian battlefields for centuries — charged the French infantry squares repeatedly and were mowed down by disciplined musket volleys and artillery. The Mamluk commander Murad Bey lost several thousand men; the French casualties were minimal. Within days, Napoleon entered Cairo.

Napoleon's famous address to his troops before the battle — "Soldiers, forty centuries look down upon you" — was designed for European ears and European history books. His proclamations to the Egyptian population had a different register: he presented himself as a liberator from Mamluk oppression, a friend of Islam, and implausibly, a secret convert to Islam himself. His proclamation, distributed in Arabic, claimed that the French had come to punish the Mamluks and to protect the people, that the French "respected God, His Prophet, and the Quran," and that Napoleon himself confessed to God's unity (tawhid). The Egyptian scholars and population were largely unimpressed. The contemporary Egyptian chronicler Abd al-Rahman al-Jabarti, whose 'Aja'ib al-Athar fi'l-Tarajim wa'l-Akhbar is the primary Arabic source for the period, subjected Napoleon's proclamation to a detailed and devastating philological and logical critique, noting its grammatical errors in Arabic, its internal contradictions, and its patent cynicism.

## The Battle of the Nile and Strategic Disaster

Napoleon's control of Egypt lasted barely three years, and its strategic failure was determined within weeks of his arrival. On August 1-2, 1798 CE, the British Admiral Horatio Nelson located the French fleet at anchor in Aboukir Bay, east of Alexandria, and destroyed it in the Battle of the Nile (also called the Battle of Aboukir Bay). In a few hours of fighting, thirteen of seventeen French ships of the line were captured or sunk. Napoleon's army was stranded in Egypt, cut off from France by British naval supremacy in the Mediterranean.

This naval defeat transformed the expedition. Napoleon had planned Egypt as a stepping stone to further eastern ambitions — potentially a march to India to threaten the British there. Instead, he commanded an army that could not easily be reinforced or withdrawn. He was isolated in a country of three million people, most of whom were becoming increasingly hostile.

## The Diwan and Administrative Experimentation

The period of French occupation included a remarkable experiment in political institutions. Napoleon established a General Diwan (Diwan al-Kibar) composed of Egyptian religious scholars (ulama), merchants, and notables from across Egypt, assembled to advise on governance. This was, in functional if not formal terms, a representative body of a kind that had no precedent in Egyptian political history — an assembly of local notables consulted on matters of administration.

The ulama of Cairo — most prominently the scholars of al-Azhar mosque-university — occupied an ambiguous position. Al-Azhar, founded in 359 AH / 969 CE by the Fatimid dynasty, was the most prestigious institution of Sunni Islamic learning in the Arab world. Its scholars were the de facto leaders of the Muslim Egyptian population and the principal interpreters of Islamic law in everyday life. Napoleon recognized their importance and cultivated their collaboration, treating the Azhari scholars as interlocutors in governance. Some scholars participated in the diwan; others refused. The tensions between collaboration and resistance ran through the occupation.

Al-Jabarti's chronicle records the street-level experience of French occupation in Cairo: the requisitioning of property, the imposition of new taxes, the behavior of French soldiers in public spaces, the disruption of established hierarchies. His tone is that of a learned man trying to understand a disaster — not quite condemning the French as simply evil, but recording the disorientation produced by their methods and their evident power.

## The Syria Campaign and Napoleon's Departure

In February 1799, Napoleon marched his army into Ottoman Syria in an attempt to preempt an Ottoman counterattack. He captured Jaffa — where he controversially ordered the massacre of captured Ottoman soldiers — and besieged Acre (Akka). The siege of Acre lasted two months and failed, partly due to British naval support for the Ottoman defenders under Ahmad Pasha al-Jazzar and partly due to plague devastating the French forces. Napoleon retreated to Egypt.

He then defeated an Ottoman landing force at the second Battle of Aboukir (July 1799), before receiving news that political conditions in France demanded his return. He slipped away secretly in August 1799, leaving his army under General Kléber without informing his soldiers of his departure. The army remained in Egypt until the British and Ottoman forces finally expelled them in 1801 — a three-year occupation that ended ignominiously.

## Al-Jabarti and the Muslim Scholarly Response

The most important contemporary account of the French occupation, Abd al-Rahman al-Jabarti's 'Aja'ib al-Athar (a title roughly translated as Marvelous Works of the Ages), is a document of extraordinary historical and cultural significance. Al-Jabarti (1167-1240 AH / 1753-1825 CE) was an Azhari scholar and chronicler who observed the French occupation at close range. His account oscillates between grudging acknowledgment of French technical achievements — he visited the French scientific institute and was impressed by their telescopes, chemistry apparatus, and scientific library — and horror at the moral and political disorder the occupation brought.

His critique of Napoleon's Arabic proclamation is particularly acute: he went line by line through the document, noting grammatical errors that indicated French unfamiliarity with Arabic, logical contradictions in the claims about respecting Islam while dismantling Islamic governance, and the absurdity of a non-Muslim claiming leadership over Muslims. His reaction captures the response of the Islamic scholarly establishment to the French expedition: not simple rejection but a complex engagement with what was clearly a superior military and technical power whose moral and religious claims were nonetheless found wanting.

## The Discovery of the Rosetta Stone

Among the scientific achievements of the expedition, none had more lasting consequence than the discovery of the Rosetta Stone in July 1799 CE near the town of Rashid (Rosetta) in the Nile Delta. The stone bore an identical decree in three scripts — Ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs, Demotic script, and Greek — and provided the key that eventually allowed Jean-François Champollion to decipher hieroglyphics in 1822, opening the entire civilization of ancient Egypt to scholarly investigation. The stone was seized by the British after France's defeat and is now in the British Museum, where it remains a subject of Egyptian requests for repatriation.

The broader scientific harvest of the expedition — the systematic mapping of Egypt, the cataloguing of its flora, fauna, and ancient monuments, the ethnographic observation of its population and customs — was assembled into the Description de l'Égypte and effectively founded modern Egyptology. This scientific achievement came at the cost of Egyptian sovereignty and was accompanied by the plunder of ancient artifacts taken to European museums — an ambiguity in the expedition's legacy that is still felt.

## The Shock to the Muslim World and the Nahda

The ease with which a relatively small European force had conquered the Arab heartland — including two of the most important cities in the Islamic world, Alexandria and Cairo — was a profound shock to Muslim political and intellectual consciousness. Egypt had been under Muslim governance (Umayyad, Abbasid, Fatimid, Mamluk, Ottoman) for over a thousand years. Within days, an infidel European army was in control of Cairo, issuing decrees and reorganizing governance.

The implications were not lost on contemporary observers. If a force of 38,000 Frenchmen could conquer Egypt in a matter of weeks, what did this say about the relative condition of European and Muslim civilization? The question drove what historians call the Nahda — the Arab awakening or renaissance of the nineteenth century — a broad intellectual movement that attempted to diagnose Muslim decline and prescribe reforms.

The responses varied. Some reformers argued that the Islamic world needed to adopt European technology and administrative methods while retaining Islamic values — a synthesis that Muhammad Ali Pasha of Egypt would attempt in the following decades. Others argued that the problem was the departure of Muslims from authentic Islamic practice, and that reform must be primarily religious and spiritual rather than technological. Others still argued for political modernization along European lines. The French expedition did not cause these debates, but it crystallized them with uncomfortable urgency.
