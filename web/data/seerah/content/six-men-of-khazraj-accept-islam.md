# Six Men at Aqaba: The Seed of the Medinan Community

In the history of Islam, some moments carry consequences that their participants could not have anticipated. The meeting of six young men from the Khazraj tribe of Yathrib with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ during the Hajj season — in approximately the eleventh or twelfth year of the prophethood, three or four years before the Hijra — is one such moment. These six men did not come to Mecca seeking a prophet. They came for the pilgrimage, as Arabs did annually. But they left as Muslims, and their return to Yathrib set in motion a chain of events that would produce the community of Medina, the Islamic state, and the transformation of human history.

## The Six Men: Names and Identity

The classical sources — primarily Ibn Hisham drawing from Ibn Ishaq, supplemented by Ibn Saad and Ibn Hajar — preserve the names of the six, though minor variations appear across different accounts. The most consistent version gives: Asad ibn Zurara ibn Adas al-Khazraji, Awf ibn al-Harith ibn Rifa'a (also identified as Ibn Afra), Rafi' ibn Malik ibn al-Ajlan, Qutba ibn Amir ibn Hadida, Uqba ibn Amir ibn Nabi, and Jabir ibn Abdallah ibn Ri'ab.

All six were from the Khazraj, one of the two major tribal groupings of Yathrib (the other being the Aws). The Khazraj and Aws had been engaged in a long-running inter-tribal conflict, most recently culminating in the devastatingly costly Battle of Bu'ath, which had taken place shortly before this meeting. The war had exhausted both tribes and left neither in a position to claim clear victory; the social fabric of Yathrib was frayed.

The six men were not elders or chiefs — the sources describe them as young men, representatives of a generation that had grown up in the shadow of the Aws-Khazraj conflict. They came to Mecca for the pilgrimage in the company of a larger group of Khazraj.

## Why Yathrib Was Ready

The receptiveness of the six Khazraj men was not coincidental. Several factors had prepared Yathrib to receive the Islamic message in a way that the other Arab tribes the Prophet ﷺ had approached had not been prepared.

The most significant was the presence of Jewish tribes in Yathrib. The Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza were established communities in the oasis, and the Arabs of Yathrib had lived alongside them for generations. The Jewish tribes believed in prophecy, in divine revelation, in an ethical monotheism that was entirely foreign to the purely pagan Arab tribes of the interior. More specifically, they spoke of an expected prophet who would come — a belief rooted in their reading of their own scriptures.

The Arab tribes had absorbed this expectation through proximity. The Aws and Khazraj had heard from their Jewish neighbors about the concept of a prophet, about the coming of a new divine messenger. When the six young men heard the Prophet ﷺ describe himself and his message, they had a framework — however imperfect — for understanding the claim. It was not alien to them in the way it was to tribes that had never lived alongside the People of the Book.

Ibn Hisham records that when the six men heard the Prophet ﷺ, some of them said to each other: "By Allah, this is the prophet that the Jews threaten us with. Let us not let them be ahead of us in accepting him." This statement — preserved in the seerah sources as a report of their inner conversation — reveals that their acceptance of Islam was partially motivated by the anticipation that the Jewish tribes would soon accept the Prophet ﷺ and gain a political and spiritual advantage over the Khazraj. It was a competitive calculation, but it was also genuine recognition of the message's truth. Both can be true simultaneously, and the subsequent conduct of these men — and the thousands who came to Islam through them — confirms that the faith was real.

## The Meeting at Aqaba

The Prophet ﷺ encountered the six at Aqaba, a geographical location near Mecca associated with a small mountain pass (the word means "rocky incline" or "difficult path"). This location would become the scene of two further pledges in subsequent years — the Second and Third Pledges of Aqaba — that directly preceded the Hijra.

At this first meeting, the Prophet ﷺ sat with the six men and presented Islam to them. He recited Quran and explained the message: tawhid (the absolute oneness of Allah), the rejection of all other deities, the truth of the prophecy, and the obligations that followed from accepting these truths. The six listened and accepted.

Their acceptance was not tentative. They made a commitment to take the message back to their people in Yathrib. Ibn Hisham records that they said to each other before leaving: "Know that this is indeed the Prophet that the Jews threatened us with. Do not let them be ahead of you in following him." They returned to Yathrib as Muslims with a mission.

## Their Return and the Spread of Islam in Yathrib

The six men returned to Yathrib and began conveying the message. There was no formal teacher, no organized instruction — just these six men telling their families, friends, and clan members what they had heard and what they believed. Yet the spread was remarkable.

Within a year, Islam had spread through significant portions of both the Khazraj and the Aws — crossing the tribal divide that had so recently been the scene of the Bu'ath conflict. This cross-tribal spread is itself significant: Islam was providing Yathrib with something it had not been able to achieve through its own resources, a common framework that transcended the old loyalties and grievances. The Prophet ﷺ was becoming, in the minds of the Yathribis, a potential solution to their fractured social situation — not through his political authority but through the unifying force of the message.

By the following year, twelve men from Yathrib came to the Prophet ﷺ at Aqaba during the Hajj season. This was the Second Pledge of Aqaba — also called the Pledge of Women because its terms were the same as those later given to women. The twelve pledged to worship Allah alone, not to steal, not to commit zina, not to kill their children, not to slander, and not to disobey the Prophet ﷺ in what was right. The Prophet ﷺ told them: "Whoever fulfills this pledge, his reward is with Allah. Whoever falls into any of these and is punished in this world, that is his expiation. And whoever conceals it, his matter is with Allah — if He wills He will forgive him, and if He wills He will punish him."

The Prophet ﷺ sent with them Musab ibn Umayr (may Allah be pleased with him) — one of the young, educated early Muslims of Quraysh — to teach them the Quran and the basics of the religion. Musab's presence in Yathrib accelerated the spread further. He was cultured, eloquent, and capable of addressing the educated circles of Yathrib. Within the year following his arrival, the most significant men of both the Aws and Khazraj had accepted Islam.

## The Third Pledge of Aqaba

By the thirteenth year of the prophetic mission — approximately one year before the Hijra — the community in Yathrib had grown to the point where seventy-two men and two women came to Mecca for the Hajj season with the specific purpose of meeting the Prophet ﷺ and making a formal pledge of support.

The Third Pledge of Aqaba was a qualitatively different commitment from the earlier ones. The twelve had pledged personal conduct; the seventy-two pledged political support and military protection. They pledged to protect the Prophet ﷺ as they would protect their own wives and children — meaning that any attack on him was an attack on their community, with all the military and honor implications that carried in tribal society.

The Prophet ﷺ asked for twelve leaders from among them — nine from Khazraj and three from Aws — to serve as naqib (leaders or guarantors) for their people, a term borrowed from the Jewish tradition of tribal leaders. This organizational detail shows the Prophet's ﷺ attention to the political structure of what he was building: not just a spiritual community but one with governance, accountability, and recognized leadership.

This pledge was the direct precursor to the Hijra. Within months, the Muslims began emigrating in small groups to Yathrib. The Prophet ﷺ was among the last to leave, making his famous journey with Abu Bakr after the Quraysh's assassination plot.

## The Theological Significance: Divine Arrangement

The scholars of seerah consistently note the divine arrangement evident in the sequence of events leading from the six Khazraj men to the Islamic state. The Prophet ﷺ had spent years systematically approaching the tribes of Arabia and receiving rejection from each. Then six young men from a city he had not specifically targeted happened to be at Aqaba during the pilgrimage season, with a background that made them uniquely prepared to hear and accept the message.

This is not coincidence in the Islamic theological framework — it is tawfiq, divine facilitation. Allah arranged for the specific people who would be receptive to the message to encounter it at the specific moment when they were ready for it. The years of rejection had not been wasted time; they had established the integrity of the mission and exhausted the alternatives.

The choice of Yathrib as the base for the Islamic state also makes geographic and social sense in retrospect: it was a city of oasis agriculture rather than caravan commerce, which gave it a different economic character from Mecca; it had existing Jewish communities who had familiarized the population with prophetic concepts; it had two tribes in a state of exhausted conflict who needed an external authority to provide social peace; and it had enough distance from Mecca to allow the Muslim community to develop without direct Qurayshi control.

## The Ansar's Special Status

The men and women of Yathrib who accepted Islam and supported the Hijra were given a permanent title in Islamic terminology: al-Ansar (the Helpers). The Quran addresses them specifically and honors them extensively. Surah al-Anfal (8:72) distinguishes between those who emigrated (al-Muhajirun) and those who gave them aid and refuge (al-Ansar), and establishes a special bond between them.

The Prophet ﷺ expressed his love for the Ansar in multiple hadith preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim. "A sign of faith is love for the Ansar, and a sign of hypocrisy is hatred for the Ansar." He described his love for Medina itself and his hope to die there. He told his Companions: "Had it not been for the Hijra, I would have been one of the Ansar." These statements of the Prophet ﷺ retroactively honored the decision of the six men who came to him at Aqaba and said yes when every other tribe had said no.

Without those six men — without their receptiveness, their return to Yathrib, their months of informal teaching among their families — there would have been no invitation to Medina, no Hijra, no Islamic state. The foundations of everything that followed were laid in that first Aqaba meeting, when six young Khazraj men from a war-torn oasis city sat with a man from Quraysh and heard something they had never heard before and found it true.
