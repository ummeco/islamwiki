# The Young Turk Revolution: Modernity, Nationalism, and the End of the Ottoman Order

## The Ottoman Empire on the Edge

By the early 20th century, the Ottoman Empire was being called "the sick man of Europe" — a phrase attributed to Tsar Nicholas I of Russia, which captured the perception of a vast state in terminal decline. The diagnosis was not entirely wrong. The empire had lost Greece (1830), Romania, Bulgaria, Serbia, and Montenegro (1878), and was facing active nationalist movements in its remaining Balkan, Arab, and Armenian populations. Its finances were in disarray — the Ottoman Public Debt Administration, established in 1881, gave European creditors formal control over significant portions of imperial revenue. Military defeats had been followed by territorial losses that Ottoman public opinion experienced as humiliations.

Sultan Abdulhamid II (r. 1293-1327 AH / 1876-1909 CE) had responded to this crisis with a particular strategy: tighten central control, suppress political opposition, and use pan-Islamic rhetoric to maintain the loyalty of Muslim subjects while buying time through diplomatic maneuvering. He had suspended the Ottoman Constitution of 1876 in 1878, dismissed the parliament, and ruled as an autocrat. His pan-Islamic policy — presenting himself as the Caliph of all Muslims worldwide — was sophisticated and had real effects, particularly in maintaining loyalty in Muslim-majority provinces.

But the Sultan's methods bred their own opposition. A generation of Ottoman officers and officials educated in European-style military academies and professional schools had absorbed Enlightenment ideas about constitutionalism, popular sovereignty, and scientific progress. They were simultaneously proud Ottomans who wanted the empire to survive and modernizers who believed that survival required fundamental transformation. They called themselves the Committee of Union and Progress (CUP) — and their opponents and Western observers called them the Young Turks.

## The Revolution of 1908

The Young Turk Revolution unfolded with remarkable speed in the summer of 1908 (1326 AH). CUP cells had been organizing within the Ottoman military in Macedonia — the Balkan provinces where the empire's grip seemed most precarious. In July 1908, officers in the Third Army Corps began openly defying Istanbul and threatening to march on the capital if the Sultan did not restore the Constitution.

Abdulhamid II, calculating that resistance would trigger civil war and perhaps European intervention, capitulated. On July 23, 1908, he announced the restoration of the 1876 Constitution and the reconvening of parliament. The news was greeted with extraordinary popular celebration across the empire — Muslims, Christians, Jews, and Armenians embraced in the streets of Istanbul, Salonica, and Beirut. The promise was a constitutional, multiethnic empire in which all citizens would have equal rights and representation.

The jubilation was premature. The CUP moved quickly from constitutional reformers to authoritarian controllers. The April 1909 counter-revolution (the 31 March Incident in Ottoman dating) — an attempted royalist and Islamist uprising in Istanbul — was crushed by CUP military units. In its aftermath, Abdulhamid II was deposed and replaced by his brother Mehmed V, who became a constitutional monarch without real power.

## The CUP's Ideology

The Committee of Union and Progress was never ideologically homogeneous. Its membership included liberals, nationalists, positivists, and pragmatists. But over time, a particular ideology dominated: Turkish nationalism combined with a form of social Darwinism that emphasized the survival of the Turkish nation above the multi-ethnic imperial framework.

Key intellectual influences included:

French positivism: August Comte's social science model, which treated religion as a primitive stage of human development to be replaced by science, had significant influence in CUP circles. The newspaper *Tevhid-i Efkar* and others promoted secularist positions.

Pan-Turkism: the idea that Turks constituted a distinct ethnic and cultural nation, with connections to Turkic peoples across Central Asia, had been developed by intellectuals like Ziya Gökalp (1876-1924 CE), often called the father of Turkish nationalism. Gökalp distinguished between "civilization" (European science and technology, which should be adopted) and "culture" (Turkish national identity, which should be preserved) — a distinction that allowed secularization in the public sphere while nominally preserving cultural Islam.

The practical result: the CUP moved the Ottoman state toward a Turkish nationalist framework that excluded or marginalized non-Turkish Muslims (Arabs, Kurds, Albanians) and non-Muslim communities (Armenians, Greeks, Jews) — the opposite of the multi-ethnic constitutionalism promised in July 1908.

## The Balkan Wars: The Catastrophe That Radicalized the CUP

The First Balkan War (1912-1913 CE / 1330-1331 AH) was the event that transformed the CUP from a reformist movement into an increasingly authoritarian and nationalist one. The Ottoman Empire was attacked by the Balkan League (Bulgaria, Serbia, Greece, and Montenegro) and suffered catastrophic military defeats. Within months, the empire lost nearly all of its remaining European territory — including Salonica, the very city where the CUP had been founded. Hundreds of thousands of Muslim refugees fled to Istanbul and Anatolia.

The human cost was staggering: widespread massacres and expulsions of Muslims in the former Ottoman territories. The CUP leadership, many of whom had been born in Salonica and the Balkans, experienced these events as existential — and drew from them the conclusion that ethnic minorities within the empire were potential fifth columns for foreign powers. This perception would have catastrophic consequences for the Armenian population.

The Second Balkan War (1913 CE) briefly reversed some losses but changed little strategically. By 1913, the three-man CUP inner circle of Enver Pasha, Talaat Pasha, and Cemal Pasha (the "Three Pashas") had effectively seized power — ruling the empire as an authoritarian triumvirate.

## World War I: The Fatal Alliance

The CUP's decision to enter World War I on the side of Germany (signed in a secret alliance, August 1914) was driven by multiple calculations: Germany was the most powerful European army; a German victory might recover lost territories; the Entente (Britain, France, Russia) were the powers most threatening to Ottoman interests (particularly Russia, which had long-term ambitions to control Istanbul and the Straits).

On November 11, 1914, Sultan Mehmed V was persuaded to declare jihad against the Entente powers. The fatwa, issued by the Sheikh al-Islam, called on Muslims worldwide — including those living under British, French, and Russian rule — to fight for the Central Powers. The response was largely a failure: Arab soldiers in the Ottoman army mostly did not mutiny, but the call for a global Muslim uprising against Britain and France produced no significant response.

The war brought unprecedented disaster. The Gallipoli campaign (1333 AH / 1915 CE) was the one significant Ottoman military success — successfully repelling the Allied attempt to force the Dardanelles and take Istanbul, costing 250,000 lives on each side. But elsewhere, the military situation deteriorated rapidly: the British captured Baghdad in 1917 and Jerusalem in December 1917.

## The Armenian Genocide

The darkest chapter of the Young Turk period is the systematic destruction of the Armenian population of Anatolia (1333-1334 AH / 1915-1916 CE). The CUP leadership, particularly Talaat Pasha as Interior Minister, ordered the deportation of the Armenian population of Eastern Anatolia to the Syrian desert — a policy implemented by regular troops, Kurdish irregular forces, and criminal gangs that resulted in mass killings, death marches, and forced starvation.

The estimates of deaths range from 600,000 to 1.5 million. The evidence from Ottoman documents, German diplomatic records (Germany was the Ottoman ally and its officers were present and documented what they witnessed), and survivor testimony is overwhelming. The International Association of Genocide Scholars and over thirty national parliaments have formally recognized these events as genocide.

The theological dimension: Muslim scholarly opinion, both at the time and subsequently, has been clear that the killing of non-combatant civilians is haram regardless of their religion or nationality. The Quran (5:32) states that killing one person unjustly is as if killing all humanity. The justification offered by CUP leaders — military necessity, Armenian collaboration with Russia — does not meet any standard of Islamic permissibility for targeting civilians. Some Kurdish tribal leaders who participated were later criticized by Kurdish religious scholars; the Ottoman state's own records show awareness that massacres were occurring.

## The Arab Revolt and the Empire's Collapse

By 1916, Arab leaders in the Hejaz under Sharif Hussein ibn Ali of Mecca were in communication with the British and had concluded that the empire's future was doubtful. Motivated by promises of Arab independence, Hussein launched the Arab Revolt in June 1916 — a direct military challenge to Ottoman authority in Arabia.

The Ottoman military response was hampered by the overall war situation. Arab forces under Hussein's sons, with British advisers including T.E. Lawrence, disrupted Ottoman supply lines and eventually participated in the march into Damascus (1918). For the CUP, the Arab Revolt was experienced as treason — a Muslim population joining non-Muslim enemies against the Caliph. For Arab Muslims, it was experienced as an assertion of Arab political rights against a regime that had shown no commitment to its non-Turkish subjects.

The armistice of Mudros (October 30, 1918) ended the war for the Ottomans. The Three Pashas fled Istanbul on a German submarine; Talaat and Cemal were later assassinated by Armenian operatives.

## The Ahl us-Sunnah Assessment

The Young Turk revolution presents a complex legacy for Muslim historical evaluation. The CUP's constitutional phase (1908-1912) represented a genuine attempt to create a modern state that respected both Islamic tradition and constitutional governance — an attempt that failed partly through its own internal contradictions and partly through the overwhelming pressures of European imperialism and Balkan nationalism.

The CUP's later phase (1913-1918) moved decisively away from Islamic governance. The Three Pashas were not pious Muslims; they were secular nationalists who used Islamic symbols when convenient. Their genocide of the Armenians was a war crime that Islamic ethics unambiguously condemns. Their decision to enter World War I proved catastrophically wrong and directly led to the abolition of the Caliphate six years later.

Muslim scholars at the time were largely opposed to the CUP's secularizing direction — the 31 March Incident of 1909 was partly an Islamic reaction to CUP policies. The CUP's suppression of this opposition and its continued marginalization of religious authority in governance represented a departure from the Ottoman tradition in which the ulama had held significant institutional power.
