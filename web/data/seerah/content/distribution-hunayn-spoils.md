# The Distribution of the Hunayn Spoils

## The Scale of the Capture

The Battle of Hunayn had been fought against a confederacy that made the tactical decision to bring its families and livestock to the battlefield — a decision that was catastrophic when the Hawazin force was routed. The Muslim army captured an enormous quantity of spoils: by the various narrations, approximately 24,000 camels, 40,000 sheep, and 4,000 silver coins, as well as the women and children of the Hawazin who had been brought to the battle. The captives numbered approximately 6,000 people.

The Prophet ﷺ waited at Ji'irrana, a location between Mecca and Taif, for the Hawazin to come and make a settlement. He held the spoils and captives in trust, giving the Hawazin time to accept Islam and reclaim what was theirs. The Hawazin sent a delegation. They accepted Islam, and a representative of the tribe — a man who was Malik ibn Awf's foster brother, and who invoked the milk kinship between the Hawazin and the Prophet ﷺ (the Prophet ﷺ had been nursed by Halimah al-Sa'diyya, a woman of the Hawazin tribe) — made the request for the return of the captives.

The Prophet ﷺ said he would return his own share and the shares of the Banu Abd al-Muttalib; the rest was in the hands of the broader Muslim army. He then addressed the army and asked them to return the captives voluntarily. Every segment of the army agreed. The 6,000 captives were freed — without a ransom, as an act of grace toward the Hawazin who had accepted Islam.

## The Distribution That Hurt the Ansar

The distribution of the non-human spoils — the camels, sheep, and silver — became the most emotionally consequential economic decision of the post-Conquest period. The Prophet ﷺ gave the new converts among the Quraysh and other tribes large and lavish shares: Abu Sufyan ibn Harb received 100 camels and 40 silver pieces; his sons Yazid and Muawiyah received similar amounts. Safwan ibn Umayyah — who had not yet taken his shahada — received 100 camels. Other leading Qurayshi and tribal figures received similarly generous allocations. These were the mu'allafat qulubuhum — those whose hearts were being won, whose Islam was recent and whose attachment to the community needed to be deepened through material connection.

The Ansar received nothing. They were asked to wait while the Prophet ﷺ distributed to others. Murmurs began to circulate among the Ansar: that the Prophet ﷺ had found his own people and had given the wealth of Hunayn to them, leaving the Ansar with nothing.

## The Prophet's Response

The Prophet ﷺ gathered the Ansar separately and asked them: "What is this I hear about you?" The Ansar confirmed that they had felt hurt. The Prophet ﷺ then spoke in a way that the seerah literature has preserved with great care, because it was among the most emotionally charged moments of his relationship with the community of Medina:

"O Ansar — did I not find you wandering and Allah guided you through me? Did I not find you poor and Allah enriched you through me? Did I not find you enemies and Allah reconciled your hearts through me?" The Ansar said yes. "Will you not answer me back, O Ansar?" They said: "What shall we say, O Messenger of Allah? Allah and His Messenger are more gracious and generous to us than anything." He said: "By Allah, if you wished, you could say — and you would speak truth and be believed — 'You came to us rejected and we believed you; you came to us as a fugitive and we sheltered you; you came to us helpless and we supported you.'"

Then he said: "O Ansar — are you troubled by worldly goods with which I have reconciled some people's hearts to Islam, when I have entrusted you to your own Islam? Are you not satisfied, O Ansar, that others go back with camels and sheep while you go back with the Messenger of Allah? By Him in whose hand is my soul, what you return with is better than what they return with." And then: "O Allah, have mercy on the Ansar, on the sons of the Ansar, and on the grandsons of the Ansar."

The Ansar wept — so intensely that their beards were wet with tears. They said: "We are satisfied with the Messenger of Allah as our share and portion."

## The Theological Architecture

The distribution of the Hunayn spoils is studied in Islamic jurisprudence as the foundational case for the permissibility of giving larger shares to recent converts to strengthen their attachment (ta'lif al-qulub). The Quran explicitly mentions the mu'allafat qulubuhum as a category of zakat recipients (Surah al-Tawbah 9:60). The Prophet's ﷺ judgment at Ji'irrana — favoring the new Qurayshi converts over the Ansar who had given everything — was not an act of tribal preference but a strategic spiritual investment in the long-term health of the community.

The Ansar's response to the Prophet's ﷺ speech — the tears and the declaration that he was their share — is one of the most moving moments in the entire seerah. It reveals what the Ansar were: a community that had not fought for wealth but for the Messenger of Allah, and who recognized, when he named it plainly, that going home with him was the greater portion.

## Malik ibn Awf's Return

The Prophet ﷺ also personally addressed the case of Malik ibn Awf al-Nasri, the young commander who had led the Hawazin confederacy and made the disastrous decision to bring the families and livestock to battle. Malik had fled to Taif after the defeat and taken refuge with the Thaqif. The Prophet ﷺ sent word that if Malik came to him as a Muslim, he would return his family and property and give him a hundred camels. Malik came, accepted Islam, and was honored by the Prophet ﷺ who appointed him over his own people — a gesture of trust and leadership that Malik honored by becoming a fierce defender of the Muslim community's southern frontier in subsequent campaigns. His story is a further dimension of the Hunayn distribution: the Prophet ﷺ not only freed captives and gave generously to the mu'allafat qulubuhum but restored leadership to a defeated enemy who had come to Islam, demonstrating that conversion and trust could turn opponents into allies with extraordinary speed.
