# Iran-Iraq War

## Origins and Context

The Iran-Iraq War (September 22, 1980 - August 8, 1988 CE / 1400-1408 AH) was one of the longest and most destructive conventional wars of the twentieth century. Fought between two Muslim-majority nations with combined populations exceeding 60 million, it produced an estimated 500,000 to 1,000,000 dead and left both countries economically devastated. For the Muslim world, the war was a catastrophe without winners — Muslim lives and Muslim blood spent in a conflict that served neither Islamic unity nor the legitimate interests of either people.

Saddam Hussein's Ba'athist Iraq invaded Iran on September 22, 1980, citing multiple pretexts: a territorial dispute over the Shatt al-Arab waterway (the boundary between the two countries at the confluence of the Tigris and Euphrates), Iranian support for Iraqi Kurdish and Shia opposition movements, and Iran's failure to implement the 1975 Algiers Agreement that had settled the border dispute. But the deeper motivation was strategic fear: Ayatollah Khomeini had publicly called on Iraqi Shia Muslims to rise up against Saddam's secular Ba'athist government, and the revolutionary Islamic Republic — with its explicitly Shia character — threatened to inspire Iraq's Shia majority (approximately 60% of Iraq's population).

Saddam calculated that the revolutionary government was weak and disorganized, its military purged of professional officers, and that a quick war would restore Iraqi territorial claims, eliminate the threat of Shia revolutionary contagion, and establish Iraq as the dominant power in the Persian Gulf region. The calculation proved catastrophically wrong.

## The Course of the War

Iran's initial military collapse was real. The purges of the Shah's officer corps and the political chaos of the early revolutionary period had weakened Iran's conventional military capacity. Iraqi forces made initial gains, capturing the Iranian port city of Khorramshahr after weeks of brutal urban combat.

But the Revolutionary Guards (Sepah-e Pasdaran) and the Basij — a paramilitary volunteer force mobilized by the Islamic Republic — proved formidable fighters motivated by revolutionary and religious conviction. Khorramshahr was recaptured by Iran in May 1982 after fierce fighting that gave the city its wartime name: "City of Blood" (Khoninshahr). By 1982, Iran had driven Iraqi forces largely back to the pre-war border.

Rather than negotiate a settlement from this stronger position, Ayatollah Khomeini decided to continue the war with the objective of overthrowing Saddam Hussein and establishing an Islamic government in Iraq — a decision that prolonged the war by six years and added hundreds of thousands of deaths on both sides. Iran launched major offensives attempting to capture Basra, Iraq's second city and a predominantly Shia area. None succeeded in taking the city, though fighting in the Basra region caused catastrophic losses.

## Chemical Weapons

Iraq's use of chemical weapons was the most morally reprehensible aspect of a morally catastrophic war. Facing Iranian human-wave infantry attacks that threatened to overwhelm Iraqi positions through sheer numbers, Saddam's military turned to chemical weapons — mustard gas and nerve agents — against both Iranian soldiers and Kurdish civilians.

The Halabja chemical attack of March 16-19, 1988 — in which Iraqi forces bombed the Kurdish city of Halabja with a combination of mustard gas, sarin, tabun, and VX — killed an estimated 3,000-5,000 civilians immediately, with thousands more dying from long-term effects. Women, children, and the elderly died in the streets of a Kurdish city in northern Iraq. The attack remains the largest chemical weapons massacre against civilians in history. The international community's response was muted — Western governments, including the United States, were supporting Iraq against Iran and did not impose meaningful consequences.

Iran documented Iraqi chemical attacks on its soldiers extensively. The long-term health consequences for Iranian veterans — including cancers, respiratory diseases, and neurological damage — affected tens of thousands for decades after the war.

## International Alignments

The Iran-Iraq War produced a remarkable international alignment. The United States, having broken relations with Iran following the hostage crisis (1979-1981), provided intelligence, satellite imagery, and diplomatic support to Iraq. The Reagan administration removed Iraq from its terrorism list in 1982 to facilitate this support. American companies, with government approval, exported biological and chemical precursors that contributed to Iraq's chemical weapons program — a fact subsequently documented by US Senate and judicial investigations.

Saudi Arabia and the Gulf states (Kuwait, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain) provided approximately $50-$60 billion in financial support to Iraq — loans that Saddam later claimed were grants, a dispute that contributed to the 1990 Kuwait invasion. The Gulf states feared revolutionary Iran's Shia ideology far more than Saddam's secular Ba'athism.

France supplied Iraq with Mirage F1 aircraft and Exocet missiles. The Soviet Union, Iran's patron during the Shah's era, also supplied weapons to Iraq. Iran was relatively isolated — receiving significant weapons only from Syria (which backed Iran against its Ba'athist rival in Baghdad) and small-scale supplies from various other sources including, notoriously, the United States itself through the Iran-Contra affair (1986), in which Reagan administration officials secretly sold arms to Iran and diverted proceeds to Nicaraguan Contras.

## The Tanker War and Gulf Attacks

From 1984 onward, the conflict extended to the Persian Gulf in what became known as the "Tanker War." Iraq attacked Iranian oil terminals and tankers; Iran retaliated against Gulf state shipping supporting Iraq. Kuwait, whose oil exports supported Iraq, requested American naval protection in 1987. The US reflagged Kuwaiti tankers and deployed a naval task force to the Gulf. In July 1988, the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, killing all 290 civilians aboard, having misidentified the commercial aircraft as a military target. The US government paid compensation but did not apologize.

## UN Ceasefire and End of War

UN Security Council Resolution 598 (July 20, 1987) called for an immediate ceasefire. Iraq accepted; Iran initially refused, still pursuing its objective of overthrowing Saddam. After Iran's military setbacks in 1988 — losing territory in the Faw Peninsula and elsewhere — Khomeini agreed to accept the ceasefire. He described the decision in famously painful terms: "accepting this is more deadly for me than taking poison. I submit myself to God's will and drink this cup of poison for His satisfaction."

The ceasefire took effect on August 8, 1988. Final prisoner exchanges were completed slowly over subsequent years. No territorial changes resulted from the war — the Shatt al-Arab boundary reverted to the pre-war demarcation. The war was fought to no conclusion. Every life lost — estimated at 300,000+ Iranian and 250,000+ Iraqi dead, with many sources estimating higher — was spent for nothing gained.

## The Human Cost and the Islamic Perspective

The Iran-Iraq War's death toll placed it among the deadliest conflicts of the twentieth century's second half. The Iranian "Sacred Defense" (Defa'-e Moqaddas) narrative — in which the war is portrayed as a defense of the Islamic Republic against aggression — became central to the Islamic Republic's identity and commemorative culture. Tens of thousands of Iranian graves in cemeteries across the country testify to the scale of loss.

From a Sunni Islamic perspective, the war represented a profound tragedy. Two Muslim nations — each claiming Islamic legitimacy for their governments — fought each other for eight years in a conflict that killed hundreds of thousands of Muslims, devastated two Muslim countries, consumed resources that could have served Muslim welfare, and strengthened non-Muslim powers' leverage over both. The hadith of the Prophet ﷺ — "A Muslim is the brother of a Muslim: he does not wrong him, nor does he abandon him" (Muslim) — is precisely what the Iran-Iraq War violated on a massive scale.

Classical Islamic scholars, including Ibn Taymiyyah and others, addressed the prohibition on Muslims killing one another except in cases of clear and established necessity. The Iran-Iraq War could not be squared with this Islamic framework. It was, from any Islamic perspective, a catastrophe that should never have occurred.

## Legacy

The Iran-Iraq War's legacy shaped the subsequent history of the Middle East profoundly. Iraq emerged from the war deeply indebted to Gulf states, which Saddam used as justification for demanding debt forgiveness and accusing Kuwait of overproducing oil to depress prices. This dispute led directly to Iraq's invasion of Kuwait on August 2, 1990, which in turn brought American forces into the Arabian Peninsula and initiated a chain of events whose consequences continue to define the region.

Iran emerged from the war with its revolutionary identity consolidated — the "Sacred Defense" became the foundational myth of the Islamic Republic's survival and legitimacy — and with battle-hardened revolutionary forces and extensive experience in guerrilla and irregular warfare. The Islamic Republic's geopolitical orientation toward building armed proxy networks in neighboring countries was shaped significantly by the isolation and existential threat Iran experienced during the war.
