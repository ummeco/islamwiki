# Prophet Nuh (Peace Be Upon Him)

## The Second Father of Humanity

Nuh (Noah, peace be upon him) holds a station in Islamic history that parallels and complements that of Adam. He is called the "second father of humanity" by the classical scholars because the Great Flood wiped out all of his people, and every human being alive after that cataclysm descended from the survivors on his ark. The Quran names Nuh among the five greatest prophets — the Ulu al-'Azm, the Prophets of Firm Resolve — alongside Ibrahim, Musa, 'Isa, and Muhammad ﷺ. An entire surah of the Quran bears his name, and his story is recounted in multiple surahs with varying emphasis, each illuminating a different dimension of his trial and his faith.

## A People Drowned in Idolatry

Nuh was sent to a people who had fallen into the worship of idols — not in ignorance, but through a process of deliberate theological corruption. The Quran records the names of the five primary idols they worshipped: Wadd, Suwa', Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr. (Quran 71:23) Classical tafsir scholars, drawing on the hadith reported by Ibn Abbas and recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, explain the origin of these idols in a tradition that is noted as Isra'iliyyat (reports transmitted through Jewish and earlier prophetic traditions, accepted here for contextual understanding but not as primary Islamic doctrine): these were the names of righteous men from an earlier generation. When they died, Shaytan inspired their people to erect statues in their memory at their gathering places. The generation that erected the statues did not worship them — they were memorials. But as time passed, the knowledge of their original purpose was lost, and later generations came to worship them as deities.

This is the mechanism the Quran warns against: beginning with what seems like veneration of the righteous and ending in shirk (polytheism). The lesson is embedded in Nuh's mission — tawhid must be actively protected in every generation, or it erodes.

## Nine Hundred and Fifty Years of Calling

The length of Nuh's mission staggers the imagination. The Quran states explicitly: "And We certainly sent Nuh to his people, and he remained among them a thousand years less fifty years, and the flood seized them while they were wrongdoers." (Quran 29:14) Nine hundred and fifty years of continuous preaching to a people who refused to believe.

The Quran, in Surah Nuh, preserves Nuh's own account of his efforts — an extraordinary record of prophetic perseverance. He called them publicly and privately, by day and by night, openly and in secret. "Then I said, seek forgiveness of your Lord. Indeed, He is ever Forgiving. He will send rain to you in abundance and give you an increase in wealth and children and provide for you gardens and provide for you rivers." (Quran 71:10-12) He appealed to their reason, their self-interest, their observation of the natural world, and their need for divine mercy — and still they refused, covering their faces with their garments and persisting in arrogance.

The Quran records that the powerful and wealthy among his people led the rejection. They did not want a message that would place them on equal footing with the poor and the slaves who had accepted. "But the eminent among those who disbelieved from his people said: We do not see you but as a man like ourselves, and we do not see you followed except by those who are the lowest of us." (Quran 11:27) The sociological pattern — elites resisting prophets because prophethood challenges their supremacy — runs through the entire Quranic narrative of prophetic history.

## The Great Supplication and the Command to Build

After centuries of preaching, the moment of divine judgment arrived. Allah revealed to Nuh: "None of your people will believe except those who have already believed, so do not be distressed by what they have been doing. And construct the ship under Our observation and Our inspiration and do not address Me concerning those who have wronged; indeed, they are to be drowned." (Quran 11:36-37)

The supplication that Nuh made across those nine centuries is preserved in Surah Nuh (Chapter 71) — a supplication of exhausted, faithful endurance. He documented his methods: "I called my people by night and by day, but my calling did not increase them except in flight." (Quran 71:5-6) And at the end, when all human means had been exhausted, he called upon Allah as only a prophet can: "My Lord, do not leave upon the earth from among the disbelievers an inhabitant. Indeed, if You leave them, they will mislead Your servants and not beget except wicked disbelievers." (Quran 71:26-27)

This supplication — comprehensive, deliberate, and issued only after Allah Himself had closed the door on the disbelievers — was accepted. It became both a du'a recorded in the Quran for all believers and a precedent for understanding that a prophet's curse against the persistently unjust is not a deviation from mercy but an expression of divine justice.

Nuh built the ark, and his people mocked him as he built it. The Quran records: "And whenever an assembly of the eminent of his people passed by him, they ridiculed him. He said: If you ridicule us, then we will ridicule you just as you ridicule." (Quran 11:38) He continued building, unshaken.

## The Flood and the Rejection of His Son

When the command came, the Quran describes it in terms of cosmic scale: "So We opened the gates of the heaven with rain pouring down, and caused the earth to burst with springs, and the waters met for a matter already predestined." (Quran 54:11-12) Allah commanded Nuh to take on board a pair of every animal and his believing family and those who had believed — and the number of believers, after nine hundred and fifty years, was small.

Among those who did not board was Nuh's own son, whom the Quran refers to as being "from another family" in the deepest sense. Nuh, seeing him, called out in anguish: "O my son, come aboard with us and be not with the disbelievers." The son replied: "I will take refuge on a mountain to protect me from the water." Nuh said: "There is no protector today from the decree of Allah except for whom He gives mercy." (Quran 11:42-43) A wave came between them, and the son was among those who drowned.

Nuh, in his grief, called out to his Lord, invoking the promise that had been made to him about his family. Allah corrected him with profound gentleness and firmness: "O Nuh, indeed he was not of your family; indeed he was the product of bad deeds, so ask Me not for that about which you have no knowledge. Indeed, I advise you, lest you be among the ignorant." (Quran 11:46) Nuh immediately sought Allah's forgiveness and acknowledged the boundary: "My Lord, I seek refuge in You from asking that of which I have no knowledge. And unless You forgive me and have mercy upon me, I will be among the losers." (Quran 11:47)

The lesson is among the most important in Quranic theology: lineage does not save. A prophet's son is not saved by being a prophet's son. Faith is the only currency that matters before Allah.

## After the Flood — Repopulating the Earth

When the water receded, the ark came to rest on the mountain of al-Judi, as the Quran states. (Quran 11:44) The survivors disembarked onto an earth that had been cleansed and renewed. From the children of Nuh's believing sons — Shem, Ham, and Japheth — the peoples of the post-flood world descended. Classical Islamic scholars of genealogy trace the broad lineages of humanity to these three sons, which is why Nuh holds the title "the second father of humanity" (Abi al-Bashar al-Thani).

The honor accorded to Nuh on the Day of Judgment reflects this status. In the great hadith of intercession recorded in both Bukhari and Muslim — the hadith of the shafa'a — the gathered people approach the prophets one by one, asking each to intercede with Allah for the commencement of the Judgment. They approach Adam, who directs them onward. They come to Nuh. The hadith records that Nuh will say: "My Lord has become angry today with an anger the like of which He has never become angry before, nor will again. And I had a du'a that I used." Nuh, who called for the destruction of the disbelievers with his supplication in this world, will on that Day direct the people to Ibrahim rather than intercede for them himself — a detail that underlines the weight of the supplication he made and his continuing awareness of the boundary between his role and the role reserved for the final intercessor, Muhammad ﷺ.
