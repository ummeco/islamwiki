# The Persecution of the Early Muslims

## The Quraysh Response

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ began preaching Islam publicly in Mecca, the Quraysh establishment moved swiftly against him and his followers. The opposition was not merely religious disagreement — it was an existential response to a message that threatened the social, political, and economic foundations of Meccan society. The Ka'bah's 360 idols generated pilgrimage revenue that sustained Mecca's economy. The tribal hierarchy that Islam challenged was the basis of political power. The Prophet's ﷺ message — that all people were equal before Allah regardless of tribe, wealth, or social status — was a revolutionary declaration in a society built on inherited privilege.

The Quraysh leadership could not immediately lay hands on the Prophet ﷺ himself, because he was protected by his uncle Abu Talib and the honor of the Banu Hashim. But those who had converted without powerful clan protection — slaves, freedmen, poor people, and anyone from minor or allied clans — were entirely vulnerable. The persecution of these vulnerable Muslims was brutal, systematic, and sustained.

## The Martyrs

**Sumayyah bint Khayyat** became the first martyr of Islam. She was the mother of Ammar ibn Yasir, a companion of the Prophet ﷺ. She was an elderly slave woman, and her clan masters — the Banu Makhzum, under Abu Jahl (Amr ibn Hisham) — tortured her and her family with extreme cruelty. They were dragged to the hot sands of Mecca at midday, where metal armor was placed on them and they were left to bake. When the Prophet ﷺ passed and saw them being tortured, he said: "Patience, O family of Yasir! Your appointment is Paradise." Sumayyah was killed by Abu Jahl himself — making her the first martyr of Islam, and her husband Yasir ibn Amir became the second.

Their son Ammar ibn Yasir endured torture as well and was eventually permitted to voice a false statement of disbelief under extreme duress — after which the Quranic verse was revealed: "Whoever disbelieves in Allah after his belief — except one who is forced while his heart is secure in faith..." (Surah al-Nahl, 16:106). This verse established the Islamic principle that verbal statements made under mortal coercion do not constitute genuine apostasy when the heart remains firm.

**Bilal ibn Rabah** was an Abyssinian slave owned by Umayyah ibn Khalaf of the Banu Jumah clan. When Umayyah discovered Bilal had accepted Islam, he subjected him to torture that was designed to kill the spirit before the body: Bilal was placed on the scorching sand under the midday Meccan sun with a large rock placed on his chest to prevent breathing freely. He was given the choice: renounce Islam or die. Bilal's response was: "Ahad, Ahad" — "One, One" — the declaration of Allah's unity. He would not recant. Abu Bakr, witnessing this torture, paid Umayyah his price and freed Bilal immediately. Bilal would later become the first muadhdhin (caller to prayer) of Islam, his powerful voice resonating across Medina.

**Khabbab ibn al-Aratt** was a blacksmith and slave who was tortured with hot coals — made to lie on them until they were extinguished by the touch of his body. He survived and became a respected companion, later reporting: "We complained to the Prophet ﷺ of our suffering while he was in the shade of the Ka'bah. We said: Will you not pray for us? He sat upright, his face reddened, and said: Among those before you, a man would be placed in a pit, a saw placed on his head, and cut in two — and this would not cause him to abandon his religion.'"

## The Social and Economic Boycott

For Muslims with some clan protection — particularly from minor clans — the persecution took the form of social and economic exclusion: traders refused to deal with them, neighbors abandoned them, families expelled them. Some were imprisoned by their own families. Others were stripped of their possessions and livelihoods.

The prophet ﷺ himself was protected by Abu Talib's pledge of clan honor — which meant that to kill the Prophet ﷺ, the Quraysh would have had to face the blood debt of the entire Banu Hashim. This protection was tribal, not religious; Abu Talib never became Muslim, but his code of honor was absolute. The Quraysh repeatedly petitioned him to withdraw this protection or hand over the Prophet ﷺ. Abu Talib refused each time, even when facing the serious political and personal costs.

## The Wisdom of Patient Endurance

The Prophet's ﷺ instruction to his companions during this period of persecution was consistent: patience (sabr), prayer, and endurance. He did not authorize armed resistance in Mecca — there were too few Muslims, and the community was not yet in a position to defend itself militarily. Instead, the Quranic revelations of this period repeatedly called on the Muslims to have patience and trust in Allah, promising that those who endured oppression for their faith would be rewarded in the next life.

The Quranic accounts of previous prophets and their persecuted followers — Nuh, Ibrahim, Musa, and 'Isa — were revealed in this period as both comfort and precedent: persecution of those who brought the truth was the historical norm, and Allah's promise of vindication was equally historical. Every prophet had been opposed; every prophet had ultimately prevailed.
