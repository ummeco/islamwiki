# Algerian War of Independence

## Algeria Under French Colonization

France invaded Algeria in June 1830, ostensibly to suppress piracy and avenge a diplomatic insult to the French consul. The true motivations were political — a failing French monarchy seeking foreign distraction — and imperial. The conquest was brutal and protracted. Algerian resistance, most famously led by Amir Abd al-Qadir (1808-1883), a scholar and military leader who drew on Islamic principles of legitimate defense, lasted until 1847. Abd al-Qadir's resistance became a touchstone of Islamic and Algerian identity for subsequent generations.

France declared Algeria not a colony but an integral part of France itself — a legal fiction that would have profound consequences. European settlers, primarily French, Spanish, and Italian, emigrated to Algeria over the following century, coming to number approximately one million by the 1950s. These colons, or pieds-noirs, owned the best agricultural land, monopolized commerce, and occupied political office, while the indigenous Muslim majority — approximately nine million Algerians — were consigned to second-class legal status under the Code de l'Indigénat, which denied them full French citizenship and subjected them to special restrictions.

The French colonial project in Algeria was among the most thoroughgoing in the history of European imperialism. It dismantled traditional Islamic educational institutions, imposed French as the language of administration and education, expropriated land on a vast scale, and systematically subordinated the indigenous population socially, economically, and politically. By the mid-twentieth century, adult male Algerian Muslims had a literacy rate in French of approximately 13%.

## Islamic Identity and the 'Ulama: Building Resistance

Despite French efforts to suppress Islamic institutions, Islamic identity proved the most durable basis of Algerian resistance. In 1931, Sheikh Abd al-Hamid Ibn Badis founded the Association of Algerian Muslim Scholars (Jam'iyyat al-'Ulama' al-Muslimin al-Jaza'iriyin), commonly known as the Ben Badis Association. Ibn Badis's famous statement — "Islam is my religion, Arabic is my language, Algeria is my homeland" — articulated an Islamic-Arab identity that directly challenged French assimilationist ideology.

For two decades before the outbreak of the war, the Ben Badis Association ran Quranic schools, taught Arabic to generations of Algerians, published newspapers and journals, and maintained an Islamic intellectual culture under French pressure. This cultural resistance created the social foundation and Islamic consciousness that would later fuel and sustain the armed uprising. The 'ulama did not lead the FLN (Front de Libération Nationale), but their decades of Islamic education had cultivated the popular will that made the revolution possible.

## Outbreak of the War: November 1, 1954

The FLN launched coordinated attacks against French military and government installations across Algeria in the early hours of November 1, 1954 / 1374 AH — All Saints Day, chosen deliberately as a symbolic act of defiance against the Catholic colonial power. The attacks were limited in scope but enormous in political significance. The FLN's proclamation called for "the restoration of the Algerian state, sovereign, democratic, and social, within the framework of the principles of Islam."

The FLN emerged from several strands of Algerian nationalism. Its leadership included figures such as Ahmed Ben Bella, Hocine Aït Ahmed, Belkacem Krim, Larbi Ben M'hidi, Mostefa Ben Boulaïd, Mohamed Boudiaf, and Mohamed Khider — the nine "historic leaders" of the revolution. The movement was broadly nationalist and Islamic in orientation, with socialist and pan-Arab influences.

## French Military Response and Documented Atrocities

France's response to the uprising was enormous and ferocious. By 1958, France had deployed more than 400,000 soldiers in Algeria. The French strategy included relocation of rural populations into "regroupment centers" — a policy that displaced an estimated two million Algerians from their villages between 1955 and 1962, destroying traditional agricultural communities and creating a massive internal refugee crisis.

The French military employed systematic torture as a counterinsurgency tool, a fact documented contemporaneously by French journalists and subsequently by soldiers, officers, and government officials. Henri Alleg, a French Algerian journalist, was tortured by French paratroopers and published his account, *La Question* (1958), which became an international scandal. General Paul Aussaresses later publicly confessed to torture and summary executions in his memoir *Services Spéciaux: Algérie 1955-1957* (2001). French journalist Gilles Perrault and historian Pierre Vidal-Naquet extensively documented the practice.

The overall human cost of the Algerian War is estimated at 1.5 million Algerians killed over eight years (1954-1962) — a figure endorsed by Algerian state sources, though some Western historians estimate lower figures. This represents one of the highest per-capita casualty rates of any 20th-century conflict. The scale of French violence — including napalm, collective punishment of villages, mass internment, and systematic torture — has been recognized by subsequent French governments, though France did not formally acknowledge the war as a war (as opposed to a "police operation") until 1999.

## The Battle of Algiers

The Battle of Algiers (1956-1957) was the most famous episode of the war — the FLN's urban guerrilla campaign within the capital, and the French paratrooper response. The FLN's networks in the Casbah (the old Arab quarter of Algiers) launched bombings in European areas of the city. France deployed the elite 10th Paratroop Division under General Jacques Massu, who was given sweeping powers of arrest.

The French destroyed the FLN's Algiers network through a combination of intelligence gathering and systematic torture of detainees. FLN commanders Ali la Pointe, Larbi Ben M'hidi (who was executed after capture), and Yacef Saadi (captured) were the key figures. The French won the tactical battle of Algiers but at a severe political cost — the exposure of torture as official policy turned international opinion against France and alienated French public opinion at home. Gillo Pontecorvo's 1966 film *The Battle of Algiers* recreated these events with remarkable historical fidelity and became one of the most influential political films in history.

## The International Dimension

The Algerian War was internationalized from its earliest stages. The FLN established a government-in-exile (Gouvernement Provisoire de la République Algérienne, GPRA) in Tunis in 1958. Egypt under Nasser provided support, weapons, and diplomatic backing. The Arab League supported the Algerian cause. Newly independent African and Asian states, meeting at the Bandung Conference (1955) and in UN forums, championed Algerian independence.

The United Nations General Assembly debated Algeria repeatedly. France, insisting Algeria was an internal matter, resisted internationalization, but the cause gained overwhelming support from the growing bloc of newly independent states. The United States, despite its Cold War alliance with France and France's membership in NATO, faced growing pressure to distance itself from French colonialism.

## The Evian Accords and Independence

Peace negotiations between the GPRA and the French government under President Charles de Gaulle — who had returned to power partly on the strength of military support in Algeria, then pivoted to pursue negotiated independence — culminated in the Evian Accords of March 18, 1962. The accords recognized Algerian sovereignty, guaranteed civil rights for the European minority, and established transitional arrangements. A referendum held on July 1, 1962, produced a 99.7% vote for independence. Algeria formally declared independence on July 5, 1962 — the 132nd anniversary of the French invasion.

Ahmed Ben Bella became Algeria's first Prime Minister and later President. The transition was tumultuous: within months of independence, the OAS (Organisation de l'Armée Secrète), a French settler extremist organization, carried out a scorched-earth campaign destroying infrastructure and institutions, seeking to render Algeria ungovernable. Most of the one million European settlers (pieds-noirs) left Algeria within months of independence, an exodus of extraordinary scale.

The harkis — Algerians who had served with the French military and administration — faced a catastrophic fate. An estimated 70,000-150,000 harkis were killed in reprisal killings after independence. France largely abandoned those who had not already evacuated, leaving them to their fate.

## Aftermath and Legacy

The Algerian War left profound wounds in both Algerian and French society. Algeria's FLN government ruled as a one-party state for three decades, pursuing socialist and Arab nationalist policies. The oil wealth of the Sahara, discovered during the war years, funded the state. The deep social disruption of 132 years of colonialism — the destruction of Islamic educational institutions, the severing of ties with classical Arabic learning, the urbanization of a formerly rural population — created a fractured society.

The 1990s saw the emergence of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS), which won the first round of parliamentary elections in December 1991 before the military cancelled the elections and banned the party. The resulting civil war (1992-2002), known as the "Black Decade," killed an estimated 100,000-200,000 Algerians in an extraordinarily brutal conflict between the military state and Islamist insurgent groups.

The Algerian revolution remains a defining reference in Islamic and Third World political thought — a demonstration that colonized Muslim peoples could successfully resist even the most powerful European military through sustained popular struggle grounded in Islamic identity and national consciousness.

## Islamic Significance

The Algerian War demonstrated several things of enduring significance for Islamic political thought. First, Islamic identity and Arabic language — maintained through the 'ulama's grassroots educational work against French suppression — proved more durable than French assimilation policies. Second, the principle of Islamic solidarity was invoked across the Muslim world in support of the Algerian cause. Third, the scholars' role in building the cultural consciousness that preceded and sustained the political revolution demonstrated the importance of Islamic education as a foundation for communal identity and resistance. Ibn Badis's legacy — that a colonized Muslim people could sustain their Islamic and Arab identity through education and institutional building — influenced Islamic movements throughout the postcolonial world.
