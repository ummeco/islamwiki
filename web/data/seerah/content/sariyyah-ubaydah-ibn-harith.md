# Expedition of Ubaydah ibn al-Harith to Rabiigh

The expedition of Ubaydah ibn al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib to the valley of Rabiigh (near the Red Sea coastal route) in Shawwal 1 AH (approximately March 623 CE) was one of the earliest armed expeditions sent out by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) from Medina. It is historically notable as the occasion of the first arrows exchanged between Muslims and Quraysh — making it a watershed moment in the transition from the purely non-violent phase of the early Muslim community to the armed conflict that would characterize the Medinan period.

## Ubaydah ibn al-Harith

Ubaydah ibn al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib was from the same Banu Hashim clan as the Prophet and was among the earliest Muslims. He was a cousin of the Prophet (as al-Harith was one of the Prophet's paternal uncles). He was known for his piety and courage. He would later die at the Battle of Badr — the first senior companion to die in that battle.

## The Expedition

The Prophet sent Ubaydah with 60 (or 80, in some accounts) Muhajirun — emigrants from Mecca — toward the valley of Rabiigh, which lay along the Red Sea coastal route south of the Eis area. A Qurayshi force under Abu Sufyan ibn Harb was in the area, reportedly numbering 200 fighters.

The two forces encountered each other at a watering place in the Rabiigh area.

## The First Arrows

No general battle was fought. However, the encounter produced what the Islamic historical tradition records as the first exchange of arrows in the Muslim-Quraysh conflict:

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas — who was part of the expedition — shot an arrow at the Qurayshi force. This is recorded by Ibn Saad and Ibn Hisham as "the first arrow shot in the path of Allah" — the first Muslim armed act against the enemy. Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas took pride in this his entire life, mentioning it in narrations preserved in the hadith literature.

The exchange remained at the arrow level; no general engagement followed, and the Qurayshi force eventually withdrew. Miqdad ibn al-Aswad, who participated in the expedition, also drew a sword during the encounter, though the fight did not escalate.

## The White Flag

Ibn Hisham reports that the Prophet gave Ubaydah a white flag for this expedition — the first military banner in Islamic history. The use of flags and banners in subsequent Muslim military operations became standard practice, and the white flag of this first expedition is sometimes cited as the origin of the practice of carrying banners into battle under Muslim military custom.

## Significance

**The transition to armed resistance.** The expedition of Ubaydah, like the near-simultaneous expedition of Hamzah, marks the formal beginning of the Muslim community's armed resistance to Qurayshi hostility. The Prophet had spent twelve years in Mecca without authorizing armed response to persecution; the hijra to Medina and the establishment of a political community there changed the community's legal and political situation and enabled a military response.

**Sa'd's first arrow.** Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas's pride in having shot the first arrow "in the path of Allah" reflects the companions' understanding of these early expeditions as the beginning of a jihad that would eventually transform Arabia. Sa'd went on to become one of the great generals of the early Islamic conquests, commanding the Muslim army at the decisive Battle of al-Qadisiyyah that broke the Sassanid Persian Empire.

**Ubaydah's subsequent martyrdom.** Ubaydah ibn al-Harith was wounded at the Battle of Badr and died of his wounds, becoming one of the first martyrs of that defining battle. His leadership of the early Rabiigh expedition and his death at Badr gave him a place among the founding generation of Muslim soldiers.

## Sources

The expedition is documented in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya* (drawing on Ibn Ishaq), al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*.
