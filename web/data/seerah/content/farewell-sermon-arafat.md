# The Farewell Sermon at Mount Arafat

On the ninth of Dhul Hijjah, 10 AH (9 March 632 CE), the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) delivered what would become the most consequential public address in Islamic history — a comprehensive moral and legal charter for the Muslim community, delivered from the plain of Arafat before an audience of tens of thousands.

## The Setting

The Farewell Pilgrimage (Hajjat al-Wada) drew an enormous assembly. Estimates from early sources range from 90,000 to 124,000 companions accompanying the Prophet. After mounting his she-camel al-Qaswa near the rocks of Arafat and after midday prayer, the Prophet delivered his sermon while Rabi'ah ibn Umayyah ibn Khalaf repeated his words in a loud voice so those further back could hear.

## The Content of the Sermon

The sermon's core themes constitute a framework for Islamic civilization:

**The Sanctity of Life and Property.** The Prophet declared: "Your blood, your property, and your honor are as sacred and inviolable as this day of yours, in this month of yours, in this city of yours." The comparison to the sanctity of Mecca, of the sacred month, of the Day of Arafat — all simultaneously present — made the point unmistakable. The pre-Islamic (jahiliyyah) custom of blood feuds was abolished. "Every claim of blood from the pre-Islamic period is abolished (maudu')."

**Abolition of Usury.** "All usury of the pre-Islamic period is abolished. You shall have back your principal, without wronging or being wronged." The Prophet specifically renounced the usurious debts owed to his own uncle al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib — signaling that no one was exempt.

**Rights of Women.** "Fear Allah in the matter of women. You have rights over them and they have rights over you." This bidirectional articulation of marital rights, while using the legal categories of 7th-century Arabia, represented a significant step toward acknowledging women as legal persons with enforceable claims.

**Brotherhood and Racial Equality.** "There is no superiority of an Arab over a non-Arab, nor of a non-Arab over an Arab, nor of a white person over a black person, nor of a black person over a white person, except through taqwa (God-consciousness)." This statement, recorded by Imam Ahmad and al-Baihaqi, directly confronted tribal and racial hierarchies that were central to Arabian social organization.

**The Two Sources of Guidance.** "I am leaving among you two things; if you hold firmly to them, you will never go astray: the Book of Allah and the Sunnah of His Prophet." This declaration established the dual foundation of Islamic jurisprudence and was among the final formal pronouncements on the sources of religious authority.

**Finality of Prophethood.** The Prophet signaled the completeness of the prophetic mission. The revelation delivered that same day on the plain of Arafat confirmed this: "This day I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favor upon you and have approved for you Islam as religion" (Quran 5:3).

## Witnesses and Transmission

The sermon was witnessed by tens of thousands and transmitted through multiple chains of narrators. It was not a single document but a living tradition transmitted through the Companions who attended. Different narrators preserved different portions, which is why hadith collections contain various accounts that complement rather than contradict one another. The sermon is preserved in Sahih Muslim, Sahih Bukhari, the Sunan collections, and the Musnad of Ahmad ibn Hanbal.

The Prophet asked three times: "Have I conveyed the message?" — and the assembly replied each time: "Yes." He then said: "O Allah, bear witness." He directed those present to convey the message to those absent, noting that perhaps those who hear it second-hand will understand it better than those who heard it directly.

## Legacy

The Farewell Sermon is treated in Islamic jurisprudence as one of the foundational documents of Islamic law, morality, and social organization. Its provisions on the inviolability of life, property, and honor, the abolition of pre-Islamic blood feuds and usury, and the equality of human beings across race have informed Islamic ethics across fourteen centuries.

Western scholars, beginning in the 19th century, noted the sermon's content as anticipating many principles articulated in later human rights documents — including the prohibition of arbitrary killing, the protection of property, and the rejection of racial hierarchy. The sermon predates the Magna Carta by six centuries and the French Declaration of the Rights of Man by over eleven.

For Muslims, the sermon represents the Prophet's own summary of his mission — his farewell message to the community he had built, delivered in the place and at the moment that the Quran identified as the completion of the religion.
