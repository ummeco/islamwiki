# The Siege of Ta'if

Following the Battle of Hunayn in Shawwal 8 AH, the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) led the Muslim army in a siege of Ta'if — the mountain city that was the stronghold of the Thaqif tribe, the most powerful tribal confederation remaining in the Hijaz that had not yet submitted to Islam. The siege lasted approximately 15 to 40 days (sources vary) and was ultimately unsuccessful. Ta'if submitted voluntarily approximately a year later, when a delegation of Thaqif came to Medina and accepted Islam.

## Background

Ta'if was a prosperous agricultural city situated on a mountain plateau southeast of Mecca, at an elevation of approximately 1,800 meters. Its cool climate and productive orchards made it the summer resort of Mecca's wealthy merchants. The Thaqif tribe were the city's dominant population and had their own regional significance and pride.

The Thaqif had participated in the Hawazin coalition at Hunayn. After the Muslim victory, Malik ibn Awf al-Nasri (the coalition commander) retreated to Ta'if along with the remnants of his forces. The city had been prepared for a siege, with provisions stored and walls fortified.

## The Siege

The Prophet marched from Hunayn to Ta'if and established a siege camp. The city's walls made a direct assault difficult. The Prophet employed siege equipment that had not been previously used by the Muslim army:

**The manjaniq (catapult/trebuchet):** Large stone-throwing devices were used to hurl projectiles at the city's walls — the first recorded use of such equipment by Muslim forces.

**The dabbabah (siege tower/armored testudo):** An armored approach vehicle was used to protect soldiers advancing toward the walls. When it was deployed, the defenders dropped heated iron bars from the walls onto it, setting it afire and forcing the occupants out.

The defenders of Ta'if fought back effectively. They had ample provisions and high walls. When Muslim soldiers attempted to advance under the walls, archers picked them off. Muslim casualties included approximately 12 killed in the siege.

## The Prophet's Difficulty

The siege was the most technically challenging military operation of the Prophet's career. The Prophet had previously relied on mobility, surprise, and overwhelming numbers rather than siege warfare. The walls of Ta'if blunted these advantages.

During the siege, the Prophet allowed the slaves of Ta'if who escaped and came to the Muslim lines to be freed. He also, during this period, received the escaped slaves of the Thaqif and granted them freedom — a policy that undercut the city's labor force.

## The Withdrawal

After weeks of unsuccessful siege, the Prophet consulted with his companions. Umar ibn al-Khattab and others advised withdrawing. The Prophet agreed, saying: "Then we will leave."

When the soldiers appeared reluctant to leave without taking the city, the Prophet reportedly said: "Then make a final attack tomorrow." When the next attack also failed against the walls, he announced the departure.

The withdrawal from Ta'if was accompanied by a prayer: some companions asked the Prophet to curse the Thaqif. He raised his hands and said: "O Allah, guide the Thaqif and bring them in." — turning the request for a curse into a supplication for their guidance.

## The Subsequent Submission

The prophetic prayer was answered within a year. In 9 AH (the Year of Delegations), a delegation of Thaqif leaders came to Medina and accepted Islam on behalf of their tribe. Their negotiations with the Prophet about the terms of their acceptance were significant in jurisprudential history — they requested exceptions from certain Islamic obligations, which the Prophet declined to grant (except for some practical accommodations), and the final terms required complete submission.

Among the terms the Thaqif sought was the continuation of their famous temple to al-Lat (one of the three major pre-Islamic Arabian goddesses) for three years before its destruction. The Prophet refused. After negotiation, they asked for one year, then one month — all refused. The Thaqif finally accepted that the temple would be destroyed immediately. The Prophet sent Abu Sufyan ibn Harb and al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah to destroy it.

## Significance

**The limits of military force.** The unsuccessful siege of Ta'if demonstrated that the Prophet did not always succeed in his military objectives immediately. The eventual submission of Ta'if came through persuasion and the changed political landscape rather than conquest.

**Siege warfare in Islamic law.** The use of catapults and other siege equipment at Ta'if, authorized by the Prophet, established precedents in Islamic military law for the permissibility of such weapons even when they could not discriminate between combatants and non-combatants.

**The prayer for guidance.** The Prophet's response to requests to curse the Thaqif — turning it into a prayer for their guidance — is cited in Islamic moral teaching as the prophetic model for responding to enemies: seeking their guidance rather than their destruction.

## Sources

The siege of Ta'if is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*.
