# The Year of Sorrow (Aam al-Huzn)

## When Two Deaths Changed Everything

The tenth year of prophethood brought two deaths in close succession that the Prophet ﷺ himself named as constituting a single year of sorrow: Aam al-Huzn. Abu Talib ibn Abd al-Muttalib died first — the uncle who had been the Prophet's ﷺ guardian since he was eight years old, the man whose tribal authority had shielded the entire prophetic mission from physical elimination for over a decade. Within weeks, Khadijah bint Khuwaylid followed — the first Muslim, the Prophet's ﷺ only wife for twenty-five years, the woman who had held him in the darkness of the first revelation and told him that Allah would never disgrace him.

These two deaths, coming in the aftermath of the three-year boycott of Banu Hashim that had already taken a severe physical toll on the community, left the Prophet ﷺ in a position of unprecedented vulnerability. Khadijah had been the domestic anchor of his life and the provider of the material security that allowed him to contemplate and preach. Abu Talib had been the political anchor — the protector whose word had kept the Quraysh from acting against the Prophet ﷺ with violence.

Both anchors were now gone.

## The Transformation of the Situation

The speed with which the Quraysh tested the new situation was remarkable. Within days of Abu Talib's death, the Prophet ﷺ experienced physical abuse in the streets of Mecca — a man threw dirt on his head. The perpetrators were people who would never have dared to touch the Prophet ﷺ while Abu Talib lived. The tribal grammar of Meccan society was entirely clear: with Abu Talib gone, the blood obligation that had protected the Prophet ﷺ was gone too. Unless a new patron could be found, the Prophet ﷺ was available to be harmed.

The Prophet ﷺ walked home from this incident and his daughter cleaned him and wept. He said: "Do not weep, my daughter. Allah will protect your father." But the situation was not theoretical. He had to find a protector.

He approached three of the most powerful leaders of the Quraysh — Suhayl ibn Amr, Mut'im ibn Adi, and others — asking for their tribal protection (jiwar). The first two refused. Mut'im ibn Adi, a man who was not a Muslim but who had been one of the key figures in ending the boycott of Banu Hashim, agreed. He extended his formal protection over the Prophet ﷺ, announced it publicly, and sent his sons with swords to the Kaaba to make the protection visible and credible. The Prophet ﷺ reentered Mecca under Mut'im's protection — a non-Muslim's protection, which was all that stood between him and the violence of the Quraysh.

Years later, after the Battle of Badr, when the question arose of what to do with Qurayshi prisoners, the Prophet ﷺ said: "If Mut'im ibn Adi were alive and interceded for these prisoners, I would have released them all for his sake." Mut'im had been dead for over a year by then. The Prophet ﷺ did not forget what he had done.

## The Search for a New Base

The Year of Sorrow was not only a year of grief — it was a year of transition. The death of Abu Talib and the withdrawal of tribal protection made clear to the Prophet ﷺ that the future of the mission was not in Mecca. Mecca had rejected the message at every social level. Thirteen years of preaching had produced a community of believers, but it had not produced a governing social reality in which Islam could grow. The Quraysh had tried torture, boycott, social ostracism, and now the removal of protection — and through all of it, the Prophet ﷺ had not been able to establish the conditions in which Islam could function as a community.

He needed a new base. He began systematically presenting Islam to tribal delegations and visitors who came to Mecca during the pilgrimage season, asking them to receive him and allow him to preach among them. Tribe after tribe refused — either from lack of interest or from the calculation that accepting the Prophet ﷺ would mean conflict with the Quraysh.

One attempt stood out for its severity: the journey to Taif.

## The Journey to Taif

The Prophet ﷺ traveled to Taif — approximately sixty miles south of Mecca — to approach the Thaqif tribe and their leaders, the three sons of Amr ibn Umayr al-Thaqafi. He asked them to receive him and to protect him so that he could convey the message of Allah. Their responses ranged from scorn to dismissal. One said: "If you are truly a prophet from Allah, you are too important for me to speak with. If you're lying about Allah, then I shouldn't speak with you either." Another said: "Couldn't Allah find anyone better than you?"

As the Prophet ﷺ left Taif, the Thaqif sent their servants and the young men of the city to line the road and throw stones at him until his feet bled and his sandals filled with blood. The Prophet ﷺ stopped at the wall of a garden outside the city and made a prayer — one of the most remarkable prayers in the entire seerah literature.

He said: "O Allah, I complain to You of my weakness, my insufficient means, and my insignificance before people. O Most Merciful of those who show mercy! You are the Lord of the weak, and You are my Lord. To whom will You entrust me? To one far away who receives me with hostility? Or to an enemy to whom You have given power over me? If You are not angry with me, I do not care — but Your favor is the most expansive thing for me. I seek refuge in the light of Your face by which darkness is illuminated and the affairs of this world and the next are set right, that Your wrath should fall upon me or Your anger descend upon me. I will continue to appeal to You until You are pleased with me. There is no power and no strength except through You."

An angel then appeared, the angel of the mountains, offering to collapse the two mountains flanking Taif upon its people if the Prophet ﷺ wished. The Prophet ﷺ declined. He said: "No — I hope that Allah will bring from among their descendants people who will worship Allah alone and not associate anything with Him." The city that had thrown stones at him in 619 CE accepted Islam before the Prophet ﷺ died in 632 CE.

## The Night of the Jinn

On the return journey from Taif, at a place called Nakhla, the Prophet ﷺ prayed the night prayer. A group of jinn heard his recitation of the Quran, listened, and accepted Islam. The Quran records this encounter in Surah al-Ahqaf (46:29-32) and in Surah al-Jinn (72:1-15). The irony was sharp: the humans of Taif had rejected him; the jinn of Nakhla had accepted. The community of believers in Islam extended, from this moment forward, beyond the human world.

## The Nighttime Ascent and the New Horizon

Following the Year of Sorrow — according to the most widely accepted chronological readings, though the precise date is disputed — came the Isra' wal-Mi'raj: the miraculous night journey to Jerusalem and the ascent through the heavens. The proximity of this event to the Year of Sorrow is understood by scholars as a divine mercy: the Prophet ﷺ had endured the deaths of his greatest protectors, the rejection of Taif, and the isolation of Mecca — and Allah responded with the most singular honor given to any human being in any religion: the direct, immediate encounter with the Divine.

The Year of Sorrow, in retrospect, was not the end of the mission's Meccan phase — it was the turn toward the Medinan phase. The deaths of Abu Talib and Khadijah had closed one chapter and forced the opening of another. That other chapter — the migration to Medina, the establishment of the first Islamic state, the battles of Badr and Uhud and the Trench, and the eventual return to Mecca — was the chapter in which Islam became what it has remained ever since: not just a message but a community, not just a teaching but a civilization.
