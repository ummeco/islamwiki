# Death of Imam al-Qurtubi

Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Abi Bakr al-Qurtubi (d. 671 AH / 1273 CE) was one of the foremost Maliki scholars and Quranic exegetes of the medieval period. His monumental tafsir, *al-Jami li-Ahkam al-Quran* (The Comprehensive Collection of the Legal Rulings of the Quran), remains one of the most important and widely used works of Quranic commentary in the Islamic scholarly tradition.

## Life

Al-Qurtubi was born in Cordoba (Qurtuba), the great city of Andalusian civilization, in the early seventh century AH. The date of his birth is uncertain, but he was born before the final Christian conquest of Cordoba by Ferdinand III of Castile in 634 AH (1236 CE). The loss of Cordoba — the intellectual and cultural capital of Islamic Spain — was a devastating blow to the Muslim community of al-Andalus, and al-Qurtubi was among the scholars who emigrated from the city.

He made his way east, eventually settling in Upper Egypt, in the village of Munyat Ibn Khasib (present-day al-Qusayr al-Bahari on the Nile, near Assiut). He spent the remainder of his life there, teaching and writing, until his death in 671 AH (1273 CE).

## Al-Jami li-Ahkam al-Quran

Al-Qurtubi's tafsir is a work of extraordinary comprehensiveness. Running to approximately 20 volumes in modern editions, it approaches the Quran primarily from the perspective of Islamic law (fiqh), though it also addresses linguistic, theological, and historical dimensions.

For every verse or passage that bears legal implications, al-Qurtubi surveys the positions of all four Sunni schools of law (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) as well as significant earlier scholars, explains the reasoning behind each position, and often expresses a preference based on the evidence. This makes it an invaluable comparative legal reference as well as a tafsir.

The work is particularly notable for:

**Completeness.** Al-Qurtubi rarely passes over a significant legal question raised by a verse. His treatment of verses touching on worship, commerce, family law, criminal law, and governance is thorough.

**Hadith citation.** He cites relevant hadith extensively and often comments on their authenticity.

**Linguistic precision.** He attends carefully to the grammatical and lexical dimensions of verses, drawing on the classical Arabic philological tradition.

**Theological engagement.** For theologically significant verses, he engages with the Ash'ari tradition while remaining true to the Maliki school.

## Other Works

Beyond the great tafsir, al-Qurtubi wrote several other significant works:

*al-Tadhkira fi Ahwal al-Mawta wal-Akhira* (Remembrance of the States of the Dead and the Afterlife) — a comprehensive eschatological work describing death, the grave, resurrection, judgment, paradise, and hellfire in detail, drawing on a vast range of hadith and scholarly commentary. This work remains popular and has been reprinted and read widely through the centuries.

*Qam al-Hirs wal-Hawa* — on controlling worldly desires.

*al-Asna fi Sharh Asma Allah al-Husna* — on the names and attributes of Allah.

## Context: The Loss of Al-Andalus

Al-Qurtubi's life spanned one of the most tragic periods for the Muslims of the Iberian Peninsula. By the time of his death in 671 AH, Islamic political control of Iberia had been reduced to the small Nasrid Emirate of Granada in the south. Cordoba, Seville, Valencia, and most of the great cities of al-Andalus had fallen to Christian kingdoms.

The scholarly exodus from al-Andalus that al-Qurtubi exemplified was one channel through which Andalusian intellectual culture — its unique synthesis of Maliki jurisprudence, philosophical sophistication, and literary refinement — was transmitted to North Africa and Egypt, enriching those traditions for generations.

## Legacy

Al-Qurtubi's tafsir is one of the most cited works in classical Quranic scholarship. It is a standard reference for jurists seeking to understand the legal dimensions of Quranic verses and for scholars seeking a comprehensive survey of classical scholarly opinions. The tafsir has been published in numerous editions and is freely available digitally, ensuring its continued use by scholars and students worldwide.

His eschatological work *al-Tadhkira* has been similarly widely read, particularly for its vivid and hadith-grounded descriptions of the afterlife.
