# Umm Salamah's Crucial Advice at Hudaybiyyah

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) was negotiated under conditions that caused deep distress among the Muslim pilgrims who had come to perform Umrah and were turned back at Hudaybiyyah. When the Prophet commanded the companions to sacrifice their animals and end their pilgrim state, they were so grieved and disoriented that they did not immediately respond. It was Umm Salamah — one of the Prophet's wives, traveling with the caravan — who provided the counsel that resolved the impasse.

## The Context

The Muslim caravan of approximately 1,400 pilgrims, intending to perform the Umrah at Mecca, was intercepted at Hudaybiyyah and negotiations ensued with the Quraysh. The resulting treaty's terms were deeply unfavorable in appearance:

- Muslims who came to Mecca as refugees would be returned to Mecca, but Muslims who left Mecca would not be returned to Medina
- No Umrah this year — the pilgrims would have to return to Medina without entering Mecca
- A ten-year truce, but under terms that seemed to disadvantage the Muslims

The Prophet accepted the terms. From a position of apparent moral and spiritual superiority (peaceful pilgrims turned back by armed men), the Muslims were being sent home without performing their pilgrimage. Even senior companions were deeply disturbed. Umar ibn al-Khattab famously expressed his distress to Abu Bakr.

## The Command and the Silence

After the treaty was sealed, the Prophet commanded the companions: "Arise, sacrifice your animals, and shave your heads." No one moved.

He repeated the command. Still no one moved. The Prophet was troubled. No one was actively defying him — they were simply paralyzed by their grief and disorientation. They could not understand how they had come for pilgrimage and were going home having performed none of it.

## Umm Salamah's Advice

The Prophet went to his tent and spoke to Umm Salamah about what had happened. She said:

*"O Prophet of Allah, do you want your command to be carried out? Then go out and do not speak a word to anyone of them until you have slaughtered your camel and called your barber to shave your head."*

The advice was brilliant in its psychological understanding. The companions were not refusing — they were waiting, hoping that something would change, unable to bring themselves to act. Seeing the Prophet himself act — make the sacrifice, shave his head, irrevocably commit to the course of return — would break the spell of hesitation.

## The Outcome

The Prophet followed Umm Salamah's advice. He went out and, without speaking to anyone, sacrificed his camel and called his barber Khirash ibn Umayyah to shave his head. The sight of the Prophet sacrificing and shaving immediately galvanized the companions. They rose and rushed to sacrifice their animals and shave their heads — so many moving at once, in their rush to follow the Prophet's example, that the accounts say they nearly harmed one another.

## The Significance of This Moment

**A woman's counsel saved the crisis.** Umm Salamah's advice was sound, immediately effective, and clearly correct. The Prophet acted on it directly and immediately. This episode is one of the clearest examples in the seerah of the Prophet receiving counsel from a woman, recognizing its wisdom, and acting on it.

**The psychology of leadership.** Umm Salamah's insight — that the companions needed to see the Prophet act, not just hear him command — was a sophisticated understanding of how people in states of emotional shock respond to leadership. Words were not sufficient at that moment; only example would work.

**The Quran's assessment of Hudaybiyyah.** The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, which appeared to be a humiliation at the time, is described by the Quran as "a manifest victory" (Quran 48:1). The following years bore this out: the truce gave the Muslims two years to grow in number and strength, and it was the political recognition of the Muslim state's legitimacy as a treaty partner for the Quraysh that proved most valuable.

## Umm Salamah

Hind bint Abi Umayyah al-Makhzumiyya, known as Umm Salamah, was among the earliest converts to Islam. She had made the first hijra to Abyssinia with her first husband Abu Salamah. When Abu Salamah died after returning to Medina wounded from Uhud, she was widowed with four children. The Prophet married her in 4 AH.

She was known for her intelligence, her independent judgment, and her willingness to offer counsel to the Prophet. She lived until approximately 61 or 62 AH — one of the last of the Prophet's wives to die — and is one of the major transmitters of hadith among the women of the early Muslim community.

## Sources

The account of Umm Salamah's advice at Hudaybiyyah is in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Shurut and Kitab al-Maghazi) and Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*.
