# Abu Sufyan Accepts Islam Before the Conquest of Mecca

Abu Sufyan ibn Harb ibn Umayya (c. 567-653 CE) was for two decades the principal political and military leader of Mecca's opposition to the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) — the de facto general and strategist behind the battles of Uhud and the Trench, and one of the most formidable opponents the early Muslim community faced. His acceptance of Islam in the days immediately before the conquest of Mecca in Ramadan 8 AH stands as one of the most dramatic conversions of the seerah.

## Background

Abu Sufyan was the chief of Banu Umayya, the leading family of Quraysh after Banu Hashim (the Prophet's own family). He was wealthy, politically shrewd, and a gifted military organizer. He led the Meccan forces at Uhud (3 AH) and organized the Confederacy that besieged Medina at the Battle of the Trench (5 AH). He had sustained the Qurayshi opposition to the Prophet for years.

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) had established a ten-year truce. The truce was broken — depending on the account, through an attack by Quraysh allies on Muslims — and the Prophet resolved to march on Mecca.

## The Journey to Medina

As the Muslim army was assembling for the march on Mecca (Ramadan 8 AH), Abu Sufyan traveled to Medina, reportedly seeking to renew the peace terms. His wife Hind bint Utba (who had previously mutilated the body of Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib at Uhud) was not with him on this journey.

Abu Sufyan first went to his daughter Umm Habibah (Ramla bint Abi Sufyan) — who was among the wives of the Prophet — seeking her intercession. According to the account preserved in the seerah, when Abu Sufyan tried to sit on the Prophet's mat, Umm Habibah immediately folded it up, saying it was the Prophet's mat and she did not want her polytheist father to sit on it. Abu Sufyan was visibly stung by this rebuff from his own daughter.

He then went to Abu Bakr and Umar, both of whom declined to intercede on his behalf for the truce renewal. Finally he went to Ali ibn Abi Talib, who told him that the Prophet had made his decision and would not be deflected.

Ali then suggested that Abu Sufyan could go to the mosque, announce that he was giving the people his protection (aman), and thereby return to Mecca. Abu Sufyan did this, announced it to the assembled Muslims, and went to the Prophet. The Prophet did not say whether his announcement constituted a valid aman — the subtlety of Ali's advice being that it returned Abu Sufyan to Mecca without giving him the political intercession he had sought.

## The Acceptance of Islam

As the Muslim army marched toward Mecca, Abu Sufyan was effectively intercepted by the advance forces. He was brought before Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (the Prophet's uncle, who had recently accepted Islam). Abbas brought him to the Prophet's camp and interceded for him.

The Prophet granted him safety and the following morning accepted his Islam. Abu Sufyan pronounced the shahada (testimony of faith) and formally entered Islam. The Prophet then said to Abbas: "Take Abu Sufyan and place him at the narrow part of the valley where the mountains close in, so that the army passes by him and he sees them."

## Witnessing the Army

Abu Sufyan stood with Abbas and watched the Muslim army pass. First came the tribal contingents with their banners, then the Muhajirun and Ansar under their commanders, then the green-turbaned Prophet riding with his close companions. The army was unprecedented in scale — approximately 10,000 men, the largest Muslim force ever assembled.

Abu Sufyan reportedly said to Abbas: "O Abbas, your nephew's kingdom has become great." Abbas replied: "O Abu Sufyan, this is prophethood, not kingdom." — or in some accounts: "Woe to you — say: 'La ilaha illallah.'"

## The Legacy of Abu Sufyan's Conversion

Abu Sufyan's conversion illustrates the complex nature of late acceptance of Islam. The Prophet's closest companions knew that Abu Sufyan's Islam was motivated partly by political calculation and the recognition of inevitable defeat. Yet the Prophet accepted it, established the principle of sincerity toward apparent acceptance, and did not interrogate anyone's inward motives.

The family of Abu Sufyan went on to play enormous roles in Islamic history. His son Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan became the founder of the Umayyad Caliphate (41-132 AH). His daughter Umm Habibah was one of the Prophet's wives. His grandson Yazid ibn Muawiyah's caliphate encompassed the tragic events of Karbala.

Abu Sufyan himself participated in the Battle of Ta'if and other campaigns, reportedly lost an eye at the Battle of Ta'if, and died in 31 or 32 AH.

## Sources

The account of Abu Sufyan's acceptance of Islam is in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and related narrations across the major hadith collections.
