# Abd al-Muttalib Rediscovers the Zamzam Well

## The Well Lost to Time

The Zamzam well is one of the most sacred sites in Islam, flowing continuously since Allah commanded it to spring forth for Hajar and her infant son Ismail after Ibrahim left them in the valley of Mecca. For generations, pilgrims drank from it and it remained the lifeblood of any settlement that could grow in that arid valley. Yet by the time Mecca had grown into a thriving city of trade, Zamzam had been sealed and buried — its precise location forgotten.

The burial of Zamzam occurred during the turbulent centuries between Prophet Ismail and the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Some narrations trace it to the Jurhum tribe who, when expelled from Mecca by the Khuza'ah, buried the well along with treasure and weapons to prevent their enemies from benefiting. Others attribute it to the general encroachment of structures and fill around the Kaaba area over centuries. Whatever the precise cause, by the era of Abd al-Muttalib ibn Hashim — the Prophet's grandfather — Zamzam had been completely sealed under the ground, its memory preserved only in oral tradition.

## The Vision That Changed Everything

Abd al-Muttalib, son of Hashim and grandson of Abd Manaf, was a man of singular honor among Quraysh. He held the twin custodianships of siqaya — providing water to pilgrims — and rifada — feeding those who came for hajj. Yet the water he provided came from other sources. The sacred Zamzam, had it been known, would have been the most fitting source.

According to narrations recorded by Ibn Hisham and Ibn Kathir, Abd al-Muttalib received a vision — in some narrations over multiple consecutive nights — in which a voice commanded him: "Dig Tayyibah." On subsequent nights the voice specified further, calling it "Barrah," then "Madnunah," then finally "Zamzam" — describing its location precisely between the two idols Isaf and Nailah, near the spot where pilgrims slaughtered their sacrificial animals. The description placed it directly in the area between those two well-known landmarks that the Quraysh had erected near the Kaaba.

Abd al-Muttalib awoke from the final vision with firm conviction. He brought his son al-Harith — the only son he had at that time — and began to dig at the indicated spot. The other clans of Quraysh watched with skepticism and even scorn; Abd al-Muttalib had only one son to help him, while the other clan chiefs had large families and retinues. But he persisted.

## The Discovery

When the pick struck the ancient well and water gushed forth, Abd al-Muttalib fell to his knees in gratitude. Along with the water, excavation revealed artifacts from antiquity: golden deer statues, swords, and coats of mail — treasure buried by the Jurhum in their retreat from Mecca. These objects, along with the flowing water, signaled to all that this was indeed the sacred well of Ibrahim and Ismail.

The other clans of Quraysh immediately disputed Abd al-Muttalib's right to be the sole custodian of Zamzam. They argued that as a shared sacred site, all tribes should have joint administration. The dispute was serious enough that they agreed to seek arbitration from a female diviner (kahinah) in the region of the Banu Sa'd. During their journey to this arbitrator, Abd al-Muttalib's group was nearly overcome by thirst in the desert before water miraculously appeared — a sign the others took as confirmation of divine favor toward Abd al-Muttalib. The arbitration ultimately vindicated his custodianship.

## The Vow of One Hundred Camels

Confronted with both the extraordinary nature of his discovery and the opposition he faced doing it alone with only one son, Abd al-Muttalib made a vow: if Allah blessed him with ten sons who reached the age of manhood, he would sacrifice one of them at the Kaaba in gratitude. This vow would shape the next generation of his lineage profoundly.

Allah indeed blessed him with ten sons who reached adulthood. When Abd al-Muttalib cast lots to determine which son to sacrifice, the lot fell upon Abdullah — the most beloved of his sons, and the one who would become the Prophet's father. Abd al-Muttalib prepared to fulfill his vow, but the people of Quraysh implored him not to kill his son. He again sought the counsel of a soothsayer who instructed him to redeem the human sacrifice by casting lots between Abdullah and camels, increasing the number of camels by ten each round until the lot fell on the camels instead. After ten rounds of casting — with the tally reaching one hundred camels — the lot finally fell upon the camels. Abd al-Muttalib sacrificed all one hundred in gratitude, and Abdullah lived.

This event established two significant traditions: the redemption of human sacrifice through animals (which the Prophet ﷺ later confirmed in Islamic law as the principle of aqiqah), and the blood money for a human life set at one hundred camels — a ruling the Prophet ﷺ also upheld.

## The Water of Ibrahim

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ spoke of Zamzam with deep reverence throughout his life. He is reported to have said: "The best water on the face of the earth is Zamzam. In it is nourishment for the hungry and healing for the sick." In another narration: "Zamzam is for whatever one drinks it for." He himself drank from it frequently and used to pour it over his head during ritual washing.

When the Prophet ﷺ was carried on the Night Journey (Isra), Jibril opened his chest and washed his heart with Zamzam water before the journey began — indicating that Zamzam's sacred properties were recognized even in the heavenly realm.

Scholars of seerah understand the rediscovery of Zamzam as one of the divine preparations for the coming of the final Prophet. The lineage of Ibrahim and Ismail was being renewed and restored: the sacred well was brought back to light, the custodianship of the Kaaba was consolidated in the Prophet's grandfather, and the noble lineage was protected through the near-sacrifice and redemption of Abdullah. All these events formed a providential context for the birth that would follow.

## The Legacy of Zamzam

The well has flowed without interruption for over 4,000 years by the account of Islamic tradition, and it continues to flow today. Scientific analyses have found that Zamzam water possesses a uniquely high mineral content and lacks any biological contamination, despite the millions of pilgrims who come into contact with it annually. It is the only water on earth that carries the specific du'a (supplication) of millions who drink it with spiritual intention — an ongoing fulfillment of the Prophet's statement that it is "for whatever one drinks it for."

Abd al-Muttalib's rediscovery thus was not merely an archaeological event but a spiritual restoration — the reconnection of an ummah to the blessed water of its founding father Ibrahim, achieved through the man whose grandson would complete and fulfill that prophetic inheritance.
