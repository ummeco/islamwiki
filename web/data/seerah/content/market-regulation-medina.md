# Establishment and Regulation of the Medina Market

Shortly after the hijra in 1 AH (622-623 CE), the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) established a dedicated market for the Muslim community in Medina — an act that was both practically necessary and theologically significant, as it created an Islamic commercial space governed by principles of honesty, fair dealing, and freedom from exploitation.

## The Context: Medina's Existing Market

Before the Prophet's arrival, the primary market of Medina was controlled by Banu Qaynuqa — one of the Jewish tribes of the city, who were its established tradesmen and artisans. The Muhajirun (emigrants from Mecca) who arrived in Medina had no commercial space of their own and were dependent on existing market structures that operated according to pre-Islamic norms, including usurious lending practices and deceptive trading customs that the Prophet's message was directly challenging.

## Establishing the Market

The Prophet went to a location in Medina and designated it as a dedicated market for the Muslim community, saying: "This is your market — do not let it be narrowed, and do not let taxes be levied on it." He reportedly struck the ground with his foot at the chosen location, designating it as free for trade.

The location chosen was near the mosque — reflecting the Prophet's understanding that commerce and worship, properly governed, were not in conflict but were both aspects of a rightly ordered life.

## The Principles Established

The Prophet's market regulations became foundational texts in Islamic commercial law (fiqh al-mu'amalat):

**No exploitation charges (kharaj).** The Prophet prohibited the levying of arbitrary market taxes or booth fees. The market was to be open and free of extractive charges imposed by market controllers.

**No hoarding (ihtikar).** The Prophet prohibited merchants from buying up essential commodities and hoarding them to create artificial scarcity and drive up prices. He said: "Whoever hoards food from the Muslims, Allah will strike him with poverty and leprosy." — this prohibition became one of the most significant in Islamic commercial law.

**No selling what one does not possess.** The Prophet forbade selling things one had not yet taken possession of — a prohibition designed to prevent speculative transactions that created no real economic value.

**Truthfulness in describing goods.** The Prophet strongly emphasized honest representation: "The buyer and seller have the right of return as long as they have not parted. If both are truthful and transparent, their transaction will be blessed. If they conceal and lie, the blessing of their transaction will be erased." (Sahih Bukhari and Muslim)

**Prohibition of ribā (usury).** The Quranic prohibition on usury (which came in stages during the Medinan period) directly affected commercial practice. The market regulations were the practical implementation of these Quranic prohibitions.

**No price-fixing by the state.** When asked to fix prices during a period of high prices, the Prophet said: "Allah is the Price-Fixer, the Withholder, the Bestower, the Provider. I hope to meet my Lord without any of you having a grievance against me for wrongdoing in blood or in property." This established the principle that the Islamic state should not normally interfere with market pricing — though scholars have debated the circumstances under which price intervention may be permissible in cases of monopoly or crisis.

## Commercial Ethics in the Prophetic Model

The Prophet's own practice as a merchant before his prophethood was the foundation of his commercial ethics. He had been known in Mecca as al-Amin (the Trustworthy) — a commercial reputation built through honest dealing. His wife Khadijah had trusted him with her trading ventures before their marriage precisely because of this reputation.

The market regulations of Medina translated this personal ethic into a social and legal framework. The Prophet also appointed a market overseer (muhtasib) to patrol the market and ensure compliance — a role that evolved into the classical Islamic institution of the hisba, the system of market regulation and public morals enforcement.

## Significance

**The origin of Islamic commercial law.** The regulations established at the Medina market are the primary source-texts for Islamic commercial jurisprudence. All four major Sunni legal schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) developed elaborate bodies of commercial law drawing on these hadith and the Quranic prohibitions on usury.

**Commercial morality as religious obligation.** The Prophet's insistence that honest commercial dealing was a religious duty — not merely a pragmatic virtue — shaped Islamic civilization's understanding of the relationship between commerce and faith. Dishonest dealing was a spiritual failure, not merely a legal violation.

## Sources

The account of the market establishment and regulations is in Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, Ibn Saad's *al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, and the commercial law sections of all major hadith collections (Kitab al-Buyu' in Sahih Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, and the Sunan collections).
