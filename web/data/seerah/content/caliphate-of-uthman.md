# Caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan

Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) became the third Caliph of Islam in 23 AH (644 CE) following the assassination of Umar ibn al-Khattab. He was chosen by a consultative council (shura) of six senior Companions appointed by Umar before his death. His caliphate lasted twelve years until his martyrdom in 35 AH (656 CE).

## Early Achievements

Uthman's caliphate saw the Muslim state reach its greatest territorial extent under the Rashidun period. Major conquests included:

- The completion of the conquest of Persia and the eastern provinces
- The expansion into North Africa, reaching as far as modern-day Tunisia
- The first Muslim naval operations in the Mediterranean under Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan
- The decisive naval Battle of the Masts (34 AH / 655 CE) against the Byzantine fleet

Uthman was known for his generosity. He had purchased the well of Rumah in Medina to provide free water to the Muslims during the lifetime of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him), and he financed the expansion of Masjid an-Nabawi.

## The Compilation of the Quran

Perhaps the most enduring achievement of Uthman's caliphate was the standardization of the Quran. As Islam spread to diverse populations speaking different languages and dialects, variations in Quranic recitation emerged. Uthman ordered a committee led by Zayd ibn Thabit to compile a master copy (mushaf) based on the sheets collected during Abu Bakr's caliphate, verified against the memorizations of senior Companions.

Multiple copies of this standardized text were sent to the major garrison cities (Kufa, Basra, Damascus, and Mecca), and variant personal copies were ordered to be destroyed to prevent confusion. This decisive action preserved the Quran in its original form and remains one of the most important acts of preservation in religious history.

## Controversy and Opposition

The latter half of Uthman's caliphate was marked by growing discontent, particularly from the garrison cities of Kufa, Basra, and Fustat (Egypt). Critics accused him of nepotism for appointing members of his clan, the Banu Umayyah, to important governorships. His governors included Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan in Syria and Abdullah ibn Abi Sarh in Egypt.

Others objected to his financial policies, including his distribution of conquered lands and his management of the public treasury. While many of these accusations were exaggerated or politically motivated, they provided fuel for agitators who sought to destabilize the caliphate.

## The Siege and Martyrdom

In 35 AH (656 CE), groups from Egypt, Kufa, and Basra marched on Medina demanding the removal of certain governors. After initial negotiations, the situation deteriorated. Uthman's house was besieged for approximately forty days. Despite offers of armed protection from senior Companions and their sons, Uthman refused to allow Muslim blood to be shed on his account.

On the 18th of Dhul Hijjah, 35 AH, rebels broke into his home and killed him while he was reading the Quran. He was eighty-two years old. His blood fell on the mushaf he was reading, a scene that became one of the most poignant images in Islamic memory.

## Legacy

Uthman ibn Affan is honored as one of the ten Companions promised Paradise (al-Asharah al-Mubashsharah). His standardization of the Quran ensured the preservation of Allah's word in a single, authoritative text that the entire Muslim world shares to this day. His martyrdom, however, opened a period of civil strife (fitnah) that would profoundly shape the political and sectarian landscape of Islam for centuries.
