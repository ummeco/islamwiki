# Expedition to Hamra al-Asad After Uhud

The expedition to Hamra al-Asad in Shawwal 3 AH — launched the day after the Battle of Uhud — was a strategic pursuit operation by the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) that demonstrated his military presence of mind immediately after one of the most difficult battles in early Islamic history and served to discourage the Meccan army from returning to complete what Uhud had begun.

## The Context: Aftermath of Uhud

The Battle of Uhud (7 Shawwal 3 AH) had ended with seventy Muslim dead, the Prophet himself wounded (having been struck by a sword blow to the face that broke two links of his helmet into his cheek), and the Meccan army in nominal possession of the battlefield. The Meccans had departed northward, seemingly heading home.

That evening, the Prophet received intelligence that the Meccan army was debating whether to return and finish what they had started. Abu Sufyan, commanding the Meccan forces, reportedly considered turning back but was dissuaded.

## The Expedition

The morning after Uhud, the Prophet called the companions to pursue the Meccan army. He specified that only those who had fought at Uhud could join — he did not want to wait for fresh reinforcements. The wounded — including the Prophet himself, despite his injuries — prepared to march.

Approximately 70 companions who had fought the previous day joined the pursuit, including many who were still bandaged from their wounds. They marched approximately 16 km to Hamra al-Asad — a location along the road the Meccan army had taken.

## The Psychological Operation

The expedition served as a powerful statement. The Meccan army, receiving intelligence that the Muslims had immediately resumed their march despite the previous day's losses, was unnerved. The willingness of the Muslims to pursue despite their casualties demonstrated that they had not been broken at Uhud.

At Hamra al-Asad, the Prophet had large fires lit at multiple points — creating the impression of an even larger encampment. According to the seerah accounts, a man named Ma'bad al-Khuza'i (who was not Muslim but was sympathetic to the Muslims) was traveling north and encountered Abu Sufyan's deliberating army. He told Abu Sufyan that Muhammad was in pursuit with a larger force than at Uhud and that the Muslims were angry and determined. This account turned Abu Sufyan's deliberations decisively toward withdrawal. He sent a false message to the Prophet through a passing caravan warning that the Meccans were returning in full force.

## The Quranic Reference

The Quran addressed the companions' response to this warning in Surah Al Imran:

*"Those to whom hypocrites said, 'Indeed, the people have gathered against you, so fear them.' But it [merely] increased them in faith, and they said, 'Sufficient for us is Allah, and [He is] the best Disposer of affairs.'"* (Quran 3:173)

The verse captures the companions' mindset: faced with a reported threat larger than themselves, they responded with increased reliance on Allah rather than fear. The famous phrase "Hasbunallah wa ni'mal wakil" (Allah is sufficient for us, and He is the best disposer of affairs) — still recited in times of difficulty by Muslims — comes from this verse and this context.

## The Return

The Muslim force waited at Hamra al-Asad for three days, during which time the Prophet captured and executed two Meccan spies (Muawiyah ibn al-Mughirah, a relative of Uthman ibn Affan, who had been at Uhud; and Abu Azzah al-Jumahi, a poet who had participated against the Muslims at Badr and been released with an oath not to fight against them, then broke his oath at Uhud).

After three days, with no sign of the Meccan return, the Muslim force returned to Medina.

## Significance

**The immediate pursuit.** The willingness to march the day after a bloody battle — while wounded — was a powerful demonstration that Uhud had not broken Muslim resolve. It prevented the Meccans from exploiting their tactical success at Uhud into strategic victory.

**The "Hasbunallah" verse.** The Quranic verse revealed in this context became one of the most frequently recited expressions in Islamic devotional practice — a verbal embodiment of tawakkul (trust in Allah) in the face of overwhelming odds.

**Military psychology.** The expedition illustrates the Prophet's understanding of military psychology: the appearance of strength, maintained even after defeat, prevents an enemy from pressing their advantage. Hamra al-Asad transformed the aftermath of a military setback into a demonstration of unbroken Muslim resolve.

## Sources

The expedition is documented in Sahih Bukhari (Kitab al-Maghazi), Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya*, al-Tabari's *Tarikh al-Rusul wal-Muluk*, and al-Waqidi's *al-Maghazi*.
