# The First Friday Prayer (Jumu'ah)

## The First Congregational Jumu'ah in Islam

The first formal Friday prayer (Salat al-Jumu'ah) in the history of Islam was led by the Prophet ﷺ in the valley of the Banu Salim ibn Awf, on his journey from Quba to the main city of Medina. It took place on a Friday — the day after he departed Quba — making it the first Friday prayer he led in the Medinan region. The location was the neighborhood of the Banu Salim ibn Awf, where approximately one hundred Muslims gathered in an open space that served as the early prayer ground.

The significance of this event extends beyond its immediacy. The Friday prayer — with its obligatory congregational format, the khutbah (sermon) before the prayer, and the substitution of the four-unit dhuhr prayer with the two-unit Jumu'ah prayer — became one of the defining rhythms of Islamic communal life. The Prophet ﷺ delivered the first recorded Jumu'ah khutbah on this day, establishing the format that every Friday sermon in every mosque across the world has followed since.

## The Khutbah

The first Jumu'ah khutbah of the Prophet ﷺ is preserved in fragmentary form in Ibn Hisham and Ibn Kathir. Its content was foundational: praise of Allah, the importance of piety (taqwa), the reality of death and accountability, and the obligation of the community to obey Allah and His Messenger. The narration records the Prophet ﷺ saying:

"Praise belongs to Allah. I praise Him, seek His help, ask His forgiveness, and seek refuge in Allah from the evils of our souls and the evils of our deeds. Whoever Allah guides, none can misguide him; whoever He leaves astray, none can guide him. I testify that there is no god but Allah, alone without partner, and I testify that Muhammad is His servant and messenger, sent with the truth and guidance and light before the Hour comes, for whoever obeys Allah and His Messenger has been guided, and whoever disobeys them has erred..."

The khutbah also addressed the community's obligations to each other, to the poor, and to the maintenance of communal order — themes that would define the Prophet's ﷺ governance in Medina throughout his ten years there.

## Why Friday

The sanctity of Friday in Islam is rooted in several layers of religious and cosmological significance. The Prophet ﷺ said in a hadith preserved in Sahih Muslim: "The best day on which the sun has risen is Friday. On it Adam was created, on it he was made to enter Paradise, on it he was taken out of it, and the Hour will not come except on a Friday." The day carries within it the entirety of human history — creation, paradise, fall, and final resurrection — in a cycle that returns every week.

The obligation of the Friday prayer was revealed in Surah al-Jumu'ah (62:9): "O you who believe, when the call to prayer is made on the day of Jumu'ah, hasten to the remembrance of Allah and leave off your trade. That is better for you, if only you knew." The verse explicitly commands the abandonment of commercial activity at the call to Jumu'ah — a statement about the relative weight of the sacred and the commercial in Islamic life that the Muslim community has understood and practiced in every generation.

## Musab's Jumu'ah

It should be noted that the first Jumu'ah in Yathrib predated the Prophet's ﷺ arrival. Musab ibn Umayr, sent by the Prophet ﷺ as the first Islamic teacher in Yathrib, had established a Jumu'ah prayer in the house of Asad ibn Zurara approximately a year before the Hijra — gathering the nascent Yathribi community for the weekly congregational prayer. This first institutional Jumu'ah by Musab is preserved in the narrations of Abu Dawud and Ibn Sa'd. The Prophet's ﷺ own first Jumu'ah, at the Banu Salim valley, was therefore the first led by him personally in the Medinan period — not the first Jumu'ah in Yathrib's history.

The sequence — Musab establishing the weekly pattern, the Prophet ﷺ formalizing and leading it personally upon arrival — illustrates the relationship between the teacher sent ahead and the mission he prepared. Musab had done the groundwork; the Prophet ﷺ arrived to complete it.

## Jumu'ah as Community

The Friday prayer has always been more than a prayer. It is the weekly moment at which the Muslim community — dispersed through the week into individual households, workplaces, and lives — gathers as a community to stand before Allah together and to hear the khutbah. The Prophet ﷺ used the khutbah to address current events, to call the community to action, to announce expeditions, to settle disputes, and to teach. It was the central public communication medium of the prophetic community — the weekly address of the leader to his people, the teacher to his students, the imam to his congregation.

In the first Friday prayer at Banu Salim, we see all of this in embryo: a gathering of approximately one hundred people in an open space, a leader addressing them from a position of religious and communal authority, a sermon followed by a congregational prayer, and the establishment of a weekly rhythm that would continue — in that city, in every other city and town that Islam reached, and on every Friday until the Day of Judgment.

The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever bathes, purifies himself, attends early, sits close to the imam and listens without being distracted — for every step he takes, he will receive the reward of a year's fasting and a year's night prayer." The Friday prayer is among the most heavily rewarded acts of worship in Islamic practice — and its first occurrence, in the valley of Banu Salim, a few miles from the city that would host it for the rest of the Prophet's ﷺ life, was an act of worship led by a man who had arrived in the region only the day before.
