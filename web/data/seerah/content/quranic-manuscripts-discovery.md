# Quranic Manuscript Discoveries

The twentieth and twenty-first centuries CE have produced several significant discoveries of ancient Quranic manuscripts, providing important evidence for the early history of the Quranic text and its transmission. The most significant finds include the Sanaa manuscripts discovered in Yemen in 1972 CE, the Birmingham Quran leaf dated to circa 645-690 CE, and ongoing scholarly work on manuscript collections in libraries across the Muslim world.

## The Sanaa Manuscripts

In 1972 CE (1392 AH), workers restoring the Great Mosque of Sanaa in Yemen discovered a hidden space in the roof containing a large collection of old manuscripts and parchments. The materials included fragments of approximately 926 Quranic texts, along with non-Quranic materials.

Yemeni authorities eventually allowed German scholars — primarily Gerd-Rüdiger Puin of the Saarland University — to examine the collection. Carbon dating and paleographic analysis indicated that some of the parchments date to the early Islamic period, possibly the first or second Islamic century (seventh or eighth century CE).

Puin's analysis revealed that some of the manuscripts showed textual variations from the standard Uthmanic text — in terms of spelling, diacritical marks, and in some cases the arrangement of suras. He also noted traces of earlier writings under some texts (palimpsests). These findings generated significant scholarly discussion about the early history of the Quranic text.

**The Scholarly Response.** Muslim scholars and many Western scholars of Quranic studies noted that:
- Orthographic and spelling variations were well-known in early Quran manuscripts and reflected the development of Arabic script before the standardization of vowel marking and diacritics
- The Uthmanic standard text refers to consonantal text (rasm), and variation in dots and marks does not necessarily represent variant readings
- The traditions of the seven (or ten) canonical recitations (qira'at) already acknowledged diversity in vocalization within a single consonantal text
- No discovered manuscripts challenge the fundamental consonantal skeleton of the standard text in ways that would constitute "different Qurans"

The Sanaa materials remained partially unpublished for decades, generating frustrated speculation in Western media, but scholarly analysis has generally concluded that the materials confirm rather than undermine the essential stability of the Quranic text.

## The Birmingham Quran Leaf

In 2015 CE (1436 AH), researchers at the University of Birmingham's Cadbury Research Library radiocarbon-dated two parchment leaves of Quranic text that had been in the library's collection since the 1920s. The dating placed the parchment with 95.4% probability between 568 and 645 CE — potentially making it one of the oldest surviving Quranic manuscripts.

If the earliest end of the date range is correct, the manuscript could date to before or during the lifetime of the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him). More cautious analysis places it in the early caliphate period.

The text written on the leaves — Surahs 18-20 — corresponds closely to the standard Uthmanic text, providing physical evidence for the early stability of the written Quranic transmission.

## The Tübingen Manuscript

The University of Tübingen holds a Quranic manuscript also dated by carbon analysis to the late seventh century CE, representing another data point for early Quranic preservation.

## Significance for Islamic Scholarship

These manuscript discoveries are significant from several perspectives:

**Confirmation of preservation.** The early manuscripts, despite showing the natural variation in orthographic conventions of nascent Arabic script, confirm the essential stability of the Quranic text. The claim that the Quran was reliably preserved from the time of the Prophet finds physical corroboration in these early manuscripts.

**Understanding early Arabic.** The manuscripts provide invaluable evidence for the development of early Arabic script, including the evolution of diacritical marks, the standardization of consonants, and the development of vocalic notation.

**Historical connection.** For Muslims, possessing physical objects from the first generation of Islamic history creates a powerful sense of connection to the Prophet's time.
