## The Fall of Jerusalem to the Crusaders (1099 CE)

The siege and fall of Jerusalem on 15 July 1099 CE (492 AH) stands as one of the most devastating events in Islamic history. After five weeks of siege, the armies of the First Crusade breached the walls of al-Quds and carried out a massacre so thorough that it became a defining moment in Muslim collective memory for centuries to come.

## Background: The First Crusade

In 1095 CE, Pope Urban II called upon the Christians of Europe to march east and seize Jerusalem from Muslim control. The city had been under Muslim governance since the conquest by Umar ibn al-Khattab (radi Allahu anhu) in 637 CE. At the time of the Crusade's arrival, Jerusalem was held by the Fatimid dynasty of Egypt, who had recaptured it from the Seljuk Turks only the year before.

The Muslim world was deeply fractured. The Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad held little real power. The Seljuk sultanate had splintered into rival principalities across Syria and Anatolia. The Fatimids in Cairo, who followed an Ismaili Shia creed rejected by the Sunni majority, were politically isolated. This disunity allowed the Crusader armies to advance through Anatolia and the Levant, capturing Antioch in 1098 before turning south toward Jerusalem.

## The Siege

The Fatimid governor Iftikhar al-Dawla prepared for the siege by expelling the city's Christian inhabitants, fearing they would collaborate with the invaders. He poisoned wells outside the walls and strengthened the garrison. The Crusader force, numbering roughly 13,000 soldiers by this point, arrived on 7 June 1099 and encircled the city.

The defenders held firm for five weeks. The Crusaders suffered from severe heat and lack of water. Their first assault on 13 June failed. However, the arrival of Genoese ships at Jaffa provided timber and supplies, and the Crusaders constructed siege towers. On 15 July, forces under Godfrey of Bouillon breached the northern wall near Bab al-Sahira (Herod's Gate), and the city fell.

## The Massacre

What followed was one of the most brutal episodes of the medieval period. The Crusaders slaughtered the Muslim population indiscriminately. Men, women, children, and the elderly were cut down in the streets, in their homes, and in places of worship. The Jewish community of Jerusalem, who had gathered in a synagogue for refuge, were burned alive when the Crusaders set the building on fire.

The great scholar Ibn al-Athir (d. 630 AH), drawing on earlier sources, recorded in his al-Kamil fi al-Tarikh that over 70,000 people were killed in the area of al-Masjid al-Aqsa alone. While modern historians debate the exact figures, even Latin chroniclers who accompanied the Crusade described wading through blood. The anonymous author of the Gesta Francorum wrote of blood reaching the ankles of the soldiers. Raymond of Aguilers described it reaching the bridles of horses near the Temple Mount.

Iftikhar al-Dawla and a small group of the garrison negotiated safe passage from the Tower of David and withdrew to Ascalon. They were among the very few to survive.

## The Contrast with Umar's Conquest

Muslim historians consistently drew a sharp contrast between the Crusader conquest and the original Muslim entry into Jerusalem under Umar ibn al-Khattab. When Umar accepted the surrender of the city from Patriarch Sophronius in 637 CE, he guaranteed the safety of all inhabitants, their property, and their churches. Not a single person was harmed. Umar personally refused to pray inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre so that future Muslims would not claim it as a mosque.

This contrast became a central theme in Islamic historical writing. The peaceful Muslim conquest demonstrated the justice of Islamic governance, while the Crusader massacre revealed the nature of the invading force.

## Aftermath and Legacy

Jerusalem remained under Crusader control for 88 years, during which al-Masjid al-Aqsa was converted into a palace and stables for the Knights Templar. The Dome of the Rock was turned into a church. Muslims were forbidden from living in the city.

The fall of Jerusalem eventually galvanized the Muslim world. Scholars and rulers called for unity and jihad to liberate al-Quds. This effort culminated in the campaign of Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi (Saladin), who defeated the Crusaders at the Battle of Hattin in 1187 CE and recaptured Jerusalem. In deliberate contrast to 1099, Saladin's reconquest was marked by mercy. The Christian inhabitants were allowed to ransom themselves, and those who could not pay were often released. Churches were left intact.

The events of 1099 remain deeply significant in Islamic historical consciousness. They serve as a reminder of the consequences of Muslim disunity, and of the stark difference between governance rooted in the Prophetic model and that driven by conquest and vengeance.
