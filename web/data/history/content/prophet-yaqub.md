## Prophet Yaqub (Jacob) in Islam

Yaqub ibn Ishaq ibn Ibrahim (peace be upon them all) holds a distinguished place among the prophets of Allah. Known in the Biblical tradition as Jacob, he is referred to in the Quran as Isra'il, from which the designation Banu Isra'il (Children of Israel) derives. Allah chose him as a prophet and guide, continuing the prophetic legacy established by his grandfather Ibrahim and his father Ishaq.

## Lineage and Early Life

Yaqub was born to Prophet Ishaq and his wife Rifqah (Rebecca) in the land of Canaan. He belonged to the noblest prophetic lineage in human history. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "The noble son of the noble son of the noble son of the noble: Yusuf ibn Yaqub ibn Ishaq ibn Ibrahim" (Sahih al-Bukhari 3390). This hadith affirms four consecutive generations of prophethood, a distinction unmatched in the Quran.

Allah mentions choosing Yaqub among the guided: "And We gave him Ishaq and Yaqub; all of them We guided" (Surah al-An'am 6:84). He is also named among those whom Allah specifically chose and purified: "And remember Our servants Ibrahim, Ishaq, and Yaqub, those of strength and vision. Indeed, We chose them for an exclusive quality: remembrance of the Hereafter" (Surah Sad 38:45-46).

## The Name Isra'il

The Quran refers to Yaqub by the name Isra'il in Surah Al Imran: "All food was lawful to the Children of Isra'il except what Isra'il had made unlawful to himself before the Torah was revealed" (3:93). Ibn Kathir, citing earlier authorities, explains that Yaqub had made certain foods forbidden upon himself as a personal vow due to an illness, and this became a point of discussion regarding what was lawful before the revelation of the Torah.

## Yaqub's Family and the Twelve Tribes

Yaqub married and fathered twelve sons, who became the ancestors of the twelve tribes of Banu Isra'il. Among them, Yusuf and his full brother Binyamin (Benjamin) held a special place in their father's heart. The other ten brothers grew jealous of Yusuf, setting in motion one of the most detailed and moving narratives in the Quran.

## The Trial of Separation from Yusuf

The story of Yaqub is inseparable from the story of his son Yusuf, told in full in Surah Yusuf. When the young Yusuf came to his father describing a dream in which eleven stars, the sun, and the moon prostrated before him, Yaqub immediately recognized its prophetic significance. He warned his son: "O my son, do not relate your vision to your brothers or they will contrive against you a plan. Indeed, Shaytan is to man a clear enemy" (12:5).

His fears proved justified. The brothers cast Yusuf into a well and returned to their father with a blood-stained shirt, claiming a wolf had devoured him. Yaqub, with the insight granted to prophets, perceived the lie. His response became one of the most quoted expressions of trust in Allah in Islamic tradition: "Rather, your souls have enticed you to something, so patience is most fitting. And Allah is the one sought for help against that which you describe" (12:18).

## Beautiful Patience

The years of separation tested Yaqub profoundly. He grieved until his eyes turned white from weeping (12:84), yet he never attributed injustice to Allah nor abandoned hope. When his sons rebuked him for his unceasing grief, he replied with words that define prophetic reliance on Allah: "I only complain of my suffering and my grief to Allah, and I know from Allah that which you do not know" (12:86).

Ibn al-Qayyim noted in his commentary that Yaqub's patience was not passive resignation but an active, conscious turning toward Allah. He continued to instruct his sons, sending them repeatedly to Egypt, and he never ceased supplicating for Yusuf's return.

## The Reunion and Restoration

After long years, Allah reunited Yaqub with Yusuf in Egypt. When Yusuf sent his shirt back with his brothers, Yaqub's sight was restored the moment the garment touched his face (12:96). The family traveled to Egypt where Yusuf received them with honor, and his parents and brothers fell in prostration before him, fulfilling the dream from his childhood: "O my father, this is the interpretation of my vision of before. My Lord has made it reality" (12:100).

## The Bequest at Death

The Quran records Yaqub's final counsel to his sons on his deathbed: "Or were you witnesses when death approached Yaqub, when he said to his sons, 'What will you worship after me?' They said, 'We will worship your God and the God of your fathers, Ibrahim and Isma'il and Ishaq, one God. And we are Muslims in submission to Him'" (Surah al-Baqarah 2:133). This passage confirms that the central concern of the prophets at the hour of death was the preservation of tawhid among their descendants.

## Lessons from His Life

Yaqub's story teaches that trials may be prolonged and severe, but the believer's response is patience combined with unbroken hope in Allah. His grief was real and human, yet it never crossed into despair or complaint against divine decree. He exemplified what scholars call al-sabr al-jamil (beautiful patience), a patience free from complaint to creation while maintaining full honesty before the Creator.

His life stands as proof that Allah's plans unfold across decades, that loss can precede restoration, and that prophetic certainty in Allah's promise is never misplaced.
