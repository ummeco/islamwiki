## Prophet Yusuf in Islam

Yusuf ibn Ya'qub ibn Ishaq ibn Ibrahim (peace be upon them all) holds a unique place among the prophets of Allah. He is the only prophet whose life story is narrated in full within a single surah of the Quran. Allah Himself describes this account as "the best of stories" (Quran 12:3), and the depth of its lessons in patience, chastity, forgiveness, and divine planning make Surah Yusuf one of the most studied chapters in Islamic scholarship.

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "The noble son of the noble son of the noble son of the noble — Yusuf ibn Ya'qub ibn Ishaq ibn Ibrahim" (Sahih al-Bukhari 3390). Four consecutive prophets in a single lineage, a distinction shared by no other family in recorded history.

## The Dream and the Betrayal

As a young boy, Yusuf saw in a dream that eleven stars, the sun, and the moon prostrated before him (Quran 12:4). His father Ya'qub, himself a prophet, recognized this as a sign of future prophethood and warned him not to share it with his brothers, lest jealousy consume them. But the brothers already resented the special bond between Ya'qub and Yusuf.

Their jealousy drove them to a terrible act. They convinced Ya'qub to let Yusuf accompany them on an outing, then cast him into the depths of a well. They returned to their father with a blood-stained shirt and a fabricated story of a wolf attack. Ya'qub, recognizing the lie, responded with "beautiful patience" (sabr jamil) — patience without complaint to anyone but Allah (Quran 12:18). This became a defining concept in Islamic spirituality.

A passing caravan discovered Yusuf in the well and sold him as a slave in Egypt, where the chief minister, known in the Quran as al-Aziz, purchased him and instructed his wife to treat him well.

## The Trial of Temptation

As Yusuf grew into manhood, Allah granted him wisdom, knowledge, and extraordinary physical beauty. The wife of al-Aziz, overwhelmed by desire, attempted to seduce him. She locked the doors and called him to herself. Yusuf's response stands as one of the greatest declarations of moral courage in prophetic history: "I seek refuge in Allah. Indeed, He is my master who has made good my residence. Indeed, wrongdoers will not succeed" (Quran 12:23).

When the women of the city mocked al-Aziz's wife, she invited them to a banquet and had Yusuf walk before them. They cut their hands in astonishment at his beauty and exclaimed, "This is not a man; this is none but a noble angel!" (Quran 12:31). Yet Yusuf remained firm. When pressed further, he made a supplication that reveals the heart of true faith: "My Lord, prison is more to my liking than that to which they invite me. And if You do not avert from me their plan, I might incline toward them and be of the ignorant" (Quran 12:33).

Allah answered his prayer and turned their plot away from him. Yusuf was imprisoned, choosing confinement over sin.

## From Prison to Power

In prison, Yusuf interpreted the dreams of two fellow inmates with precision. One was restored to the king's service, and Yusuf asked him to mention his case to the king. But the man forgot, and Yusuf remained in prison for several more years — a test of patience that refined him further.

When the king of Egypt dreamed of seven fat cows devoured by seven lean ones, and seven green ears of grain beside seven dry, none in his court could interpret it. The former prisoner finally remembered Yusuf. His interpretation was direct: seven years of abundant harvest followed by seven years of severe famine, with a prescription to store grain during the years of plenty (Quran 12:47-49).

The king, impressed, summoned Yusuf. But Yusuf refused to leave prison until his name was cleared of the false accusation. The women were questioned, and the wife of al-Aziz herself confessed: "Now the truth has become evident. It was I who sought to seduce him, and indeed he is of the truthful" (Quran 12:51). Yusuf was appointed over the storehouses of Egypt, effectively becoming its chief administrator.

## Reunion and Forgiveness

When famine struck the region, Yusuf's brothers traveled to Egypt seeking provisions. They did not recognize him, but he recognized them. Over the course of several visits, Yusuf tested them and eventually revealed his identity. His words at that moment are among the most powerful in the Quran: "No blame will there be upon you today. Allah will forgive you, and He is the Most Merciful of the merciful" (Quran 12:92).

Ya'qub, who had wept for Yusuf until he lost his sight, was reunited with his son. When Yusuf's shirt was placed over Ya'qub's face, his vision was restored. The entire family came to Egypt, and when they entered Yusuf's court, his parents and eleven brothers fell in prostration before him — the fulfillment of his childhood dream decades later (Quran 12:100).

## Legacy

Yusuf's story concludes with his own reflection: "My Lord, You have given me something of sovereignty and taught me of the interpretation of dreams. Creator of the heavens and earth, You are my protector in this world and the Hereafter. Cause me to die a Muslim and join me with the righteous" (Quran 12:101).

Ibn Kathir noted that Yusuf's story contains lessons applicable to every condition a believer may face — wealth and poverty, freedom and imprisonment, temptation and steadfastness, separation and reunion. It is, as Allah stated, truly the best of stories.
