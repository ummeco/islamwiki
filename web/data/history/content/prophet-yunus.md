## Yunus and the People of Nineveh

Yunus ibn Matta (peace be upon him) was sent as a prophet to the people of Nineveh, a great and populous city in the land of ancient Assyria, in what is now northern Iraq. He is known by two honorific titles in the Quran: Dhul-Nun (the Companion of the Whale) and Sahibul Hut (the Companion of the Fish). His story stands apart among the prophets for the nature of his trial and for the singular mercy Allah showed to his people.

## The Mission to Nineveh

Allah sent Yunus to call the people of Nineveh away from idol worship and toward the worship of Allah alone. The city was large and its inhabitants deeply attached to their ways. Yunus delivered the message with persistence, warning them of divine punishment if they refused. Yet for a long period they rejected him, showing no signs of turning back. The frustration of preaching to a resistant people weighed heavily upon him.

## The Departure

When Yunus saw no response from his people, he left the city in anger and frustration, departing before Allah granted him permission to do so. The Quran describes this moment: *"And [mention] Dhul-Nun, when he went off in anger and thought that We would not decree anything upon him"* (Al-Anbiya 21:87). This was not a departure born of disbelief or disobedience to Allah's fundamental command, but an act of haste. He assumed his duty was fulfilled when the people refused to listen, and he did not wait for Allah's instruction.

He reached the coast and boarded a ship. When the vessel was caught in a violent storm and began to founder under the weight of its passengers, the sailors determined by drawing lots that one person must be cast overboard to save the rest. The lot fell upon Yunus, and it fell upon him again when they repeated it, and a third time after that. He was thrown into the raging sea.

## The Belly of the Whale

A great whale swallowed Yunus whole, by the command of Allah. He found himself in three layers of darkness: the darkness of the night, the darkness of the sea, and the darkness within the belly of the whale. In that state of utter helplessness, with no human means of escape, Yunus turned to Allah with one of the most profound supplications recorded in the Quran:

*"There is no deity except You; exalted are You. Indeed, I have been of the wrongdoers"* (Al-Anbiya 21:87).

This supplication, known among scholars as the du'a of Yunus, combines three elements: the affirmation of tawhid, the glorification of Allah above all imperfection, and the honest admission of personal error. It contains no request. It is pure acknowledgment of Allah's lordship and one's own shortcoming. Allah responded: *"So We responded to him and saved him from the distress. And thus do We save the believers"* (Al-Anbiya 21:88).

The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) said regarding this supplication: *"No Muslim calls upon Allah with it concerning any matter but that Allah answers him"* (reported by al-Tirmidhi, who graded it hasan sahih). This hadith established the du'a of Yunus as one of the most recommended prayers for anyone in distress.

## Recovery and Return

Allah commanded the whale to cast Yunus onto the shore. He emerged ill and physically weakened. Allah caused a gourd plant (yaqtin) to grow over him, providing him shade and nourishment as he recovered. The Quran states: *"And We cast him onto the open shore while he was ill. And We caused to grow over him a gourd vine"* (As-Saffat 37:145-146). Once restored, Allah sent him back to his people to complete his mission.

## The Repentance of Nineveh

What makes the story of Yunus unique among the prophets is what happened to his people after he left. When signs of the approaching punishment appeared over Nineveh, the people recognized the truth of what Yunus had warned them about. They repented sincerely, going out with their families and livestock, crying and supplicating to Allah. Allah accepted their repentance and lifted the punishment.

The Quran highlights this as exceptional: *"Then why was there not a single township that believed so its faith benefited it, except the people of Yunus? When they believed, We removed from them the punishment of disgrace in worldly life and gave them enjoyment for a time"* (Yunus 10:98). They are the only community in the Quran described as having been spared after the signs of punishment had already appeared. The Quran describes them as numbering one hundred thousand or more (As-Saffat 37:147).

## The Rank of Yunus

Despite the nature of his trial, the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) held Yunus in high regard and cautioned against diminishing his status. He said: *"No one should say that I am better than Yunus ibn Matta"* (reported by al-Bukhari and Muslim). Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani explained that this statement was made out of humility and to affirm the honor of Yunus, lest anyone use his story to lower his rank among the prophets.

## Lessons from the Story

The account of Yunus carries several enduring lessons. First, no servant of Allah may abandon a duty assigned to them without permission, regardless of how hopeless the situation appears. Second, sincere repentance is accepted by Allah even at the last moment, as demonstrated by the people of Nineveh. Third, turning to Allah with pure tawhid and honest self-accountability, as Yunus did in the belly of the whale, is the most effective form of supplication. The story teaches that Allah's mercy encompasses everything, and that patience in fulfilling one's mission is not optional for a believer.
