## The Reconquest of Jerusalem by Saladin (583 AH / 1187 CE)

The liberation of Jerusalem by Sultan Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub on 27 Rajab 583 AH (2 October 1187 CE) stands among the most celebrated events in Islamic history. That the city was reclaimed on the very anniversary of the Prophet's Night Journey (al-Isra' wal-Mi'raj) was not lost on the Muslim world. After 88 years of Crusader occupation, the third holiest site in Islam was restored to the ummah through a campaign marked by military brilliance and a magnanimity that even Saladin's enemies acknowledged.

## The Road to Jerusalem: Hattin and Its Aftermath

The reconquest of Jerusalem did not begin at its walls but on the scorched plains of Hattin. On 4 July 1187, Saladin drew the combined Crusader forces of the Kingdom of Jerusalem into battle near the Horns of Hattin in Galilee. The Crusader army, exhausted by a forced march through waterless terrain in the summer heat, was decisively destroyed. King Guy of Lusignan was captured, along with the relic the Crusaders called the True Cross. Reynald of Chatillon, who had repeatedly violated truces and attacked Muslim pilgrim caravans, was personally executed by Saladin in fulfillment of an earlier oath.

With the Crusader field army annihilated, the fortresses and coastal cities of the Levant fell in rapid succession. Acre, Jaffa, Sidon, Beirut, and Ascalon all surrendered or were taken within weeks. By September, Saladin's forces stood before Jerusalem itself.

## The Siege and Negotiated Surrender

The defense of Jerusalem fell to Balian of Ibelin, a nobleman who had been granted safe passage through Saladin's lines to retrieve his family but was then pressed into service by the city's desperate population. The city was swollen with refugees and poorly garrisoned, yet Balian organized a determined resistance.

When it became clear that the walls would be breached, Balian threatened to destroy the Dome of the Rock and al-Aqsa Mosque, slaughter the Muslim prisoners held in the city, and kill their own families rather than surrender. Saladin, unwilling to see the holy sites damaged, agreed to negotiate. The terms were generous: every Christian could ransom themselves for a fixed sum (ten dinars for a man, five for a woman, one for a child), and they would be allowed to leave with their possessions. A forty-day period was granted for payment.

## Saladin's Mercy and the Contrast with 1099

The conduct of Saladin and his commanders during and after the surrender became the defining image of the reconquest. When it became apparent that thousands of the city's poor could not afford their ransom, Saladin's brother al-Adil asked for a thousand captives and freed them without payment. Saladin himself paid ransoms from his own treasury for many of the destitute. The elderly and widows were released freely. Patriarch Heraclius was permitted to leave carrying church treasures, a decision some of Saladin's advisors criticized as too lenient.

The contrast with the Crusader conquest of 1099 was stark and immediate. When the First Crusade took Jerusalem, the victors massacred the Muslim and Jewish inhabitants wholesale. Contemporary Latin chronicles describe blood running ankle-deep in the streets. Saladin's entry, by comparison, saw no massacre, no looting sanctioned by command, and no desecration of churches. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre remained in Christian hands, and Eastern Christians were permitted to remain and worship freely.

Ibn al-Athir, the great historian, recorded that Saladin's conduct reflected the Prophet's own example at the Conquest of Makkah, where the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) declared a general amnesty and said, "Go, for you are free." Saladin consciously modeled his mercy on this prophetic precedent.

## Restoration of the Holy Sites

Upon entering the city, Saladin ordered the great golden cross removed from atop the Dome of the Rock. The al-Aqsa Mosque, which the Crusaders had converted into a royal palace and headquarters for the Knights Templar, was cleansed with rosewater and restored to congregational prayer. The first Friday khutbah was delivered by the qadi Muhyi ad-Din ibn al-Zaki, who praised Allah for the victory and reminded the congregation of the sanctity of the land.

Saladin also commissioned repairs and endowments (awqaf) for the upkeep of the mosque and surrounding institutions. Scholars, students, and Sufis were invited to settle in the city, and madrasas were established to restore Jerusalem as a center of Islamic learning.

## Legacy and Significance

Saladin's reconquest of Jerusalem resonated across the Muslim world and beyond. In the West, it triggered the Third Crusade led by Richard the Lionheart, Philip Augustus, and Frederick Barbarossa, yet even Richard came to respect Saladin's chivalry. The two exchanged courteous embassies, and Saladin sent his personal physician when Richard fell ill.

For Muslims, the liberation of Jerusalem confirmed that justice, patience, and unity could overcome decades of occupation. Saladin united Egypt, Syria, and the Jazira under one banner before turning to Jerusalem, demonstrating that Muslim disunity had been the true cause of the city's loss. His example remains a standard by which Muslim leadership is measured: strategic competence wedded to genuine piety, generosity even toward enemies, and an unwavering commitment to the sacred trust of the holy places.
