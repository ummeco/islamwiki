## Conquest of Alexandria

The conquest of Alexandria in 641 CE marked the completion of the Muslim takeover of Egypt, bringing one of the ancient world's most storied cities and the wealthiest province of the Byzantine Empire under the banner of Islam. This achievement, accomplished during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), demonstrated both the strategic brilliance of the Muslim commanders and the providential momentum that carried the early Islamic conquests across multiple fronts simultaneously.

## Background and the Egyptian Campaign

The Muslim advance into Egypt began in late 639 CE when Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him) crossed into the Sinai with approximately 4,000 troops. Amr, a seasoned commander who had embraced Islam before the conquest of Mecca, possessed intimate knowledge of Egypt from his pre-Islamic trading journeys. He sought and received permission from Caliph Umar to undertake the campaign, though some reports suggest he moved quickly to cross the border before a possible recall order could reach him.

Egypt at that time was a deeply divided province. The native Coptic Christian population had endured decades of persecution under Byzantine rule, particularly following the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE. The imperial authorities in Constantinople enforced Chalcedonian orthodoxy upon the Copts, who held to Miaphysite theology. Coptic Patriarch Benjamin had been forced into hiding, and Coptic churches were seized and handed to the imperially-appointed patriarch Cyrus (known in Arabic sources as al-Muqawqis). This internal fracture significantly weakened Byzantine Egypt's capacity to resist.

## The Road to Alexandria

After crossing into Egypt, Amr captured the fortress city of Pelusium (al-Farama) and advanced westward. Reinforcements of several thousand fighters joined him, and in July 640 CE, the Muslims won the decisive Battle of Heliopolis (Ayn Shams) against a larger Byzantine force. This victory opened the path to Babylon Fortress, the military stronghold near modern Cairo, which fell after a siege in April 641 CE.

With Babylon secured, Amr turned his attention northward to Alexandria, the imperial capital and one of the greatest cities of the Mediterranean world. Alexandria was heavily fortified, protected by thick walls, and supplied by sea, where the Byzantine navy maintained superiority. Its garrison was substantial, and the city's defenses were formidable by any standard.

## The Siege and Surrender

The siege of Alexandria lasted approximately fourteen months. Muslim forces could not blockade the city from the sea, which allowed the Byzantines to receive supplies and reinforcements. However, the political situation in Constantinople had grown chaotic following the death of Emperor Heraclius in February 641 CE, and the ensuing succession disputes left the Alexandria garrison without clear direction or reliable support.

Cyrus, who served as both religious patriarch and civil governor, entered into negotiations with Amr ibn al-As. A treaty was concluded in November 641 CE, granting the Byzantine garrison eleven months to evacuate. The terms were remarkably generous: the garrison would depart peacefully by sea, and the city's inhabitants would be guaranteed security for their persons, property, and places of worship.

In September 642 CE, the last Byzantine forces departed, and Amr ibn al-As entered Alexandria without bloodshed. He reportedly wrote to Caliph Umar describing its vastness, mentioning thousands of palaces, baths, and markets. The peaceful transfer stood in contrast to the destruction that often accompanied ancient conquests.

## Treatment of the Population

The Muslim administration under Amr ibn al-As established a pattern of governance that became characteristic of early Islamic rule in conquered territories. The Coptic population was granted dhimmi status, with protection of life, property, and religious practice in exchange for the payment of jizyah. Notably, the Coptic Patriarch Benjamin, who had been in hiding for over a decade from Byzantine persecution, was invited to return and resume his duties. Amr reportedly received him with honor and restored Coptic churches to their congregations.

This treatment earned considerable goodwill from the native population, many of whom had suffered more under their fellow Christians in Constantinople than they would under Muslim governance.

## Strategic and Historical Significance

The conquest of Egypt was transformative for the early Islamic state. Egypt's extraordinary agricultural output, particularly its grain production from the Nile Delta, provided critical resources that sustained the expanding caliphate. Amr established Fustat as his administrative capital near Babylon Fortress, which would eventually grow into the great city of Cairo.

Strategically, Egypt's capture deprived the Byzantine Empire of its richest remaining province and opened the path for further Muslim expansion westward across North Africa. The conquest also secured the southern Mediterranean coastline and established a naval base that would later challenge Byzantine dominance at sea.

## Legacy

The conquest of Alexandria represented not merely a military achievement but a turning point in world history. A civilization that had been the intellectual heart of the Hellenistic world and a center of early Christian theology now entered the orbit of Islamic civilization. Under Muslim rule, Egypt would flourish as a center of Islamic scholarship, producing generations of scholars in hadith, fiqh, and other sciences. The peaceful nature of Alexandria's surrender, secured through negotiation rather than assault, reflected the principles that Caliph Umar insisted upon in the conduct of military campaigns, principles rooted in the prophetic model of mercy toward non-combatants and respect for the peoples of the scripture.
