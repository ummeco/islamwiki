## The Conquest of Ctesiphon (al-Mada'in)

The conquest of Ctesiphon in 16 AH (637 CE) stands as one of the most consequential events in Islamic history. Known to the Arabs as al-Mada'in ("the Cities"), Ctesiphon was the magnificent capital of the Sassanid Persian Empire, a civilization that had rivaled Rome for centuries. Its fall to the Muslim forces under Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas marked the effective collapse of Persian imperial power and fulfilled a prophecy the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) had made years earlier when he struck the rock during the digging of the Trench: that the keys of Persia would be given to his ummah.

## Background and the Road from al-Qadisiyyah

The path to Ctesiphon was opened by the decisive Muslim victory at the Battle of al-Qadisiyyah in Shawwal 15 AH. In that engagement, the Persian general Rustam Farrukhzad was killed and the main Sassanid field army was shattered. The Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab, upon receiving news of the victory, instructed Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas to press the advantage and advance toward the Persian capital before the enemy could regroup.

Sa'd moved his forces from al-Qadisiyyah northeastward along the Euphrates, capturing the fortress town of Burs (Borsippa) and then the city of Babel without major resistance. Persian garrisons either fled or surrendered as word of the al-Qadisiyyah disaster spread through the empire. The advance covered roughly 300 kilometers of Mesopotamian terrain, and at each stop, Sa'd established order and appointed administrators in accordance with the instructions of Umar.

## The City and Its Defenses

Al-Mada'in was not a single city but a complex of seven interconnected settlements straddling the Tigris River. On the western bank stood the older Hellenistic city of Seleucia, while the eastern bank held the Sassanid royal quarter with its famous White Palace (Iwan Kisra), the arched throne hall of the Persian emperors. The Tigris itself served as the primary defensive barrier, and the Persians destroyed the bridges and gathered all available boats on the eastern side.

Emperor Yazdegerd III, the last Sassanid ruler, had initially attempted to organize a defense. However, the scale of the defeat at al-Qadisiyyah and the speed of the Muslim advance left him with few options. Before the Muslims reached the western bank, Yazdegerd gathered what he could of the imperial treasury and fled northward toward Hulwan, abandoning his capital.

## The Crossing of the Tigris

The most dramatic episode of the conquest was the crossing of the Tigris. With no boats available and the river swollen by seasonal flow, Sa'd consulted his officers. According to al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir, a group of Persian defectors indicated a crossing point, and Sa'd ordered his cavalry to enter the river on horseback. The Muslim horsemen plunged into the Tigris and crossed to the eastern bank, an act that astonished the remaining Persian defenders. Classical sources record that the Persians watching from the walls exclaimed that they were fighting not men but jinn, and resistance collapsed.

Al-Tabari narrates that the cavalry formed protective lines to shield those crossing behind them, and that not a single Muslim soldier was lost in the crossing. Whether one attributes this to exceptional military discipline, favorable river conditions, or divine aid, the crossing became a celebrated event in Islamic historical memory.

## The Fall of the White Palace

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas entered the White Palace of Khosrow and, upon seeing its immense grandeur, the towering arch, the gilded halls, and the ornate gardens, he recited the words of Allah: "How many gardens and springs they left behind, and fields and noble residences, and comfort in which they used to delight. Thus it was, and We made another people their inheritors" (al-Dukhan 44:25-28).

He then prayed salat al-fath (the prayer of victory) in the throne room itself, offering eight rak'ahs of thanks to Allah. This act carried profound symbolic weight: the prayer of the Muslim conqueror offered in the seat of the most powerful non-Arab empire in the world.

## The Spoils and Their Distribution

The treasury left behind by Yazdegerd was staggering. It included the royal crown, the famous carpet known as the Spring of Khosrow (a vast jeweled garden carpet), gold and silver vessels, silks, and weapons. Umar ibn al-Khattab ordered that the khums (one-fifth) be sent to Madinah and the remainder distributed among the soldiers according to the Quranic injunction. When the Spring of Khosrow carpet arrived in Madinah, Umar ordered it cut into pieces and distributed, prioritizing equity over preserving a single artifact of imperial splendor.

## Significance

The conquest of al-Mada'in ended Ctesiphon's role as an imperial capital after nearly eight centuries. Though Yazdegerd continued to resist from the Iranian plateau for another decade, his empire never recovered its western territories. For the Muslim ummah, the fall of Persia's capital confirmed the truthfulness of the Prophet's promise and demonstrated that faith and discipline could overcome the mightiest worldly powers. The event reshaped the political map of the ancient world permanently, opening the Iranian plateau, Central Asia, and beyond to the message of Islam.
