## The Battle of Mu'tah

The Battle of Mu'tah, fought in Jumada al-Ula 8 AH (September 629 CE), was the first direct military confrontation between the Muslims and the Byzantine Empire. It took place near the village of Mu'tah in the Balqa region of modern-day southern Jordan. Though the Muslim force was vastly outnumbered, the battle proved a turning point in Islamic military history and revealed the extraordinary talents of Khalid ibn al-Walid.

## Cause of the Expedition

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had sent al-Harith ibn Umayr al-Azdi as an envoy carrying a letter to the ruler of Busra, inviting him to Islam. While en route, al-Harith was intercepted and killed by Shurahbil ibn Amr al-Ghassani, the Ghassanid governor loyal to the Byzantine Emperor Heraclius. The killing of an ambassador was a severe violation of the norms observed among the Arabs and all civilized peoples. The Prophet ﷺ was deeply grieved by this and resolved to send a military expedition in response.

## The Muslim Force and Its Commanders

The Prophet ﷺ assembled approximately 3,000 fighters, the largest Muslim force dispatched up to that point aside from the battles fought near Medina itself. In a remarkable arrangement, he appointed three commanders in sequence. He said, as recorded by al-Bukhari: "The commander of the people is Zayd ibn Haritha. If Zayd is killed, then Ja'far ibn Abi Talib. If Ja'far is killed, then Abdullah ibn Rawaha."

Each of these men held a distinguished place among the Companions. Zayd ibn Haritha was the beloved freed slave and adopted son of the Prophet ﷺ. Ja'far ibn Abi Talib was the Prophet's cousin who had led the earlier migration to Abyssinia. Abdullah ibn Rawaha was one of the notable Ansar poets who had pledged allegiance at al-Aqaba.

## The Battlefield

When the Muslim army reached the region of Ma'an in southern Jordan, they received reports that the Byzantines had gathered a massive force. Classical sources, including Ibn Ishaq and Ibn Kathir, report the enemy numbered between 100,000 and 200,000, comprising Byzantine regulars and their Arab Christian allies from the Ghassanid, Lakhm, Judham, and Bahra tribes. While the exact figures may reflect the scale as perceived by the Muslim soldiers rather than a precise count, there is no dispute that the Muslims were severely outnumbered.

The Muslims deliberated for two nights at Ma'an. Some suggested sending word to the Prophet ﷺ for reinforcements. Abdullah ibn Rawaha stood and urged them forward, saying: "O people, by Allah, that which you dislike is the very thing you have come out seeking: martyrdom. We do not fight people with numbers or strength, but we fight them with this religion by which Allah has honoured us."

## The Three Martyrs

The engagement was fierce. Zayd ibn Haritha, carrying the banner of the Prophet ﷺ, fought at the front until he was struck down by enemy spears. Ja'far ibn Abi Talib seized the banner and fought on horseback. When his right hand was severed, he held it with his left. When his left hand was also cut off, he clasped the banner between his stumps until he was killed. Al-Bukhari narrates that the Prophet ﷺ later said Allah replaced Ja'far's arms with two wings in Paradise, earning him the title Ja'far al-Tayyar, "Ja'far the Flier." It is reported that over fifty wounds were found on his body, all on the front, none on the back. Abdullah ibn Rawaha then took the banner and fought until he too fell as a martyr.

## Khalid ibn al-Walid Takes Command

After the deaths of all three appointed commanders, the soldiers chose Khalid ibn al-Walid to lead them. Khalid had embraced Islam only months earlier, yet his military instincts were already formidable. He broke nine swords during the fighting that day, as he himself reported in a narration preserved by al-Bukhari.

The following day, Khalid reorganized the army. He swapped the positions of the right and left flanks and placed the rear guard at the front, creating the illusion for the Byzantines that fresh reinforcements had arrived. He then executed a controlled tactical withdrawal, extracting the Muslim force from an engagement that could easily have ended in their complete destruction. This manoeuvre ranks among the most remarkable retreats in military history.

## The Prophet's Response

In Medina, the Prophet ﷺ informed the Companions of the battle's events through revelation before any messenger arrived. He wept for Zayd and Ja'far and prayed for them. When Khalid returned, the Prophet ﷺ bestowed upon him the title Sayf Allah al-Maslul, "the Drawn Sword of Allah," an honour that would define Khalid's legacy for all time.

## Legacy and Significance

The Battle of Mu'tah, though militarily inconclusive, carried immense strategic weight. It announced to the Byzantine Empire and the entire Arabian Peninsula that the Muslims were willing and able to confront the greatest military power of the age. It established the northern frontier as a theatre of future operations, setting the stage for the expeditions to Tabuk and eventually the great conquests of Syria and Palestine under the Rashidun Caliphs. Above all, it enshrined the memory of three noble martyrs and revealed in Khalid ibn al-Walid a commander whose genius would reshape the map of the known world within a decade.
