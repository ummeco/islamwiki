## The Conquest of Sindh

The conquest of Sindh in 711 CE stands as one of the most consequential military campaigns of the Umayyad period, bringing the Indus Valley under Muslim governance and opening the gates through which Islam would eventually spread across the Indian subcontinent. Led by Muhammad ibn Qasim al-Thaqafi, a commander of remarkable youth and ability, the campaign demonstrated both the military reach of the Umayyad Caliphate and the administrative pragmatism that characterized early Muslim rule in non-Arab lands.

## Background and Causes

The origins of the Sindh campaign lay in a series of maritime incidents along the Arabian Sea. Pirates operating from the port of Debal, within the territory of Raja Dahir ibn Chach of Sindh, had been attacking Muslim merchant vessels. Among those seized were ships carrying Muslim women and orphans returning from Sri Lanka to Iraq. Appeals to Raja Dahir to release the captives and suppress the piracy went unanswered, with the raja claiming he had no authority over the pirates.

Al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf, the powerful governor of Iraq under Caliph al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik, took personal interest in the matter. After two smaller expeditions failed, al-Hajjaj resolved to send a decisive force. He appointed his young nephew and son-in-law, Muhammad ibn Qasim, then only seventeen years old, to lead the campaign. Despite his youth, Ibn Qasim had already gained military experience serving under al-Hajjaj's command.

## The Campaign

Muhammad ibn Qasim assembled a force of approximately six thousand cavalry and six thousand infantry, supplemented by a siege train that included catapults. The army marched through Makran, securing the coastal regions before advancing toward the port city of Debal in early 712 CE.

The siege of Debal was the first major engagement. The fortified city fell after sustained bombardment, and Ibn Qasim established it as a base of operations. From there, the Muslim forces advanced northward along the Indus River, taking the cities of Nerun, Sehwan, and other strategic positions. Local communities, many of whom had chafed under Brahmin rule, frequently offered submission without battle.

## The Battle of Aror

The decisive confrontation came at Aror (also known as Alor or Rohri) in 712 CE. Raja Dahir personally led a large force, mounted on a war elephant, to halt the Muslim advance. The battle was fiercely contested. During the fighting, Dahir's elephant was struck by a fire-arrow or naphtha projectile, causing chaos in the Sindhi ranks. Raja Dahir fell in the engagement, and his death shattered the organized resistance. His queens and remaining forces retreated to various strongholds, most of which fell in the following months.

By 713 CE, Muhammad ibn Qasim had extended Muslim control across Sindh and into southern Punjab, reaching as far as Multan, which he captured after a prolonged siege.

## Administration and Governance

What distinguished Muhammad ibn Qasim's rule was not merely the speed of conquest but the administrative framework he established. Drawing on precedents from the early caliphate's governance of diverse populations, he extended dhimmi status to the Hindu and Buddhist inhabitants of Sindh. Temples were left standing, local religious practices continued, and existing administrative structures were largely retained with Muslim oversight.

The chronicler al-Baladhuri, in his Futuh al-Buldan, records that Ibn Qasim appointed local officials to assist in governance and that taxation followed established patterns modified to conform with Islamic principles. This pragmatic approach facilitated a relatively stable transition of power and earned a degree of local cooperation.

The Chachnama, a later Persian account of the conquest, preserves detailed descriptions of these administrative arrangements, though historians note that it was composed centuries after the events and reflects the literary conventions of its own era.

## The Fate of Muhammad ibn Qasim

The story of Muhammad ibn Qasim ends in tragedy. When Caliph al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik died in 715 CE, the political landscape shifted. The new caliph, Sulayman ibn Abd al-Malik, harbored enmity toward al-Hajjaj's faction. Al-Hajjaj himself had already died in 714 CE, leaving Ibn Qasim without his chief patron. Muhammad ibn Qasim was recalled, arrested, and subjected to imprisonment and torture. He died in captivity, likely executed, at scarcely twenty years of age.

## Historical Significance

The conquest of Sindh opened a permanent corridor for Islamic civilization into the Indian subcontinent. While subsequent centuries would see fluctuations in Muslim political power in the region, the communities established during and after Ibn Qasim's campaign endured. Sindh became a center of Islamic learning and Sufi activity, and the model of coexistence with local religious communities influenced Muslim governance across South Asia for generations.

The campaign also demonstrated that the Umayyad Caliphate, at its zenith under al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik, could project military and administrative power across vast distances, from the Atlantic coast of North Africa to the banks of the Indus, within a single generation.
