## The Siege of Antioch (1098 CE / 490-491 AH)

The Siege of Antioch stands as one of the most consequential episodes of the First Crusade and a painful lesson in the cost of Muslim disunity. For nearly eight months, the ancient city on the Orontes River became the stage for a prolonged struggle that would determine the fate of the entire Levant.

## Antioch Before the Siege

Antioch (Antakya) had been under Muslim rule since the early Islamic conquests, when it was taken during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him). By the late fifth century AH, the city was governed by the Seljuk commander Yaghi-Siyan on behalf of Ridwan ibn Tutush, the Seljuk ruler of Aleppo. The city was a major urban center with massive Roman-era fortifications, its walls stretching across mountains and valleys with over four hundred towers. Its population included Muslims, Armenian Christians, and Greek Orthodox communities.

The broader Muslim world at this time was fractured. The Seljuk sultanate had splintered into rival successor states. Ridwan in Aleppo and his brother Duqaq in Damascus were locked in rivalry. The Fatimid Isma'ili state in Egypt, hostile to the Sunni Seljuks, saw the crusader invasion as an opportunity rather than a threat. This fragmentation would prove decisive.

## The Crusader Arrival and Muslim Defense

The crusader armies, numbering between thirty and forty thousand, arrived before Antioch's walls in October 1097 CE (Shawwal 490 AH). Yaghi-Siyan, recognizing the scale of the threat, immediately sent appeals for help to Ridwan in Aleppo, Duqaq in Damascus, and Kerbogha, the atabeg of Mosul.

Yaghi-Siyan proved a capable defender. He expelled members of the Greek Orthodox community whom he suspected of potential collaboration with the Franks, while retaining the Armenian population. He organized sorties against crusader positions and maintained supply lines into the city for as long as possible. Ibn al-Athir recorded that Yaghi-Siyan's defense was vigorous and that his men fought with determination throughout the winter months.

The siege ground on through a brutal winter. Both sides suffered from famine and disease. The crusaders, unable to fully encircle the massive walls, struggled to maintain their blockade. Muslim relief forces attempted to break through. Duqaq of Damascus led an army that was defeated in late December 1097, and Ridwan of Aleppo brought a force in February 1098 that was similarly repulsed. These efforts were small and uncoordinated, each emir acting independently rather than mounting a combined campaign.

## The Betrayal and Fall

The city's fall came not through military assault but through treachery. An Armenian tower commander named Firuz, who had converted to Islam but harbored personal grievances against Yaghi-Siyan, secretly negotiated with the Norman leader Bohemond. On the night of June 3, 1098 CE (Rajab 491 AH), Firuz opened the Tower of the Two Sisters, allowing Bohemond's men to scale the walls under cover of darkness.

What followed was devastating. The crusaders poured into the city and carried out a general massacre. Ibn al-Athir and other Muslim historians recorded that thousands of Muslims were killed indiscriminately. Yaghi-Siyan fled the city but was captured and killed by Armenian villagers. The great mosques were desecrated and converted into churches. Much of the Muslim population that survived was enslaved or expelled.

## Kerbogha's Failed Relief

In a bitter irony, Kerbogha of Mosul arrived with a large relief army the very next day, too late to save the city. He now besieged the crusaders inside Antioch's walls, and for several weeks the Franks endured the same starvation they had inflicted. Yet Kerbogha's coalition was fatally weakened by internal rivalries. The emirs under his command distrusted his ambitions and resented his authority. When the desperate crusaders sortied out on June 28, 1098, Kerbogha's allied emirs withdrew their contingents one by one. His army collapsed without a decisive battle, and the crusaders secured their hold on the city.

## Lessons and Legacy

Muslim historians consistently identified the fall of Antioch as a direct consequence of disunity. Ibn al-Athir wrote with evident frustration about the failure of Muslim rulers to set aside their rivalries. Had Ridwan, Duqaq, and Kerbogha coordinated a single combined effort during the long months of siege, the outcome could have been very different.

The loss of Antioch opened the road south to Jerusalem, which fell the following year. It would take nearly a century before Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi united the Muslim front sufficiently to begin reclaiming what had been lost. The siege remains a sobering reminder that military strength without political unity is insufficient, a principle the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) emphasized when he said: "The believer to the believer is like a structure, each part supporting the other" (al-Bukhari and Muslim).

Antioch itself would not return to Muslim rule until the Mamluk sultan Baybars recaptured it in 1268 CE (666 AH), ending one hundred and seventy years of crusader occupation.
