## Ishaq in the Quran

Ishaq (Isaac), peace be upon him, is mentioned by name seventeen times in the Quran. He appears consistently alongside his father Ibrahim and his brother Ismail as one of the righteous prophets chosen by Allah. The Quran does not narrate a detailed biography of Ishaq in the way it does for Musa or Yusuf, but it establishes his place firmly within the prophetic chain and affirms his status as a messenger who received revelation and guided his people to the worship of Allah alone.

Allah says: "And We bestowed upon him Ishaq and Yaqub. Each of them We guided. And Nuh We guided before, and among his descendants, Dawud and Sulayman and Ayyub and Yusuf and Musa and Harun. Thus do We reward those who do good" (Surah al-An'am 6:84).

## The Miraculous Birth

The birth of Ishaq was among the signs of Allah's power over all things. His mother Sarah was elderly and barren, having never borne children throughout her long life with Ibrahim. When the angels came to Ibrahim on their way to execute Allah's decree upon the people of Lut, they brought glad tidings of a son.

Sarah, who was present, reacted with astonishment. The Quran records her words: "Woe to me! Shall I give birth while I am an old woman and this, my husband, is an old man? Indeed, this is a strange thing!" The angels replied: "Are you amazed at the decree of Allah? May the mercy of Allah and His blessings be upon you, people of the house. Indeed, He is Praiseworthy and Honorable" (Surah Hud 11:72-73).

In another passage, the glad tidings are described with additional detail: "And We gave him good tidings of Ishaq, a prophet from among the righteous. And We blessed him and Ishaq. But among their descendants are those who do good and those who clearly wrong themselves" (Surah as-Saffat 37:112-113). This verse establishes that the blessing upon Ishaq was not unconditional for all his descendants. Righteousness remained a matter of individual choice, and many among Banu Isra'il would later stray from the path of their forefathers.

## The Son of Ibrahim's Gratitude

Ibrahim, who had waited decades for children, expressed profound gratitude to Allah upon the births of both his sons. The Quran preserves his supplication: "Praise to Allah, who has granted to me in old age Ismail and Ishaq. Indeed, my Lord is the Hearer of supplication" (Surah Ibrahim 14:39).

Ibn Kathir notes in his tafsir that Ibrahim was granted Ismail first, when he was eighty-six years old, and Ishaq later, when he was even more advanced in age. Both sons were gifts from Allah in response to Ibrahim's sincere prayers, and both would carry forward the mission of tawhid to their respective peoples.

## Ishaq's Prophethood and Legacy

The Quran affirms that Ishaq received divine guidance and was counted among the elect. Allah says: "And We gave him Ishaq and Yaqub — all of them We guided. And Nuh We guided before" (Surah al-An'am 6:84). In Surah Maryam, Allah commands the Prophet Muhammad to remember Ibrahim and states: "And We gave him Ishaq and Yaqub, and each of them We made a prophet. And We gave them of Our mercy, and We made for them a truthful mention of high esteem" (Surah Maryam 19:49-50).

Ishaq settled in the land of Canaan, in the region of Palestine and greater Syria, continuing the call to the worship of Allah alone that his father had established. He married and fathered Yaqub (Jacob), who would later be given the title Isra'il. Yaqub's twelve sons became the ancestors of the twelve tribes of Banu Isra'il, from whom an extraordinary succession of prophets would emerge, including Yusuf, Musa, Harun, Dawud, Sulayman, Zakariyya, Yahya, and Isa ibn Maryam.

## The Two Branches of Ibrahim's Mission

Ishaq holds a pivotal position in the broader architecture of prophethood. Through him, the Israelite prophetic tradition was born, a line that would span centuries and encompass dozens of messengers. Through his brother Ismail, the Arabian prophetic line was preserved, culminating in the final messenger, Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him.

The Quran draws attention to this dual legacy when it records the supplication of Ibrahim and Ismail as they raised the foundations of the Ka'bah: "Our Lord, and send among them a messenger from themselves" (Surah al-Baqarah 2:129). The two sons of Ibrahim thus represent the two great branches of the Abrahamic mission: one culminating in the prophets of Banu Isra'il and the other in the Seal of the Prophets.

## Lessons from Ishaq's Story

The story of Ishaq reinforces several principles central to Islamic belief. First, that Allah's power is unconstrained by natural causes. A barren woman of advanced age bore a prophet by Allah's command alone. Second, that prophethood is a gift from Allah, not earned by lineage. The Quran's explicit mention that among Ishaq's descendants were both righteous and wrongdoers dispels any notion of inherited spiritual privilege. Third, that gratitude to Allah for His blessings is the mark of a true believer, as demonstrated by Ibrahim's prayer upon receiving his sons.

Ishaq, peace be upon him, stands as a noble link in the golden chain of prophets, a righteous servant through whom Allah fulfilled His promise to Ibrahim and established a prophetic tradition that would guide humanity for millennia until the coming of the final revelation.
