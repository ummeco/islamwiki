## The Fall of Acre (1291 CE / 690 AH)

The fall of Acre in Jumada al-Ula 690 AH (May 1291 CE) stands as one of the most consequential events in Islamic military history. It marked the definitive end of the Crusader political presence in the Levant, closing a chapter of foreign occupation that had persisted for nearly two centuries since the First Crusade captured Jerusalem in 492 AH (1099 CE).

## Background and Context

After Salah ad-Din al-Ayyubi's liberation of Jerusalem in 583 AH (1187 CE), the Crusaders had been pushed into an increasingly narrow coastal strip. Acre, known in Arabic as Akka, served as the capital of what remained of the Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem. The city was heavily fortified with double walls and defended by the combined forces of the Knights Templar, the Hospitallers, the Teutonic Knights, and Italian merchant garrisons from Venice and Genoa.

The Mamluk sultanate, which had risen to power after defeating both the Crusaders and the Mongols at Ayn Jalut in 658 AH (1260 CE), pursued a policy of systematically dismantling Crusader strongholds. Sultan Baybars and his successor Qalawun captured fortress after fortress throughout the 1260s-1280s. By the late 1280s, Acre stood virtually alone as the last significant Crusader holding.

Sultan Qalawun had planned the final campaign against Acre but died in 689 AH (1290 CE) before he could carry it out. His son, al-Ashraf Salah ad-Din Khalil ibn Qalawun, inherited both the throne and his father's determination to complete the liberation of the coast.

## The Siege

Al-Ashraf Khalil assembled one of the largest siege forces of the medieval period. Historical sources, including the accounts of Abu al-Fida and al-Maqrizi, describe an army drawn from across the Mamluk domains, with contingents arriving from Egypt, Syria, and the frontier regions. The sultan reportedly gathered over one hundred siege engines, including massive trebuchets capable of hurling stones weighing hundreds of pounds.

The siege began in early Rabi al-Awwal 690 AH (April 1291 CE). The Mamluk forces surrounded the city from the landward side while maintaining pressure on its defences through continuous bombardment. The Crusader garrison, though outnumbered, mounted a determined defence. They launched several sorties against the siege lines, and their fortifications proved formidable.

After approximately six weeks of sustained assault, the Mamluk engineers and soldiers breached the outer walls. On 17 Jumada al-Ula 690 AH (18 May 1291 CE), al-Ashraf Khalil ordered a general assault. The fighting within the city was fierce and prolonged. The Knights Templar retreated to their fortified tower near the harbour, where they held out for several more days before the structure collapsed, killing both defenders and attackers.

## The Aftermath

Much of Acre's Christian civilian population had already fled by sea during the siege. Those who remained faced the consequences of the city's fall. Al-Ashraf Khalil ordered the systematic demolition of Acre's fortifications, walls, and major buildings. This was a deliberate strategic decision to prevent the city from ever again serving as a Crusader bridgehead.

In the weeks following, the remaining Crusader outposts along the coast fell in rapid succession. Tyre, Sidon, Beirut, and the Templar fortress of Atlit were either captured or abandoned. By the end of 690 AH, no Crusader garrison remained on the Levantine mainland.

## Historical Significance

The fall of Acre carried profound significance for the Muslim world. It represented the fulfilment of a struggle that had been waged across generations, from Imad ad-Din Zangi's capture of Edessa in 539 AH, through Nur ad-Din's unification of Syria, Salah ad-Din's liberation of Jerusalem, and the Mamluk campaigns that followed.

Al-Ashraf Khalil was celebrated across the Muslim lands. Poets composed verses in his honour, and the news was received with great joy in Cairo and Damascus. The sultan took the title "Sultan of Islam and the Muslims" in recognition of his achievement.

In Europe, the fall of Acre provoked shock and grief. Calls for new crusades echoed through the courts of Christendom, but no effective expedition ever materialized. The era of the Crusades in the Holy Land was over.

## Legacy

The Mamluk victory at Acre confirmed Muslim sovereignty over Palestine and the greater Levant, a sovereignty that would continue unbroken under the Mamluks and later the Ottomans for over six centuries. The event demonstrated the strength of the Mamluk military system and its capacity to achieve what earlier Muslim states had pursued for generations. The Holy Land, sacred to Muslims as the land of al-Isra and home to Masjid al-Aqsa, was secured under Muslim governance, fulfilling a responsibility that Muslim leaders from Umar ibn al-Khattab onward had shouldered with seriousness and resolve.
