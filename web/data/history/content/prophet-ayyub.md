## Prophet Ayyub (Job)

Ayyub (peace be upon him) is among the prophets mentioned in the Quran as a model of patience, gratitude, and unwavering devotion to Allah through prolonged affliction. His story stands as one of the most profound accounts of trial and divine mercy in all of prophetic tradition.

## Lineage and Status

Ayyub is mentioned by name four times in the Quran. Allah lists him among the guided prophets: "And Isma'il and al-Yasa' and Yunus and Lut — and all of them We preferred over the worlds" (Al-An'am 6:86). He is also mentioned in An-Nisa 4:163 among those who received revelation. Ibn Kathir and other scholars of tafsir place his lineage among the descendants of Ibrahim (peace be upon him), and some narrations trace his ancestry through al-'Is ibn Ishaq. His wife is identified in classical sources as Rahmah, though some scholars mention her name as Liyya.

## A Life of Blessing and Piety

Before his trial, Ayyub lived in prosperity. He possessed wealth, land, livestock, and a large family. He was known for his deep gratitude to Allah, his generosity toward the poor, and his constant worship. Classical scholars emphasize that his blessings were not merely material but were accompanied by sincere devotion — he used every provision as a means of drawing closer to Allah and serving others.

## The Great Trial

Allah tested Ayyub with a severity rarely matched among the prophets. He lost his wealth, his children perished, and a devastating illness struck his body. Classical mufassirun such as Ibn Jarir al-Tabari mention that his illness lasted for an extended period — some narrations state eighteen years, though this figure comes from tafsir tradition and Israelite reports rather than from the Quran or authenticated hadith.

His affliction was so severe that people abandoned him. Only his wife remained faithful, caring for him through years of hardship. Yet through all of this, Ayyub did not waver in his worship or his trust in Allah. He did not complain against his Lord or question the divine decree.

## The Supplication

When his suffering reached its peak, Ayyub called upon Allah with one of the most moving prayers recorded in the Quran:

> "And remember Our servant Ayyub, when he called to his Lord: 'Indeed, Satan has touched me with hardship and torment.'" (Sad 38:41)

And in Surah Al-Anbiya:

> "And Ayyub, when he called to his Lord: 'Indeed, adversity has touched me, and You are the Most Merciful of the merciful.'" (Al-Anbiya 21:83)

The beauty of this supplication lies in its character. Ayyub did not demand relief or express bitterness. He simply stated his condition and affirmed Allah's attribute of mercy. He made no complaint against the decree — only a humble acknowledgment of his need for his Lord. Scholars of tafsir note that this is the adab (etiquette) of the prophets in supplication: expressing need while maintaining complete trust.

## Allah's Response and Healing

Allah responded to Ayyub's prayer with immediate mercy:

> "Strike the ground with your foot — this is a spring for a cool bath and drink." (Sad 38:42)

Ayyub was commanded to strike the earth, and a spring of water gushed forth. He bathed in it and drank from it, and his illness was removed completely. Allah then restored his family, his wealth, and multiplied his blessings:

> "So We responded to him and removed what afflicted him of adversity. And We gave him back his family and the like thereof with them as mercy from Us and a reminder for the worshippers of Allah." (Al-Anbiya 21:84)

## Divine Praise

Allah praised Ayyub with words that carry immense weight:

> "Indeed, We found him patient — an excellent servant. Indeed, he was one repeatedly turning back to Allah." (Sad 38:44)

The term *awwab* — one who constantly returns to Allah — captures the essence of Ayyub's character. His patience was not passive resignation but an active, conscious turning toward his Lord in every moment of difficulty.

## The Oath Regarding His Wife

The Quran also records a detail about Ayyub's wife. During his illness, Ayyub made an oath to strike her — classical scholars differ on the reason, but the majority view is that she said something displeasing during a moment of desperation. When he recovered, Allah instructed him to fulfill his oath gently: "Take in your hand a bundle of grass and strike with it, and do not break your oath" (Sad 38:44). This was a mercy from Allah to both Ayyub and his loyal wife, who had stood by him through years of hardship.

## Lessons from Ayyub's Story

The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "The most severely tested among people are the prophets, then the next best, then the next best" (Tirmidhi, graded sahih). Among the prophets, Ayyub stands as the supreme embodiment of this principle.

His story teaches that trial is not a sign of divine displeasure but can be a sign of divine love. Patient endurance through suffering is itself a form of worship. Gratitude is not only for times of ease but is most meaningful when maintained through hardship. And Allah's mercy, when it comes, is complete — He does not merely remove affliction but restores and multiplies what was lost.

Ayyub's name is invoked across the Muslim world whenever believers face difficulty, a living reminder that the path of the prophets passes through trial, and that the one who endures with patience and turns to Allah will find relief, restoration, and praise from the Lord of all creation.
