## The Expedition of Tabuk

The Expedition of Tabuk (Ghazwat Tabuk), which took place in Rajab 9 AH (October 630 CE), was the last and largest military campaign led by the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ during his lifetime. Mobilising approximately 30,000 soldiers to march over 600 kilometres northward through the Arabian desert, Tabuk stands as a defining moment of the late Prophetic era, separating the sincere believers from the hypocrites in a trial of extraordinary difficulty.

## Background and Context

By 9 AH, the Arabian Peninsula had largely come under Muslim authority following the Conquest of Makkah and the victory at Hunayn. The Prophet ﷺ received intelligence reports that the Byzantine Emperor Heraclius was amassing a large force along the northern frontier of Arabia, near the region of al-Balqa in modern-day Jordan. Some reports indicated that Arab client tribes allied to Byzantium, such as the Ghassanids and Lakhmids, had joined the mobilisation.

The timing posed severe hardship. It was the height of summer, the dates were ripening for harvest, and Madinah faced scorching heat. Unlike previous campaigns where the Prophet ﷺ sometimes concealed the destination, he announced the target openly so that the Muslims could prepare for the long and gruelling march.

## The Mobilisation and the Test of Faith

The call to march became a crucible that exposed the inner condition of every Muslim in Madinah. The wealthy Companions responded generously. Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him) funded the equipping of roughly one-third of the army, earning the Prophet's ﷺ praise: "Nothing Uthman does after today will harm him" (al-Tirmidhi). Abu Bakr al-Siddiq brought his entire household wealth, and Umar ibn al-Khattab contributed half of his.

By contrast, many of the Munafiqun (hypocrites) fabricated excuses. Some asked for permission to remain behind, claiming they feared temptation if they saw the women of the Byzantine lands. Others simply stayed without seeking any permission at all. Surah at-Tawbah was revealed extensively addressing this campaign, exposing the hypocrisy of those who abandoned the cause and commending the faithful who endured.

The army that assembled was called Jaysh al-Usrah, the "Army of Hardship," because provisions were so scarce that groups of soldiers shared single dates and took turns riding a single camel.

## The March and Outcome

The Muslim army marched northward through the Hijaz and into the desert of northwestern Arabia, arriving at Tabuk after approximately twenty days. Upon arrival, they found no Byzantine force waiting to engage. Whether the Byzantines had withdrawn upon learning of the massive Muslim mobilisation or the initial reports had been exaggerated remains debated among historians. Ibn Kathir and other scholars note that the Byzantines chose not to confront the Muslim army.

The Prophet ﷺ remained at Tabuk for approximately twenty days. During this period, several northern border chieftains, including Yuhanna ibn Ru'bah of Aylah (modern Aqaba) and the people of Jarba and Adhruh, came to negotiate peace treaties. They agreed to pay jizyah in exchange for Muslim protection. Khalid ibn al-Walid was dispatched to Dumat al-Jandal, where he secured the submission of the Christian ruler Ukaydir ibn Abd al-Malik without a fight.

## The Three Who Were Left Behind

Among the most significant events connected to Tabuk is the story of three sincere Companions who stayed behind without valid excuse: Ka'b ibn Malik, Murarah ibn al-Rabi', and Hilal ibn Umayyah. Unlike the hypocrites who fabricated lies, these three men chose to tell the Prophet ﷺ the truth about their failure to join.

As a consequence, the Prophet ﷺ ordered the Muslims to boycott them completely. For fifty days, no one spoke to them, and their wives were instructed to separate from them. The earth, despite its vastness, felt constricted upon them, as described in the Quran. Finally, Allah revealed their forgiveness in Surah at-Tawbah (9:118): *"And upon the three who were left behind, until the earth became strait for them despite its vastness, and their own souls became strait for them, and they were certain that there was no refuge from Allah except unto Him. Then He turned to them so they could repent. Indeed, Allah is the Accepting of repentance, the Merciful."*

Ka'b ibn Malik later described this as the greatest blessing of his life after Islam, because his truthfulness saved him while the lies of the hypocrites led to their ruin.

## Strategic and Religious Significance

Though no battle was fought, Tabuk achieved several critical objectives. It demonstrated that the Muslim state possessed the logistical and military capacity to project force to the very borders of the Byzantine Empire, the superpower of the age. The treaties secured with northern border tribes established a buffer zone that would prove essential during the Riddah Wars and the subsequent conquests of al-Sham under Abu Bakr and Umar.

Religiously, Tabuk served as a final separation between true faith and hypocrisy. The extensive Quranic commentary in Surah at-Tawbah laid down permanent principles regarding sincerity, sacrifice, and the obligation of jihad when called upon by legitimate authority. The story of the three who were left behind remains one of the most powerful lessons in Islamic tradition on the virtue of truthfulness and the mercy of divine repentance.
