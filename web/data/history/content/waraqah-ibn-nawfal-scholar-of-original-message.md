## Waraqah ibn Nawfal: The Scholar Who Recognized the Final Prophet

Among the most significant figures of pre-Islamic Makkah stands Waraqah ibn Nawfal ibn Asad ibn Abd al-Uzza — a man whose life bridged the gap between the scripture of old and the revelation to come. A paternal cousin of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with her), Waraqah was one of the rare individuals in the Arabian Peninsula who had rejected the idolatry of Quraysh and devoted himself to the study of previous scriptures. His encounter with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ at the very dawn of revelation remains one of the most carefully preserved moments in Islamic historical literature.

## A Monotheist Among Idolaters

Waraqah lived in Makkah during the late fifth and early sixth centuries of the Common Era — a time when the city's religious life was dominated by the worship of idols housed in and around the Ka'bah. While most of Quraysh followed the polytheism of their forefathers, a small number of individuals known as the Hunafa' rejected this path and sought the original monotheism of Ibrahim (peace be upon him). Waraqah went further than most of these seekers. He embraced Christianity, not the popular paganism of his tribe, and undertook the study of the Torah and the Injil in Hebrew — a depth of scriptural knowledge that was exceptional in the Arabian context of his era.

Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani notes in *Fath al-Bari* that Waraqah was literate in Hebrew scripture and had spent years copying and studying the sacred texts. This was not casual interest. Waraqah had oriented his entire life around the knowledge preserved in those scriptures, and this preparation would prove providential.

## The Encounter After the First Revelation

The story of Waraqah's most consequential moment is preserved in the opening hadith of *Sahih al-Bukhari* (no. 3) — one of the most important and widely transmitted narrations in all of hadith literature. When the Prophet ﷺ received the first verses of Surah al-Alaq in the Cave of Hira through Jibril (peace be upon him), he descended from the mountain in a state of trembling and sought the comfort of Khadijah. She wrapped him in a garment and, once he had calmed, took him to her cousin Waraqah.

The Prophet ﷺ described what he had experienced. Waraqah's response has been transmitted to every generation of Muslims since:

> *"This is the Namus (the angel of revelation) whom Allah sent to Musa. Would that I were young and could live to the time when your people will turn you out."*

The Prophet ﷺ asked in surprise: "Will they really drive me out?" Waraqah replied: "Yes. No man has ever come with something like what you have brought except that he was treated with hostility. If I reach that day, I will support you with all the help I can."

## The Significance of His Words

Waraqah's declaration carries weight on several levels. First, he identified the experience as prophethood immediately and without hesitation, recognizing that the same Jibril who had come to Musa (peace be upon him) had now come to Muhammad ﷺ. This was not guesswork but the conclusion of a lifetime spent with the scriptures that foretold this very event.

Second, he predicted the persecution that would follow — drawing from his knowledge of the consistent pattern of prophetic history. Every messenger before had been opposed by his own people, and Waraqah understood that Muhammad ﷺ would be no exception.

Third, he pledged his support. Despite his old age and frailty, his instinct upon recognizing the truth was to commit himself to its defense.

## His Death and His Fate

Waraqah died shortly after this encounter, before the public call to Islam had begun. The *fatrah* — the brief pause in revelation — had not yet ended. Regarding his fate, the Prophet ﷺ indicated in an authentic narration that he had seen Waraqah in a vision wearing white garments, which scholars such as al-Nawawi and Ibn Hajar interpreted as a sign of his being among the people of Paradise. He had recognized the truth with sincerity the moment it was presented to him and affirmed it with his tongue. For one who died before the full message could reach him, this sincere recognition was accepted by Allah.

## His Place in Islamic History

Waraqah ibn Nawfal represents a type that appears at critical junctures in the prophetic narrative: the sincere monotheist shaped by an earlier tradition who preserves enough of the original truth to recognize the real thing when it arrives. He did not worship idols. He did not follow the majority of Quraysh into paganism. He sat with the ancient scriptures and kept his heart oriented toward the God of Ibrahim, Musa, and Isa (peace be upon them all).

When the final prophet appeared in his own household, Waraqah was the first to name what had happened. In doing so, he drew the unbroken line from Musa to Isa to Muhammad ﷺ — confirming that the message was one and the same, and that its final bearer had arrived.

His story, placed at the very opening of *Sahih al-Bukhari*, serves as a reminder that the coming of the Prophet ﷺ was not an isolated event but the fulfillment of what the earlier scriptures had foretold. Those with knowledge and sincerity recognized it. Waraqah was among the first.
