## Ibrahim: The Friend of Allah

Ibrahim (peace be upon him) stands among the greatest of all prophets and messengers sent by Allah. The Quran refers to him as Khalil Allah, the intimate friend of Allah, a title bestowed upon no other prophet. Allah says: "And Allah took Ibrahim as an intimate friend" (An-Nisa 4:125). He is also known as Abu al-Anbiya, the father of the prophets, for both the Israelite prophetic line through his son Ishaq and the Arab prophetic line through his son Ismail trace back to him, culminating in the final messenger Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him).

## Early Life and Rejection of Idolatry

Ibrahim was born in ancient Iraq during the reign of the tyrant king Nimrod, into a family and community devoted to idol worship. His own father Azar carved and sold idols. Yet from a young age, Ibrahim's sound nature (fitrah) led him to question the worship of created things. The Quran records his profound spiritual reasoning in Surah Al-An'am (6:75-79), where he observed a star, the moon, and the sun, each time declaring when it set: "I do not love those that set." He concluded with the declaration: "I have turned my face toward He who created the heavens and the earth, inclining toward truth, and I am not of those who associate others with Allah."

He confronted his father with gentleness, saying: "O my father, why do you worship that which does not hear and does not see and will not benefit you at all?" (Maryam 19:42). When his people refused his call, Ibrahim devised a plan to demonstrate the powerlessness of their gods. He smashed their idols, leaving only the largest one intact. When questioned, he challenged them: "Rather, this, the largest of them, did it. So ask them, if they should speak" (Al-Anbiya 21:63). Unable to answer, they resorted to violence.

## The Trial of Fire

Nimrod and his people built a massive fire and cast Ibrahim into it. Allah commanded: "O fire, be coolness and safety upon Ibrahim" (Al-Anbiya 21:69). The fire obeyed its Lord and Ibrahim emerged completely unharmed. This miracle stands as one of the most striking signs recorded in the Quran, demonstrating that all creation submits to Allah's command. Ibn Kathir relates that the fire burned only his bonds, leaving Ibrahim free and at peace within it.

## Migration and the Settlement of Mecca

After his deliverance, Ibrahim migrated from Iraq, traveling through the lands of Sham (greater Syria) and Egypt. He married Sarah and later Hajar. By divine command, Ibrahim took Hajar and their infant son Ismail to the barren, waterless valley of Mecca and left them there with only a leather bag of dates and a water-skin. When Hajar asked if Allah had commanded this, and Ibrahim confirmed it, she said: "Then He will not forsake us." This trust was rewarded when the angel Jibril struck the ground and the spring of Zamzam gushed forth, a source of water that flows to this day. The Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "May Allah have mercy on the mother of Ismail. Had she left Zamzam alone, it would have been a flowing stream" (Sahih al-Bukhari 3364).

## The Supreme Sacrifice

The greatest trial in Ibrahim's life came when Allah commanded him in a dream to sacrifice his son. Ibrahim informed his son, who responded with extraordinary submission: "O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, of the steadfast" (As-Saffat 37:102). When both had submitted and Ibrahim laid his son's forehead upon the ground and raised the knife, Allah called out: "O Ibrahim, you have fulfilled the vision" (As-Saffat 37:104-105). A ram was sent down as a ransom. This act of total surrender to Allah is commemorated annually by the Muslim ummah during Eid al-Adha, when millions sacrifice animals following the Sunnah of Ibrahim.

The majority of early scholars, including Ibn Abbas, Ibn Mas'ud, and others from the Companions, held that the son offered for sacrifice was Ismail, not Ishaq. Ibn Kathir discussed both positions and leaned toward Ismail based on the Quranic sequence of events.

## Building the Ka'bah

Ibrahim and Ismail were later commanded to raise the foundations of the Ka'bah in Mecca, the first house of worship established for mankind. They prayed together: "Our Lord, accept this from us. You are the Hearing, the Knowing" (Al-Baqarah 2:127). Ibrahim then called humanity to perform the Hajj pilgrimage, as Allah commanded: "And proclaim to the people the Hajj; they will come to you on foot and on every lean camel" (Al-Hajj 22:27). The Ka'bah remains the qiblah of over a billion Muslims, and the rites of Hajj preserve the memory of Ibrahim, Hajar, and Ismail in every step.

## Legacy

Allah describes Ibrahim's religion as the Hanif creed, pure monotheism free from any association with Allah. The Quran commands the Prophet Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) and his followers: "Follow the religion of Ibrahim, inclining toward truth" (Al Imran 3:95). His legacy is not merely historical. Every Muslim invokes blessings upon Ibrahim and his family in every prayer through the Ibrahimic salawat. He was a nation unto himself (An-Nahl 16:120), a model of patience, gratitude, and complete submission to Allah.
