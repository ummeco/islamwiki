## Prophet Ismail in Islam

Ismail (Ishmael), peace be upon him, is one of the most honored prophets in Islam. The firstborn son of Prophet Ibrahim and his wife Hajar, he is recognized as the forefather of the Arab peoples and the ancestor through whom the lineage of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ descends. His life, from infancy to prophethood, was defined by submission to Allah and unwavering patience through extraordinary trials.

## Birth and the Journey to Mecca

Ibrahim had long prayed for a righteous son. Allah answered his supplication, and Hajar bore him Ismail. When the child was still an infant, Allah commanded Ibrahim to take Hajar and Ismail to a barren, waterless valley in the Hijaz — the place that would one day become Mecca.

Ibrahim obeyed, leaving his wife and infant son in a desolate land with only a leather bag of dates and a water skin. As he turned to leave, Hajar called after him, asking whether this was by Allah's command. When Ibrahim confirmed it was, she said: "Then He will not neglect us." This response, recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, demonstrates the depth of her trust in Allah's provision.

## The Miracle of Zamzam

With their supplies exhausted and the infant Ismail crying from thirst, Hajar ran frantically between the hills of Safa and Marwa seven times, searching the horizon for any sign of help. At the moment of greatest desperation, the angel Jibril struck the ground near the infant's feet, and the spring of Zamzam burst forth. This water has never ceased flowing, sustaining the city of Mecca for thousands of years.

The tribe of Jurhum, passing through the area and observing birds circling above, discovered Hajar and the spring. They sought permission to settle near her, and Mecca grew from this settlement. Ismail grew up among the Jurhum, learning Arabic from them and eventually marrying from among their women.

The ritual of sa'y — walking between Safa and Marwa — performed by every Muslim during Hajj and Umrah directly commemorates Hajar's desperate search and her trust in Allah.

## The Supreme Test: The Sacrifice

The greatest trial in Ismail's life came when his father Ibrahim saw in a dream that he must sacrifice his son. Prophetic dreams are a form of revelation, and Ibrahim understood this as a divine command. The Quran records the exchange between father and son in Surah As-Saffat (37:102):

> "O my son, indeed I have seen in a dream that I must sacrifice you, so see what you think." He said: "O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, of the steadfast."

The response of the young Ismail is remarkable. He did not flee, resist, or despair. He submitted willingly and encouraged his father to fulfill the command. When Ibrahim laid his son down and drew the knife, Allah called out to him: "O Ibrahim, you have fulfilled the vision." A ram was sent down as a substitute sacrifice.

This event is the foundation of Eid al-Adha, the greatest annual celebration in Islam. The majority of scholars from the Salaf, including Ibn Abbas, Ibn Umar, and Ka'b al-Ahbar, held that it was Ismail who was the son offered for sacrifice, not Ishaq. Ibn Kathir affirmed this position in his tafsir, noting that the context of Surah As-Saffat places the sacrifice before the announcement of Ishaq's birth.

## Building the Ka'bah

After the trial of sacrifice, Allah honored Ibrahim and Ismail with the task of raising the foundations of the Ka'bah, the first house of worship established for mankind. The Quran records their supplication in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:127):

> "Our Lord, accept this from us. Indeed, You are the Hearing, the Knowing."

They prayed that Allah would make them and their descendants a nation submissive to Him, and that He would raise from among their offspring a messenger — a prayer fulfilled centuries later with the coming of Muhammad ﷺ.

## Prophethood and Character

Ismail was not merely Ibrahim's son. He was himself a prophet and messenger sent to the people of the Hijaz. The Quran praises him in Surah Maryam (19:54-55):

> "And mention in the Book Ismail. Indeed, he was true to his promise, and he was a messenger and a prophet. And he used to enjoin on his people prayer and zakah, and was to his Lord pleasing."

The title "true to his promise" (sadiq al-wa'd) is singled out as his defining quality. Classical commentators note that this referred both to his general character and specifically to his willingness to fulfill his word when he consented to the sacrifice.

## Legacy

Ismail settled in Mecca, and his descendants became the Arab tribes of the Hijaz. Through his son Kedar (Qaydar), the lineage continued until it reached the Quraysh and ultimately the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. The Prophet himself said: "Allah chose Ismail from the sons of Ibrahim" (Sahih Muslim).

The rites of Hajj — the sa'y, the sacrifice, the gathering at the Ka'bah — all trace back to the family of Ibrahim and Ismail. Every year, millions of Muslims relive their story, affirming the same principle that defined their lives: complete submission to Allah, whatever the cost.
