## Prophet Hud and the People of 'Ad

Hud (peace be upon him) was one of the earliest prophets sent after the time of Nuh. Allah sent him to the people of 'Ad, a powerful Arab civilization that inhabited the region of al-Ahqaf in southern Arabia, in the area of present-day Yemen and the Empty Quarter. The story of Hud and the 'Ad is one of the most frequently referenced accounts of destroyed nations in the Quran, serving as a warning against arrogance and the rejection of divine guidance.

## The Civilization of 'Ad

The 'Ad were among the mightiest nations of the ancient world. Allah had blessed them with extraordinary physical strength, tall stature, and abundant resources. They built lofty structures and fortified dwellings, as the Quran records: "Do you build on every elevation a sign, amusing yourselves, and take for yourselves constructions that you might abide eternally?" (Al-Shu'ara 26:128-129). The city of Iram, described in Surah al-Fajr as "Iram of the pillars, the like of which had not been created among the lands" (89:7-8), is associated with this civilization.

Despite these blessings, the 'Ad turned to idol worship, oppressed the weak, and grew arrogant in the land. They believed their power made them invincible, boasting: "Who is greater than us in strength?" (Fussilat 41:15).

## The Call of Hud

Allah sent Hud from among the 'Ad themselves, so that they could not dismiss him as a foreigner or outsider. His message was the same as every prophet before and after him: pure monotheism.

"And to 'Ad We sent their brother Hud. He said: 'O my people, worship Allah; you have no deity other than Him. Will you not then fear Him?'" (Al-A'raf 7:65).

Hud called his people with wisdom and patience. He reminded them that their strength, their wealth, and their civilization were all gifts from Allah, not achievements earned independently of Him. He urged them to seek forgiveness and repent: "O my people, ask forgiveness of your Lord and then repent to Him. He will send rain from the sky upon you in showers and add strength to your strength" (Hud 11:52).

## The Rejection

The chiefs and nobles of 'Ad dismissed Hud with contempt. They accused him of foolishness and of fabricating lies against their gods. "The eminent ones who disbelieved among his people said: 'Indeed, we see you in foolishness, and indeed, we think you are among the liars'" (Al-A'raf 7:66).

They challenged him to bring the punishment he threatened, confident that their idols would protect them and that no force could overcome their might. They said: "O Hud, you have not brought us clear evidence, and we are not ones to leave our gods on your say-so, and we are not believers in you" (Hud 11:53).

Hud responded with dignity and reliance upon Allah: "I put my trust in Allah, my Lord and your Lord. There is no creature but that He holds its forelock. Indeed, my Lord is on a path that is straight" (Hud 11:56).

## The Punishment

When the 'Ad persisted in their rejection, Allah sent upon them a drought. When they saw clouds gathering on the horizon, they rejoiced, thinking rain had finally come. But the Quran corrects their assumption: "Then, when they saw it as a cloud approaching their valleys, they said: 'This is a cloud bringing us rain.' Rather, it is that for which you were impatient — a wind in which is a painful punishment" (Al-Ahqaf 46:24).

The wind was unlike anything witnessed before or since. It raged for seven nights and eight consecutive days without ceasing: "He unleashed it against them for seven nights and eight days in succession, so you would see the people therein fallen as if they were hollow trunks of palm trees" (Al-Haqqah 69:7). Everything the 'Ad had built was leveled. Their bodies were tossed about like uprooted trees. The civilization that had boasted of its strength was erased entirely.

## The Salvation of the Believers

Hud and those who believed with him were saved by the mercy of Allah. "So We saved him and those with him by mercy from Us, and We eliminated those who denied Our signs" (Al-A'raf 7:72). Ibn Kathir notes that the believers were spared from the wind entirely, while the disbelievers were destroyed around them.

## Significance in Islamic Tradition

Hud holds a distinguished place as one of the four Arab prophets, alongside Salih, Shu'ayb, and Muhammad (peace be upon them all). An entire surah of the Quran bears his name (Surah Hud, chapter 11), and the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said that Surah Hud and its sister surahs had caused him to age, due to the weight of the accounts of destroyed nations contained within them (reported by al-Tirmidhi).

The destruction of the 'Ad stands as one of the Quran's clearest demonstrations that worldly power without faith is ultimately worthless. No amount of physical strength, architectural achievement, or material wealth can protect a people who turn away from their Creator. As Allah states: "Did they not consider that Allah, who created them, was greater than them in strength?" (Fussilat 41:15).
