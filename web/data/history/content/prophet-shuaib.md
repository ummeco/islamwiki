## Prophet Shuaib in Islam

Shuaib (peace be upon him) was a prophet sent by Allah to the people of Madyan, a prosperous trading community located in the Hejaz-Sinai region near the Gulf of Aqaba. He is also identified as the prophet sent to the Companions of the Thicket (Ashab al-Aiykah). Known among classical scholars as "Khatib al-Anbiya" (the Orator of the Prophets) for his remarkable eloquence, Shuaib's mission centered on the call to tawhid and the establishment of justice in commercial dealings.

## The People of Madyan

The people of Madyan were descendants of Madyan ibn Ibrahim, and they inhabited a region blessed with trade routes and natural resources. Despite their material prosperity, they had fallen into deep moral corruption. They cheated in weights and measures, defrauded customers, robbed travelers on the roads, and practiced every form of economic injustice. They combined this commercial dishonesty with polytheism and open rejection of divine guidance.

Allah sent Shuaib as one of their own, a brother from among them, to call them back to the straight path. The Quran records his message in Surah al-A'raf (7:85):

> "O my people, worship Allah; you have no deity other than Him. There has come to you clear evidence from your Lord. So fulfill the measure and weight and do not deprive people of their due and do not cause corruption upon the earth after its reformation. That is better for you, if you should be believers."

## His Call to Justice

What distinguishes Shuaib's mission is its particular emphasis on economic ethics alongside the foundational call to tawhid. While every prophet called to the worship of Allah alone, Shuaib was specifically commanded to address the financial corruption that defined his people's way of life. He warned them against sitting on every road, threatening and turning away from the path of Allah those who believed in Him, seeking to make it crooked (al-A'raf 7:86).

In Surah Hud (11:84-85), Shuaib urged them:

> "O my people, give full measure and full weight in justice, and do not deprive the people of their due, and do not commit abuse on the earth, spreading corruption."

He reminded them that what remained lawful from Allah was better for them than their ill-gotten gains, and that he was not set over them as a guardian — only a sincere advisor.

## The Response of His People

The people of Madyan met Shuaib's call with mockery and contempt. They ridiculed him, saying: "O Shuaib, does your prayer command you that we should leave what our fathers worshipped or not do with our wealth what we please?" (Hud 11:87). They considered him weak and openly threatened that were it not for the protection of his clan, they would have stoned him.

Shuaib's response demonstrated his complete reliance on Allah. He said: "O my people, is my clan more respected by you than Allah? And you have put Him behind your backs" (Hud 11:92). He made clear that his trust was in Allah alone, not in tribal loyalty or human protection.

The chiefs among his people gave him an ultimatum: either return to their religion or face expulsion from their land. Shuaib replied that he could not return to what Allah had saved him from, and he left the matter to Allah as the best of judges.

## The Punishment

After exhausting all avenues of guidance, the divine punishment came upon both the people of Madyan and the Companions of the Thicket. The Quran describes their destruction in multiple surahs. In Surah Hud (11:94), it was a devastating shriek (al-sayhah) that left them fallen prone in their homes. In Surah al-Shu'ara (26:189), referring to the Companions of the Thicket, it was the punishment of the Day of the Shade (yawm al-zullah) — an oppressive cloud that gathered above them before raining down a terrible torment.

Ibn Kathir narrates from the early scholars that intense heat struck the people, and when a cloud appeared offering shade, they gathered beneath it seeking relief, only for it to pour upon them fire and destruction. Allah saved Shuaib and those who believed with him.

## Connection to Prophet Musa

Classical scholars, including Ibn Kathir, identify Shuaib as the righteous man of Madyan whom Musa (peace be upon him) encountered after fleeing Egypt. When Musa arrived in Madyan and watered the flocks of the old man's two daughters, the man said: "I wish to wed one of these two daughters of mine to you on condition that you serve me for eight years; but if you complete ten, it will be of your own accord" (al-Qasas 28:27). Musa spent those years in Madyan before returning to Egypt to fulfill his prophetic mission against Pharaoh.

## Lessons from Shuaib's Story

The story of Shuaib carries enduring lessons. It establishes that economic justice is inseparable from faith. Cheating in trade, exploiting the vulnerable, and accumulating wealth through fraud are not merely civil offenses but acts of rebellion against Allah's command. Shuaib's eloquent persistence in the face of ridicule and threat demonstrates the prophetic model of patience and reliance upon Allah. His people's destruction stands as a warning that material prosperity built on injustice invites divine reckoning.
