## The Conquest of Jerusalem (637 CE)

The Muslim conquest of Jerusalem in 637 CE stands among the most significant events of the Rashidun Caliphate and one of the most remarkable episodes of religious tolerance in military history. The peaceful surrender of the holy city to Umar ibn al-Khattab established a model of governance over sacred space that would be cited for centuries.

## Background and Strategic Context

Following the decisive Muslim victory at the Battle of Yarmouk in 636 CE, Byzantine power in the Levant collapsed. The armies under Abu Ubayda ibn al-Jarrah, the supreme commander appointed by Umar, moved systematically through Syria and Palestine. Cities fell in rapid succession: Damascus, Homs, and the coastal towns submitted to Muslim authority.

Jerusalem, known to the Muslims as Iliya (from the Roman Aelia Capitolina) and later as Bayt al-Maqdis, held immense religious significance. It was the site of al-Masjid al-Aqsa, the first qiblah of the Muslims, and the destination of the Prophet Muhammad's night journey (al-Isra wal-Mi'raj). Its conquest was not merely a military objective but a fulfilment of what many companions understood as a prophetic promise.

## The Siege and Sophronius's Condition

Muslim forces surrounded Jerusalem and maintained a siege for several months. The city's defences were strong, and its inhabitants, predominantly Christian under Byzantine administration, initially resisted. The siege was conducted without the brutality that characterised many ancient and medieval military campaigns.

The Patriarch Sophronius, the highest ecclesiastical authority in the city, eventually recognised that resistance was futile. However, he imposed a singular condition: he would surrender the keys of Jerusalem only to the Caliph of the Muslims himself. When this message reached Abu Ubayda, he relayed it to Umar in Medina. After consulting the companions, Umar decided to travel to Jerusalem in person.

## Umar's Journey and Entry

Umar's journey from Medina to Jerusalem became legendary in Islamic historiography. He travelled with minimal escort, carrying only basic provisions. The most frequently cited account describes him arriving with a single servant and one camel, which they rode in turns. When it came time to enter the city, it was the servant's turn to ride, and Umar walked beside him leading the camel by its halter.

Abu Ubayda, seeing the Caliph approach in this humble state, reportedly suggested he ride a horse to make a more impressive entrance before the Romans. Umar struck Abu Ubayda lightly on the chest and said: "We were the most humiliated of people, and Allah honoured us with Islam. If we seek honour in anything other than what Allah honoured us with, Allah will humiliate us."

This entry contrasted sharply with every previous conqueror of Jerusalem, from the Babylonians to the Romans, who had entered in military triumph, often accompanied by destruction and massacre.

## The Covenant of Umar

Upon receiving the city's surrender, Umar issued a guarantee of safety to its inhabitants, known as the Assurance of Safety (al-Uhdah al-Umariyyah). The text, preserved by al-Tabari and other historians, guaranteed the Christians of Jerusalem safety for their persons, their property, their churches, and their crosses. No church was to be demolished or confiscated. No one was to be compelled in matters of religion.

The covenant read in part: "This is the assurance of safety which the servant of God, Umar, the Commander of the Faithful, has given to the people of Iliya. He has given them an assurance of safety for themselves, their property, their churches, their crosses... Their churches will not be inhabited by Muslims and will not be destroyed."

## Treatment of the Jewish Community

One of the most historically significant aspects of the conquest was Umar's treatment of the Jewish population. Under Byzantine rule, Jews had been expelled from Jerusalem and forbidden from residing there. Umar reversed this policy, permitting Jewish families to return and settle in the city. This act of justice was remembered by Jewish historians for centuries and stood in contrast to both earlier Byzantine and later Crusader policies.

## Umar at the Temple Mount

When Sophronius took Umar on a tour of the city's holy sites, including the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the time for prayer arrived. Sophronius invited Umar to pray inside the church. Umar declined, explaining that if he prayed there, future Muslims might claim it as a mosque. Instead, he prayed outside, preserving the church's status as a Christian place of worship. A mosque was later built at the spot where he prayed, away from the church.

Umar then asked to be taken to the site of Masjid al-Aqsa. He found the Temple Mount being used as a rubbish dump by the Byzantines. Umar personally began clearing the debris, and a simple prayer space was established, which would later develop into the al-Aqsa mosque complex.

## Legacy and Historical Significance

The conquest of Jerusalem under Umar set the standard for Muslim governance of multi-faith cities. For over four centuries, Christians, Jews, and Muslims lived together under Muslim rule with their religious rights protected. When the Crusaders captured Jerusalem in 1099, they massacred the Muslim and Jewish inhabitants indiscriminately. When Salah al-Din recaptured the city in 1187, he consciously emulated Umar's model, sparing the population and restoring access to all faiths.

The event remains a powerful demonstration that Islamic governance, when practised according to the prophetic model, upheld justice and protected the rights of non-Muslims. The Covenant of Umar continues to be cited as one of the earliest documents of religious tolerance in recorded history.
